# <span id="page-2-0"></span>**UPDATED COMPARATIVE EVALUATION OF GROUNDWATER DATA**

## **1.0 INTRODUCTION**

The California Department of Transportation (Caltrans) Modesto Encapsulated Soil Stockpiles (the site) is located along recently constructed State Route (SR) 132 south of the SR 99/Kansas Avenue interchange in Modesto, Stanislaus County, California (Site Location Map, Figure 1).

In 2014, EMKO Environmental, Inc. conducted a comparative evaluation of groundwater data from the Caltrans Modesto Soil Stockpiles and FMC Corporation's (FMC) Modesto barite/celestite processing plant (EMKO, 2014, referred to herein as the 2014 Evaluation). The 2014 Evaluation was based primarily on various statistical parameters, such as background values calculated using the 95-percent upper confidence limit, mean concentrations, and trend analysis. This report provides an updated comparative evaluation by incorporating groundwater data collected from Caltrans monitoring wells since 2014 into the analysis.

According to the Central Valley Regional Water Quality Control Board (RWQCB, 1998), FMC and its predecessors operated a chemical processing facility at 1200 Barium Road (now Graphics Drive) beginning in 1929. The facility processed barium and strontium minerals (barite and celestite) and other materials to produce a variety of industrial chemicals. From the early 1950s to the late 1970s, liquid wastes were discharged to seven unlined ponds. Figure 2 shows the location of the former FMC facility.

Operations at the FMC facility resulted in impacts to soil and groundwater. Constituents identified in groundwater include elevated pH levels, nitrate, sulfate, sulfide, total dissolved solids (TDS), arsenic, barium, and strontium (RWQCB, 1998). Almost 1 billion gallons of groundwater have been extracted and treated since 1996 (Parsons, 2013).

According to Shaw (2007), the State of California purchased a 4.3-acre parcel at the southwest corner of the FMC facility in 1961 for right-of-way needed to construct SR 99. Part of the southernmost unlined pond on the FMC property was within the area purchased by the State. During construction of SR 99, soil in and around the impoundments was excavated and placed in three separate stockpiles within the Caltrans rightof-way at the location of the planned SR 99/SR 132 interchange project. As shown on Figure 2, the stockpiles consisted of:

- Stockpile 1, located south of Kansas Avenue and west of North Emerald Avenue;
- Stockpile 2, located south of Kansas Avenue, between North Emerald Avenue and SR 99; and
- Stockpile 3, located south of Kansas Avenue and east of SR 99.

In 2006, Caltrans installed eight groundwater monitoring wells (MW1 through MW8) along the stockpiles. In 2012, two additional wells were installed just south of Kansas Avenue, MW9 just west of SR 99 and MW10 just east of SR 99. The Caltrans monitoring well locations are shown on Figure 2.

The wells installed for Caltrans in 2006 and 2012 were constructed to evaluate the potential for FMCrelated constituents in the soil stockpiles to affect groundwater quality. Therefore, the Caltrans monitoring wells were screened across first-encountered groundwater. The boreholes were drilled to depths ranging from 41 to 48 feet below ground surface, with typically 10 feet of screen above the bottom of the borehole. The FMC facility-related monitoring wells, which are located mostly to the north of the soil stockpiles with a few wells located to the east, south, and west of the stockpiles, in contrast, were installed to evaluate the nature and extent of a known groundwater contaminant plume and to monitor the effectiveness of the groundwater extraction system. Therefore, the FMC monitoring wells were constructed with 20 to 30 feet of screen, with the top of the screened interval typically occurring at about 45 to 55 feet below ground surface, within sand and silty-sand units that are generally more continuous than those encountered at the water table. The Caltrans monitoring wells were constructed for a different purpose than the FMC monitoring wells, resulting in different screen depths and intervals. These differences are appropriate given the different purposes of the wells.

Since the 2014 Evaluation was conducted, several significant changes have occurred that affect the soil stockpiles and the Caltrans groundwater monitoring network. In 2015, the groundwater monitoring frequency was reduced from quarterly to annually. In 2019, six of the ten Caltrans groundwater monitoring wells were decommissioned, such that only wells MW4, MW6, MW9 and MW10 are still present. Construction of Phase I of the SR 99/SR 132 interchange project began in January 2020. Stockpile 3 was removed and the material placed on Stockpiles 1 and 2. In addition, Stockpiles 1 and 2 were encapsulated beneath the roadway for SR 132 and a layer of clean fill and vegetated soil on the northern slope of SR 132 by October 2021. SR 132 was opened to traffic in November 2022.

## **2.0 SUMMARY OF 2014 EVALUATION**

<span id="page-3-0"></span>Review of site information and monitoring data for the FMC facility (Parsons, 2009, 2013; RWQCB 1998) indicated that there are several constituents that have been detected in groundwater beneath the FMC property at elevated concentrations, including elevated pH levels, nitrate, sulfate, sulfide, TDS, arsenic, barium, and strontium. Based on an initial screening, which compared regional data and localized background levels with data from the Caltrans wells, the 2014 Evaluation concluded that TDS, sulfate, barium, and strontium may be present in groundwater beneath the Caltrans right-of-way at concentrations statistically different than naturally-occurring levels.

The detailed analysis of concentration trends and distributions of TDS, sulfate, barium, and strontium in the 2014 Evaluation resulted in the following conclusions:

1. There is a regional distribution, or background, of several constituents at concentrations that are equivalent to or greater than those observed in Caltrans monitoring wells, including for barium and strontium.

- 2. Comparison of elevated upgradient background concentrations with those in Caltrans wells MW1, MW2, MW3, and MW4, and the trend over time in MW1, clearly demonstrates that Caltrans Stockpile 1 and the western part of Stockpile 2 are not impacting groundwater.
- 3. Caltrans wells MW5, MW6, and MW10 typically have elevated levels of most constituents of concern. The concentrations in these wells are decreasing over time. However, over the period during which the Caltrans wells have been monitored, there have been no changes to the conditions at the Caltrans soil stockpiles that would affect contaminant source mass or leaching. Thus, the most plausible explanation for the decreasing trends is the reduction of source mass by the FMC groundwater extraction and treatment system, demonstrating that the groundwater impacts in wells MW5, MW6, and MW10 did not originate from the Caltrans soil stockpiles.
- 4. The concentrations of the target analytes in downgradient Caltrans wells MW7 and MW8 are lower than those in upgradient wells MW6 and MW10. The decreasing trends in upgradient wells MW6 and MW10 (Conclusion 3, above) and the lower concentrations in downgradient wells MW7 and MW8 demonstrate that Caltrans Stockpile 3 and the eastern part of Stockpile 2 are not impacting groundwater.
- 5. The data obtained from the Caltrans monitoring wells does not provide any additional insight or unique results with respect to groundwater concentrations. Stated another way, the Caltrans data would not significantly alter contour maps of constituent concentrations that could be drawn using only FMC facility data. For example, the concentrations in Caltrans wells MW1, MW2, and MW4 are typically between the concentrations measured in FMC wells M-154 and M-157, located northwest and south of the stockpiles, respectively. Likewise, the concentrations in Caltrans wells MW6 and MW10 are typically between the concentrations measured in FMC wells M-101 and M-113, located north and southeast of the stockpiles, respectively. Therefore, it would be appropriate to substantially reduce the monitoring frequency of the Caltrans wells, or to eliminate monitoring of the Caltrans wells.

As noted above, based in part on the recommendations of the 2014 Evaluation, the monitoring frequency of the Caltrans wells was reduced from quarterly to annually in 2015 and six of the ten wells were decommissioned in 2019 prior to highway construction activities.

## **3.0 UPDATED EVALUATION**

<span id="page-4-0"></span>As noted above, only Caltrans wells MW4, MW6, MW9 and MW10 are still present. Thus, this updated evaluation addresses the monitoring results for TDS, sulfate, barium, and strontium that have been obtained in the four remaining wells since 2014. A summary of laboratory analysis results is in Table 1.

Due to decreasing regional groundwater levels, the four remaining Caltrans groundwater monitoring wells have been dry on numerous occasions since 2014. As discussed above, the Caltrans wells were screened across the water table at the time they were installed, whereas the FMC wells were screened at depths that are at least 10 feet to 20 feet deeper. As indicated in Table 1, the four remaining Caltrans monitoring wells had insufficient water volume available to sample during the following times:

MW4 – September 2014, May 2016, March 2022 through February 2023 (4 of 11 measurements).

MW6 – September 2014 through April 2017, March 2022 through February 2023 (6 of 11 measurements).

MW9 – September 2014 through April 2017, April 2019, March 2021 through February 2023 (8 of 11 measurements).

MW10 – September 2014 through April 2017, April 2019 through February 2023 (9 of 11 measurements).

Figures 3 through 6 show the TDS, sulfate, barium, and strontium data trends, respectively, for the four remaining Caltrans wells. These figures show the data used for the 2014 Evaluation in addition to the results obtained since that time[1](#page-5-0) .

For TDS, Figure 3 shows that the concentrations in the remaining monitoring wells are in the same range as those previously reported. The same trends and relationships are shown for sulfate on Figure 4. In addition, since 2019 the sulfate concentration in the remaining Caltrans monitoring wells has been less than 50 milligrams per liter (mg/l).

Barium concentrations (Figure 5) remain consistent with those that were measured prior to the 2014 Evaluation and are comparable to the FMC upgradient background concentration of 151 micrograms per liter (µg/l). The barium concentrations in the remaining Caltrans wells are well below the U.S. EPA drinking-water health advisory (DWHA) concentration of 700 µg/l and are about 40 times less than the health-based site-specific target level for barium in groundwater at the FMC facility of 6,210 µg/l approved by the Central Valley Regional Water Quality Control Board in the Remedial Action Plan (ERM, 2020).

As with the other constituents discussed above, strontium concentrations (Figure 6) remain consistent with those that were measured prior to the 2014 Evaluation. The strontium concentrations in the remaining Caltrans wells are, on average, about four times less than the U.S. EPA DWHA concentration of 4,000 µg/l and are more than 20 times less than the health-based site-specific target level for strontium in groundwater at the FMC facility of 61,900 µg/l approved by the Central Valley Regional Water Quality Control Board in the Remedial Action Plan (ERM, 2020).

# **4.0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS**

<span id="page-6-0"></span>The 2014 Evaluation concluded that FMC constituents within the Modesto soil stockpiles within the Caltrans right-of-way have not impacted groundwater. Since that time, Stockpile 3 has been removed and the soil placed on top of Stockpiles 1 and 2. In addition, construction of the SR 99/SR 132 interchange and the SR 132 roadway have encapsulated Stockpiles 1 and 2, with the northern sides covered with clean fill and vegetation.

Groundwater data obtained from the four remaining Caltrans monitoring wells confirms that the constituent concentrations have remained stable. Furthermore, due to the shallow nature of the Caltrans wells and decreasing regional groundwater levels, the wells are often dry during the annual sampling events that have occurred since 2014.

Phase II construction for SR 132 is scheduled to begin in 2026 and will fully encapsulate the soil stockpiles beneath highway pavement and perimeter retaining walls. In addition, the Phase II work is anticipated to require the removal of well MW9 due to construction of a retaining wall and the removal of MW10 due to construction of a new pump station.

The evaluation and analysis presented in this report demonstrate that the encapsulated soil stockpiles do not have the potential to impact groundwater and that future monitoring data from the four remaining Caltrans wells will not provide any useful data with respect to further analysis of the stockpiles. Based on the consistency of the more recent data with that used for the 2014 Evaluation, the declining water table, and the final encapsulation of the remaining stockpiles during Phase II construction, we recommend that the remaining four Caltrans monitoring wells be decommissioned and that groundwater monitoring related to the soil stockpiles be discontinued.
