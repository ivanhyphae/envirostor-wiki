## Definition

A **Supplemental Site Characterization Workplan** is a project-specific plan that describes additional field investigation and data collection needed to better define site conditions beyond an initial or prior site characterization. In environmental remediation and geotechnical/soil management contexts, it is used when existing information is not sufficient to support decisions about contamination extent, exposure pathways, soil handling, or remedial design.

The workplan typically identifies:
- the purpose of the supplemental investigation,
- the areas to be sampled or inspected,
- the media of concern (soil, groundwater, sediment, air/fenceline dust, etc.),
- sampling methods and analytical parameters,
- field quality assurance/quality control requirements,
- health and safety considerations,
- data reporting and decision criteria.

In the source documents, the concept appears in the context of Caltrans-related environmental characterization and a fenceline investigation around soil stockpiles, where the plan is used to gather additional information needed before or during site management actions.

## How it works

A Supplemental Site Characterization Workplan functions as a bridge between an existing site understanding and the next management decision.

### 1. Defines the information gap
The first step is to identify what is not yet known. Common gaps include:
- horizontal or vertical extent of impacted soil,
- whether stockpiles are contributing dust or other offsite migration,
- whether observed field conditions match previous assumptions,
- whether additional contaminant delineation is needed for disposal or reuse decisions.

### 2. Sets investigation objectives
The workplan converts the information gap into explicit objectives, such as:
- delineate impacted soil boundaries,
- assess fenceline conditions,
- confirm whether contamination is localized or widespread,
- support selection of remedial alternatives,
- verify compliance with management thresholds.

### 3. Specifies field activities
The plan usually details the investigation design:
- sampling locations, number, and spacing,
- depth intervals,
- screening and collection methods,
- laboratory analyses,
- field observations and photographic documentation,
- survey controls or mapping methods.

For a fenceline workplan, activities may include perimeter sampling, dust or particulate monitoring, visual inspections, and comparison of conditions upwind/downwind of stockpiles or other source areas.

### 4. Builds in quality controls
To make the data defensible, the workplan describes:
- duplicate samples,
- blanks,
- equipment decontamination,
- chain of custody,
- calibrated instruments,
- data validation procedures.

### 5. Establishes decision logic
The supplemental characterization is usually tied to decision points. For example:
- if contaminants exceed a threshold, expand sampling;
- if fenceline data indicate migration, implement controls;
- if results show conditions are bounded, proceed to the next project phase.

### 6. Produces updated site understanding
The final output is a refined conceptual site model and a stronger basis for action. Results may inform:
- excavation limits,
- stockpile management,
- dust suppression measures,
- risk assessment,
- remediation design,
- regulatory reporting.

## Variants

Common variants and closely related implementations include:

- **Supplemental Soil Characterization Workplan**: focused specifically on soil delineation or confirmation sampling.
- **Fenceline Workplan**: emphasizes perimeter monitoring to evaluate offsite migration from stockpiles or active work areas.
- **Phase II / Additional Site Investigation Plan**: broader terms used when supplemental data collection follows an initial investigation.
- **Confirmatory Sampling Plan**: narrower in scope, aimed at verifying that a specific condition or cleanup endpoint has been achieved.
- **Remedial Investigation Addendum**: an update to an existing investigation document rather than a standalone workplan.
- **Stockpile Characterization Plan**: used where excavated soil stockpiles must be classified for reuse, treatment, or disposal.

The terminology often varies by agency, project phase, and regulatory framework, but the underlying function is the same: targeted additional characterization to reduce uncertainty.

## Trade-offs

### Benefits
- Improves decision quality by reducing uncertainty.
- Can prevent under- or over-excavation, saving cost and time.
- Provides a defensible record for regulators and stakeholders.
- Helps verify whether mitigation measures are working.

### Limitations
- Adds schedule, labor, and laboratory costs.
- Results may still be limited by sampling density or seasonal conditions.
- A supplemental plan can reveal more complexity, leading to additional work.
- If the conceptual site model is weak, even good field data may not fully resolve site questions.

### Key considerations
- Sampling density must balance cost against the need to adequately delineate conditions.
- Analytical methods should match the contaminants of concern and decision thresholds.
- Fenceline or perimeter monitoring should account for wind direction, weather, and operational variability.
- Workplans should be precise enough to produce usable data but flexible enough to adapt to field conditions.
- Data quality objectives should be explicit so the results support the intended regulatory or engineering decision.
