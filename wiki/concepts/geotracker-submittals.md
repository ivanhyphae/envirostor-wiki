## Definition

**Geotracker Submittals** are formal environmental monitoring submissions prepared from geotracker-associated groundwater data and reporting workflows. In the context of the Modesto Stockpiles groundwater monitoring reports, they refer to the packaged monthly or periodic submittal materials that compile field measurements, laboratory results, and interpretive summaries for regulatory or project oversight review.

These submittals typically document groundwater conditions at one or more monitoring events, often including:
- water levels and well measurements,
- sample collection dates,
- analytical results for constituents of concern,
- comparison to prior monitoring rounds,
- notes on data quality, anomalies, or field conditions,
- and a submission-ready report structure suitable for agency review.

In practice, a Geotracker submittal is not just raw data; it is a regulatory reporting deliverable built from monitored environmental data and prepared in a standardized format.

## How it works

Geotracker submittals follow a recurring monitoring-and-reporting cycle:

1. **Field monitoring**
   - Groundwater monitoring wells are visited on a scheduled basis.
   - Technicians measure groundwater elevations, inspect wells, and collect groundwater samples according to the project plan.
   - Field observations may include purge volumes, stabilization notes, weather, and any deviations from standard procedures.

2. **Sample analysis**
   - Collected samples are sent to a laboratory for analysis of target constituents.
   - Results are returned with detection limits, qualifiers, and method information.

3. **Data review and validation**
   - Results are checked for completeness and consistency.
   - Comparisons are made against earlier sampling events to identify trends or changes.
   - Any data qualifiers, non-detects, or anomalies are interpreted in the report context.

4. **Report assembly**
   - Monitoring data are organized into a structured submittal.
   - The report usually includes tables, figures, groundwater elevation maps or contouring, concentration summaries, and narrative discussion.
   - The document is formatted to align with agency or project requirements.

5. **Submission**
   - The final package is uploaded or transmitted as the official submittal for the monitoring period.
   - In projects using Geotracker, the submittal may be tied to a specific regulatory record, site, or monitoring event.

In the source material for the Modesto Stockpiles project, the documents are groundwater monitoring reports for different months and years, which indicates that Geotracker submittals function as time-stamped compliance deliverables rather than one-time reports. Each round becomes part of the site’s environmental monitoring record.

## Variants

Common variants or closely related implementations include:

- **Monthly groundwater monitoring submittals**  
  Routine reporting packages prepared on a monthly schedule.

- **Quarterly or periodic monitoring reports**  
  Similar submissions prepared less frequently, depending on permit or corrective-action requirements.

- **Site-specific Geotracker uploads**  
  Digital submissions associated with a specific site record in an environmental data management system.

- **Narrative-only compliance submittals**  
  Reports emphasizing interpretation and compliance status, sometimes with fewer tables or figures than a full technical report.

- **Data-heavy technical appendices**  
  Supplementary submissions that focus on analytical tables, field logs, chain-of-custody records, or laboratory certificates.

- **Alternative environmental reporting systems**  
  In some jurisdictions or projects, equivalent monitoring submittals may be prepared for other databases or regulatory portals rather than Geotracker specifically.

## Trade-offs

Geotracker submittals provide clear regulatory and operational benefits, but they also introduce constraints:

- **Standardization vs. flexibility**  
  Standard formats improve consistency and reviewability, but can limit how much project-specific nuance is presented.

- **Traceability vs. administrative burden**  
  Detailed documentation supports auditability and historical comparison, but requires significant preparation and QA effort.

- **Timeliness vs. completeness**  
  Rapid submittals help maintain compliance schedules, but late laboratory results or unresolved data issues can delay final reporting.

- **Digital efficiency vs. data quality dependence**  
  Electronic submission workflows streamline filing, but they rely on accurate field data, correct metadata, and careful validation.

- **Comparability vs. changing methods**  
  Repeated monitoring rounds are valuable because they are comparable over time, but changes in sampling methods, well conditions, or laboratory procedures can complicate trend interpretation.

For groundwater programs like the Modesto Stockpiles monitoring reports, the main limitation is that the value of the submittal depends heavily on the quality and consistency of the underlying field and laboratory work.
