## Definition

A **CERCLA/NCP Feasibility Study** is the formal evaluation step used in the CERCLA cleanup process to compare and develop remedial alternatives for a contaminated site in accordance with the NCP (National Contingency Plan). It is typically the technical and engineering analysis that follows a remedial investigation and supports selection of a cleanup action in a Record of Decision.

In practice, a feasibility study answers questions such as:

- What cleanup options are technically practicable?
- How effective is each option at reducing risk?
- What are the implementability, cost, and long-term maintenance implications?
- Which alternative best satisfies statutory and regulatory cleanup criteria?

The source material reflects this concept in the context of a site-specific remedial design and cleanup plan for a stockpile area, where alternatives were developed for containment, capping, and long-term management.

## How it works

A CERCLA/NCP Feasibility Study is usually built around a structured alternatives analysis.

### 1. Site understanding
The study begins with information from the Remedial Investigation and site characterization:

- nature and extent of contamination
- contaminant concentrations and distribution
- exposure pathways
- hydrogeology, soil conditions, and land use
- receptors and risk drivers

For the source document, this kind of analysis is represented by figures such as site plans, aerial photographs, conceptual site exposure models, and cross-sections, which are used to understand the stockpile geometry and how contaminants might be contained or exposed.

### 2. Development of remedial alternatives
Potential cleanup alternatives are identified and grouped into categories, often including:

- no action or monitored natural attenuation
- institutional controls
- excavation and off-site disposal
- containment/capping
- treatment
- combination remedies

Alternatives are usually designed to meet the threshold requirement of protecting human health and the environment while addressing Applicable or Relevant and Appropriate Requirements (ARARs) or other cleanup criteria.

### 3. Screening
Unworkable alternatives are screened out based on feasibility criteria such as:

- technical impracticability
- inability to meet cleanup objectives
- unacceptable risk
- excessive implementation constraints

### 4. Detailed evaluation
Remaining alternatives are compared against the common CERCLA/NCP balancing factors, which generally include:

- overall protection of human health and the environment
- compliance with ARARs
- long-term effectiveness and permanence
- reduction of toxicity, mobility, or volume through treatment
- short-term effectiveness
- implementability
- cost
- state acceptance
- community acceptance

### 5. Remedy recommendation
The feasibility study usually concludes with a recommended alternative. That recommendation becomes the basis for the proposed remedy and subsequent decision document.

### 6. Engineering and design support
In some projects, the feasibility study also feeds directly into remedial design by describing:

- cap thickness and materials
- containment layers
- grading and drainage
- monitoring requirements
- maintenance obligations
- contingency measures

The source material indicates this type of relationship by including stockpile containment plans and cross-sections, suggesting the study was not just conceptual but tied to a buildable remedy.

## Variants

Common variants of a CERCLA/NCP Feasibility Study include:

### Site-specific feasibility study
The standard form used for a particular contaminated site. It evaluates alternatives tailored to site conditions and contamination patterns.

### Focused feasibility study
A narrower study used when the remedy space is already constrained, such as when only a few practical alternatives remain.

### Treatability study-supported feasibility study
A feasibility study that relies on Treatability Study results to determine whether treatment technologies are viable.

### Interim action feasibility study
Used when an early or partial cleanup is needed before final remediation. These are common when immediate risk reduction is necessary.

### Alternatives analysis under state or non-CERCLA programs
Similar in structure to a CERCLA/NCP feasibility study but not governed directly by CERCLA. Comparable evaluations may appear in state cleanup programs, brownfield projects, or Resource Conservation and Recovery Act corrective action contexts.

### Engineering feasibility study
A term sometimes used in practice for the design-oriented version of the analysis, especially when the selected remedy is largely an engineering containment solution such as a cap, slurry wall, or stockpile cover system.

## Trade-offs

CERCLA/NCP feasibility studies involve several recurring trade-offs:

### Protection vs. permanence
More permanent remedies, such as excavation and treatment, may provide stronger long-term protection but can be expensive and disruptive. Containment solutions may be faster and cheaper but depend on ongoing maintenance.

### Treatment vs. containment
Treatment reduces contaminant toxicity, mobility, or volume, but may be technically difficult or infeasible for some waste types. Containment is often easier to implement but leaves contaminants in place.

### Short-term vs. long-term impacts
A remedy that is effective long term may create more short-term construction impacts, dust, emissions, noise, or worker exposure.

### Cost vs. robustness
Lower-cost alternatives may be less durable or require more institutional controls and monitoring. Higher-cost options may reduce future liabilities and maintenance needs.

### Implementability constraints
Some alternatives look attractive on paper but are difficult to construct because of site access, weather, geotechnical conditions, utility conflicts, or waste handling limitations.

### Regulatory compliance
The remedy must satisfy CERCLA and NCP requirements, including ARARs where applicable, which can eliminate otherwise attractive options.

### Uncertainty
Feasibility studies often rely on incomplete subsurface information and uncertain contaminant behavior. This can affect remedy selection and may require conservative design assumptions.

For the source context, a stockpile remedy appears to favor containment and capping, which is often chosen when excavation or treatment of the stockpile is less practical than isolating it with an engineered cover and long-term controls.
