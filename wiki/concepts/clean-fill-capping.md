## Definition

Clean fill capping is an environmental remediation technique in which a layer of uncontaminated soil, aggregate, or other approved material is placed over contaminated soil or waste to physically isolate the underlying pollutants. The cap acts as a barrier that prevents direct human contact with contaminants, reduces dust and erosion, limits infiltration of precipitation that could leach contaminants into groundwater, and provides a stable surface for future land use. The approach is commonly used at brownfield sites, landfills, and former industrial properties where excavation of all contaminated material is not feasible or cost-effective.

## How it works

Clean fill capping typically follows these steps:

1. **Site characterization and consolidation** – Contaminated soil is identified, excavated as needed, and consolidated into designated containment zones. For example, at a former highway construction site, barium-containing soil (BCS) stockpiles were consolidated into two main containment zones totaling over 100,000 cubic yards.

2. **Grading and preparation** – The contaminated surface is graded to promote drainage and prevent ponding. If the cap is placed over stockpiles, the piles may be shaped to a stable slope.

3. **Material selection and approval** – The capping material must be approved by the regulatory agency (e.g., California Department of Toxic Substances Control, DTSC). In the documented case, a minimum of 4 inches of Class 3 aggregate subbase was used as an interim cap, approved through variances that waived certain clean-fill standards because the material was already onsite and deemed suitable.

4. **Placement and compaction** – The clean fill is spread evenly across the contaminated area and compacted to engineering specifications to ensure stability and minimize settlement. Compaction testing is performed (e.g., by a geotechnical firm such as Kleinfelder).

5. **Erosion control and vegetation** – If the cap is intended as a permanent or long‑term cover, it may be seeded with grass or planted with native vegetation to reduce erosion and provide aesthetic benefit. Records from the source project indicate that a rain event in January 2021 caused erosion on unvegetated slopes, which had to be repaired.

6. **Monitoring and verification** – After capping, ongoing monitoring is typically required:
   - **Air monitoring** for particulate matter (PM‑10) and specific contaminants (barium, lead) to ensure action levels are not exceeded.
   - **Groundwater monitoring** to detect any leaching or off‑site migration.
   - **Stormwater monitoring** to confirm runoff meets discharge limits.
   - **Statistical evaluation** (e.g., using ProUCL) to verify contaminant levels remain below thresholds. In the example, verification thresholds were barium ≤ 1,000 mg/kg and lead ≤ 80 mg/kg.

7. **Land use controls** – A land use covenant is recorded to restrict future activities that could breach the cap (e.g., excavation). The covenant often requires periodic reviews (e.g., every five years) and a supplemental remediation plan before any final closure.

## Variants

Clean fill capping can take several forms depending on site conditions, regulatory requirements, and intended end use:

- **Interim vs. final caps** – An interim cap is a temporary cover intended to control exposure during a longer remediation process; it may be thinner and may later be replaced or supplemented. A final cap is part of the site’s permanent closure.
- **Minimal thickness caps** – As in the example, a 4‑inch aggregate base provides a low‑cost, rapid cover that satisfies interim requirements.
- **Engineered covers** – More robust designs incorporate multiple layers: a low‑permeability layer (e.g., clay or geomembrane), a drainage layer, and a topsoil layer for vegetation. These are common for landfill closures.
- **Soil vs. aggregate caps** – Clean soil can be used when native material is available, but aggregate (e.g., Class 3 subbase) may be preferred for its strength and drainage characteristics.
- **Vegetated vs. non‑vegetated caps** – Vegetated caps reduce erosion but require maintenance; non‑vegetated aggregate caps are lower maintenance but may be less aesthetically pleasing.
- **Clean fill as a cover for consolidation** – Sometimes contaminated soil from multiple stockpiles is merged and then covered with clean fill to create a single, manageable containment zone (as with the BCS stockpiles in the source).

## Trade-offs

- **Cost vs. permanence** – Clean fill capping is generally less expensive than full excavation and off‑site disposal. However, a cap does not remove contamination; it merely isolates it. Long‑term monitoring and maintenance costs must be factored in.
- **Land use restrictions** – A covenant restricts future use (e.g., no underground utilities, no residential construction without further remediation). This limits property value and future development options.
- **Erosion vulnerability** – Unvegetated caps, especially thin aggregate layers, can be damaged by heavy rain, as seen in the January 2021 event. Repairs add cost and delay.
- **Regulatory timelines** – Approval processes (e.g., variances for clean fill specifications) can extend schedules. The documented project required two DTSC variances just for the cap material.
- **Uncertainty in verification** – Statistical methods (e.g., 95% UCL of contaminant concentrations) may indicate that the cap is suitable, but hotspots below the cap can remain. The example found that MSE wall footing material had barium levels exceeding site‑specific background, yet it was still approved as capping material.
- **Groundwater protection** – A thin or permeable cap may not fully prevent infiltration. If groundwater contamination is already present, a cap alone is insufficient; active groundwater remediation may be needed.
- **Public perception** – “Capping” can be seen as a less permanent solution than removal, which may affect community acceptance.
