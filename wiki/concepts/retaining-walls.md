## Definition
A retaining wall is a structure designed to hold back soil or rock from a building, structure, or area. Retaining walls are used to prevent erosion, create level terraces on sloped terrain, and support vertical or near-vertical grade changes. They are typically constructed from materials such as reinforced concrete, masonry, stone, timber, or steel, and must resist lateral earth pressure, surcharge loads, and sometimes water pressure.

## How it works
Retaining walls function by transferring the lateral forces exerted by retained soil into their foundation and, in many cases, into the soil mass behind them. The key forces include:

- **Active earth pressure**: The outward push from the retained soil, which depends on soil type, cohesion, and angle of internal friction.
- **Passive earth pressure**: The resisting force from soil in front of the wall (toe side) that helps stabilize it against sliding.
- **Surcharge loads**: Additional vertical loads on the retained soil (e.g., vehicles, buildings, or stockpiles) that increase lateral pressure.
- **Hydrostatic pressure**: Water buildup behind the wall (from rainfall or groundwater) can significantly increase lateral forces; drainage provisions (weep holes, gravel drains) are essential.

The structure resists these forces through its own weight (gravity walls), bending strength (cantilever walls), or tensile reinforcement (anchored or mechanically stabilized earth walls). Proper design requires geotechnical analysis to determine soil properties, stability against overturning (rotation about the toe), sliding along the base, bearing capacity failure of the foundation soil, and tolerable settlement.

## Variants

### Gravity walls
Rely on their mass (concrete, stone, or masonry) to resist lateral forces. Generally used for low to moderate heights (typically ≤ 2–3 m). They do not require reinforcement but require a wide base. Examples: massive concrete walls, stone structures.

### Cantilever walls
Reinforced concrete walls shaped like an inverted T (or L) – a vertical stem and horizontal base slab. The weight of the soil on the base heel provides additional stabilizing force. Common for heights up to 6–8 m.

### Counterfort walls
Similar to cantilever walls but with vertical slab stiffeners (counterforts) on the back side at regular intervals, reducing bending moments. Used when cantilever design becomes uneconomical (heights > 6 m).

### Anchored walls
Thin concrete or shotcrete walls supported by tension anchors drilled into the soil or rock behind. Anchors may be grouted and post-tensioned. Suitable for large heights and constrained spaces (e.g., highway cuts).

### Mechanically Stabilized Earth (MSE) walls
Alternate layers of compacted fill and reinforcing elements (geogrids or metal strips) faced with concrete panels or blocks. The fill’s weight and internal friction provide stability. Economical for moderate to high walls and tolerant of differential settlement.

### Sheet pile walls
Thin, interlocking steel, vinyl, or timber piles driven into the ground to form a continuous barrier. Typically used for temporary excavations, waterfront structures, or soft soil conditions.

### Secant pile walls and diaphragm walls
Drilled shafts (piles) or slurry trench walls forming a continuous concrete barrier with reinforcement. Used for deep excavations and waterproofing (basements, tunnels).

## Trade-offs

| Aspect | Advantages | Limitations |
|--------|------------|-------------|
| **Cost** | MSE and gravity walls often cheaper for low heights; anchor walls save material for tall walls | Sheet pile and diaphragm walls expensive due to installation equipment |
| **Height** | Counterfort and anchored walls can exceed 15 m | Gravity walls uneconomical above ~3 m without very wide base |
| **Space** | Cantilever walls require sufficient toe and heel length; MSE walls need wide backfill zone | Sheet pile walls minimal footprint but need headroom for driving |
| **Drainage** | Proper drainage reduces hydrostatic pressure; weep holes and drainage blankets standard | Poor drainage can cause wall failure; clogging requires maintenance |
| **Aesthetics** | MSE and modular block walls can be faced with attractive panels; stone gravity walls natural | Concrete cantilever walls often utilitarian; sheet pile walls industrial |
| **Settlement tolerance** | MSE and gravity walls tolerate moderate differential settlement | Cantilever and anchored walls sensitive to foundation movement |
| **Construction speed** | Precast panels (MSE) and sheet piles fast; anchored walls require staged grouting | Cast-in-place concrete walls involve formwork and curing time |
| **Maintenance** | Drainage systems need periodic inspection; steel sheet piles prone to corrosion in aggressive soils | Concrete walls generally low maintenance but can crack from thermal stress or seismic loading |
