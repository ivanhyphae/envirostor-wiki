## Definition

A **Storm Water Sampling and Analysis Plan** (often abbreviated **SWSAP**) is a formal document that defines how stormwater runoff will be collected, tested, evaluated, and reported for a site, project, or regulated facility. It specifies **what** will be sampled, **where** and **when** samples will be taken, **which analytical parameters** will be measured, **how** samples will be handled and preserved, and **how results** will be interpreted against applicable permit or regulatory criteria.

In practice, a SWSAP is used to support compliance with stormwater management requirements, document runoff quality, identify pollutant sources, and verify whether site controls are effective.

## How it works

A Storm Water Sampling and Analysis Plan typically follows a sequence of operational steps:

1. **Define the purpose and regulatory basis**
   - The plan identifies the compliance driver, such as an industrial stormwater permit, construction permit, consent order, or internal environmental monitoring program.
   - It establishes the monitoring objectives, which may include characterizing runoff quality, demonstrating permit compliance, or evaluating best management practices (BMPs).

2. **Identify sampling locations**
   - Sampling points are selected where stormwater runoff is representative of site discharge.
   - Locations often include permitted outfalls, drainage basins, detention basin discharges, or specific conveyance points.
   - Selection aims to avoid unrepresentative conditions such as excessive sediment deposition, stagnant flow, or obvious cross-connections with non-stormwater sources.

3. **Establish event-based sampling conditions**
   - Stormwater sampling is generally triggered by qualifying rainfall events.
   - The plan sets event criteria such as minimum antecedent dry period, rainfall depth, and runoff generation requirements.
   - Sampling is usually performed early in the storm event, because the “first flush” often contains the highest pollutant concentrations.

4. **Define sample type and collection method**
   - The plan specifies whether samples will be:
     - **Grab samples** taken at a specific time during runoff, or
     - **Composite samples** made from multiple aliquots collected over time or flow.
   - Stormwater compliance programs often prefer grab samples for event-based characterization, while some programs use flow-weighted composites where permitted.

5. **Specify analytes and analytical methods**
   - The plan lists the parameters to be analyzed, which commonly include:
     - total suspended solids (TSS),
     - pH,
     - oil and grease,
     - conductivity,
     - metals,
     - nutrients,
     - chemical oxygen demand (COD),
     - specific pollutants associated with site activities.
   - It also identifies the laboratory methods, detection limits, and any required method standards.

6. **Set quality assurance and quality control procedures**
   - A robust plan includes QA/QC requirements such as:
     - field blanks,
     - equipment blanks,
     - duplicate samples,
     - chain-of-custody documentation,
     - calibration checks,
     - preservation and holding times.
   - These controls ensure the results are defensible and traceable.

7. **Describe field procedures**
   - The plan instructs personnel on:
     - storm tracking and event notification,
     - mobilization timing,
     - sample bottle preparation,
     - safe access to sampling points,
     - collection technique,
     - labeling and documentation,
     - coolers and preservation,
     - transport to the laboratory.

8. **Interpret and report results**
   - Analytical results are compared to permit benchmarks, thresholds, action levels, or historical baseline data.
   - The plan may require trend analysis, exceedance investigation, and corrective action evaluation.
   - Reporting can include discharge monitoring forms, internal summaries, or regulatory submittals.

9. **Trigger corrective actions if needed**
   - If results exceed criteria or reveal recurring problems, the plan may require:
     - source investigation,
     - maintenance of controls,
     - spill response review,
     - housekeeping improvements,
     - engineering modifications,
     - retraining of personnel.

A SWSAP is therefore both an **operational protocol** and a **compliance document**: it standardizes field work while ensuring data can be used for regulatory and environmental decision-making.

## Variants

Common variants and implementations include:

- **Industrial stormwater sampling plans**
  - Focus on permit compliance for industrial facilities.
  - Often target pollutants associated with manufacturing, materials handling, or outdoor storage.

- **Construction stormwater sampling plans**
  - Used for erosion and sediment control monitoring.
  - Emphasis is usually on turbidity, TSS, pH, and sediment-related indicators.

- **Municipal stormwater monitoring plans**
  - Support municipal separate storm sewer system (MS4) programs.
  - May include watershed-scale monitoring, outfall screening, and illicit discharge detection.

- **Site-specific discharge monitoring plans**
  - Tailored to a single outfall or drainage system.
  - Often used where contamination concerns are localized.

- **Event-triggered first-flush sampling plans**
  - Designed to capture the initial runoff phase of a rainfall event.
  - Useful when pollutant buildup on surfaces is expected to wash off rapidly.

- **Flow-weighted composite sampling plans**
  - Collect proportional aliquots over the discharge period.
  - Better for estimating average event concentrations when continuous flow measurement is available.

- **Screening-level vs. compliance-level plans**
  - Screening plans are used for preliminary assessment and source identification.
  - Compliance plans are more rigorous and emphasize strict procedural documentation.

## Trade-offs

Key considerations and limitations include:

- **Representativeness vs. practicality**
  - Sampling during storms can be logistically difficult, but collecting data outside qualifying events yields less meaningful results.
  - A highly representative design may require significant staff readiness and equipment.

- **Grab samples vs. composites**
  - Grab samples are simpler and better for first-flush conditions, but they may miss concentration changes during the event.
  - Composite samples better represent average conditions, but can obscure short-duration spikes and require more instrumentation.

- **Detection limits vs. cost**
  - More sensitive laboratory methods improve data quality, but they increase analytical cost.
  - Selecting too many parameters can make the program expensive without improving decision value.

- **Coverage vs. resource constraints**
  - Monitoring multiple outfalls improves site understanding, but each added location increases labor, sampling supplies, and lab fees.

- **Safety vs. data quality**
  - Sampling in heavy rain, darkness, or near moving water can be hazardous.
  - Safety protocols may limit access and can affect timing and completeness of sample collection.

- **Regulatory rigor vs. flexibility**
  - Strict plans support defensible compliance data, but they can be difficult to execute consistently.
  - Flexible plans are easier to operate, but they may produce data that are harder to compare over time.

- **Event variability**
  - Stormwater quality can vary widely depending on rainfall intensity, antecedent dry period, seasonal conditions, and site activity.
  - Single-event results may not be fully representative of long-term discharge quality.
