## Definition

**Aerially Deposited Lead Soils (ADL soils)** are soils contaminated by lead that was dispersed into the environment through the historical use of **leaded gasoline**. As vehicles burned leaded fuel, fine lead particles were emitted in exhaust and deposited along roadsides, in urban areas, and on adjacent soils. Over time, these repeated atmospheric deposits created shallow surface soil contamination with elevated lead concentrations.

ADL soils are typically identified by their **near-surface contamination pattern**, broad geographic distribution, and association with former high-traffic transportation corridors or long-term urban deposition areas.

## How it works

Lead was added to gasoline as an anti-knock additive for much of the 20th century. During combustion, not all of the lead stayed in the engine or vehicle exhaust system. A portion was released as airborne particulate matter, which then settled onto surrounding land surfaces.

The process generally involved:

1. **Emission from tailpipes**  
   Lead-containing exhaust particles were emitted during fuel combustion.

2. **Atmospheric transport**  
   Wind and local dispersion moved the particles short distances from roadways and other emission sources.

3. **Deposition onto soil**  
   Particles accumulated on exposed ground, pavement edges, drainage areas, medians, and nearby yards or open land.

4. **Long-term accumulation**  
   Because lead does not degrade, repeated deposition over decades built up a persistent contamination layer, usually concentrated in the upper soil horizon.

5. **Redistribution by disturbance**  
   Construction, grading, erosion, farming, and excavation can mix or move contaminated soil, spreading lead beyond its original deposition footprint.

ADL contamination is important because lead is a **persistent toxic metal**. Once deposited, it remains in soil for long periods and can pose exposure risks through ingestion of soil or dust, especially for children, or through reuse of contaminated soil in other settings.

## Variants

ADL soils are not a single engineered material, but the term is used in several related ways:

- **Roadside ADL soils**  
  Soils along highways, arterials, and older roads impacted by vehicle emissions from the leaded-gasoline era.

- **Urban ADL soils**  
  Soils in residential, commercial, and mixed-use areas exposed to widespread atmospheric lead fallout.

- **Stockpiled ADL soils**  
  Excavated lead-impacted soils temporarily or permanently stored in piles, sometimes for characterization, reuse, or disposal planning.

- **Managed ADL materials**  
  Soils that have been tested, segregated, or placed under controlled conditions because of known lead contamination.

- **ADL versus other lead-contaminated soils**  
  ADL specifically refers to lead from atmospheric deposition, which differs from soils contaminated by:
  - lead-based paint
  - industrial emissions
  - mining and smelting waste
  - shooting ranges
  - treated wood or other localized sources

A practical alternative concept is simply **lead-contaminated soil**, but ADL is more specific about the contamination source and deposition mechanism.

## Trade-offs

Key considerations associated with ADL soils include:

- **Persistent contamination**  
  Lead does not break down, so contamination can remain for decades or longer.

- **Shallow but widespread distribution**  
  ADL often affects the surface layer over large areas, making it difficult to manage by simple spot treatment.

- **Human exposure risk**  
  The main concern is ingestion or inhalation of contaminated dust, particularly for young children and workers handling soil.

- **Excavation and disposal costs**  
  Managing ADL can require testing, segregation, approved reuse locations, or off-site disposal.

- **Reuse limitations**  
  Soil that might otherwise be reusable for fill or landscaping may be restricted if lead concentrations exceed applicable thresholds.

- **Heterogeneous contamination**  
  Lead levels can vary significantly over short distances, so representative sampling is essential.

- **Regulatory dependence**  
  Management decisions depend on project-specific cleanup goals, land use, and jurisdictional criteria.
