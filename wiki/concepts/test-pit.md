## Definition

A **test pit** is a shallow excavation (typically 1–5 m deep) dug to expose subsurface soil, fill, or rock for visual inspection, in‑situ testing, and sample collection. In environmental and geotechnical work, test pits are used to assess material composition, contamination, and stratification before remediation or construction.

## How it works

Test pits are excavated using hand tools (shovel, pickaxe) or mechanical equipment (backhoe, excavator). The process follows a standard protocol:

1. **Location selection** – Based on sampling plan grid, previous data, or targeted areas (e.g., hotspots).
2. **Excavation** – Pit dimensions are commonly 1 × 2 m at the top, with benched sides or a slight taper for safety. Depth is controlled to the target horizon or refusal (e.g., bedrock, groundwater).
3. **Observation & recording** – The exposed sidewall is described for colour, texture, odour, visible debris, and moisture. Photos and sketches are taken.
4. **Sampling** – Discrete or composite samples are collected from specific intervals (e.g., 0–0.3 m, 0.3–1 m) using a trowel, scoop, or stainless‑steel spoon. Samples are placed in laboratory‑supplied containers and chilled if needed.
5. **Backfill & restoration** – After sampling and logging, the pit is backfilled with the excavated spoil in reverse order (or clean fill if required), compacted, and restored to original grade.

The method provides direct visual evidence of subsurface conditions that cannot be obtained from core drilling alone. In the context of the provided source (“Basin 5 Clean Fill Sampling Plan”), test pits are used to verify that imported fill meets clean fill standards (e.g., low contaminant concentrations) before spreading.

## Variants

| Variant | Description | Typical Use |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| **Hand‑dug test pit** | Excavated with shovels; shallow (<1.5 m), small footprint. | Limited access areas, utilities avoidance. |
| **Mechanically excavated pit** | Using a backhoe or excavator; deeper (up to 5 m), faster. | Large‑scale fill or soil inspections. |
| **Test trench** | Elongated pit (width < length) for linear exposures (e.g., pipeline routes). | Geotechnical profile for linear infrastructure. |
| **Trial pit** | Synonymous term, often used in geotechnical reports for foundation investigations. | Bearing capacity tests, shallow rock inspection. |

## Trade‑offs

**Advantages**  
- Direct visual observation of stratigraphy and contamination.  
- Ability to collect large or representative samples (e.g., whole‑bucket composites).  
- Relatively low cost compared to boreholes.  
- Can be performed in remote or rugged terrain with minimal equipment.

**Limitations**  
- **Destructive** – site is disturbed; cannot be reused for long‑term monitoring.  
- **Safety** – risk of collapse, especially in loose or saturated soils; requires shoring or sloping.  
- **Depth restricted** – typically <5 m; groundwater may cause inflow and instability.  
- **Less precise depth control** than direct‑push or sonic drilling.  
- **Larger footprint** – may conflict with existing structures or utilities.
