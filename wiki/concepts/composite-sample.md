## Definition

A **composite sample** is a sample formed by physically combining multiple individual sub‑samples (or “grab samples”) collected from distinct locations, depths, or time intervals within a defined material volume or area. The objective is to produce a single, homogenized specimen that represents the **average composition** of the entire population, thereby reducing analytical costs and handling effort while preserving representativeness for bulk properties.

In the context of the provided sources—*Stockpile 2 BCS Foundation Spoil Workplan Tech Memo* and *Carpenter Shoofly Area Clean Fill Sampling Plan*—composite samples are used to assess the overall chemical quality of soil, spoil, or fill material without analyzing every discrete increment.

## How it works

The procedure for creating a composite sample typically follows these steps:

1. **Sampling plan design** – Determine the number and location of sub‑samples based on the heterogeneity of the material and the desired statistical confidence. Common approaches include random, systematic grid, or stratified random sampling.

2. **Collection of sub‑samples** – A fixed number of grab samples (e.g., 5–10) are taken from different points (spatially or temporally). In stockpile sampling, these sub‑samples are often collected at varying depths and across the pile’s footprint to account for vertical and lateral variability.

3. **Homogenization** – All sub‑samples are combined in a clean container and thoroughly mixed (e.g., by tumbling, stirring, or manual blending). Large particles may be crushed or sieved to ensure uniformity.

4. **Reduction to laboratory size** – The mixture is reduced to a manageable mass (e.g., 1–2 kg) using techniques such as **quartering (coning and quartering)** or a riffle splitter. This yields a final composite that is representative of the whole.

5. **Analysis** – The composite is submitted for physical or chemical testing; the result reflects the average concentration of constituents over the sampled volume.

The underlying assumption is that the material is reasonably homogeneous after mixing, and that the arithmetic mean of the sub‑sample values approximates the true population mean.

## Variants

- **Spatial composite** – Combines samples from different geographic locations (e.g., grid points across a stockpile or a contaminated site). This is the most common form in environmental soil and fill sampling.
- **Time‑composite** – Sub‑samples collected at regular intervals over a period (e.g., from a process stream or waste discharge) and combined to represent average quality over that period.
- **Depth‑composite** – Sub‑samples from multiple depth intervals within a single core or borehole, blended to give an average for the full profile.
- **Incremental composite** – A variant where a large number of small, precisely taken increments (e.g., 30–100) are combined to minimise bias, often used in **representative sampling** of heterogeneous materials (e.g., stockpiles of waste rock or coal).
- **Field composite vs. laboratory composite** – The mixing may occur on‑site (field composite) or sub‑samples may be sent to a lab where they are combined (lab composite). Field composites are more common for soil and fill because they reduce shipping mass.

## Trade-offs

| **Advantages** | **Disadvantages** |
|----------------|-------------------|
| **Cost‑effective** – Fewer analyses needed; analytical costs scale with number of composites, not number of sub‑samples. | **Loss of variance information** – Composite hides variability; cannot identify hot‑spots or spatial trends without additional samples. |
| **Reduced sample mass** – Easier to transport and store. | **Potential for mixing bias** – Incomplete homogenization or segregation can produce a non‑representative final sample. |
| **Improved representativeness for average** – Naturally smoothes random sampling error if sub‑samples are unbiased. | **Limited to average‑based decision criteria** – Not suitable when maximum concentrations or exceedance limits are the regulatory focus. |
| **Efficient for bulk material assessment** – E.g., determining whether a stockpile meets clean‑fill thresholds. | **Requires careful planning** – Poorly designed composite (too few sub‑samples, inappropriate grid) can misrepresent the population. |

**Key consideration:** Composite sampling is appropriate when the question is about the *mean* concentration (e.g., total mass of contaminant in a stockpile) but inappropriate when the question is about *maximum* concentration or spatial outlier detection. In clean‑fill or spoil work plans, composites are often combined with a small number of discrete samples as a check for extreme values.
