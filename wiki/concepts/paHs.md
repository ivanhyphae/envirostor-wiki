## Definition

**Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbons (PAHs)** are a class of organic compounds composed of two or more fused aromatic rings. They are formed primarily through incomplete combustion of organic matter, such as fossil fuels, wood, and tobacco. PAHs are persistent environmental pollutants with recognized carcinogenic, mutagenic, and teratogenic properties. They are commonly monitored in air, soil, water, and sediment due to their toxicity and potential for bioaccumulation.

## How It Works

PAHs are produced via pyrolysis and incomplete combustion. Their chemical structure consists of fused benzene rings (e.g., naphthalene (2 rings), pyrene (4 rings), benzo[a]pyrene (5 rings)). The larger the ring system, the lower the volatility and water solubility, and the higher the tendency to adsorb to particulate matter. In the environment, PAHs undergo:

- **Atmospheric transport** – fine particles carry PAHs long distances.
- **Deposition** – wet/dry deposition onto soil and water bodies.
- **Partitioning** – in water, PAHs sorb strongly to organic matter and sediments.
- **Degradation** – primarily through microbial activity and photolysis (sunlight); rates depend on ring size and environmental conditions.
- **Bioaccumulation** – lipophilic PAHs accumulate in fatty tissues of organisms.

Human exposure occurs via inhalation, ingestion (food/water), and dermal contact. Metabolism in the liver often transforms PAHs into more reactive (and carcinogenic) intermediates, such as diol-epoxides.

## Variants

PAHs are classified by ring count or source:

- **Low-molecular-weight PAHs** (2–3 rings): e.g., naphthalene, acenaphthene, fluorene. More volatile, less toxic.
- **High-molecular-weight PAHs** (4–6 rings): e.g., chrysene, benzo[k]fluoranthene, benzo[a]pyrene. More persistent, more carcinogenic.
- **Alkylated PAHs**: parent compounds with methyl or ethyl groups; often more toxic and abundant in crude oil.
- **Oxygenated and nitrated PAHs**: transformation products formed via photochemical reactions; some are more mutagenic than parent compounds.

In environmental monitoring, the US EPA lists 16 priority PAHs (including benzo[a]pyrene, naphthalene, phenanthrene). Various international bodies (IARC, ATSDR) have additional classifications.

## Trade-offs

- **Toxicity vs. Abundance**: High-molecular-weight PAHs are more toxic but less abundant in air; low-molecular-weight PAHs are more common but less acutely toxic.
- **Persistence vs. Transportability**: Larger PAHs persist longer but are less mobile; smaller PAHs travel farther but degrade faster.
- **Analytical Challenges**: PAH analysis requires sensitive instrumentation (GC-MS, HPLC). Co-elution, matrix interference, and the need for clean-up steps increase complexity and cost.
- **Remediation Trade-offs**: Bioremediation works well for low-molecular-weight PAHs but is slow for high-molecular-weight ones. Chemical oxidation may create harmful by-products.
- **Human Health vs. Regulation**: Setting safe limits is complicated by mixture effects and non-linear dose-response; continuous low-level exposure may still pose risk.
