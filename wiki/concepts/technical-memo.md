## Definition

A **technical memo** (short for technical memorandum) is a concise, internally‑focused document used to communicate specific technical information, analysis, recommendations, or decisions within an organization. Unlike formal reports, technical memos are typically brief, direct, and intended for a limited audience (e.g., project teams, engineers, management). They serve as a permanent record of technical discussions, findings, or instructions, and are often used to document preliminary results, field observations, or design decisions before a more detailed report is produced.

Technical memos may be standalone documents or attachments to email, as suggested by the source file `E-mail Acceptance of Bent 2 Soils Tech Memo.pdf`, which indicates a technical memo was reviewed and accepted via email correspondence.

## How It Works

Technical memos follow a standard structure to ensure clarity and quick reference:

1. **Header** – Includes to/from fields, date, subject, and possibly project name/number.
2. **Scope** – States the purpose and limits of the memo (e.g., “This memo summarizes the geotechnical analysis for Bent 2 foundation soils.”).
3. **Background** – Provides relevant context, assumptions, or prior work.
4. **Technical Content** – The core body, often with tables, figures, calculations, or graphs. Uses precise technical language.
5. **Conclusions / Recommendations** – Summarizes findings and proposes action.
6. **References** – Lists sources (standards, test data, previous memos).
7. **Appendices** (optional) – Detailed data or calculations.

Memos are typically produced by engineers, scientists, or analysts and are reviewed by peers or supervisors before release. The approval path (e.g., “Accepted by Reviewer” as seen in the source file) is part of the quality assurance process.

## Variants

Technical memo formats vary by industry, organization, and purpose:

- **Internal Technical Memo** – Used within a company; may be informal.
- **Design Memo** – Documents a design decision or calculation (e.g., structural, geotechnical).
- **Field Memo** – Records observations, measurements, or site changes.
- **Calculation Memo** – Presents detailed calculations; often referenced in larger reports.
- **Decision Memo** – Recommends a course of action with supporting technical rationale.
- **Meeting Memo** – Captures technical decisions made in a meeting.
- **Email‑based Memo** – The technical content is embedded directly in an email (as in the source file), with the email thread serving as informal approval.

Some organizations combine memo formats with a formal transmittal letter, while others treat them as separate deliverables.

## Trade‑offs

| Advantage | Limitation |
|-----------|------------|
| Fast to produce and distribute | May lack the rigor of a full technical report |
| Focused on a single topic | Can be lost in email chains if not properly archived |
| Facilitates quick decision‑making | May bypass peer review if not managed properly |
| Serves as official record | Informal tone can lead to ambiguity |
| Easily referenced in future work | May omit necessary context for outside readers |

Key considerations:
- **Audience** – A technical memo written for senior managers might omit heavy calculations, while one for specialists should include raw data.
- **Traceability** – Memos should be numbered, dated, and linked to project files.
- **Approval** – A clear sign‑off process (e.g., “Accepted” annotation) ensures accountability.
- **Storage** – Technical memos are often filed with project correspondence, not with final reports.
