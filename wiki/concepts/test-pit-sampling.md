## Definition

Test Pit Sampling is a field investigation method used to evaluate soil, fill, and buried materials by excavating small test pits with machinery or hand tools and directly observing the exposed subsurface conditions. It is commonly used for clean fill verification, environmental screening, geotechnical characterization, and construction planning when surface observations alone are insufficient.

The method provides direct visual and tactile access to soil layers, fill thickness, debris content, staining, odors, moisture conditions, and evidence of contamination or unsuitable material.

## How it works

Test pit sampling typically follows a planned grid or targeted layout based on project objectives, site history, and suspected areas of concern.

1. **Select test pit locations**
   - Locations are chosen to represent the site or target specific suspect areas.
   - Spacing and density depend on the size of the area, the expected variability of fill, and the level of confidence required.

2. **Excavate the pit**
   - A backhoe, excavator, or similar equipment is used to dig to the planned depth or until refusal, groundwater, or unsafe conditions are encountered.
   - Excavation is done in controlled lifts so the exposed materials can be examined.

3. **Log observations**
   - Field personnel describe soil type, color, moisture, structure, inclusions, stratigraphy, and evidence of anthropogenic material.
   - For clean-fill work, special attention is paid to concrete, brick, asphalt, metal, plastics, wood, glass, ash, staining, odor, and other indicators of import fill quality.

4. **Collect samples**
   - Representative samples are taken from discrete horizons or intervals.
   - Samples may be used for laboratory analysis, field screening, or archive purposes.
   - Sampling often targets visually distinct material, suspicious staining, or areas where the fill appears non-uniform.

5. **Screen and assess**
   - Depending on the project, field screening may include photoionization detector readings, visual screening, odor evaluation, or other rapid tests.
   - Laboratory analyses may be used to confirm whether material meets site-specific acceptance criteria.

6. **Document and restore**
   - Each test pit is photographed, logged, and mapped.
   - After sampling, pits are backfilled and the surface restored as required.

In clean-fill sampling plans, test pits are often used to verify that material is free of unacceptable debris and does not show evidence of contamination. The approach is especially useful where imported fill has been placed in layers or where site history suggests variable reuse of material.

## Variants

- **Backhoe test pits**: The most common approach for shallow subsurface investigation; efficient for relatively large excavation and direct observation.
- **Hand-dug test pits**: Used where machinery access is limited or where very careful excavation is needed.
- **Environmental test pits**: Focus on contamination indicators, waste debris, and screening for impacted soil or fill.
- **Geotechnical test pits**: Emphasize soil classification, bearing conditions, groundwater, and construction suitability.
- **Clean-fill verification pits**: Designed to confirm that imported fill meets defined acceptance criteria for debris content and contamination indicators.
- **Targeted vs. grid-based sampling**:
  - **Targeted** sampling focuses on suspect or historically disturbed areas.
  - **Grid-based** sampling provides broader statistical coverage across a site.
- **Alternative methods**:
  - Borehole sampling and soil boring provide deeper but less visually direct information.
  - Trial trench investigations cover linear features or wider exposure than a test pit.
  - Ground penetrating radar may supplement, but not replace, direct excavation.

## Trade-offs

- **Strengths**
  - Direct observation of subsurface materials.
  - Good for identifying debris, fill heterogeneity, and obvious contamination indicators.
  - Faster and often more informative than boreholes for shallow investigations.
  - Supports immediate field decisions about whether material is suitable.

- **Limitations**
  - Shallow relative to drilling methods; depth is constrained by equipment and safety.
  - Disturbs the site and requires restoration.
  - Results are localized; a small number of pits may miss variability between locations.
  - Excavation can be unsafe in unstable soils, near utilities, or where groundwater is present.
  - Access may be limited by site layout, structures, slopes, or sensitive surfaces.

- **Sampling considerations**
  - The number and spacing of pits strongly affect confidence in conclusions.
  - Visual inspection alone may not detect all contaminants.
  - Reuse, burial, or mixed fill can create highly variable conditions even within a small area.
  - Field observations should be integrated with historical records, maps, and laboratory data where decisions have regulatory or contractual consequences.
