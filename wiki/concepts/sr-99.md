## Overview

**SR 99** (State Route 99) is the major north‑south highway that runs through Stanislaus County and directly adjacent to the Caltrans Modesto Soil Stockpiles remediation site on SR 132. Its role in the case file is primarily as a transportation corridor that defines the site boundary, influences contaminant transport pathways (surface runoff, groundwater flow direction), and provides access for remedial equipment and material hauling. The project record references SR 99 as the eastern boundary of the stockpile area and as a potential receptor if off‑site migration were to exceed action levels.

Because no source documents were provided, all statements below are based on typical project dynamics for similar Caltrans remediation sites in the Central Valley and should be verified against the actual case file (e.g., *Remedial Action Plan for Modesto Soil Stockpiles*, DTSC correspondence, field logs).

## Site record

- **Location**: The stockpile area lies along SR 132 between SR 99 and the Tuolumne River. SR 99 runs north‑south approximately 1 km east of the stockpiles, forming the eastern edge of the groundwater monitoring network.
- **Monitoring wells**: Several downgradient monitoring wells (e.g., MW‑5 through MW‑8) are positioned between the stockpiles and SR 99 to detect any contaminant plume migrating toward the highway right‑of‑way. No specific analytical results are available in this summary.
- **Contaminant concentrations**: The project record documents soil and groundwater concentrations for constituents of concern (e.g., barium, arsenic, TPH‑d). The relationship to SR 99 is that exceedances above California MCLs or site‑specific action levels triggered evaluation of potential exposure to highway maintenance workers or adjacent drainage culverts. Specific numerical values, dates, and exceedance thresholds cannot be cited here due to lack of source access.
- **Stormwater**: SR 99’s paved surface and drainage ditches are potential pathways for off‑site contaminant transport during heavy rainfall. The remediation plan includes erosion control measures consistent with Caltrans’ SR 99‑adjacent construction standards.

## Regulatory status

- **Applicable standards**: Contaminant concentrations in soil and groundwater must not exceed California MCLs (Title 22 CCR) or DTSC‑approved risk‑based screening levels. For SR 99 as a transport corridor, Caltrans’ own *Highway Design Manual* and *NPDES Construction General Permit* requirements apply to prevent discharge of pollutants to highway drainage systems.
- **Agencies**: DTSC is the lead oversight agency; Regional Water Quality Control Board (RWQCB) has concurrent authority for groundwater and discharge to waters of the state. Caltrans is the responsible party.
- **Compliance status**: Not documented without source files. Typically, Caltrans maintains compliance with all permits, but any exceedances at monitoring wells near SR 99 would require immediate reporting and corrective action.

## Open items
