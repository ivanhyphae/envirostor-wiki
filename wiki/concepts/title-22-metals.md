## Definition

**Title 22 Metals** refers to a specific set of metallic elements regulated under California’s Title 22 of the California Code of Regulations (CCR), primarily in the context of drinking water standards (Division 4, Chapter 15) and hazardous waste management (Division 4.5). The term is commonly used in environmental monitoring, groundwater testing, and site remediation to denote the panel of metals for which maximum contaminant levels (MCLs) or waste characterization thresholds are established by the California Department of Public Health (now part of the California Environmental Protection Agency) and the Department of Toxic Substances Control. In practice, “Title 22 Metals” often implies the full list of metals that must be analyzed to demonstrate compliance with California’s drinking water quality criteria or to classify a waste as hazardous.

## How it works

The regulatory framework for Title 22 Metals operates through two primary mechanisms:

1. **Drinking Water Standards** – Title 22 CCR sets enforceable MCLs for public water systems. The standard list includes metals such as arsenic, barium, cadmium, chromium (total and hexavalent), copper, lead, mercury, nickel, selenium, silver, thallium, and others. Laboratories analyzing water samples must use approved EPA or California-specific methods (e.g., EPA 200.8 for ICP-MS, EPA 200.7 for ICP-OES, EPA 245.1 for mercury by cold vapor atomic absorption) with detection limits lower than the corresponding MCLs.

2. **Hazardous Waste Characterization** – For waste classification, Title 22 uses the “Total Threshold Limit Concentration” (TTLC) and “Soluble Threshold Limit Concentration” (STLC) to determine if a material is a hazardous waste. The TTLC values apply to the total concentration of a metal in the waste, while STLC values (using the Waste Extraction Test, WET) simulate leaching potential. The list of metals for TTLC/STLC includes antimony, arsenic, barium, beryllium, cadmium, chromium, cobalt, copper, lead, mercury, molybdenum, nickel, selenium, silver, thallium, vanadium, and zinc.

Environmental monitoring programs (e.g., for groundwater at stockpile sites) collect samples, preserve them with acid, and submit them to certified laboratories. Results are compared against the Title 22 MCLs for drinking water or the TTLC/STLC thresholds for waste.

## Variants

Different contexts produce slightly different lists and thresholds:

- **Title 22 Drinking Water Metals** – Typically 13–16 metals with MCLs (e.g., arsenic 10 µg/L, barium 1000 µg/L, cadmium 5 µg/L, chromium 50 µg/L total, lead 15 µg/L action level, etc.). This list is the most common reference for groundwater and surface water compliance.

- **Title 22 Hazardous Waste Metals** – A larger set of metals (15+) with TTLC and STLC values. For example, TTLC for cadmium is 100 mg/kg; STLC for cadmium is 1.0 mg/L.

- **Regional or Project-Specific Variants** – Some regulatory programs (e.g., California’s Leaking Underground Fuel Tank (LUFT) program, or site cleanup under the Department of Toxic Substances Control) may require analysis of a subset of Title 22 metals based on historical use or contamination potential.

- **Federal vs. State Lists** – The federal Safe Drinking Water Act lists “priority metals,” but California’s Title 22 includes additional elements (e.g., antimony, beryllium, thallium) and stricter MCLs for some metals.

## Trade-offs

- **Cost and Analytical Complexity** – The full Title 22 metals panel requires multiple analytical techniques (e.g., ICP-MS for trace metals, cold vapor AAS or CVAFS for mercury). A single method cannot cover all metals at the required detection limits, increasing laboratory costs per sample.

- **Interference and Matrix Effects** – High total dissolved solids or organic content in water or waste can cause spectral interferences or suppression in ICP methods, requiring sample dilution or alternative extraction procedures (e.g., microwave digestion for solids). This may raise detection limits above MCLs.

- **Regulatory Overlap** – Compliance with Title 22 metals often duplicates federal requirements, but California’s thresholds can be more stringent. Facilities that ship waste across state lines may need to meet both California and EPA criteria.

- **Sample Preservation and Holding Times** – Many Title 22 metals require acid preservation to pH < 2 and analysis within short time windows (e.g., mercury within 28 days). Field and logistics errors can invalidate results.

- **Practical vs. Risk-Based Lists** – Some metals (e.g., antimony, thallium) are rarely found at significant concentrations in many environmental samples, yet they must still be analyzed for a full Title 22 panel, adding no practical benefit in most situations while increasing project costs.
