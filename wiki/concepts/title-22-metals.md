## Title 22 Metals

### Definition

**Title 22 Metals** refers to the set of metal contaminants regulated under Title 22 of the California Code of Regulations (CCR), Division 4.5, Chapter 11, Article 3 (Waste Classification). These regulations establish **maximum allowable concentrations** for metals in solid waste to determine whether a material qualifies as **non‑hazardous**, **hazardous waste**, or can be used as **clean fill** under California’s strict environmental standards. The list typically includes arsenic, barium, cadmium, chromium, lead, mercury, selenium, silver, and others — often mirroring but potentially varying from federal RCRA metals due to California’s more stringent thresholds.

### How it works

Title 22 metals are used as a benchmark in soil and groundwater characterization studies, particularly for construction and transportation projects (e.g., Caltrans work). A typical workflow involves:

1. **Sample collection** from stockpiles, borrow areas, or excavation zones.
2. **Laboratory analysis** for the full suite of Title 22 metals using approved methods (e.g., EPA 6010B for metals).
3. **Comparison of results** against the CCR Title 22 Soluble Threshold Limit Concentrations (STLC) and Total Threshold Limit Concentrations (TTLC). The STLC values are typically lower and apply to the soluble fraction (measured via the Waste Extraction Test), while TTLC values apply to total metal concentrations.
4. **Classification decision**:  
   - If all metals are below both STLC and TTLC, material is considered **non‑hazardous** and may be reused as clean fill.  
   - If any metal exceeds its TTLC, the material is **hazardous waste**.  
   - If TTLC is met but STLC exceeded, it may be classified as **designated waste** requiring special handling.

The technical memos referenced (e.g., “Clean Fill Tech Memo” and “Stockpile BCS Foundation Spoil Workplan”) demonstrate how this classification is applied in practice — for example, determining whether excavated soil from a highway project can be reused on‑site or must be transported to a permitted disposal facility.

### Variants

- **STLC vs. TTLC thresholds**: Two separate regulatory limits exist; the soluble limit (STLC) is often the controlling criterion for metals like lead and cadmium.
- **Federal vs. State lists**: While Title 22 metals overlap with RCRA metals (federal D‑list), California’s limits for certain metals (e.g., chromium, antimony) can be lower, creating a stricter classification standard.
- **Clean fill criteria**: Some projects define “clean fill” as soil meeting Title 22 non‑hazardous thresholds AND additional project‑specific background goals (e.g., comparison to naturally occurring concentrations).
- **Groundwater protection**: In some studies, Title 22 metal limits are also used to evaluate groundwater quality (e.g., monitoring wells near stockpiles), even though groundwater standards are generally governed by other regulations (e.g., California MCLs).

### Trade-offs

| Consideration | Impact |
|---------------|--------|
| **Stringency** | Lower thresholds (especially STLC) increase disposal costs for materials that might be non‑hazardous under federal rules. |
| **Analytical cost** | Full Title 22 metal panel analysis is more expensive than targeted metal testing. |
| **Operational delay** | Waiting for laboratory results can slow project timelines, especially when multiple sample rounds are needed. |
| **Risk of misclassification** | Very slight exceedances (e.g., lead at 5.1 mg/L STLC vs. 5.0 limit) force material into hazardous waste category, with significant cost implications. |
| **Variability** | Background metal concentrations in natural soils (e.g., arsenic in California alluvium) can exceed Title 22 thresholds, leading to “false positives” for hazardous classification unless site‑specific cleanup levels are established. |
