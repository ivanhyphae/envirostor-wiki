## Definition

**Title 22 Metals** refers to the list of regulated heavy metals and metalloids specified in Title 22 of the California Code of Regulations (CCR), Division 4.5, Chapter 11, Article 5, which establishes maximum concentration limits for constituents in solid wastes to determine whether a material qualifies as a "clean fill" or "hazardous waste." These metals are the primary chemical analytes used to assess soil and groundwater quality for disposal or reuse under California's hazardous waste classification system. The Title 22 metals list includes: antimony, arsenic, barium, beryllium, cadmium, chromium, cobalt, copper, lead, mercury, molybdenum, nickel, selenium, silver, thallium, vanadium, and zinc.

## How it works

The Title 22 metals framework operates within California's hazardous waste classification hierarchy, serving as the threshold for determining whether excavated soil, sediment, or groundwater can be disposed of as non-hazardous clean fill. The regulatory mechanism works as follows:

1. **Sample Collection**: Representative samples of soil or groundwater are collected from the site of interest, typically following standard protocols for environmental sampling (e.g., EPA Method 200.7 or 200.8 for metals analysis).

2. **Laboratory Analysis**: Samples are analyzed for each listed metal using appropriate analytical methods (e.g., ICP-MS, ICP-OES, or CVAA for mercury). Results are reported in milligrams per liter (mg/L) for water samples or milligrams per kilogram (mg/kg) for soil samples.

3. **Comparison to Regulatory Thresholds**: The measured concentrations are compared against the Title 22 maximum allowable concentrations. For example, from the provided sources, the California Title 22 maximum concentrations include:
   - Antimony: 0.006 mg/L
   - Arsenic: 0.010 mg/L  
   - Barium: 1.0 mg/L
   - Beryllium: 0.004 mg/L
   - Cadmium: 0.005 mg/L
   - Chromium: 0.050 mg/L
   - Cobalt: 0.200 mg/L
   - Copper: 1.0 mg/L
   - Lead: 0.015 mg/L
   - Mercury: 0.002 mg/L
   - Molybdenum: 0.050 mg/L
   - Nickel: 0.100 mg/L
   - Selenium: 0.050 mg/L
   - Silver: 0.050 mg/L
   - Thallium: 0.001 mg/L
   - Vanadium: 0.240 mg/L
   - Zinc: 5.0 mg/L

4. **Decision Classification**: 
   - If all metals are below Title 22 limits, the material may be classified as non-hazardous clean fill.
   - If any metal exceeds the limit, the material may be classified as hazardous waste and require special handling, treatment, and disposal at permitted facilities.

The regulatory framework is particularly relevant for construction projects, environmental remediation, and industrial site redevelopment, where large volumes of excavated soil or extracted groundwater must be managed. The sources provided (SR 132 Basin 5 Clean Fill Tech Memo and Caltrans Modesto Stockpiles groundwater reports) demonstrate direct regulatory application, where groundwater from stockpile sites was sampled and analyzed specifically for Title 22 metals to determine if discharge or off-haul was permissible.

## Variants

Several variants and related regulatory frameworks exist:

- **California Hazardous Waste Characterization** (Title 22, Division 4.5): The full regulatory context for these metals, including soluble threshold limit concentrations (STLC) and total threshold limit concentrations (TTLC).

- **EPA RCRA Eight Metals**: A subset of federally regulated metals under the Resource Conservation and Recovery Act (arsenic, barium, cadmium, chromium, lead, mercury, selenium, silver) — a smaller list than California's Title 22.

- **STLC vs. TTLC**: California distinguishes between soluble metals (STLC, measured using the Waste Extraction Test) and total metals (TTLC, measured via total acid digestion). Title 22 metals thresholds apply to both, but with different numeric limits.

- **Clean Fill Criteria (California)**: Specific metals thresholds for materials designated as "clean fill" for beneficial reuse, which often align with but may be more restrictive than the Title 22 maximums for water.

## Trade-offs

- **Regulatory Stringency**: Title 22 metals thresholds are among the most stringent in the United States, exceeding federal RCRA limits and many state standards. This increases compliance costs but provides greater environmental protection.

- **Analytical Cost**: Full Title 22 metals analysis (17 metals) is more expensive than the EPA eight-metal suite. For large projects, this can represent significant costs. However, a phased approach (screening with fewer metals) can reduce initial expense.

- **Site-Specific Variability**: Natural background concentrations in California soils (e.g., chromium, nickel, arsenic) may exceed Title 22 limits due only to geology, creating classification challenges where naturally occurring metals trigger hazardous waste status.

- **Disposal Implications**: Exceedance of any single metal threshold can render thousands of cubic yards of soil as hazardous waste, dramatically increasing disposal costs ($300-1,000 per ton vs. $10-50 per ton for clean fill).

- **Analytical Interference**: Certain metals (notably chromium, arsenic, and selenium) can be present in multiple valence states with different toxicities, but the regulatory framework typically uses total concentrations without speciation.
