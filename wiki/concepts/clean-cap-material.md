## Definition

**Clean cap material** is the relatively free-draining, structurally competent material placed at the top of a stockpile or fill mass to form a cap or working surface. In the context of Stockpiles and MSE Wall construction, it typically refers to material selected to be free of objectionable debris, oversized fragments, or other contaminants so that it can safely support equipment, provide a smooth grading layer, and avoid introducing unstable or unsuitable materials into the completed section.

In practical terms, clean cap material is used as the uppermost layer in a placement sequence to create a stable, maintainable surface above underlying fill or reinforced zones.

## How it works

Clean cap material works by serving as the interface between the final grade and the underlying placed material. Its functions commonly include:

- **Surface protection:** It shields lower layers from direct trafficking, erosion, and localized disturbance.
- **Load distribution:** It helps spread wheel loads from construction equipment over a broader area.
- **Grade control:** It provides a finish layer that can be trimmed to the required elevation and slope.
- **Containment and separation:** It can reduce the chance that undesirable material at the surface becomes mixed into adjacent layers during placement or maintenance.

For an MSE Wall, a clean cap layer may be used where the completed system needs a clean, uniform top surface above the reinforced zone or stockpile. The material is typically placed in controlled lifts and compacted as needed to meet the project’s grading and stability requirements. The exact specification depends on the engineering design, but the governing idea is that the cap material should be suitable for final placement and should not compromise drainage, compaction, or long-term surface condition.

## Variants

Common variants or uses of clean cap material include:

- **Granular clean cap material:** Free-draining sand, gravel, or similar granular fill used where drainage and constructability are priorities.
- **Select structural cap fill:** Better-graded material chosen for higher compaction and improved bearing performance.
- **Drainage cap layer:** A clean, permeable material used when the cap must also promote drainage away from the surface or from within the stockpile.
- **Temporary cap material:** Material placed as a provisional protective layer during staged construction or until final grading is completed.
- **Final cover cap:** A permanent top layer intended to remain in place as the finished surface.

Alternatives may include native fill, engineered backfill, or other select materials, depending on the desired surface condition and performance requirements.

## Trade-offs

Key considerations when using clean cap material include:

- **Cost vs. performance:** Cleaner, more select material is usually more expensive than general fill.
- **Drainage vs. compactability:** Open, granular material drains well but may be harder to compact into a tight, stable surface than finer-grained select fill.
- **Availability:** Suitable material may not be locally available in sufficient quantity.
- **Erosion resistance:** A clean cap can be vulnerable to raveling or erosion if it is too granular or left unprotected.
- **Bearing capacity:** The material must support anticipated equipment and maintenance loads without excessive deformation.
- **Compatibility with underlying layers:** The cap should not allow migration of fines or create differential settlement issues.
