## Definition

Arsenic is a chemical element with symbol **As** and atomic number 33. It is a metalloid, meaning it exhibits properties intermediate between metals and nonmetals. In its pure form, arsenic is a brittle, steel-grey solid that sublimes when heated. In nature, it is rarely found as a free element and is most commonly encountered as a sulfide mineral or as a compound with oxygen, chlorine, or carbon. Arsenic is highly toxic in its inorganic forms and is a known carcinogen, yet it has been used historically in pesticides, wood preservatives, and metallurgy, and is being investigated for certain medical applications.

## How it Works

### Chemical and Physical Properties
Arsenic has three common allotropes: gray (metallic), yellow, and black. The gray form is the most stable at room temperature and is a good conductor of electricity. Arsenic exhibits oxidation states of −3, +3, and +5, with the +3 (arsenite) and +5 (arsenate) states being the most prevalent in environmental and biological systems.

### Toxicological Mechanism
The toxicity of arsenic arises primarily from its ability to replace phosphorus in biochemical reactions. Arsenate (As⁵⁺) mimics phosphate and can disrupt ATP synthesis, while arsenite (As³⁺) binds to thiol groups in proteins, inhibiting enzymatic activity. Chronic exposure leads to multi-organ damage, including skin lesions, peripheral neuropathy, cardiovascular disease, and cancers of the skin, lung, bladder, and liver.

### Environmental Cycling
Arsenic is mobilized through natural weathering of rocks, volcanic emissions, and anthropogenic activities such as mining, smelting, and agricultural use of arsenical pesticides. In water, the predominant species depend on redox conditions and pH. Under oxidizing conditions, arsenate (H₂AsO₄⁻, HAsO₄²⁻) dominates; under reducing conditions, uncharged arsenite (H₃AsO₃) is more mobile and toxic. Microorganisms can transform arsenic through methylation, volatilization, and reduction/oxidation (redox) reactions, affecting its transport and bioavailability.

### Detection and Measurement
Common analytical methods for arsenic include atomic absorption spectrometry (AAS), inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS), and hydride generation techniques. The detection limit is typically in the parts per billion (µg/L) range. In environmental sampling (e.g., groundwater, soil, building materials), total arsenic concentration is often measured, but speciation (distinguishing As(III) from As(V)) is critical for risk assessment.

## Variants

- **Inorganic arsenic**: The most toxic forms, primarily found as arsenite (As³⁺) and arsenate (As⁵⁺). Exposure typically occurs via drinking water from naturally contaminated aquifers or industrial pollution.
- **Organic arsenic**: Compounds such as arsenobetaine, arsenocholine, and monomethylarsonic acid (MMA) and dimethylarsinic acid (DMA). These are generally less toxic than inorganic forms. Arsenobetaine is abundant in seafood and is excreted unchanged by the human body.
- **Elemental arsenic**: The pure metallic form used in some alloys (e.g., lead shot, battery grids) and in semiconductor doping.
- **Arsine (AsH₃)**: A colorless, highly toxic gas used in the semiconductor industry and produced accidentally when arsenic-containing materials react with acids.

## Trade-offs

### Benefits vs. Risks
- Historically, arsenic compounds (e.g., Fowler's solution, Paul Ehrlich's Salvarsan) were used as medicines for syphilis and other diseases. Modern research explores arsenic trioxide as a treatment for acute promyelocytic leukemia (APL), where it induces apoptosis of cancer cells.
- In agriculture, arsenic-based pesticides and wood preservatives (e.g., chromated copper arsenate, CCA) effectively controlled pests and decay but were phased out in many countries due to environmental persistence and health hazards.
- In some industries, trace arsenic is deliberately added to improve performance (e.g., in lead-acid batteries to reduce corrosion), but handling requires strict containment.

### Environmental and Regulatory Challenges
- Remediation of arsenic-contaminated soil and water is expensive and technically challenging. Common methods include adsorption (e.g., iron oxide filter media), anion exchange, reverse osmosis, and phytoremediation using hyperaccumulator ferns.
- Global drinking water standards are typically 10 µg/L (WHO guideline), but many regions exceed this due to natural geogenic sources. Implementing affordable treatment in low-resource settings remains a critical problem.
- Proper disposal of arsenic-bearing waste (e.g., from mining, water treatment sludge) is essential to prevent secondary contamination.
