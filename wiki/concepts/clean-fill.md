## Clean Fill

### Definition
**Clean fill** refers to soil or other earthen material that has been tested and verified to be free of contamination at concentrations exceeding regulatory thresholds. It is suitable for reuse in construction projects without requiring special handling, disposal, or remediation. In regulatory contexts (e.g., California’s Department of Toxic Substances Control – DTSC), clean fill must meet specific chemical criteria for metals, volatile organic compounds (VOCs), semivolatile organic compounds (SVOCs), and other contaminants to protect human health and the environment. Clean fill is commonly used as backfill, grading material, or structural fill in transportation and infrastructure projects.

### How it works
The designation of material as clean fill follows a structured process:

1. **Sampling Plan Development** – A site-specific sampling plan is prepared and submitted to the regulatory agency (e.g., DTSC) for approval. The plan defines the number and location of samples, analytical methods, and target analytes. For example, the *Carpenter Road Shoofly Clean Fill Sampling Plan* and *Basin 5 Clean Fill Sampling Plan* outline such protocols.

2. **Field Sampling** – Samples are collected from stockpiles, borrow areas, or excavated material following the approved plan. Sampling strategies may be grid‑based, random, or targeted based on known history.

3. **Laboratory Analysis** – Samples are tested for constituents of concern, typically including:  
   - Total metals (e.g., arsenic, lead, chromium, mercury)  
   - VOCs (e.g., benzene, toluene)  
   - SVOCs (e.g., polynuclear aromatic hydrocarbons)  
   - Polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs)  
   - Pesticides/herbicides (if historical use warrants)  
   - Asbestos (if suspect)

4. **Comparison to Background/Criteria** – Results are compared to established background concentrations or regulatory screening levels. In California, DTSC’s *Human Health Risk Assessment (HHRA) Note 3* or *Environmental Screening Levels (ESLs)* are often used.

5. **Technical Memorandum & Approval** – A technical memorandum summarizing findings is submitted. Regulatory concurrence (via email or formal letter) confirms the material qualifies as clean fill, allowing its reuse. Examples include the *Approval E-mail for Carpenter Shoofly Clean Fill Sampling Plan* and *Revised Basin 5 Clean Fill Tech Memo Approval*.

6. **Variance for Special Circumstances** – In some cases, a variance from standard reuse protocols (e.g., the *RDIP Variance* for Caltrans Modesto soil stockpiles) can be obtained to expedite use of material that meets clean fill criteria but may fall under alternative management processes.

### Variants
The concept of clean fill is applied in several forms:

- **Stockpile Clean Fill** – Material from existing stockpiles (e.g., excavated soil from previous projects) that is sampled and approved for reuse within the same or nearby projects. The *Carpenter Road Shoofly* and *Basin 5* projects used stockpile clean fill for embankment and grading.

- **Borrow Area Clean Fill** – Material from designated borrow pits or extraction areas that is tested in advance to confirm its suitability as clean fill before excavation and transport.

- **Structural Fill for MSE Walls** – Clean fill used as reinforced backfill behind Mechanically Stabilized Earth (MSE) walls often requires additional quality control (e.g., particle size, compaction) but must still meet chemical clean fill standards.

- **Project‑Specific Clean Fill** – Some projects (e.g., highway widening, detention basins) may adopt site‑specific criteria based on land use (residential vs. commercial) and depth of placement.

### Trade-offs
| Trade-off | Considerations |
|-----------|----------------|
| **Cost of testing** | Sampling and laboratory analysis can be significant, especially for large volumes or complex contaminants. However, failing to test may lead to costly remediation or liability. |
| **Regulatory timeline** | Approval processes (plan review, memo concurrence) may delay project schedules. Variances can accelerate but require justification. |
| **Risk acceptance** | Clean fill thresholds are conservative; material that marginally fails may still be reusable under a different classification (e.g., “restricted use” fill), reducing waste but increasing management costs. |
| **Supply availability** | Sources of clean fill (stockpiles, borrow areas) may be limited, leading to procurement from external sites with additional transportation and testing costs. |
| **Liability** | Once designated clean fill, the generator or user assumes responsibility for its future use. Incorrect designation can result in environmental damage and legal penalties. |
