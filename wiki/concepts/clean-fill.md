## Definition

**Clean fill** refers to uncontaminated soil, rock, gravel, sand, or similar inert material that is suitable for use as backfill, grading, or structural fill without posing a risk to human health or the environment. It is typically sourced from borrow sites, stockpiles, or construction excavations and must meet regulatory standards for chemical and physical purity before placement. The term is most commonly used in environmental remediation and infrastructure projects where fill material must not introduce pollutants into clean areas.

## How it works

Clean fill is defined and verified through a systematic testing and approval process, as illustrated by the source documents (e.g., *SR 132 Basin 5 Clean Fill Tech Memo*, *Carpenter Shoofly Area Clean Fill Sampling Plan*). The process generally includes:

1. **Source Characterization**: A borrow area or stockpile is identified and investigated. Background samples are collected to establish natural soil chemistry (e.g., metals, pH, organic content). Potential contaminant sources (historical land use, adjacent spills) are documented.

2. **Sampling Plan**: A detailed plan (e.g., *Borrow Area Sampling Plan*) specifies:
   - Number and location of samples based on volume or area (composite or discrete).
   - Analytical parameters: total metals (arsenic, lead, chromium, etc.), volatile organic compounds (VOCs), semi-volatile organic compounds (SVOCs), pesticides, PCBs, and asbestos.
   - Laboratory methods (EPA methods) and quality assurance/quality control (QA/QC) requirements.

3. **Testing and Acceptance Criteria**: Material is accepted as clean fill if all detected concentrations fall below regulatory screening levels (e.g., California Human Health Screening Levels, DTSC’s “clean fill” thresholds). The *DTSC E-mail Approval* document indicates that state oversight may be required for final acceptance.

4. **Documentation and Approval**: A technical memorandum (e.g., *Carpenter Road Shoofly Clean Fill Tech Memo*) summarizes results and certifies compliance. After approval (e.g., by the Department of Toxic Substances Control), the material is cleared for use.

5. **Placement and Monitoring**: During placement, visual inspections and occasional retesting may be conducted to ensure no cross-contamination occurs. Final certification reports are submitted to regulatory agencies.

## Variants

- **Structural Clean Fill**: Engineered fill with specific compaction and gradation requirements (e.g., for road subbase, behind retaining walls). Testing focuses on physical properties as well as chemistry.
- **Environmental Clean Fill**: Used in remediated sites to restore grade; criteria are stricter (e.g., lower contaminant thresholds) and may require background comparisons.
- **On‑Site vs. Imported Fill**: On‑site fill from excavations often requires less testing if already characterized, while imported borrow sources require full upfront characterization.
- **Regulatory Variants**: Different jurisdictions (California DTSC, EPA, local agencies) define “clean” with varying levels of stringency (e.g., unrestricted use vs. restricted use under a cap).

## Trade‑offs

- **Cost vs. Certainty**: Extensive sampling and lab analysis ensure high confidence but increase project costs and timeline. Minimal testing risks accepting contaminated material, leading to costly re‑excavation and liability.
- **Availability**: Genuinely clean fill may be scarce in industrial or urban areas, forcing reliance on imported sources with higher transport costs and carbon footprint.
- **Regulatory Burden**: Obtaining DTSC or equivalent approval can delay construction if sampling plans are not pre‑approved. Conversely, using unapproved fill may void environmental permits.
- **Material Heterogeneity**: A few samples may not capture localized contamination; composite sampling improves representativeness but may mask hot spots.
- **Long‑Term Liability**: Even after acceptance, if fill later leaches contaminants, the responsible party (contractor, owner) may face cleanup orders.
