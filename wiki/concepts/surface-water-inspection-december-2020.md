## Definition

**Surface Water Inspection December 2020** refers to a documented inspection and review of surface-water conditions conducted in December 2020, likely as part of a site environmental assessment or regulatory compliance process. In this context, "surface water" generally means visible water bodies or runoff features such as ponds, streams, ditches, drainage channels, swales, and stormwater collection areas. The inspection would have aimed to identify physical conditions, contamination indicators, flow patterns, erosion, sediment transport, and potential impacts to nearby water resources.

Based on the source title, this concept is best understood as a specific inspection event recorded in a letter or report, rather than a general technical method. It is therefore both an event and a record of findings related to surface water observation and assessment.

## How it works

A surface water inspection typically follows a field-observation workflow:

1. **Site reconnaissance**
   Inspectors visit the property or affected area and identify all relevant surface-water features, including drainage paths, discharge points, accumulation areas, and nearby receptors.

2. **Visual assessment**
   The inspector evaluates:
   - water clarity and discoloration
   - odors
   - sheen or floating material
   - sediment buildup
   - erosion, scour, or bank instability
   - debris, trash, or visible pollutants
   - signs of active runoff or stagnant water

3. **Hydrologic and drainage review**
   The inspection may document how precipitation or site runoff moves across the area:
   - where water enters and exits the site
   - whether water ponds or infiltrates
   - whether drainage structures are functioning
   - whether overland flow may carry sediment or contaminants

4. **Compliance or impact screening**
   Findings are compared against environmental expectations, permit conditions, or site-management requirements. This may include checking for:
   - illicit discharges
   - erosion control failures
   - sediment migration
   - unauthorized alterations to drainage
   - impacts to wetlands or receiving waters

5. **Documentation**
   The results are typically recorded in a letter, memo, checklist, or report. The record may include:
   - inspection date
   - location descriptions
   - observations
   - photographs
   - recommendations or corrective actions

In a December 2020 inspection, seasonal conditions may have influenced what was observed. For example, winter precipitation, frozen ground, reduced vegetation, or storm events can change runoff behavior and make some impacts more or less visible.

## Variants

Surface water inspection can appear in several forms:

- **Routine visual inspection**  
  A periodic walk-through to note drainage, sediment, and discharge conditions.

- **Stormwater inspection**  
  Focused on runoff controls, outfalls, basins, and conveyance structures, often tied to stormwater management.

- **Environmental compliance inspection**  
  Performed to assess whether site conditions meet permit or regulatory requirements.

- **Construction-site inspection**  
  Targets erosion and sediment controls, temporary drainage, and disturbed soils.

- **Post-event inspection**  
  Conducted after heavy rain, flooding, or a spill to evaluate water movement and impacts.

- **Water quality-focused inspection**  
  May include field measurements or sampling in addition to visual observations.

- **Remote or desktop-supported inspection**  
  Uses maps, imagery, or records alongside site observations, especially when field access is limited.

## Trade-offs

Surface water inspections are useful, but they have clear limitations:

- **Strengths**
  - Low cost compared with instrumented monitoring
  - Fast to perform
  - Effective for identifying obvious problems
  - Useful for compliance documentation and corrective action planning

- **Limitations**
  - Largely qualitative unless paired with sampling or measurements
  - Conditions vary with weather, time of day, and season
  - Subsurface or intermittent contamination may be missed
  - One-time inspections may not represent long-term conditions
  - Visual observations can be subjective without standardized criteria

- **Operational considerations**
  - Access to all water features may be restricted
  - Winter conditions can obscure details or change flow paths
  - Safety concerns may limit close inspection of steep banks, deep water, or icy areas
  - Clear documentation standards are important for defensibility and repeatability
