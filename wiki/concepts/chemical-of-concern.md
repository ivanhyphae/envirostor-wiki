## Definition

A **Chemical of Concern** (COC) is a chemical substance identified in environmental monitoring programs as having the potential to pose an unacceptable risk to human health, ecological systems, or groundwater quality. In the context of groundwater investigations—such as the Modesto Stockpiles studies referenced in documents S9525-01-44B and S9800-01-17—COCs are typically those compounds detected above regulatory screening levels or background concentrations, requiring ongoing monitoring, reporting, and possible remediation.

## How it works

COC designation follows a structured process:

1. **Screening and identification** – Analytes are selected based on historical site use, known contaminants of concern (e.g., volatile organic compounds, metals, pesticides), and regulatory lists (e.g., RCRA, CERCLA, state-specific TPH categories). Samples from monitoring wells are analyzed using standard methods (EPA SW‑846, 8260B, 8270D, 6020B).

2. **Comparison to benchmarks** – Detected concentrations are compared to risk-based screening levels (RBSLs), maximum contaminant levels (MCLs), site-specific target levels (SSTLs), or background concentrations. Chemicals that exceed these thresholds are flagged as COCs.

3. **Temporal and spatial tracking** – Once designated, COCs are tracked across sampling events to evaluate trends (increasing, decreasing, stable) and plume migration. Groundwater elevation and contaminant concentration data are plotted to assess movement.

4. **Reporting and decision-making** – Find
ings are documented in quarterly or annual monitoring reports. Exceedances trigger further investigation, feasibility studies, or remedial action. In the Modesto Stockpiles groundwater monitoring reports, COCs would be listed in tables summarizing analytical results from each well, along with flagging codes (e.g., “J” for estimated value, “U” for not detected).

## Variants

While the term **Chemical of Concern** is used broadly, several related categories exist based on regulatory context and analytical chemistry:

- **Constituents of Concern (COCs)** – Often used interchangeably, especially in RCRA corrective action programs. Frequently includes both organic and inorganic compounds.

- **Parameters of Concern** – A broader term used in surface water or wastewater monitoring that may include non‑chemical indicators (pH, conductivity, dissolved oxygen) alongside chemical analytes.

- **Hazardous Constituents** – Specific to RCRA and Part 264/265 groundwater monitoring, listed in Appendix IX of 40 CFR Part 264. Includes heavy metals, chlorinated solvents, pesticides, and industrial chemicals.

- **Emerging Contaminants of Concern** – Compounds not traditionally regulated but increasingly detected (e.g., PFAS, 1,4‑dioxane, pharmaceuticals). May be added to monitoring suites as analytical methods mature.

- **Site‑Specific COCs** – Custom lists developed for a particular site based on historical releases, area geology, and receptor sensitivity. For example, Modesto Stockpiles sampling likely targets petroleum hydrocarbons, BTEX, lead, chromium, and chlorinated solvents typical of industrial stockpile areas.

## Trade-offs

| Consideration | Benefit | Limitation |
|---------------|---------|------------|
| **Breadth of analyte list** | Comprehensive screening captures unexpected contaminants. | Higher analytical cost; potential for false positives or detections below meaningful risk levels. |
| **Focus on detection limits** | Low detection limits protect sensitive receptors. | May trigger regulatory concern for trace-level detections that are not biologically significant. |
| **Temporal monitoring frequency** | Frequent sampling detects trends early. | Increased program cost; seasonal/hydrologic noise may obscure real changes. |
| **Use of screening levels** | Provides consistent, defensible benchmarks. | Generic levels may not reflect local background or bioavailability; site‑specific adjustments require additional study. |
| **Inclusion of emerging contaminants** | Proactive risk management. | Methods may not be fully validated; action levels may be absent or controversial. |
| **Exclusion of non‑detected parameters** | Reduces data clutter and focus. | May miss infrequent pulses or episodic releases if monitoring intervals are too wide. |
