## Definition

A **feasibility study** is a systematic evaluation of the practicality, viability, and likely success of a proposed project, system, or course of action. It examines multiple dimensions—technical, economic, legal, operational, and scheduling—to determine whether the undertaking should proceed, be modified, or be abandoned. The primary output is a recommendation supported by evidence, often leading to a go/no‑go decision.

In the context of environmental remediation, such as the *Modesto Soil Stockpiles Final FS Report* (the encrypted PDF provided), a feasibility study typically analyzes alternative cleanup strategies, their costs, regulatory compliance, and long‑term effectiveness.

## How it works

A standard feasibility study follows a structured process:

1. **Preliminary analysis** – Define the problem or opportunity, establish objectives, and identify constraints.
2. **Scope definition** – Determine the boundaries of the study (e.g., geographic area, time horizon, affected stakeholders).
3. **Data collection** – Gather technical specifications, cost estimates, regulatory requirements, market data, and any existing site‑specific information (e.g., soil contamination levels in the Modesto report).
4. **Alternative development** – Generate a set of plausible options (e.g., different remediation technologies, business models, engineering designs).
5. **Evaluation criteria** – Apply quantitative and qualitative metrics such as net present value (NPV), return on investment (ROI), risk exposure, environmental impact, and legal compliance.
6. **Analysis and comparison** – Use tools like cost‑benefit analysis, multi‑criteria decision analysis (MCDA), or simulation models to rank alternatives.
7. **Recommendation** – Present the most feasible option(s) with supporting rationale, including any necessary contingencies or next steps.

The output is typically a written report (e.g., the *Final FS Report* for the Modesto soil stockpiles) that documents the methodology, assumptions, and findings. The PDF source file, though encrypted, suggests the study followed this standard framework for an environmental remediation project.

## Variants

Feasibility studies are adapted to various domains. Common variants include:

- **Technical feasibility** – Assesses whether the required technology, materials, and engineering expertise exist and can be implemented (e.g., evaluating in‑situ soil stabilization versus excavation for the Modesto stockpiles).
- **Economic/financial feasibility** – Focuses on cost estimates, funding sources, revenue projections, and payback periods.
- **Legal/regulatory feasibility** – Examines compliance with laws, permits, zoning, and environmental regulations.
- **Operational feasibility** – Evaluates whether the organization can support the project (staffing, workflows, maintenance).
- **Schedule feasibility** – Determines if the project can be completed within an acceptable timeframe.
- **Market feasibility** – Common in business contexts to assess demand, competition, and market entry.
- **Environmental feasibility** – Specific to projects affecting natural resources; often required under laws like the U.S. National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) or the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act (CERCLA). The Modesto soil stockpiles study likely falls under this variant.

Each variant can be performed standalone or combined into a comprehensive feasibility study.

## Trade-offs

Key considerations when conducting or relying on a feasibility study:

| Trade‑off | Explanation |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Depth vs. cost** | A thorough study requires significant time, data, and expert resources; shortcuts can lead to flawed conclusions. |
| **Optimism bias** | Sponsors may unconsciously favor options that appear more attractive, skewing assumptions. |
| **Uncertainty** | Future conditions (e.g., regulatory changes, commodity prices, contaminant behavior) are inherently unpredictable; sensitivity analysis helps but cannot eliminate risk. |
| **Scope creep** | The study’s boundaries can expand beyond the original question, delaying decisions and increasing costs. |
| **Data availability** | In projects like contaminated soil remediation, site‑specific data may be sparse or incomplete, forcing reliance on generic estimates (as might be the case in the Modesto report). |
| **Stakeholder alignment** | Different parties (regulators, community, investors) may have conflicting criteria, making a single “feasible” solution difficult. |
| **Sedimentation of alternatives** | Once a recommendation is made, decision‑makers may become locked into that path, ignoring new information that could alter feasibility. |

Despite these trade‑offs, a well‑executed feasibility study remains a foundational decision‑making tool, reducing the risk of failure and providing a defensible rationale for action.
