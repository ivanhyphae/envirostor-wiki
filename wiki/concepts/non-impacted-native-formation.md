## Definition

**Non Impacted Native Formation** refers to native geological material (soil, sediment, or bedrock) that has not been altered, contaminated, or disturbed by anthropogenic (human-caused) activities. In environmental assessment and remediation contexts, it represents the natural baseline condition of subsurface materials prior to any industrial, agricultural, or development-related impacts. This material is distinct from both fill materials and impacted formations that exhibit elevated contaminant concentrations due to human activity.

## How it works

Non Impacted Native Formation is identified and characterized through systematic field investigation and analytical testing:

1. **Field Classification**: Geologists or environmental professionals examine recovered soil cores or cuttings for visual indicators of disturbance (e.g., absence of fill materials like concrete fragments, coal, or slag) and evidence of natural soil development (pedogenic horizons, root zones, consistent color and texture).

2. **Laboratory Analysis**: Representative samples undergo chemical testing for target analytes (e.g., metals, volatile organic compounds, petroleum hydrocarbons) to determine whether concentrations fall within expected natural background levels for the region.

3. **Comparison to Background**: Results are compared against site-specific background concentrations or published regional reference values. If detected concentrations are statistically indistinguishable from natural background and no visible evidence of contamination exists, the formation is classified as non impacted.

4. **Stratigraphic Correlation**: The vertical and lateral extent of non impacted native formations is mapped using geological logging and field screening tools to differentiate them from overlying fill or impacted zones.

In the referenced context (FW SR 132 Basin 5 Clean Fill Sampling Plan), the term is used to define material suitable for clean fill applications at a construction site—material that can be safely handled and reused without posing environmental risk.

## Variants

- **Native Formation (Unimpacted)**: Any indigenous geological unit that has never been subjected to contamination—the broadest category.
- **Clean Native Formation**: Native material meeting specific regulatory thresholds for reuse as clean fill, often requiring chemical demonstration.
- **Mineralized Native Formation**: Naturally enriched geological material (e.g., metal-rich bedrock) that may exhibit elevated analyte concentrations but is not anthropogenically impacted; requires careful differentiation from true contamination.
- **Reworked Native Formation**: Native sediment that has been disturbed by natural processes (e.g., erosion, bioturbation) but not by human activity—still considered non impacted if no contamination is introduced.
- **Urban Native Formation**: Geological units beneath urban areas that may have been physically disturbed during construction but remain chemically unimpacted.

## Trade-offs

- **Distinguishing from Fill**: Differentiating non impacted native formation from anthropogenic fill can be difficult, especially in heterogeneous urban settings where native soil may be mixed with demolition debris. Visual inspection alone may be insufficient.
- **Background Variability**: Natural background concentrations can vary significantly across a site due to soil type, geology, and hydrology. Defining "non impacted" requires robust baseline data.
- **Analytical Costs**: Demonstrating non impacted status requires laboratory testing, which adds project costs compared to relying solely on field classification.
- **Regulatory Acceptance**: Some regulators require specific sampling protocols and statistical comparisons to demonstrate native material is truly non impacted, particularly for large-scale fill operations.
- **Depth and Accessibility**: Deep native formations may be expensive to sample and verify, leading to potential underestimation of impacted conditions.
- **Dynamic Boundaries**: The boundary between impacted and non impacted zones can be irregular and difficult to define precisely, requiring careful horizontal and vertical delineation.
