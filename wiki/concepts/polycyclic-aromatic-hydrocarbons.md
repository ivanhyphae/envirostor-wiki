## Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbons

### Definition
Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) are a class of organic compounds composed of two or more fused aromatic rings made solely of carbon and hydrogen. They are neutral, nonpolar molecules formed during incomplete combustion of organic materials such as coal, oil, gas, wood, and tobacco. PAHs are ubiquitous environmental contaminants, many of which are known or suspected carcinogens, mutagens, and endocrine disruptors. The simplest PAH is naphthalene (two fused benzene rings), while larger structures like benzo[a]pyrene (five rings) are among the most studied due to their high toxicity.

### How It Works
**Formation and Sources**  
PAHs form through pyrolysis (thermal decomposition) and pyrosynthesis (recombination of small free radicals) at temperatures between 500 °C and 800 °C. Incomplete combustion in engines, power plants, forest fires, cigarette smoke, and grilled foods releases PAHs bound to particulate matter. Industrial processes—including coal tar production, asphalt sealing, and aluminum smelting—also contribute. Once released, PAHs partition between air (absorbed on soot), water (adsorbed to sediment), and soil (bound to organic matter).

**Environmental Fate**  
Due to their low water solubility and high lipophilicity (log K<sub>ow</sub> 3–7), PAHs accumulate in soils, sediments, and fatty tissues. Photodegradation and microbial biodegradation (especially by bacteria like *Mycobacterium* and *Sphingomonas*) are primary breakdown pathways, but rates vary with ring size: two- to three-ring PAHs degrade relatively quickly, while five- and six-ring PAHs persist for years.

**Mechanism of Toxicity**  
PAHs require metabolic activation to exert genotoxicity. The cytochrome P450 (CYP) enzyme family, particularly CYP1A1, epoxidates PAHs. The resulting diol epoxides (e.g., benzo[a]pyrene‑7,8‑diol‑9,10‑epoxide) can covalently bind to DNA, forming adducts that cause mutations if unrepaired. PAHs also induce oxidative stress and interact with the aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AhR), triggering expression of xenobiotic‑metabolizing enzymes. Chronic exposure is linked to lung, skin, and bladder cancer, as well as reproductive and developmental effects.

### Variants
PAHs are categorized by ring count and arrangement:

- **Low molecular weight (LMW)** – 2–3 rings. Examples: naphthalene, acenaphthene, fluorene, phenanthrene. More volatile, less carcinogenic, and more susceptible to biodegradation. Often found in crude oil and gasoline.
- **High molecular weight (HMW)** – 4–7 rings. Examples: pyrene, benzo[a]anthracene, chrysene, benzo[a]pyrene, dibenzo[a,h]anthracene. Highly stable, strongly sorbed to particles, and potent carcinogens. Benzo[a]pyrene (BaP) is the most studied and used as a toxicity surrogate.

**Structural variants** include:
- **Alternant vs. non‑alternant** PAHs – Alternant (all rings benzenoid) are more stable; non‑alternant (containing odd‑membered rings like cyclopenta) are rarer but more reactive.
- **Alkylated PAHs** – Methyl‑, ethyl‑, or dimethyl‑substituted forms (e.g., 1‑methylnaphthalene). Common in petroleum, often more toxic than parent compounds.
- **Oxygenated, nitrated, and heterocyclic PAHs** – Derivatives formed during atmospheric reactions (e.g., nitropyrene). Some are direct‑acting mutagens—no metabolic activation required.

### Trade‑offs

**Beneficial uses vs. health risks**  
PAHs are valuable chemical intermediates (naphthalene for phthalates, phenanthrene for resins) and occur in pharmaceuticals (e.g., anthralin for psoriasis). However, the same stability that makes them industrially useful also causes environmental persistence. The carcinogenicity threshold is controversial: no safe level of exposure is generally accepted for genotoxic PAHs, yet complete elimination from combustion processes is impractical.

**Remediation challenges**  
- **Physical methods** (soil excavation, dredging) are fast but costly and transfer contamination.  
- **Bioremediation** (using fungi or bacteria) is cheaper but slow and incomplete for HMW PAHs.  
- **Chemical oxidation** (e.g., Fenton’s reagent) can break down PAHs rapidly but may create toxic by‑products.  
- **Thermal desorption** efficiently removes PAHs but consumes energy and alters soil properties.

**Analytical detection**  
PAHs are typically quantified by GC‑MS or HPLC with fluorescence detection. Limits of detection are in the picogram range, but extraction (Soxhlet, pressurized solvent) from complex matrices (soil, tissue) is labor‑intensive. Interferences from co‑extracted organics can bias results, requiring rigorous clean‑up. The U.S. EPA lists 16 priority PAHs, but many toxic alkylated congeners are unregulated.

**Regulatory trade‑offs**  
Risk assessment often uses BaP‑equivalent toxicity factors (TEFs) to sum exposures, but TEFs ignore mixture interactions (antagonism/synergy). Regulating individual PAHs misses the additive effect of unmeasured derivatives.
