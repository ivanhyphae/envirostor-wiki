## Definition

**Encapsulated Soil Stockpiles** are engineered temporary storage structures for excavated soil, where the soil is enclosed within a geomembrane liner system to prevent contaminant migration, dust generation, and rainwater infiltration. The encapsulation isolates potentially contaminated soil from the surrounding environment, enabling safe staging during remediation projects before final treatment or disposal.

In the context of well destruction and environmental remediation, encapsulated stockpiles are used to contain soils excavated during well abandonment that may contain hydrocarbons, drilling fluids, or other hazardous materials. The liner system typically includes a bottom liner, a top cover, and containment features to collect any leachate or runoff.

## How it works

The encapsulation system operates through a multi-layer barrier design:

1. **Base Preparation**: The ground is graded to create a stable, sloped base that directs potential leachate toward a collection point. A geotextile fabric is often placed to protect the liner from sharp objects.

2. **Primary Liner**: A geomembrane (typically 30–60 mil HDPE or LLDPE) is installed as the bottom barrier. Seams are heat-welded or chemically bonded to ensure fluid impermeability.

3. **Soil Placement**: Excavated soil is placed on the liner in layers, often with compaction to minimize void spaces. The stockpile height is limited (usually ≤10 feet) to prevent liner rupture from pressure.

4. **Cover System**: After soil placement, a top geomembrane liner is installed and anchored using sandbags, tire weights, or perimeter trenching. The cover prevents rainwater infiltration and dust emissions.

5. **Containment Features**: 
   - **Perimeter Berms**: Soil or sand berms around the stockpile base to contain any runoff.
   - **Leachate Collection**: Perforated pipes or sumps beneath the soil layer to capture liquids that percolate through the stockpile.
   - **Gas Venting**: In cases of volatile organic compounds (VOCs), passive or active ventilation pipes are installed to prevent gas accumulation.

6. **Monitoring**: Stockpiles are periodically inspected for liner integrity, accumulation of liquids, and gas emissions. Sampling of contained soil may occur to verify remediation progress.

## Variants

1. **Above-Grade Encapsulated Stockpiles**: The most common type, where soil is mounded on a prepared liner pad, covered with a top liner, and surrounded by berms. Suitable for large volumes (e.g., thousands of cubic yards).

2. **Below-Grade Encapsulated Stockpiles**: Soil is placed in excavated pits lined with geomembrane, then covered. Used when visual impact or space constraints are concerns. Requires groundwater control to prevent hydrostatic uplift.

3. **Cellular Encapsulation**: Multiple smaller stockpiles within separate liner cells, allowing segregation of soils with different contamination profiles. Facilitates targeted treatment or disposal.

4. **Temporary vs. Long-Term**: Temporary stockpiles (weeks to months) may use simpler covers (e.g., reinforced polyethylene), while long-term stockpiles (years) require UV-resistant liners and more robust anchoring.

5. **Active Ventilation Variants**: For soils with high VOC content, vacuum pumps or blowers are attached to vent pipes to remove gases for treatment, reducing explosion risk and enabling vapor recovery.

## Trade-offs

| **Aspect** | **Advantages** | **Limitations** |
|------------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Environmental Control** | Prevents soil migration, dust, and contaminant leaching; reduces groundwater impact | Risk of liner puncture during placement; requires ongoing inspection |
| **Cost** | Lower than immediate off-site disposal; allows batch treatment | High initial capital for liner and installation; monitoring and repair costs add up |
| **Volume Capacity** | Can handle large volumes (e.g., 500–10,000 m³) | Limited by site footprint; tall stockpiles (>15 ft) increase liner stress |
| **Duration** | Flexible storage time (weeks to years) | Liners degrade under UV (if uncovered); long-term storage may require secondary containment |
| **Regulatory Compliance** | Satisfies many agency requirements for temporary soil storage | Requires permits; leachate collection may be mandatory in some jurisdictions |
| **Treatment Integration** | Can incorporate aeration, biopiles, or chemical amendments through liner penetration | Adding amendments compromises liner integrity; specialized ports needed |

**Key Limitations**:
- **Liner Failure**: Punctures from rocks, equipment, or wildlife can go undetected, leading to contaminant release.
- **Leachate Management**: Collected liquids may be hazardous and require treatment or disposal, increasing operational complexity.
- **Space Requirements**: Encapsulated stockpiles occupy large footprints, which may conflict with concurrent site activities.
- **Gas Hazards**: Anaerobic decomposition in organic-rich soils can generate flammable methane or toxic hydrogen sulfide.
- **End-of-Life**: Decommissioning requires careful removal and disposal of liners as potentially contaminated waste.
