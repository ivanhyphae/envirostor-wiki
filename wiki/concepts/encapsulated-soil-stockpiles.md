## Definition

Encapsulated soil stockpiles are temporary storage piles of excavated or contaminated soil that are enclosed within a impermeable barrier system to prevent the release of pollutants—such as sediment, nutrients, heavy metals, or hydrocarbons—into the surrounding environment. The encapsulation typically consists of a geomembrane liner (e.g., HDPE) or a reinforced fabric cover sealed at the base and seams, creating a waterproof containment envelope. This practice is common in construction stormwater management, remediation projects, and industrial sites where soil must be stockpiled for extended periods or where the soil has known contamination.

## How it works

The encapsulation system functions by physically isolating the stockpiled material from precipitation, runoff, and groundwater. The process typically involves:

1. **Site preparation**: The stockpile area is graded to promote drainage, and a compacted subbase is installed to minimize settlement.
2. **Liner installation**: A flexible membrane (e.g., HDPE, LLDPE, or reinforced polypropylene) is laid over the prepared area with adequate overlap and seam welding to create a continuous barrier. The liner extends upward along the sides of the pile and is anchored in a perimeter trench or weighted with sandbags.
3. **Soil placement**: Clean or contaminated soil is placed on the liner in a controlled manner, often in layers to manage slope stability.
4. **Top cover**: A second layer of liner or a UV‑resistant cover (e.g., reinforced polyethylene fabric) is placed over the top of the pile and sealed to the base liner, forming a complete envelope. This cover may include a vent system to allow gas escape if the soil contains volatile organics.
5. **Sealing and anchoring**: The top and bottom liners are sealed together using thermal welding or adhesive tapes. The edges are buried in a key trench (≥30 cm deep) or weighted with sandbags/soil berms to prevent uplift from wind.
6. **Monitoring and maintenance**: Regular inspections check for tears, seam failures, or ponding water. Stormwater runoff from the stockpile area is diverted to sediment basins or treatment systems. Sampling of water accumulated on top of the cover (if present) may be required under permits.

The encapsulation prevents rainwater from infiltrating the soil pile, thereby eliminating the generation of contaminated leachate. It also prevents airborne dust emissions and limits fugitive odors.

## Variants

Several implementations exist, tailored to soil type, regulatory requirements, and duration of storage:

- **Temporary stockpile covers**: Heavy‑duty woven polypropylene tarps secured with sandbags or stakes. Used for short‑term (days to weeks) storage of clean soil during active construction.
- **Geomembrane‑lined berms**: A permanent‑looking containment cell where soil is placed inside a pit lined with HDPE and covered with a floating membrane. Common in long‑term (months to years) remediation stockpiles.
- **Self‑contained bag systems**: Large, flexible geotextile containers (geobags) filled with soil and sealed. Used when stockpile space is limited or for transport.
- **Bio‑encapsulation**: Addition of organic amendments (e.g., compost) inside the liner to promote microbial degradation of contaminants while maintaining physical containment.
- **Active ventilation stockpiles**: Encapsulation with forced air circulation through perforated pipes to keep aerobic conditions for bioremediation or to dissipate flammable gases.

## Trade-offs

| Advantage | Limitation |
|-----------|------------|
| Eliminates leachate generation | High initial cost for liners, welding, and anchoring |
| Reduces dust and odor | Liners require regular inspection and repair; UV degradation possible |
| Enables long‑term storage | Seams can fail if not properly welded or protected from mechanical damage |
| Compatible with bioremediation | Gas buildup (e.g., methane) may require vents and monitoring |
| Lowers stormwater compliance risk | May increase water vapor condensation inside, potentially raising soil moisture |

Key considerations:
- **Cost**: Geomembrane encapsulation can cost $5–15 per square meter for material and installation, plus labor for trenching and sealing.
- **Permitting**: In many jurisdictions, encapsulated stockpiles must be included in Stormwater Pollution Prevention Plans (SWPPPs) and are subject to weekly visual inspections and quarterly water sampling if runoff contact is possible.
- **Closure**: When the stockpile is removed, the liner must be decommissioned—either removed and disposed of or left in place with institutional controls.
- **Climate**: In freeze‑thaw cycles, liners may become brittle; in hot climates, UV exposure requires UV‑stabilized materials.
