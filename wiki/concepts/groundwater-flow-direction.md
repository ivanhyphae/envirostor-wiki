## Definition

**Groundwater flow direction** is the direction in which groundwater moves through the subsurface, from points of higher hydraulic head to points of lower hydraulic head. It reflects the combined effects of elevation, pressure, aquifer properties, and boundary conditions, and is typically inferred from groundwater-level measurements in monitoring wells.

In practice, flow direction is usually represented on a map or cross section by plotting water-level elevations in wells and drawing contours of equal hydraulic head. Groundwater flows approximately perpendicular to these contours, from higher head toward lower head.

## How it works

Groundwater movement is governed by hydraulic gradient and Darcy's law. Water in an aquifer moves through pore spaces or fractures in response to differences in hydraulic head, which is the energy state of groundwater at a point. Hydraulic head includes:

- **Elevation head**: the vertical position of the water relative to a datum
- **Pressure head**: the pressure exerted by water in the aquifer
- In some settings, **velocity head** is included, though it is usually negligible in groundwater studies

### Determining flow direction

The standard workflow is:

1. **Measure groundwater levels** in wells or piezometers.
2. **Convert water levels to hydraulic head** using a common reference datum.
3. **Map contours of equal head** across the site or aquifer.
4. **Infer flow direction** as roughly perpendicular to the contour lines, from higher values to lower values.

If a site has multiple aquifer zones or screened intervals, flow direction may differ by depth. In layered systems, shallow groundwater can flow in a different direction than deeper groundwater because of vertical hydraulic gradients, local pumping, recharge, or low-permeability layers.

### Factors that control flow direction

Groundwater flow direction can be influenced by:

- **Topography**: recharge areas often create outward or downslope groundwater flow
- **Aquifer heterogeneity**: variations in permeability can deflect flow paths
- **Pumping wells**: drawdown can locally reverse or capture flow direction
- **Recharge and infiltration**: can create mound-like head patterns
- **Surface-water features**: rivers, drains, and lakes can act as gaining or losing boundaries
- **Geologic barriers**: clay layers, faults, and confining units can redirect flow

### Interpretation in site investigations

In environmental and geotechnical studies, groundwater flow direction is essential for predicting:

- migration of dissolved contaminants
- capture zones for extraction wells
- interaction between stockpiles, ponds, and adjacent groundwater
- whether a source area is hydraulically upgradient or downgradient of a receptor

The Modesto Stockpiles groundwater reports cited in the source material are the kind of monitoring studies used to establish site-specific groundwater elevations and infer flow direction over time.

## Variants

### Horizontal flow direction
The most commonly reported form, describing flow direction in plan view across a water table or potentiometric surface.

### Vertical flow direction
Describes upward or downward movement between aquifer zones. This is determined from head differences between shallow and deep wells or nested piezometers.

### Water table flow direction
Used in unconfined aquifers, based on the slope of the water table.

### Potentiometric flow direction
Used in confined aquifers, based on the potentiometric surface rather than the water table.

### Site-specific seasonal flow direction
Flow direction may change between monitoring events due to seasonal recharge, irrigation, pumping, or river stage changes. A site may therefore have different directions in groundwater monitoring campaigns conducted in different months.

### Regional vs local flow direction
- **Regional flow direction** reflects broad basin-scale gradients.
- **Local flow direction** reflects site-scale influences such as pumping, excavation, stockpiles, or nearby drains.

## Trade-offs

### Advantages
- Directly useful for identifying upgradient and downgradient relationships
- Supports contaminant transport and remedial design
- Can be estimated from relatively simple field data

### Limitations
- Accuracy depends on well placement, screen depth, and measurement precision
- Sparse data can obscure local flow variability
- Flow direction may be transient and change over time
- Contour interpretation may be misleading in highly heterogeneous or anisotropic aquifers
- Vertical gradients are often missed if only shallow wells are monitored

### Practical considerations
- Use a consistent datum and synchronized water-level measurements
- Compare wells screened at the same depth interval when possible
- Consider pumping, recharge, and nearby surface water when interpreting results
- Verify whether the site behaves as an unconfined or confined system
- Report uncertainty when contours are poorly constrained
