## Definition

The **Carpenter Shoofly** is a designated area within an environmental remediation or construction project where clean fill material is placed, managed according to a specific sampling plan approved by the California Department of Toxic Substances Control (DTSC). The term “shoofly” historically refers to a temporary bypass or diversion (e.g., a railroad shoofly), but in this context it describes a controlled fill zone used to restore or stabilize a site after contamination has been addressed. The Carpenter Shoofly Clean Fill Sampling Plan outlines the procedures for verifying that imported fill meets regulatory criteria for clean backfill, ensuring no recontamination occurs.

## How it works

The Carpenter Shoofly Clean Fill Sampling Plan establishes a systematic process for approving and monitoring fill material before it is placed in the shoofly area. Key elements include:

1. **Source Verification** – All fill material must come from approved sources (e.g., certified clean fill sites or construction sites with documented background quality).  
2. **Sampling Frequency** – A statistically based sampling schedule is defined, often one composite sample per a specified volume (e.g., per 500 cubic yards) or per source batch.  
3. **Analytical Parameters** – Samples are tested for contaminants of concern (COCs) relevant to the site, such as heavy metals, petroleum hydrocarbons, volatile organic compounds (VOCs), and semi-volatile organic compounds (SVOCs). The plan specifies the analytical methods (e.g., EPA 8260 for VOCs).  
4. **Acceptance Criteria** – Results must fall below site-specific health‑based or regulatory action levels (often DTSC’s California Human Health Screening Levels, CHHSLs). Any exceedance triggers rejection or additional evaluation.  
5. **Documentation and Approval** – Sampling results are compiled into a technical memorandum submitted to DTSC. The DTSC e‑mail approval (provided in the referenced source) confirms that the plan and resulting data meet regulatory requirements, allowing fill placement to proceed.

The shoofly area itself is typically excavated to remove historical contamination, then backfilled with the approved clean fill under a construction quality assurance (CQA) program.

## Variants

While the Carpenter Shoofly is a specific project, the general concept of a “shoofly” fill area appears in several related forms:

- **Railroad Shoofly** – A temporary track diversion used during construction, sometimes repurposed as a fill zone.  
- **Remedial Shoofly** – A stockpile or fill area used to stage clean backfill during a larger cleanup operation (e.g., former industrial sites).  
- **Clean Fill Screening** – Alternative programs (e.g., DTSC’s Clean Imported Fill Policy) that apply similar sampling but may use different statistical plans or acceptance thresholds.  
- **Beneficial Use Shoofly** – A fill area receiving material from on‑site remediation (e.g., treated soil) rather than imported clean fill.

The Carpenter Shoofly variant is distinguished by its explicit DTSC‑approved sampling plan and the requirement for an e‑mailed concurrency prior to placement.

## Trade-offs

- **Cost vs. Certainty** – Intensive sampling ensures high confidence in fill quality but increases project costs (labor, laboratory analysis). Reducing sample density risks accepting contaminated material, leading to future liability.  
- **Regulatory Delays** – Awaiting DTSC e‑mail approval can stall construction schedules. The Carpenter Shoofly plan mitigated this by defining clear pre‑approval criteria, but any deviation requires a new approval cycle.  
- **Limited Flexibility** – The approved plan specifies exact analytes and frequencies; if unexpected contaminants are later identified, the plan may need amendment.  
- **Volume Constraints** – The shoofly area has finite capacity; oversourcing fill or rejecting batches can cause logistical bottlenecks.  
- **Long‑Term Liability** – Even with robust sampling, some residual risk remains (e.g., heterogeneous contamination not captured). The plan must include a closure process (e.g., cap or deed restriction) if necessary.
