## Definition

A **Stiff diagram** is a graphical method used in hydrochemistry to summarize the ionic composition of a water sample. It plots major cations on one side of a central vertical axis and major anions on the other, using equivalent concentrations. The resulting polygon gives a quick visual “fingerprint” of water chemistry and is commonly used to compare groundwater samples across wells, depths, seasons, or monitoring events.

In practice, the diagram is an interpretive tool rather than a direct measurement: it converts laboratory ion concentrations into milliequivalents per liter or similar charge-based units so that chemically equivalent amounts can be compared.

## How it works

A Stiff diagram is built from the major dissolved ions typically reported in groundwater or surface water analysis:

- **Cations:** calcium (Ca²⁺), magnesium (Mg²⁺), sodium plus potassium (Na⁺ + K⁺)
- **Anions:** bicarbonate plus carbonate (HCO₃⁻ + CO₃²⁻), sulfate (SO₄²⁻), chloride (Cl⁻)

The steps are:

1. **Collect chemical data** from a water sample.
2. **Convert concentrations to charge equivalents**  
   Because ions carry different electrical charges, values are converted from mg/L to milliequivalents per liter (meq/L) or another equivalent-based unit.
3. **Plot the ions on fixed horizontal scales**  
   - Cations are usually plotted to the left of a vertical centerline.
   - Anions are plotted to the right.
4. **Connect the plotted points** to form a polygon.

The shape of the polygon reflects the relative dominance of ions. For example:

- A sample dominated by calcium and bicarbonate tends to produce a wide shape on the left and right at those positions.
- A sodium- and chloride-rich sample produces a different polygon, often indicating more saline or chemically evolved water.
- Similar shapes across multiple samples suggest similar water sources or geochemical processes.

Stiff diagrams are especially useful for rapidly comparing multiple groundwater samples in reports such as monitoring well investigations, where the chemistry of different wells or sampling rounds needs to be summarized visually.

## Variants

Common variants and related diagram types include:

- **Classic Stiff diagram**  
  The standard polygon plot of major cations and anions on a shared axis.
- **Multi-sample Stiff diagrams**  
  Several diagrams shown together for spatial or temporal comparison.
- **Normalized or scaled Stiff diagrams**  
  Use consistent axis limits so sample shapes are directly comparable.
- **Alternatives in hydrochemistry**
  - Piper diagram: compares cation and anion proportions in ternary fields and is often used for water type classification.
  - Schoeller diagram: plots ion concentrations on a logarithmic scale for comparison across samples.
  - Durov diagram: combines cation, anion, and salinity information in a more interpretive matrix.

## Trade-offs

### Advantages
- Fast visual summary of major-ion chemistry
- Easy to compare many samples
- Helpful for identifying shifts in water type, mixing, or salinization
- Simple to produce and interpret once the scale is standardized

### Limitations
- Does not show trace constituents, isotopes, or contamination compounds
- Strongly dependent on consistent scaling and equivalent-unit conversion
- Can be misleading if different diagrams use different axis ranges
- Best for comparative interpretation, not detailed geochemical modeling
- Does not uniquely identify processes; similar shapes can arise from different combinations of mixing, dissolution, or ion exchange

### Considerations
- Use only major ions with appropriate charge-balance handling
- Ensure bicarbonate/carbonate speciation is consistent with pH and alkalinity data
- Compare samples only when the plotting convention and scale are the same
- Interpret alongside field parameters, laboratory chemistry, and hydrogeologic context
