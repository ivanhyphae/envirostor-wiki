## Definition

**Stockpile 1** is a designated temporary soil storage location within the Basin 5 Clean Fill project area, part of the Caltrans SR‑132 improvement. It is specifically used for the staging of clean fill material—excavated soil that meets uncontaminated criteria—during construction. The stockpile is one of several (e.g., Stockpile 2) shown in the project’s grading plans and cross‑sections, and its design, operation, and closure are governed by the approved **Revised Basin 5 Clean Fill Tech Memo** and the associated **RDIP Variance** (Regional Deficiency Improvement Program).

## How it works

### Purpose and sourcing  
Stockpile 1 receives soil from onsite excavations or approved offsite sources that has been tested and classified as clean fill. The material is temporarily stored to support construction sequencing—balancing cut‑and‑fill operations, reducing hauling distances, and providing a ready supply for backfill or grading.

### Design and grading  
The stockpile’s footprint and final configuration are defined in **Figure 3‑1** (Stockpile Grading Plan 1) and the generalized cross‑section (**Figure 4A**) from the RDIP All Figures document. Key design parameters include:

- **Maximum height and slopes** – to maintain stability and prevent erosion.  
- **Cover and erosion controls** – temporary seeding, hydroseed, or plastic sheeting as needed.  
- **Buffer zones** – separation from drainage features, property lines, or sensitive areas.

### Environmental and regulatory controls  
Because Stockpile 1 stores clean fill, it must comply with:

- **RDIP Variance** – allows deviation from standard Caltrans soil stockpile requirements (e.g., longer retention times, larger volume) provided stormwater and dust controls are implemented.  
- **Construction General Permit** (NPDES) – requires a Storm Water Pollution Prevention Plan (SWPPP).  
- **Basin 5 Clean Fill Memo** – specifies testing protocols, acceptance criteria, and recordkeeping for all material placed in Stockpile 1.

### Operations  
- **Material placement** – soil is dumped, spread, and compacted in lifts to minimize voids and erosion.  
- **Monitoring** – visual inspections for dust, sediment runoff, and stability are conducted weekly and after significant rain events.  
- **Removal** – when needed for construction, material is hauled from Stockpile 1 to the fill location. The stockpile is then regraded and stabilized.

### Closure  
After the project’s grading is complete, Stockpile 1 is either completely removed (exported) or left in place if part of the final landscape, with permanent vegetation and erosion control installed. Closure must be documented as per the Tech Memo and approved by the resident engineer.

## Variants

While “Stockpile 1” is a specific project entity, the general concept of a **designated clean‑fill stockpile** has several variants observed in similar highway projects:

- **Stockpile 2, 3, 4, 5** – shown in the RDIP figures (Fig3‑2 through Fig3‑5), these may have different dimensions, capacities, or source materials.  
- **Contaminated soil stockpile** – separated from clean fill, requiring additional containment (e.g., liner, cover, leachate collection) and regulatory oversight.  
- **Topsoil stockpile** – preserves organic‑rich surface soil for later re‑vegetation; often shaped differently to avoid compaction.  
- **Long‑term stockpile** – material stored for months or years; requires more stringent erosion and invasive species control.

## Trade-offs

| Consideration | Advantage | Limitation |
|---------------|-----------|------------|
| **Space use** | Reduces off‑site hauling, saving costs and emissions. | Occupies land that could otherwise be used for staging or construction. |
| **Schedule buffer** | Allows separation of excavation and backfill operations, improving project flexibility. | May require extended stockpile maintenance (dust, weeds, erosion repairs). |
| **Environmental risk** | Clean fill has low risk if properly managed. | Failure of controls can lead to sediment discharge or dust complaints. |
| **Regulatory burden** | Following an approved Tech Memo streamlines inspections. | Variance conditions (e.g., periodic soil retesting, additional BMPs) add paperwork. |

Operators must balance the need for temporary storage against the cost of maintaining Stockpile 1 in compliance with all permits. The RDIP Variance allows greater volume and duration but requires more frequent monitoring.
