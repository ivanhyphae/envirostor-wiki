## Definition

A **Final Removal Action Completion Report (FRACR)** is a formal closure document used in remediation and environmental cleanup projects to demonstrate that the approved removal action has been completed, that the work was performed in accordance with the project plan or decision document, and that the site or operable unit is ready for final agency review and possible closeout.

In practice, a FRACR summarizes the removal work completed, documents verification activities, and provides the evidence needed for the responsible agency to determine whether the cleanup objectives have been met. It is closely related to the Removal Action process and often serves as the final technical record for an interim or final removal response.

## How it works

A FRACR is typically prepared by the party responsible for conducting the cleanup after field activities are finished. Its role is to translate completed field work into a formal record that can be reviewed by regulators, project managers, and other stakeholders.

Typical steps include:

1. **Confirm completion of planned removal work**  
   The report documents which actions were performed, such as excavation, disposal, treatment, decontamination, restoration, or institutional controls implementation.

2. **Summarize objectives and scope**  
   It restates the removal action goals, the area addressed, and the cleanup criteria or performance standards that governed the work.

3. **Provide field and verification data**  
   The report includes sampling results, survey data, manifests, photographic evidence, as-built drawings, or other proof that the action was completed as intended.

4. **Describe deviations and resolutions**  
   Any changes from the original plan are identified, along with explanations of why they were necessary and how they affected the final outcome.

5. **Document residual conditions and follow-up needs**  
   If contamination remains, the report explains what remains in place, whether any Institutional Controls are required, and whether additional monitoring or future action is anticipated.

6. **Support agency acceptance**  
   The FRACR is used by the reviewing authority to decide whether the removal action is complete and whether an acceptance letter, closeout determination, or further work is appropriate.

In the source material, the phrase **“Acceptance Letter Draft Interim RACR”** suggests that the FRACR can be directly tied to an agency acceptance process, where a completion report is reviewed and then formally accepted through a letter or memorandum.

## Variants

Common variants and closely related forms include:

- **Interim Removal Action Completion Report**  
  Used when a removal action addresses an immediate or partial cleanup need but is not the final site remedy.

- **Final Removal Completion Report**  
  A shorter or more generic label used in some programs for the same closure function.

- **Remedial Action Completion Report**  
  Similar in purpose, but usually associated with remedial rather than removal activities, and often used in longer-term cleanup programs.

- **Closeout report**  
  A broader term that may include administrative, legal, and technical closure information beyond the removal action itself.

- **Acceptance letter / approval memorandum**  
  Not the report itself, but often the formal agency response that follows review of the completion report.

The exact title and required contents vary by agency, program, and governing framework. Some programs use a standardized template; others rely on project-specific reporting guidance.

## Trade-offs

Key considerations when using a FRACR include:

- **Documentation burden vs. closure certainty**  
  A detailed report creates a stronger record for closure, but it requires more time, data compilation, and review effort.

- **Technical completeness vs. readability**  
  The report must be detailed enough for regulatory review while remaining clear enough for non-technical decision-makers.

- **Finality vs. flexibility**  
  Calling a report “final” can imply the action is fully complete, but in some projects residual contamination or future obligations remain.

- **Verification quality vs. cost**  
  More extensive confirmatory sampling and surveying improve confidence, but increase project cost and schedule.

- **Regulatory alignment vs. project variation**  
  Different agencies may expect different levels of detail, which can make a single universal format impractical.

A FRACR is most effective when it clearly shows that the executed work matches the approved scope and that any remaining issues are explicitly tracked rather than obscured.
