## Petroleum Hydrocarbons

### Definition

Petroleum hydrocarbons are organic compounds derived from crude oil and natural gas, composed primarily of hydrogen and carbon atoms arranged in chains (aliphatic) or rings (aromatic). In environmental testing, the term typically refers to the range of hydrocarbons found in gasoline, diesel, kerosene, lubricating oils, and other petroleum products. They are often measured as total petroleum hydrocarbons (TPH) to quantify the concentration of these compounds in soil, water, or air samples. In regulatory contexts such as clean fill source testing, petroleum hydrocarbons are assessed to determine whether a material meets acceptable thresholds for reuse or disposal.

### How It Works

Petroleum hydrocarbons enter the environment through spills, leaks, or improper disposal, binding to soil particles or dissolving into groundwater. Their behavior depends on chemical structure: light hydrocarbons (e.g., C6–C10, found in gasoline) are volatile and mobile, while heavier fractions (e.g., C10–C40, found in diesel and oils) are less volatile but persist longer.

Testing for petroleum hydrocarbons involves extraction of the sample with a solvent (e.g., hexane or dichloromethane), followed by analysis using gas chromatography with flame ionization detection (GC-FID). The standard method, such as EPA Method 8015, separates hydrocarbons by carbon chain length and compares the total area under the chromatogram to a calibration standard (often a mixture of diesel or motor oil). Results are reported as TPH in milligrams per kilogram (mg/kg) for soil or milligrams per liter (mg/L) for water. For clean fill assessments, specific carbon ranges (e.g., C10–C19 for diesel-range organics, C19–C36 for oil-range organics) are quantified to evaluate risk.

### Variants

Petroleum hydrocarbons are categorized by carbon chain length and product origin. Common variants include:

- **Gasoline-Range Organics (GRO)**: C6–C10, volatile, high mobility; typical in fuel spills.
- **Diesel-Range Organics (DRO)**: C10–C28, less volatile, moderate mobility; from diesel fuel and kerosene.
- **Oil-Range Organics (ORO)**: C28–C40, low volatility, high persistence; from lubricating oils and greases.
- **Total Petroleum Hydrocarbons (TPH)**: Sum of all extractable hydrocarbons, often used as a screening metric.
- **Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbons (PAHs)**: A subset of petroleum hydrocarbons with fused aromatic rings, of particular regulatory concern due to toxicity (e.g., naphthalene, benzo[a]pyrene).

In clean fill source testing, variants may be reported as either TPH-D (diesel) or TPH-G (gasoline) depending on the calibration standard used.

### Trade-offs

- **Detection vs. Specificity**: TPH measurement provides a bulk concentration but does not identify individual compounds, which may have vastly different toxicities. This can lead to over- or under-estimation of risk.
- **Method Variability**: Different extraction and analytical methods yield different TPH results; comparison across projects must account for method (e.g., standard vs. modified EPA 8015).
- **Regulatory Thresholds**: Acceptable levels vary by jurisdiction and land use (e.g., residential vs. commercial). Clean fill requires very low TPH concentrations, often below 10–50 mg/kg, which can be challenging for material containing heterogeneous contamination.
- **Aging and Weathering**: Over time, petroleum hydrocarbons degrade via volatilization, biodegradation, and photolysis. Fresh spills have different composition and mobility than aged ones, complicating both testing and risk assessment.
- **Interference**: Natural organic matter (e.g., humic acids) in soil can produce chromatographic peaks that resemble petroleum hydrocarbons, leading to false positives. Clean fill testing must employ cleanup steps (e.g., silica gel) to remove non‑petroleum interferences.
