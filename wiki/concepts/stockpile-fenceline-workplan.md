## Definition

A **Stockpile Fenceline Workplan** is a site-specific plan that defines how a soil stockpile area will be enclosed, monitored, and managed to reduce dust, prevent material migration, and limit off-site impacts. In practice, it describes the arrangement and maintenance of fencing or other perimeter controls around stockpiles, along with the operational measures used to keep stockpiled material contained and the site compliant with applicable environmental and nuisance-control requirements.

In the context of a soil stockpile site, the fenceline workplan serves as a coordinated implementation document for perimeter protection, dust control, housekeeping, inspection, and corrective action.

## How it works

A stockpile fenceline workplan typically works by combining physical containment with routine operational controls:

1. **Perimeter definition**
   - The stockpile area is bounded by a fence or similar perimeter barrier.
   - The fenceline establishes the managed boundary of the stockpile operation and helps prevent unauthorized access, uncontrolled dumping, or spread of materials.

2. **Containment and dust reduction**
   - The fence itself may not fully contain soil, but it supports a larger control strategy that can include:
     - stabilized entrances/exits
     - wind fencing or screening
     - stockpile shaping to minimize erosion
     - dust suppression measures such as watering or chemical suppressants
   - These measures reduce the likelihood of windblown dust leaving the site.

3. **Operational management**
   - The workplan typically assigns responsibilities for:
     - regular inspections of the fenceline and stockpile conditions
     - repair of damaged fencing or perimeter controls
     - cleanup of spilled material
     - documentation of site conditions and corrective actions
   - Inspections are usually focused on identifying breaches, sediment migration, erosion, and dust-generating conditions.

4. **Environmental compliance**
   - The workplan is used to demonstrate that the stockpile area is being actively managed to reduce impacts on surrounding properties, roadways, and air quality.
   - It can support permit conditions, corrective action requirements, or other regulatory commitments.

5. **Response actions**
   - If a deficiency is found, the workplan should specify what happens next:
     - restrict activity
     - re-grade or re-cover stockpiles
     - replace or reinforce fencing
     - apply additional dust control
     - document completion of repairs

## Variants

Common variants or related implementations include:

- **Temporary fenceline workplan**  
  Used for short-term stockpile areas with portable fencing or temporary boundary controls.

- **Permanent perimeter control plan**  
  Used where the stockpile area is long-lived and has fixed fencing, gates, and access controls.

- **Dust-control-focused fenceline plan**  
  Emphasizes windblown dust mitigation, often combining fencing with watering, cover, or vegetation.

- **Stormwater and erosion control variant**  
  Focuses on preventing runoff, sediment transport, and washout around stockpiles.

- **Compliance-oriented implementation plan**  
  Structured around permit conditions, inspection logs, and corrective-action reporting.

Alternatives to fencing-based management can include berms, silt barriers, tarps/covers, enclosed storage, or relocation of stockpiles to indoor or fully contained facilities.

## Trade-offs

Key trade-offs and limitations include:

- **Fencing does not fully contain fine material**
  - A fence alone usually cannot stop dust or very fine particles; it must be paired with other controls.

- **Inspection and maintenance burden**
  - The effectiveness of the workplan depends on regular monitoring and timely repairs.

- **Cost versus protection**
  - More robust perimeter systems improve control but increase installation and maintenance costs.

- **Site flexibility**
  - Temporary fencing and movable controls are flexible, but they may be less durable or reliable than permanent systems.

- **Weather sensitivity**
  - High winds, rain, and dry conditions can overwhelm weak controls, requiring adaptive management.

- **Operational constraints**
  - Access points, loading activities, and vehicle traffic can create weak spots in the perimeter system.
