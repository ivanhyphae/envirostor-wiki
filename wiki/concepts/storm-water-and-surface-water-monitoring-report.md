## Definition

A **Storm Water and Surface Water Monitoring Report** is a regulated environmental document that records the collection, testing, and interpretation of water samples taken from stormwater runoff and nearby surface waters. Its purpose is to determine whether facility operations are affecting water quality and whether discharges comply with applicable permit conditions, cleanup requirements, and environmental standards.

These reports are commonly prepared for industrial sites, remediation projects, landfills, construction sites, or other facilities where rainfall runoff, drainage channels, ponds, creeks, or other receiving waters may be impacted by site activities. They typically summarize sampling locations, analytical results, comparison to regulatory benchmarks, observed field conditions, trends over time, and any corrective actions or follow-up monitoring needs.

## How it works

Stormwater and surface water monitoring is usually organized around a sampling plan tied to site-specific permits or regulatory obligations.

1. **Identify monitoring locations**
   - Stormwater outfalls, discharge points, detention basins, canals, streams, wetlands, or other surface water stations are selected.
   - Locations are chosen to represent potential influent, site runoff, and downstream or off-site receiving conditions.

2. **Collect samples**
   - Samples are taken during storm events, after qualifying rainfall, or on a routine schedule for surface water.
   - Field personnel document weather, rainfall amount, antecedent dry period, flow conditions, and any unusual observations such as sheen, odor, turbidity, staining, or debris.

3. **Analyze water quality**
   - Laboratories test for parameters relevant to the site and permit, such as:
     - metals
     - petroleum hydrocarbons
     - pH
     - turbidity
     - total suspended solids
     - volatile or semivolatile organic compounds
     - nutrients or other site-specific constituents
   - Results are compared against permit action levels, background values, water quality objectives, or screening thresholds.

4. **Evaluate compliance and trends**
   - The report interprets whether results indicate a release, runoff impact, or exceedance.
   - It may compare current results with historical data to identify patterns such as recurring contamination, seasonal effects, or improving conditions after controls are installed.

5. **Document corrective actions**
   - If issues are found, the report may describe follow-up measures such as:
     - source investigation
     - drainage repair
     - erosion control
     - housekeeping improvements
     - containment or treatment upgrades
     - additional sampling

6. **Submit to regulators or retain for records**
   - Depending on the program, the report is submitted to a state agency, regional water board, or kept as part of the site’s compliance record.

In practice, the report functions as both a compliance record and an operational diagnostic tool. It helps show whether stormwater controls are working and whether the site may be affecting surface water quality.

## Variants

Common variants include:

- **Stormwater Monitoring Report**  
  Focuses primarily on runoff captured at site outfalls or discharge points.

- **Surface Water Monitoring Report**  
  Focuses on ambient waters such as creeks, ponds, wetlands, or drainage channels near a site.

- **Combined Storm Water and Surface Water Monitoring Report**  
  Integrates both runoff data and receiving-water data in one document.

- **Construction Stormwater Monitoring Report**  
  Used for active construction sites, often emphasizing turbidity, sediment control, and erosion prevention.

- **Industrial General Permit Monitoring Report**  
  Used by industrial facilities under stormwater permit programs, often with routine sampling, visual observations, and benchmark comparisons.

- **Remedial Site Monitoring Report**  
  Used at cleanup sites where monitoring is tied to contamination investigation or remedy performance.

- **Event-based vs. routine monitoring**
  - *Event-based* monitoring samples specific storm events.
  - *Routine* monitoring uses fixed schedules or seasonal intervals.

Alternatives are not really substitutes, but related documents may include Site Inspection Report, Environmental Monitoring Report, NPDES Permit, and Corrective Action Plan.

## Trade-offs

Key considerations and limitations include:

- **Weather dependence**
  - Stormwater sampling is often difficult to schedule because it requires qualifying rainfall events.
  - Sparse storms can reduce the number of usable samples.

- **Representativeness**
  - A limited number of samples may not capture all site conditions or pollutant sources.
  - First-flush effects can overrepresent initial runoff concentrations.

- **Analytical cost and logistics**
  - Laboratory testing, chain-of-custody procedures, and field mobilization can be expensive.
  - Some parameters require rapid preservation or short holding times.

- **Regulatory complexity**
  - Requirements may differ by permit, region, site type, and receiving water classification.
  - Reports often must reconcile multiple standards, such as permit benchmarks and water quality criteria.

- **Interpretation challenges**
  - Elevated concentrations do not always prove a site discharge caused a downstream impact.
  - Natural background conditions, sediment resuspension, seasonal variation, and upstream inputs can complicate conclusions.

- **Data comparability**
  - Changes in methods, detection limits, sampling timing, or hydrologic conditions can make year-to-year comparisons less reliable.

- **Actionability**
  - Reports are only useful if findings lead to timely controls, maintenance, or investigation.
  - Without follow-up, repeated monitoring can document problems without resolving them.
