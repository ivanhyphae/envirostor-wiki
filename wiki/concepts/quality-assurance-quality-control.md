## Definition

Quality Assurance / Quality Control (QA/QC) is the combined system used to ensure that data, products, or processes meet defined standards of quality. In environmental and engineering monitoring, QA refers to the planned, systematic actions designed to prevent errors, while QC refers to the operational checks used to detect and correct errors after work is performed.

In practice, QA/QC is a framework for making results defensible, repeatable, and fit for their intended use. The distinction is often summarized as:

- Quality Assurance: “Are we following the right process?”
- Quality Control: “Did the output meet the required standard?”

## How it works

QA/QC is implemented as a lifecycle of planning, execution, review, and corrective action.

### 1. Quality assurance: design the process correctly
QA begins before fieldwork or analysis starts. It typically includes:

- defining project objectives and data-quality requirements
- establishing standard operating procedures
- selecting approved methods, instruments, and laboratories
- training personnel
- specifying calibration, preservation, chain-of-custody, and documentation requirements
- setting acceptance criteria for data usability

The goal is to reduce the chance that invalid data are produced in the first place.

### 2. Quality control: verify results during and after work
QC consists of checks embedded into sampling, measurement, laboratory analysis, and data review. Common QC elements include:

- field duplicates and replicate samples
- equipment blanks and trip blanks
- calibration checks and instrument maintenance
- method blanks, matrix spikes, and lab control samples
- sample holding-time verification
- completeness checks on records and reporting
- comparison to historical trends or background conditions
- independent review of calculations and transcription

QC identifies whether a specific sample, dataset, or measurement batch is acceptable, suspicious, or rejected.

### 3. Data validation and review
After QC checks, results are reviewed against project-specific criteria. This may include:

- checking whether analytical detections are consistent with blanks or duplicates
- flagging results affected by contamination, low recovery, or poor precision
- determining whether missing data compromise interpretation
- documenting data qualifiers and usability decisions

This step is especially important in groundwater monitoring, where time-series trends and low-level detections can be sensitive to small analytical or sampling errors.

### 4. Corrective action and continuous improvement
When QC results fail acceptance criteria, the response may include:

- resampling
- reanalysis
- recalibration or equipment replacement
- procedure revision
- retraining
- reclassification of data quality or usability

QA/QC is iterative: findings from QC are used to improve QA procedures for future work.

## Variants

QA/QC is used across many domains, with different implementations depending on the context.

### Environmental monitoring QA/QC
Used in groundwater, soil, surface water, and air monitoring. Typical features include:

- field blanks, duplicates, and equipment rinsates
- laboratory method blanks and surrogate recoveries
- chain-of-custody records
- project-specific data quality objectives
- validation qualifiers for concentrations and detections

### Construction and materials QA/QC
Used in civil engineering and construction to confirm that materials and workmanship meet specifications. Examples include:

- density and compaction testing
- concrete slump and strength tests
- asphalt gradation checks
- weld inspection
- inspection hold points and sign-offs

### Laboratory QA/QC
Used to ensure analytical accuracy and precision. Common controls include:

- calibration curves
- certified reference materials
- blanks and spikes
- duplicate analyses
- control charts and acceptance limits

### Manufacturing quality systems
Often formalized through quality management systems such as ISO 9001, statistical process control, inspection plans, and corrective/preventive action systems.

### Alternatives and related frameworks
QA/QC overlaps with but is not identical to:

- Quality Management System
- Data Validation
- Statistical Process Control
- Inspection and Testing
- Corrective Action

## Trade-offs

QA/QC improves reliability, but it has costs and practical limits.

### Benefits
- increases confidence in results
- reduces the chance of false positives, false negatives, and systematic bias
- supports defensible regulatory and legal reporting
- improves comparability across time, sites, and contractors
- helps identify trends versus noise

### Costs and limitations
- adds time, staffing, and analytical cost
- can delay reporting if repeated sampling or reanalysis is required
- may not detect all errors, especially those that are systematic but consistent
- too many controls can become burdensome without improving decision quality
- strict rejection criteria can reduce usable data volume
- acceptance limits are sometimes project-specific, so results may not be directly comparable between programs

### Key consideration
QA/QC is only as good as the underlying standards and documentation. If criteria are vague, inconsistent, or not aligned with the intended use of the data, the program may produce a large amount of “checked” data that is still not decision-grade.
