## Definition

A **variance** is a formal authorization from a regulatory agency that permits a deviation from standard environmental or procedural requirements under specific, justified circumstances. In the context of environmental remediation, a variance allows a project to proceed using alternative methods or timelines when strict compliance with regulations is impractical, unnecessary, or would hinder cleanup objectives. Variances are distinct from exemptions, which permanently waive requirements, as they are typically time-limited and condition-specific.

## How it works

A variance is obtained through a formal application process, wherein the requesting party submits a **Variance Request** to the relevant regulatory body (e.g., a state environmental protection agency). The request must demonstrate that:

1. **Justification**: The standard requirement poses undue hardship or is technically infeasible without compromising environmental protection.
2. **Alternatives**: Equivalent levels of protection can be achieved through alternative means.
3. **Duration**: The variance is for a limited period, often tied to a specific project phase or milestone.

Once approved, the variance is documented in a **Variance Letter** or **Variance Agreement**, which details:
- The specific standard(s) being modified.
- The alternative procedures or timelines to be followed.
- Conditions for compliance, such as monitoring, reporting, or performance benchmarks.
- Expiration date or triggering event for the variance.

For example, in environmental cleanup projects, a **Removal Action Completion Report (RACR)** may reference a variance when soil stockpiles are managed under different containment or testing protocols than required by the original permit. The variance ensures that remediation can proceed without delays while maintaining oversight.

## Variants

Variances exist across multiple regulatory domains, but common variants include:

- **Procedural Variance**: Adjusts how a standard is implemented (e.g., modified sampling frequency or waste handling methods).
- **Timeline Variance**: Extends a deadline for meeting a requirement, such as delaying the removal of stockpiles due to seasonal constraints.
- **Technical Variance**: Permits use of alternative technologies or materials not explicitly approved under existing rules.
- **Administrative Variance**: Addresses non-substantive deviations, like changes in reporting formats or minor logistical shifts.
- **Emergency Variance**: Granted temporarily during unforeseen events (e.g., natural disasters) to expedite response actions.

## Trade-offs

Variances offer significant flexibility but involve several trade-offs:

- **Benefits**:
  - Enables cost-effective and timely remediation by avoiding rigid, impractical rules.
  - Allows site-specific adaptation where standard requirements may not be suitable.
  - Maintains regulatory oversight while fostering innovation.

- **Limitations**:
  - **Increased administrative burden**: Preparing a variance request can require extensive documentation and negotiation.
  - **Conditional approval**: Variances often include strict monitoring or reporting conditions, adding overhead.
  - **Public perception**: May be viewed as "cutting corners," requiring transparent justification.
  - **Expiration risk**: If conditions change, the variance may be revoked, forcing reversion to original standards.
  - **Legal vulnerability**: Opponents may challenge variances through litigation or public comment.
