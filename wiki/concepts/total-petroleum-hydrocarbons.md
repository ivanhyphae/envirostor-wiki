## Definition

**Total Petroleum Hydrocarbons (TPH)** is the aggregate measure of the concentration of a wide range of petroleum-derived hydrocarbon compounds present in an environmental sample (e.g., soil, water, air). It is a sum parameter, meaning it quantifies the total mass of hydrocarbons rather than individual chemical species. TPH is used as a screening indicator for petroleum contamination and to assess the extent of fuel or oil spills. The term is defined by analytical methods that extract and quantify hydrocarbons within a specific boiling range (typically C<sub>6</sub> to C<sub>40</sub>), though exact ranges vary by jurisdiction.

## How it works

TPH analysis involves extracting hydrocarbons from the sample matrix using a solvent (e.g., hexane, dichloromethane), then measuring the total extractable organic matter. Common laboratory methods include:

- **Gravimetric analysis** – Evaporate solvent and weigh the residue; suitable for high concentrations but lacks specificity.
- **Infrared spectroscopy (IR)** – Measures absorption at characteristic C–H bond frequencies (e.g., 2950 cm⁻¹); standardized as EPA Method 418.1.
- **Gas chromatography with flame ionization detection (GC-FID)** – The most common modern technique; hydrocarbons are separated by boiling point and detected, with peaks integrated over a defined carbon range (e.g., C<sub>10</sub>–C<sub>40</sub> for diesel-range organics).
- **Gas chromatography with mass spectrometry (GC-MS)** – Provides structural information and can separate aliphatic from aromatic fractions.

The result is reported as milligrams per kilogram (mg/kg) for soil or milligrams per liter (mg/L) for water, often with fraction-specific reporting (e.g., TPH-diesel range organics, TPH-gasoline range organics). The method detection limit varies but is typically in the range of 0.1–10 mg/kg.

## Variants

- **Aliphatic vs. Aromatic fractions** – Many regulatory frameworks split TPH into separate aliphatic and aromatic carbon ranges (e.g., Massachusetts VPH/EPH, Texas TPH CWG) because these fractions differ in toxicity and mobility.
- **Cumulative carbon range groups** – Common groupings: GRO (Gasoline Range Organics, ~C6–C10), DRO (Diesel Range Organics, ~C10–C28), ORO (Oil Range Organics, ~C28–C40). These help correlate with specific fuel types.
- **TPH-d (diesel)** and **TPH-g (gasoline)** – Operational definitions used in cleanup standards.
- **Ecotoxicological TPH** – Some methods use a surrogate approach, measuring a subset of hydrocarbons and applying conversion factors to estimate total petroleum hydrocarbon equivalence.
- **Fingerprinting** – More detailed than TPH; uses diagnostic ratios (e.g., pristane/phytane) to identify source and weathering state.

## Trade-offs

- **Lack of specificity** – TPH does not distinguish between toxic (e.g., benzene, PAHs) and less harmful hydrocarbons. This can overestimate risk if the mix is dominated by benign saturates, or underestimate risk if toxic aromatics are present at low TPH levels.
- **Matrix interference** – Natural organic matter (e.g., humic acids) can be co-extracted and falsely elevate TPH values.
- **Weathering effects** – Evaporation of lighter fractions and biodegradation of certain compounds change the TPH profile, making comparisons over time difficult.
- **Regulatory fragmentation** – Different jurisdictions use different analytical methods, carbon ranges, and clean‑up levels, complicating cross‑site comparisons.
- **Cost vs. detail** – TPH is quicker and cheaper than speciated hydrocarbon analysis, but may necessitate confirmatory testing for risk assessment.
