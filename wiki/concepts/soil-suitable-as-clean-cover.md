## Definition

**Soil Suitable As Clean Cover** refers to excavated earth material that meets specific chemical and physical criteria to be used as a final or intermediate covering layer over waste, contaminated soil, or landfill cells without posing a risk to human health or the environment. This material is distinct from "dirty" fill or waste-tainted soil and is typically sourced from borrow areas or construction sites. The designation "clean" is defined by regulatory thresholds—such as those established by the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) or state environmental agencies—for concentrations of heavy metals, organic contaminants, volatile organic compounds (VOCs), and other hazardous substances. The material must also exhibit suitable engineering properties, including compaction characteristics, permeability, and particle size distribution, to function effectively as a barrier against moisture infiltration and gas migration.

## How it works

The suitability of soil as clean cover is determined through a systematic process involving field sampling, laboratory analysis, and regulatory comparisons:

1. **Sampling Protocol**: Representative soil samples are collected from borrow areas at specified depths and locations, often following a grid pattern or composite sampling approach to capture spatial variability. Samples are typically taken from the upper 0–2 feet of the soil profile, but deeper horizons may be required if the cover layer will be thicker.

2. **Chemical Analysis**: Samples undergo testing for a standard suite of contaminants, including:
   - Heavy metals (arsenic, cadmium, chromium, lead, mercury)
   - Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs)
   - Total petroleum hydrocarbons (TPH)
   - Volatile organic compounds (VOCs)
   - Polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs)
   - Pesticides and herbicides
   - pH, electrical conductivity, and other general parameters

3. **Regulatory Comparison**: Measured concentrations are compared against action levels from applicable regulations (e.g., EPA's Resource Conservation and Recovery Act (RCRA) limits or state-specific clean fill standards). Soil is classified as "clean" if all contaminants fall below these thresholds. Some jurisdictions also require a risk-based assessment for non-carcinogenic endpoints.

4. **Engineering Testing**: Physical properties are evaluated to ensure the material can be compacted to achieve required density (typically 90–95% Standard Proctor), has low hydraulic conductivity (≤1×10⁻⁷ cm/s) for barrier layers, and resists erosion and settlement. Tests include grain size analysis (sieve), Atterberg limits (plasticity index), compaction curves, and permeability tests (falling head or constant head).

5. **Verification and Approval**: A technical memorandum or report summarizes results, compares them to clean criteria, and recommends whether the source material is suitable. If approved, it may be used as daily or final cover at landfills, or as a clean closure layer for contaminated sites.

## Variants

- **Landfill Daily Cover**: Clean soil spread and compacted over waste at the end of each operating day to control odor, litter, and vectors. Typically requires lower compaction and less stringent chemical criteria than final cover.

- **Final Cover (Closure Cap)**: Multi-layer system where clean soil serves as the vegetative support layer (topsoil) or protective barrier layer over a geomembrane or low-permeability clay. Strict chemical and hydraulic criteria apply.

- **Interim Cover**: Temporary layer placed over waste that may not meet full final cover specifications but provides short-term protection during active site operation.

- **Green Remediation Cover**: Clean soil used in phytoremediation or ecological restoration projects where planting vegetation is prioritized. May have relaxed chemical thresholds if contaminant uptake by plants is considered.

- **Fill Material for Construction**: Clean soil repurposed as structural fill, backfill, or base material for roads, berms, or foundations. Engineering properties (compaction and load-bearing) become the primary criteria.

- **Off-Specification Clean Cover**: Material that meets chemical but not full engineering standards (e.g., too high plasticity or permeability) and is used in less critical applications such as final grading before landscaping.

## Trade-offs

**Advantages:**
- Provides cost-effective waste management by diverting excavated soil from disposal.
- Reduces demand for virgin aggregate or imported clean fill.
- Supports sustainable practices and reduces carbon footprint of landfill operations.
- Regulatory clarity exists for many common contaminants, simplifying approval.

**Limitations:**
- Stringent chemical thresholds may reject soil that is environmentally harmless but has trace elevated baseline metals (e.g., arsenic in certain geological formations).
- Engineering testing adds time and expense; small borrow areas may be uneconomical to characterize.
- Soil quality varies spatially—representative sampling is challenging in heterogeneous deposits.
- Risk of "false negatives"—contamination hotspots may be missed in discrete sampling.
- Long-term performance of clean cover layers depends on proper compaction and maintenance; settling or cracking can compromise barrier function.
- Permitting bottlenecks: approval workflows for borrow area characterization can delay project schedules.
- Potential for "regulatory creep" if thresholds are set lower than necessary, requiring excessive treatment of natural soil.
