## Definition

The **Modesto Soil Stockpiles** are three engineered soil repositories located along State Route 99 and State Route 132 in Modesto, California, containing historic petroleum‑hydrocarbon‑impacted soil generated during Caltrans highway maintenance and construction. The stockpiles (designated Stockpile 1, Stockpile 2, and Stockpile 3) are subject to a permanent **containment remedy** overseen by the California Department of Toxic Substances Control (DTSC) and the Central Valley Regional Water Quality Control Board (RWQCB), with long‑term monitoring and operational controls. Contamination primarily consists of total petroleum hydrocarbons (TPH), polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs), and metals (barium, lead, arsenic) at concentrations above residential screening levels but below industrial/commercial thresholds.

## How it works

The selected remedy (Alternative 4, approved March 2018) relies on **engineered containment** rather than excavation or treatment. Implementation proceeds in two phases:

- **Phase 1 (2019–2020):** Mechanically stabilized earth (MSE) retaining walls were constructed around Stockpile 1 to support future bridge abutments and roadway. Unpaved portions received clean soil covers (minimum 2 feet). A low‑permeability cap (asphalt or geomembrane) minimizes surface‑water infiltration.

- **Phase 2 (2028–2029):** Similar containment will be applied to Stockpiles 2 and 3, integrating them into permanent highway infrastructure.

Post‑construction controls include:

- **Annual pavement inspections** to verify cap integrity.
- **Quarterly or semi‑annual groundwater monitoring** from a network of 10 wells (MW‑1 through MW‑10) for dissolved metals, nitrate, TDS, and field parameters.
- **Stormwater sampling** (grab samples during rain events) at the stockpile perimeter to assess runoff quality.
- **Five‑year remedy evaluations** (per CEQA/NEPA) to confirm containment effectiveness.
- A **land use covenant** restricts future excavation or residential use.

Key analytical methods include EPA Method 6020 (ICP‑MS) for metals, EPA Method 7470 (cold‑vapor AA) for mercury, EPA Method 300.0 (IC) for anions, and SM 2540C for TDS. Exceedances of California MCLs have been noted for nitrate (up to 24 mg/L at MW‑5) and manganese (MW‑8), but these are attributed to background conditions, not the stockpiles.

## Variants

While the three stockpiles are managed under a single remedial action plan, they differ in size, contaminant profile, and structural integration:

- **Stockpile 1:** Largest volume; fully enclosed by MSE walls and a roadway bridge; highest PAH concentrations.
- **Stockpile 2:** Adjacent to SR 132; includes a stormwater retention basin; some metals exceedances (barium, lead).
- **Stockpile 3:** Smallest; located near a wetland‑drainage area; groundwater monitoring shows elevated nitrate and TDS.

A **variance** was granted for Stockpile 1 to delay installation of the final cap until Phase 2 infrastructure was completed, relying on interim cover and enhanced stormwater controls.

## Trade-offs

| Advantage | Limitation |
|-----------|------------|
| Eliminates direct exposure pathway for human receptors | Contamination remains in place indefinitely; no mass removal |
| No off‑site disposal costs or transportation risks | Long‑term liability; O&M required for decades |
| Minimal disruption during construction | Phased implementation leaves some stockpiles uncovered until 2028–2029 |
| Compatible with highway expansion plans | Land use covenant restricts future development |

**Key limitations:**
- **Matrix interference** in groundwater samples (e.g., dilution flags D6) occasionally reduces data usability.
- **Annual pavement inspection** may miss early‑stage defects; five‑year evaluations rely on qualitative visual criteria.
- **Stormwater sampling** captures only discrete events; continuous flow‑monitoring is not performed.
- Regulatory oversight involves both DTSC and RWQCB, creating potential coordination delays.
