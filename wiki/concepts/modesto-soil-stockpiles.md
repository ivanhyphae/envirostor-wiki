## Modesto Soil Stockpiles

### Definition

**Modesto Soil Stockpiles** refers to a series of contaminated soil storage sites associated with California Department of Transportation (Caltrans) highway projects in and around Modesto, California. These stockpiles consist of excavated material—predominantly soil and sediment—removed during roadway construction and maintenance activities, which was later determined to contain elevated concentrations of contaminants requiring long-term environmental monitoring and remediation. The designation typically encompasses at least two primary stockpile locations (referred to as "Stockpile 1" and "Stockpile 2"), which have been subject to ongoing groundwater and stormwater sampling, risk assessment, and regulatory oversight from approximately 2012 onward.

### How it works

The lifecycle of the Modesto Soil Stockpiles involves several interconnected phases:

**Generation and Storage.** Soil excavated during Caltrans highway projects—including construction of Mechanically Stabilized Earth (MSE) walls—was placed in designated stockpile areas. These stockpiles accumulated material over time and were not initially capped or fully isolated, leaving them exposed to precipitation and surface runoff.

**Groundwater Monitoring Network.** A network of dedicated monitoring wells was installed around the stockpile perimeters starting in 2012. Wells were constructed, sampled quarterly or semi-annually, and eventually destroyed or decommissioned when no longer needed. Groundwater samples were analyzed for a suite of constituents including metals, volatile organic compounds (VOCs), semi-volatile organic compounds (SVOCs), and other site-specific parameters.

**Stormwater Sampling.** Stormwater runoff from the stockpile surfaces was collected and analyzed under site-specific Stormwater Sampling and Analysis Plans (SAPs). Sampling events occurred in both wet and dry seasons to characterize pollutant loading, with reports documenting results for each event (e.g., January 2018, March 2018, December 2015, February 2025).

**Well Installation and Destruction.** Monitoring wells were installed using standard drilling methods, developed, and surveyed. Upon completion of monitoring objectives—or when it was determined that no further groundwater monitoring was necessary—wells were properly destroyed (decommissioned) in accordance with state and local well abandonment standards.

**Risk Assessment and Remedial Action.** The data from groundwater and stormwater monitoring fed into a Screening-Level Site Investigation (SSI) and Human Health Risk Assessment (HHRA), finalized in April 2013. These assessments informed a Remedial Action Plan (RAP) for State Route 132, which was approved in March 2018. The RAP outlined specific remedies—most likely capping, removal, or treatment—to address residual contamination.

**Regulatory Reporting.** Results were compiled into periodic monitoring reports submitted to the Central Valley Regional Water Quality Control Board and other regulatory agencies. Reports followed established formats and included tabulated analytical data, comparison to water quality objectives, and discussion of trends.

### Variants

**Active vs. Capped Stockpiles.** Some portions of the stockpiles may have been capped with clean fill or geomembrane liners to reduce infiltration and runoff, while other areas remained exposed during the monitoring period.

**Groundwater-Only vs. Stormwater-Only Monitoring.** In some years, groundwater monitoring alone was conducted (e.g., September 2012, June 2013, December 2013), suggesting that stormwater monitoring occurred on a less frequent schedule or was triggered by specific rain events.

**MSE Wall-Specific Sampling.** A dedicated "MSE Wall Sampling Plan" indicates that material associated with mechanically stabilized earth wall construction was managed separately from other stockpiled soils, potentially due to different contaminant profiles or structural considerations.

**Well Decommissioning Programs.** The Well Destruction Reports (e.g., S2350-01-02, S1200-01-01) document the permanent removal of monitoring wells once monitoring objectives were satisfied, representing a terminal variant in the monitoring lifecycle.

### Trade-offs

- **Monitoring Duration vs. Closure Certainty.** Long-term groundwater monitoring (spanning over a decade in some well clusters) provides robust trend data but incurs significant costs and delays final site closure. The decision to cease monitoring and destroy wells represents a trade-off between continued data collection and resource allocation.

- **Stormwater Sampling Representativeness.** Stormwater grab samples may not capture peak contaminant loads during intense rainfall events, yet more frequent automated sampling requires additional equipment, maintenance, and data management.

- **Analytical Scope vs. Cost.** Comprehensive analytical suites (metals, VOCs, SVOCs, pesticides, PCBs) provide thorough characterization but increase per-sample laboratory costs. The choice of analytes determines which contaminants are tracked over time.

- **Capping vs. Removal.** Capping the stockpiles in place is less disruptive and less expensive than excavation and off-site disposal, but it leaves contamination in place and requires perpetual monitoring and institutional controls. Removal eliminates the source but entails higher short-term costs and transportation risks.

- **Regulatory Timelines.** The gap between initial groundwater monitoring (2012), the HHRA (2013), and the RAP approval (2018) reflects the inherent delays in regulatory review and decision-making, which can extend the overall management program.
