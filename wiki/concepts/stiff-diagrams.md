## Definition

A **stiff diagram** is a graphical plotting method used in hydrogeology and water chemistry to compare the major-ion composition of water samples. It represents concentrations of cations on one side and anions on the other, with the lengths of horizontal bars proportional to ionic concentration, typically in milliequivalents per liter (meq/L). The resulting shape provides a quick visual fingerprint of a sample’s geochemical character.

In environmental monitoring reports, stiff diagrams are often used to compare groundwater chemistry across wells, sampling events, or locations, such as in the groundwater monitoring reports for the Modesto Stockpiles site.

## How it works

A stiff diagram is built from a set of major dissolved ions, usually:

- **Cations:** calcium (Ca²⁺), magnesium (Mg²⁺), sodium plus potassium (Na⁺ + K⁺)
- **Anions:** bicarbonate plus carbonate (HCO₃⁻ + CO₃²⁻), sulfate (SO₄²⁻), chloride (Cl⁻)

### Construction
1. **Analyze water chemistry** for the major ions.
2. **Convert concentrations** from mg/L to meq/L, since charge balance matters more than mass for diagram geometry.
3. **Plot ions horizontally** on a common vertical axis:
   - cations to the left
   - anions to the right
4. **Connect the endpoints** of the ion bars to form an irregular polygon.

### Interpretation
The shape reflects the water type and relative dominance of ions:
- A narrow profile may indicate low overall mineralization.
- Broad bars for sodium/chloride may suggest salinity influence or evaporation.
- Strong calcium/bicarbonate dominance commonly indicates recharge water or interaction with carbonate minerals.
- Changes in shape between samples can show:
  - mixing of waters
  - geochemical evolution along flow paths
  - seasonal variation
  - contaminant influence

Because the diagram is based on relative ion proportions, it is especially useful for visual comparison rather than precise quantitative analysis.

## Variants

### Standard stiff diagram
The classic form uses the major ions listed above and is drawn as a left-right polygon.

### Site-comparison sets
Multiple stiff diagrams are arranged side by side to compare wells, depths, or dates. This is a common use in groundwater monitoring reports.

### Time-series comparison
The same well is plotted at different sampling times to visualize temporal change in chemistry.

### Alternatives
Other common hydrochemical visualization tools include:
- Piper diagram
- Schoeller diagram
- Durov diagram

These methods often provide more formal classification or multi-sample comparison, but stiff diagrams are simpler and faster to read.

## Trade-offs

### Advantages
- Fast visual summary of water chemistry
- Easy to compare samples at a glance
- Useful for identifying changes in dominant ions
- Compact and intuitive for reports

### Limitations
- Not a substitute for full analytical tables
- Only shows selected major ions
- Less standardized for classification than some alternatives
- Can be misleading if ion conversion or plotting conventions are inconsistent
- Does not directly show trace constituents, redox conditions, or contaminant concentrations

### Practical considerations
- Must use consistent units, usually meq/L
- Ion list should be the same across all samples to allow comparison
- Interpretation should consider site hydrogeology, source water, and geochemical reactions
