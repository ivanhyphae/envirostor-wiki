## Definition

A **Stockpile Containment System** is the engineered set of physical controls used to prevent runoff, erosion, sediment release, and pollutant migration from outdoor material stockpiles. In practice, it surrounds or isolates a stored pile of soil, aggregate, debris, or similar material so that stormwater does not carry fines, contaminants, or leachate offsite.

In the source materials, the concept is closely tied to stormwater management, best management practices, and site-specific controls for stockpiled materials. A containment system may include perimeter berms, silt fencing, wattles, covers, lined pads, drainage diversion, and collection of runoff to keep stockpile-related impacts out of surrounding stormwater and groundwater systems.

## How it works

A stockpile containment system works by combining **source control** and **runoff control**:

1. **Isolate the stockpile**
   - The stockpile is placed in a designated area, often on an impervious or stabilized surface.
   - The area is graded to limit uncontrolled runoff and to keep water from flowing through the pile.

2. **Keep clean water away**
   - Diversion features such as berms, swales, or drainage controls redirect upslope runoff around the stockpile area.
   - This reduces the amount of water that can become contaminated by contact with the stockpile.

3. **Contain runoff from the stockpile area**
   - Perimeter containment measures capture sediment-laden stormwater before it leaves the area.
   - Common elements include silt fence, straw wattles, earthen berms, curbing, and sediment traps.

4. **Prevent direct exposure**
   - Tarps, plastic sheeting, or enclosed storage reduce rain contact with the pile.
   - This is especially important for erodible, dusty, or potentially contaminated materials.

5. **Manage collected runoff**
   - Captured water may be directed to a treatment or sedimentation area, infiltrated only if appropriate, or removed for disposal depending on site rules and contamination risk.
   - The system is intended to avoid discharge of sediment, turbidity, or dissolved pollutants to stormwater systems.

6. **Support inspection and maintenance**
   - The controls need routine inspection for tears, overtopping, undercutting, accumulated sediment, and failed anchors or seals.
   - Maintenance is essential because containment performance degrades quickly if sediment builds up or barriers are damaged.

From a compliance perspective, these systems are commonly part of a site’s Stormwater Pollution Prevention Plan or similar operational control plan, where stockpile management is treated as a high-priority source of stormwater contamination.

## Variants

Common variants and implementations include:

- **Perimeter berm containment**
  - Earthen or concrete berms surround the stockpile pad to keep runoff on-site.

- **Silt fence enclosure**
  - Temporary containment using geotextile fabric supported by posts.
  - Often used for short-term stockpiles of soil or sediment.

- **Wattle or sock barriers**
  - Straw, compost, or synthetic fiber tubes placed around the pile to slow runoff and capture sediment.

- **Covered stockpile**
  - Tarps, sheeting, or full enclosure to minimize rain contact and dust generation.

- **Lined containment pad**
  - Impermeable liner or paved pad beneath the stockpile, sometimes with collection sumps or trench drains.

- **Concrete washdown or material bay**
  - More robust containment for aggregate, sludge, or contaminated material where runoff must be fully controlled.

- **Segregated stockpile cells**
  - Separate containment areas for different materials to prevent cross-contamination.

- **Temporary construction BMPs**
  - Portable or rapidly installed systems used during earthmoving or remediation work.

Alternatives to a dedicated containment system include:
- storing material indoors,
- minimizing stockpile duration,
- immediately hauling material away,
- or using stabilized bulk storage rather than open piles.

## Trade-offs

Key trade-offs and considerations include:

- **Effectiveness vs. cost**
  - More complete containment, such as lined and drained pads, is more effective but significantly more expensive than temporary barriers.

- **Temporary vs. permanent**
  - Temporary BMPs are cheaper and flexible but require frequent repair and inspection.
  - Permanent systems are more reliable but less adaptable to changing operations.

- **Space requirements**
  - Containment systems need extra footprint for berms, access, inspections, and runoff collection.

- **Material-specific performance**
  - Fine-grained soils, dusty materials, or contaminated stockpiles need stronger controls than inert aggregate.
  - Some materials may generate leachate or dissolved pollutants that require treatment, not just sediment capture.

- **Maintenance burden**
  - Barriers can fail if sediment accumulates, covers tear, or runoff overtops the system.
  - Poor maintenance can make a containment system ineffective even if well designed.

- **Operational complexity**
  - Material handling becomes slower when stockpiles must be loaded, covered, uncovered, or relocated within a contained area.

- **Regulatory fit**
  - The system must match site permit conditions, stormwater requirements, and any hazardous or contaminated material handling rules.
  - A sediment-only control may be insufficient if the stockpile has chemical contamination.

- **Weather sensitivity**
  - Heavy storms can overwhelm undersized controls.
  - Wind can damage covers and mobilize dry material, creating both stormwater and dust concerns.
