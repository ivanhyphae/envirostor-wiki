## Definition

**Background Concentration** refers to the naturally occurring or anthropogenic level of a chemical, contaminant, or constituent in a given environmental medium (soil, groundwater, sediment, or air) that is present independent of a specific site or facility’s operations. It serves as the baseline against which site-related contamination is measured. In regulatory contexts, establishing background concentrations is critical for distinguishing between contributions from a site (e.g., a construction project, a waste stockpile) and pre-existing or regional ambient levels.

In the provided documents, background concentration is used to evaluate soil and groundwater samples taken near construction zones (e.g., MSE wall footings, stockpiles) to determine whether detected constituents (such as metals, volatile organic compounds, or pesticides) are attributable to site activities or to regional or natural sources. For example, the *E-mail Acceptance, Stockpile 1 Wall Footing Sampling Tech Memo* notes that certain detections were compared to “regional background” to assess if exceedances were site-related.

## How it works

Background concentration is typically determined through one or more of the following approaches:

1. **Regional or Ambient Background Sampling**: Collecting samples from locations believed to be unaffected by the site, such as nearby undeveloped areas, upgradient locations, or regional monitoring wells. These samples are analyzed for the same constituents as the site samples.

2. **Statistical Comparison**: Site sample results are compared to background data using statistical methods (e.g., upper prediction limits, tolerance intervals, or non-parametric tests) to identify statistically significant exceedances. For instance, if a metal concentration in a site sample exceeds the 95th percentile of background, it may indicate a contribution from the site.

3. **Historical or Literature Values**: In some cases, background concentrations are derived from existing databases (e.g., California’s Background Concentrations for Heavy Metals in Soil) or published regional studies.

In the *MSE Wall Sampling Plan* and related memos, background values for metals and other parameters were established using off-site soil samples from areas not impacted by the project. The *DTSC E-mail Approval of Carpenter Shoofly Tech Memo* references the use of background data to support the conclusion that certain detections (e.g., arsenic) were derived from natural soils, not site operations.

## Variants

- **Natural Background**: Concentrations arising from geological or biological processes without human influence. For example, naturally occurring arsenic in certain California soils.
- **Anthropogenic Background**: Concentrations from widespread human activity not attributable to a specific site (e.g., urban lead from historical vehicle emissions or regional pesticide use).
- **Site-Specific Background**: Background values measured immediately adjacent to a site but outside the zone of influence, used when regional data may not be representative.
- **Regulatory Background**: Predefined values adopted by regulatory agencies (e.g., California EPA’s background standards) that serve as default thresholds when site-specific background is unavailable.
- **Time-Varying Background**: For dynamic systems (e.g., groundwater plumes), background may change seasonally or due to regional land use changes, requiring periodic re-evaluation.

## Trade-offs

- **Precision vs. Representativeness**: High-precision statistical approaches require large, representative background datasets, which can be costly and time-consuming to collect. The *Stockpile 1 Wall Footing Sampling Tech Memo* demonstrates the challenge when limited sample numbers may not capture spatial variability.
- **Natural vs. Anthropogenic Distinction**: Differentiating naturally occurring levels from diffuse anthropogenic background (e.g., lead from regional urban sources vs. site-specific spills) can be difficult and may lead to ambiguous conclusions.
- **Regulatory Acceptance**: Different agencies may accept different background definitions (e.g., regional vs. site-specific), leading to inconsistent interpretation. The *DTSC E-mail Approval* shows that the regulatory body (DTSC) accepted the use of background to justify no further action, but this may not hold in all jurisdictions.
- **Analytical Sensitivity**: When background levels are close to detection limits, statistical comparisons become unreliable. In the *Stockpile 1* data, some constituents were near detection limits, requiring careful data handling.
- **Scale Dependence**: Background for a small footprint (e.g., a stockpile foundation) may differ from regional background, especially in heterogeneous geology. The memos highlight that “background” for the MSE wall area was derived from nearby, off-site soils, which may not capture all local variability.
