## Background Concentration

### Definition

Background concentration refers to the concentration of a chemical constituent (e.g., metal, organic compound, radionuclide) in soil, sediment, or groundwater that is attributable to natural occurrence and/or diffuse anthropogenic sources, rather than to a specific release from a site under investigation. In environmental site assessment, background concentration establishes a baseline against which the presence and extent of site‑related contamination are judged. Clean fill, stockpile spoils, and foundation materials are frequently compared to background to determine whether they are suitable for reuse without further remediation or require management as contaminated media.

### How it works

Background concentration is typically established through systematic sampling of reference areas that are unaffected by the site’s known or suspected releases. The process follows these steps:

1. **Selection of reference area** – A location or set of locations that are geologically and hydrologically similar to the site but not influenced by the specific operations or releases under investigation. For example, in the SR 132 Basin 5 Clean Fill Tech Memo, regional soil surveys or off‑site samples collected upgradient of the basin were used to define background.

2. **Sampling and analysis** – A statistically sufficient number of samples (often ≥10–20) is collected from the reference area. Analytes of concern are measured using the same laboratory methods as for site samples.

3. **Statistical determination** – Background is expressed as a concentration threshold, most commonly:
   - **Upper tolerance limit (UTL)** – a value that, with a given confidence (e.g., 95%), contains a specified proportion (e.g., 95%) of the reference population.
   - **Upper prediction limit (UPL)** – used for comparison with future sample measurements.
   - **Mean ± standard deviation** – sometimes used for screening.
   - **Fixed default values** – in some jurisdictions, regulatory background values (e.g., naturally occurring metals) are adopted from state or national surveys.

4. **Application** – Site‑specific concentrations are compared against background. Exceedances suggest contributions from the site’s operations, though natural variability and analytical uncertainty must be considered. In the Bentley 2 Stockpiles Tech Memo and the Stockpile 2 BCS Foundation Spoil Workplan, stockpiled soil was compared to local background to decide whether the material met clean‑fill criteria or required disposal as contaminated spoil.

### Variants

- **Natural background** – Purely geogenic concentrations, unaffected by human activity. Examples: metals in undisturbed glacial till, uranium in black shale, arsenic in alluvial deposits.
- **Anthropogenic background** – Concentrations resulting from widespread human activity (e.g., lead from historical vehicle emissions, PCBs from regional atmospheric deposition). This is also called “urban background” or “regional background.”
- **Site‑specific background** – Determined from on‑site or immediately adjacent reference areas that are representative of the site’s pre‑existing condition. This variant most directly isolates site‑related impacts.
- **Default/regional background** – Pre‑established values from soil surveys, state databases, or literature (e.g., USGS background concentrations for a geologic province). Less accurate but often used when site‑specific sampling is impractical.
- **Background for different media** – The concept applies equally to soil, sediment, groundwater, and sometimes air. Groundwater background includes both natural water‑rock interaction and upgradient anthropogenic inputs.

### Trade‑offs

- **Spatial and temporal variability** – Background concentrations can vary widely over short distances or with depth. A single reference area may not capture the full natural range, leading to false positives (natural exceedances attributed to the site) or false negatives (site impacts masked by high background). Multiple reference locations are therefore preferred.
- **Selection bias** – Choosing a reference area too close to the site may unintentionally include low‑level site‑related impacts. Conversely, an area too far away may represent different geology.
- **Statistical power** – Small sample sizes yield wide tolerance limits, reducing the sensitivity to detect site contributions. Regulatory guidance often prescribes a minimum number of samples (e.g., 8–10 per stratum).
- **Chemical similarity** – Background concentrations for many organic compounds (e.g., PAHs, PCBs) are influenced by non‑site diffuse sources. Distinguishing “normal” urban background from a specific release requires careful source analysis.
- **Regulatory acceptance** – Some regulators require site‑specific background data, while others accept regional default values. Using defaults can lower costs but may not withstand legal or public scrutiny if challenged.
- **Analytical limitations** – Laboratory detection limits must be below background thresholds to avoid censoring data. For trace elements, background may be near or below detection limits, complicating statistical comparisons.
