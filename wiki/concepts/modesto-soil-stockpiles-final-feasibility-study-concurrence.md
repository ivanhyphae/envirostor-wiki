## How it works

A feasibility study concurrence process generally follows these steps:

1. **Project problem definition**
   - The project sponsor identifies the issue to be addressed, here related to soil stockpiles in Modesto.

2. **Feasibility analysis**
   - Technical alternatives are evaluated for feasibility, cost, effectiveness, environmental impact, and implementability.
   - The study typically compares one or more alternative actions against a no-action baseline.

3. **Review by stakeholders**
   - Relevant agencies, sponsors, or oversight bodies review the draft or final feasibility study.
   - Comments are resolved, and the document is revised if needed.

4. **Final concurrence**
   - A concurrence letter or memorandum confirms agreement with the study’s conclusions.
   - This concurrence may indicate that the preferred alternative, cost estimates, and planning assumptions are acceptable for further action.

5. **Transition to next phase**
   - Once concurrence is obtained, the project can move toward design, permitting, funding allocation, implementation, or additional authorization steps.

For this concept, the important functional role is not the engineering of the stockpiles themselves, but the **decision-making checkpoint**: the final study has been reviewed and formally accepted.

## Variants

Possible variants of this kind of document or process include:

- **Draft feasibility study concurrence** — preliminary agreement before finalization.
- **Final feasibility study approval** — a stronger authorization term than concurrence in some organizations.
- **Conditional concurrence** — agreement subject to specified revisions, clarifications, or mitigation measures.
- **Interagency concurrence** — multiple agencies formally agree to the same final study.
- **Nonconcurrence with comments** — the reviewing body does not agree and requires changes before approval.

## Trade-offs

Key considerations and limitations of a final feasibility study concurrence include:

- **Administrative value vs. technical detail**
  - The concurrence letter itself may be brief and confirmatory, while the underlying feasibility study contains the substantive analysis.

- **Binding strength varies**
  - “Concurrence” does not always mean full legal approval; its authority depends on the governing agency process.

- **Potential conditionality**
  - Concurrence may be limited by assumptions, scope boundaries, funding constraints, or required follow-up actions.

- **Risk of overreading**
  - A concurrence letter should not be treated as a complete project record; it must be interpreted alongside the final study and related correspondence.

- **Document provenance**
  - In OCR-processed PDFs, metadata may reflect later digitization rather than the original document date, so the archival context should be checked carefully.
