## Composite Sampling

### Definition
Composite sampling is a method of sample collection in which multiple discrete subsamples, taken from different locations or times within a defined area or lot, are physically combined and mixed to form a single representative sample for analysis. The goal is to obtain an average concentration or property of the overall material while reducing the number of laboratory analyses and associated costs.

### How it works
In composite sampling, the sampling plan defines the target population (e.g., a stockpile of clean fill, a borrow area, or a defined volume of soil) and the number of subsamples (increments) needed to achieve a statistically representative composite. The subsamples are collected either on a systematic grid, random pattern, or stratified basis to capture spatial or temporal variability. They are then homogenized—often by thorough mixing, grinding, or splitting—into a single laboratory specimen. The analytical result from the composite is interpreted as the mean of the constituent subsamples, assuming the material is relatively uniform or that the goal is to estimate an average.

Composite sampling is most effective when the contaminant or parameter of interest is homogeneously distributed, or when the goal is to detect the presence of a contaminant above a threshold using a pooled sample. In environmental regulation, composite samples are frequently used for soil, sediment, water, and waste characterization to reduce analytical costs while maintaining a specified level of confidence.

### Variants
- **Systematic composite**: Subsamples taken at regular intervals (e.g., every 10 m along a transect or every 5 minutes in a process stream).
- **Stratified composite**: The area is divided into strata, and subsamples are taken proportionally from each stratum to ensure representation of different zones.
- **Time‑weighted composite**: For flowing media (e.g., groundwater or effluent), subsamples are collected over time and combined in proportion to flow rate.
- **Grab composite**: A simple mixing of several grab samples from the same location (less common for spatial representation).
- **Multi‑increment composite**: Used in soil investigations where dozens of increments are collected and composited to characterize a small area with high spatial density.

### Trade‑offs
- **Cost vs. resolution**: Composite sampling reduces the number of analyses, but loses information on individual subsample variability. Outliers or hot spots may be diluted and go undetected.
- **Homogenization bias**: Incomplete mixing can lead to non‑representative results. Proper grinding, sieving, and mixing protocols are critical.
- **Interpretation limits**: Compositing assumes the parameter of interest behaves additively; it is not suitable when the goal is to estimate the proportion of non‑detects or maximum values.
- **Regulatory acceptance**: Some regulators require discrete (grab) samples for definitive compliance decisions, especially in cleanup programs. Composite results may be used only for screening or delineation.
- **Sample integrity**: Combining samples may cause chemical reactions (e.g., volatilization loss) or physical changes (e.g., oxidation) that alter the measured concentration.
