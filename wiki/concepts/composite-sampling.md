## Definition

**Composite sampling** is a method of sample collection in which multiple individual aliquots (increments) taken from different locations, depths, or times are physically combined and mixed to form a single aggregate sample for chemical or physical analysis. The resulting composite represents the average composition of the sampled material over the area, volume, or period covered by the increments, rather than characterizing any single point.

In environmental and geotechnical applications—such as evaluating clean fill, stockpiled soil, or borrow areas—composite sampling is commonly used to screen large volumes of material for contamination, reduce analytical costs, and provide a statistically defensible estimate of mean contaminant concentrations.

## How it works

### 1. Sampling design  
The area or stockpile is divided into distinct sampling units (e.g., grids, depth intervals, or volumes). A predetermined number of increments (typically 5–20) are collected from random or systematic locations within each unit.

### 2. Increment collection  
Each increment is taken using a clean sampling tool (e.g., stainless steel trowel, hand auger, or split-spoon sampler). Increments are placed directly into a dedicated composite container (e.g., Ziploc bag, glass jar, or stainless steel bucket).

### 3. Homogenization  
All increments within a composite are thoroughly mixed to achieve a uniform particle-size distribution and spatial averaging. For dry soil, this may involve quartering, riffle splitting, or mechanical blending. For wet or cohesive material, hand homogenization with a mortar and pestle or mechanical mixer is used.

### 4. Subsampling  
A representative portion of the homogenized composite is extracted for laboratory analysis. The remainder may be retained for archival purposes or re-analysis.

### 5. Analysis  
The composite subsample is submitted to an accredited laboratory for target analytes (e.g., metals, semi-volatile organic compounds). Results are reported as a single concentration per composite, interpreted as the average concentration across the sampled unit.

In regulatory context, a composite sample often must meet a minimum number of increments (e.g., 5 increments per composite) and be collected in accordance with an approved Sampling Plan (e.g., *Carpenter Road Shoofly Clean Fill Sampling Plan*, *Basin 5 Clean Fill Sampling Plan*) with prior regulatory concurrence from agencies such as the Department of Toxic Substances Control (DTSC).

## Variants

### 1. Discrete (grab) sampling  
Each increment is analyzed separately. Provides high spatial resolution but higher cost and lower statistical confidence for large volumes.

### 2. Incremental sampling methodology (ISM)  
A rigorous form of composite sampling with strict protocols for increment number, distribution, and particle size reduction. Commonly used for dioxins, metals, and pesticides in soils.

### 3. Systematic composite sampling  
Increments are collected on a regular grid or along transects, ensuring uniform coverage. Preferred for stockpiles and borrow areas.

### 4. Judgmental composite sampling  
Increments are collected based on visible indications of heterogeneity (e.g., staining, debris). Risk of bias; less accepted for regulatory decisions.

### 5. Time-weighted composite sampling  
Increments are collected at equal time intervals from a flowing stream or process line, then composited for average concentration over time. Used in wastewater and stormwater monitoring.

### 6. Stratified composite sampling  
The sampling unit is subdivided into strata (e.g., by depth or material type), and a separate composite is prepared for each stratum. Maintains some spatial resolution while reducing analytical load.

### 7. Multi-increment vs. field composite  
- **Multi-increment**: field homogenization is minimal; increments are collected and shipped separately, then composited in the lab.  
- **Field composite**: increments are combined in the field. More common in soil stockpile management.

## Trade‑offs

### Advantages
- **Lower cost**: fewer analyses per unit volume.
- **Statistical power**: large number of increments provides a robust estimate of the mean concentration, reducing the impact of small-scale variability.
- **Representativeness**: captures spatial variability across the sampled area, reducing the risk of missing a hotspot if the target contaminant is heterogeneously distributed.
- **Regulatory acceptance**: widely accepted by agencies (e.g., DTSC, EPA) when documented in a formal Sampling Plan and approved in advance.
- **Reduced analytical turnaround**: fewer samples to process.

### Disadvantages
- **Loss of spatial resolution**: cannot determine which increment, if any, is contaminated; only the average across the unit.
- **Masking of hotspots**: a highly contaminated increment can be diluted by clean increments to below detection limits—especially problematic for small, intense plumes.
- **Homogenization challenges**: inadequate mixing can introduce bias; difficult for cohesive soils or high‑moisture materials.
- **Increased sample handling**: requires additional steps (mixing, splitting) that can introduce cross‑contamination.
- **Regulatory risk**: if a composite result exceeds a threshold, the entire unit may be classified as contaminated without knowledge of the exact source.
- **Not suitable for volatile organic compounds (VOCs)**: physical mixing can cause loss of volatile analytes. Discrete or grab sampling is preferred for VOCs.

### Key considerations
- Increment number: ≥5 is common; more increments improve precision but increase collection effort.
- Particle size reduction: essential for homogeneity; typically ≤2 mm.
- Field duplicates: one composite per 10–20 should be submitted as a duplicate for quality control.
- Regulatory approval: a Composite Sampling Plan must be submitted and approved before implementation (as shown in the attached regulatory approval emails).
