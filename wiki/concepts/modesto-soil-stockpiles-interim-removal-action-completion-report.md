## Definition

The **Modesto Soil Stockpiles Interim Removal Action Completion Report** is a formal environmental remediation document that summarizes the completion of an **interim removal action** for contaminated or potentially contaminated soil stockpiles associated with the Modesto site. In Caltrans and regulatory cleanup practice, an interim removal action completion report documents what was removed, where it went, how the work was performed, and whether the cleanup objectives for that interim action were met.

In this case, the report concerns **soil stockpiles in Modesto** and is paired with an acceptance letter titled **Final Removal Action Completion Report_Modesto Soil Stockpiles**, indicating that the regulatory agency reviewed the submittal and accepted it as the final accounting of the removal action.

## How it works

An interim removal action completion report typically follows a sequence like this:

1. **Site characterization and stockpile identification**  
   Soil is excavated during construction, maintenance, or investigation activities and temporarily stockpiled on-site or at a managed location. Sampling and field observations determine whether the soil may contain contaminants requiring special handling.

2. **Interim removal action planning**  
   If the stockpiles present a risk or must be disposed of before a full site remedy is finalized, an interim removal action is designed. This may include:
   - segregation of impacted and non-impacted soil,
   - confirmation sampling,
   - loading and off-site transport,
   - treatment, disposal, or reuse at an approved facility.

3. **Implementation**  
   The stockpiles are removed according to project specifications and regulatory requirements. Documentation usually includes:
   - estimated and actual volumes,
   - excavation or loading methods,
   - chain-of-custody and laboratory results,
   - disposal manifests or facility acceptance records,
   - observations of field conditions and any deviations from the plan.

4. **Completion reporting**  
   The completion report compiles the evidence that the action was completed. It generally explains:
   - the scope of the work,
   - the number and location of stockpiles addressed,
   - analytical results and cleanup criteria,
   - the final disposition of the material,
   - whether any residual contamination remains.

5. **Regulatory review and acceptance**  
   The agency reviews the report to determine whether the action sufficiently reduced risk or met the project’s closure criteria. The accompanying acceptance letter indicates the regulator accepted the report, which is an important milestone even if broader site cleanup may continue.

For the Modesto soil stockpiles matter, the report likely served as a record that the stockpiled soil was managed and removed in accordance with approved interim removal action requirements, and that the agency accepted the documentation of that work.

## Variants

Common variants of this type of document include:

- **Interim Removal Action Completion Report**  
  Used when the cleanup is an early or partial action rather than the final remedy for the entire site.

- **Final Removal Action Completion Report**  
  Used when the removal action is the last cleanup step for the addressed material or area. The acceptance letter source suggests this wording was used for the Modesto stockpiles submittal.

- **Removal Action Work Plan / Action Memorandum**  
  The planning document that precedes the completion report and defines the scope and criteria for the action.

- **Remedial Action Completion Report**  
  A broader term sometimes used in place of “removal action” depending on the regulatory framework and scale of the cleanup.

- **Stockpile management documentation**  
  Supporting records such as sampling plans, manifests, waste profiling forms, and transportation receipts.

## Trade-offs

Key considerations and limitations of an interim removal action completion report include:

- **Interim vs. final closure**  
  An interim action can address immediate risk, but it may not resolve all contamination issues at a site.

- **Reliance on sampling density**  
  Conclusions about stockpile contamination depend on how representative the samples were. Limited sampling can leave uncertainty.

- **Documentation burden**  
  These reports require careful compilation of field records, analytical data, and disposal records, which can be time-consuming.

- **Regulatory acceptance is not universal closure**  
  Agency acceptance of the report means the submitted removal action is complete and documented, but it does not necessarily mean all site obligations are finished.

- **Waste handling constraints**  
  Contaminated soil may require special disposal, increasing cost and logistics complexity.

- **Potential for residual contamination**  
  Even after stockpiles are removed, underlying soils or adjacent areas may still require further investigation or remediation.
