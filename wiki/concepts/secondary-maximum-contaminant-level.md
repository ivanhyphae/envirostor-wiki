## Secondary Maximum Contaminant Level (SMCL)

### Definition
A **Secondary Maximum Contaminant Level (SMCL)** is a non-enforceable, health-based guideline established by the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) for contaminants in drinking water that primarily affect aesthetic qualities (taste, odor, color) or cause cosmetic effects (skin or tooth discoloration) or technical effects (corrosion, staining). Unlike primary standards (MCLs), SMCLs are not legally binding but serve as recommended limits to ensure water palatability and prevent nuisance issues.

### How it works
SMCLs are derived from data on contaminant concentrations that cause noticeable aesthetic or technical problems. The EPA sets a **secondary maximum contaminant level** based on:
- **Aesthetic effects**: Thresholds for taste (e.g., chloride, sulfate), odor (e.g., hydrogen sulfide), or turbidity.
- **Cosmetic effects**: Concentrations linked to discoloration (e.g., copper causing bluish-green staining, or iron causing reddish-brown staining) or dental fluorosis (fluoride).
- **Technical effects**: Levels that accelerate pipe corrosion or scaling (e.g., total dissolved solids, pH).

For groundwater monitoring, as in the provided Caltrans reports, SMCLs are used alongside primary MCLs to assess overall water quality. For example, in the Modesto stockpiles groundwater data, constituents like iron, manganese, and TDS are compared to SMCLs. If concentrations exceed SMCLs, it indicates aesthetic or operational concerns but does not trigger legal enforcement. Laboratories measure contaminants (e.g., via EPA methods 200.7 for metals) and compare results to the SMCL table (published in 40 CFR Part 143). Water systems may voluntarily treat or monitor if SMCLs are exceeded to avoid customer complaints.

### Variants
- **National Secondary Drinking Water Regulations (NSDWR)**: The official EPA list of SMCLs for 15 contaminants (e.g., aluminum, chloride, copper, fluoride, iron, manganese, silver, sulfate, TDS, zinc).
- **State-specific SMCLs**: Some states adopt stricter or additional SMCLs (e.g., California’s Title 22 Water Reclamation Regulations uses SMCLs for recycled water).
- **Health-based vs. aesthetic SMCLs**: While most SMCLs are aesthetic/cosmetic, a few (e.g., fluoride at 2.0 mg/L) have health implications but remain non-enforceable.

### Trade-offs
- **Regulatory gap**: SMCLs are advisory, not enforceable, so water suppliers may ignore them, leading to poor taste or staining that reduces consumer confidence.
- **Cost vs. compliance**: Treating water to meet SMCLs (e.g., softening for hardness, aeration for odor) can be expensive, especially for small systems, with no legal penalty for non-compliance.
- **Public perception**: Exceeding SMCLs does not pose acute health risks but can cause unnecessary alarm or distrust if not explained (as seen in groundwater reports where iron or manganese exceedances are flagged).
- **Confusion with MCLs**: In reports like the Caltrans documents, SMCL exceedances are often grouped with MCL exceedances, potentially misleading readers about health threat levels.
