## Borrow Area

### Definition

A **borrow area** is a designated location from which earth, rock, or other fill materials are excavated for use in construction projects, such as road embankments, dam cores, or site grading. The material sourced is typically referred to as "borrow" and is brought to a project site to make up for a deficit in on-site material. Borrow areas are distinct from Quarry|quarries in that they are often temporary and may be reclaimed after extraction.

### How it works

Borrow area development follows a structured process:

1. **Site Selection** – Geotechnical and environmental assessments identify suitable locations with adequate material quantity and quality. Proximity to the project site minimizes haul costs.
2. **Material Testing** – Samples are collected and tested for properties such as compaction, moisture content, and contamination (e.g., heavy metals, organics). Permits often require demonstrating the material meets "clean fill" standards, as seen in documents like the *SR 132 Basin 5 Clean Fill Sampling Plan*.
3. **Approval and Permitting** – Regulatory agencies (e.g., local environmental departments) review sampling plans and technical memoranda. Approval letters, such as the *Borrow Area Results Tech Memo Approval*, confirm compliance.
4. **Extraction** – Earthmoving equipment removes material to a specified depth. Grading and drainage controls prevent erosion and sedimentation.
5. **Reclamation** – After extraction, the area is graded, revegetated, or otherwise restored per permit conditions. This may be part of a Reclamation Plan|reclamation plan.

### Variants

- **On-site Borrow Area** – Within the project boundary; reduces transportation but may conflict with other construction activities.
- **Off-site Borrow Area** – Leased or purchased land outside the project footprint; often larger but requires hauling permits and longer transport.
- **Clean Fill Borrow Area** – Used for non-contaminated soil only, as specified in documents like the *SR 132 Basin 5 Clean Fill Tech Memo*. Requires rigorous testing to avoid introducing pollutants.
- **Rock Borrow** – Sources of rock for riprap or aggregate, often requiring blasting or crushing.

### Trade-offs

- **Cost vs. Quality** – Closer borrow areas reduce haul cost but may yield lower-quality material that requires processing (e.g., screening or mixing).
- **Environmental Impact** – Extraction disturbs soil, vegetation, and potentially groundwater. Mitigation measures (dust control, sediment basins) are needed.
- **Regulatory Burden** – Permitting timelines can delay projects. Extensive sampling and approvals (as indicated by multiple memos) are common.
- **Reclamation Liability** – The project owner may be responsible for restoring the area to pre-extraction conditions, adding long-term cost.
