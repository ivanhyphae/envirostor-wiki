## Definition

**Total Dissolved Solids (TDS)** is the combined concentration of dissolved inorganic and organic substances in water, usually reported in **milligrams per liter (mg/L)** or **parts per million (ppm)**. It is a broad water-quality indicator that reflects the total amount of material remaining in a water sample after suspended particles have been removed.

TDS is not a measure of any single contaminant. Instead, it summarizes the dissolved fraction of water chemistry, including common ions such as calcium, magnesium, sodium, potassium, bicarbonate, chloride, sulfate, nitrate, and dissolved silica, along with smaller amounts of dissolved organic matter.

## How it works

TDS is typically determined in one of two ways:

1. **Gravimetric measurement**
   - A water sample is filtered to remove suspended solids.
   - The filtered water is evaporated.
   - The remaining residue is weighed.
   - That mass, normalized to the sample volume, gives TDS.

2. **Electrical conductivity estimation**
   - Because dissolved ions conduct electricity, TDS is often estimated from **specific conductance** or **electrical conductivity (EC)**.
   - A conversion factor is applied to estimate TDS from measured conductivity.
   - This is faster and more common in field monitoring, but it is an approximation because the relationship depends on the specific dissolved ions present.

In groundwater and stormwater monitoring, TDS is used as a general indicator of mineralization and dissolved contamination. Elevated TDS can reflect natural geochemistry, irrigation return flow, road salts, industrial inputs, evaporation concentration, or leaching from stockpiles and disturbed soils.

In the Modesto stockpiles monitoring reports, TDS appears as one of the routine water-quality indicators used to characterize groundwater and stormwater conditions over time. In that setting, it helps show whether water quality is becoming more saline or mineralized, and whether site drainage or infiltration may be influencing dissolved constituents.

## Variants

Common related forms and measurement approaches include:

- **Gravimetric TDS**: direct residue measurement after filtration and evaporation.
- **Calculated TDS**: estimated from summed concentrations of major dissolved ions.
- **Conductivity-derived TDS**: estimated from EC or specific conductance using a site-specific or standard conversion factor.
- **Filtered dissolved solids**: conceptually similar, though not always identical in laboratory practice.
- **Related salinity metrics**:
  - **Specific conductance**
  - **Chloride**
  - **Hardness**
  - **Alkalinity**
  - **Salinity**

In environmental monitoring, TDS is often interpreted alongside specific conductance, chloride, and major ion chemistry because those parameters help explain what is driving the dissolved load.

## Trade-offs

### Strengths
- **Simple summary metric**: provides a quick snapshot of dissolved mineral content.
- **Useful screening tool**: good for detecting broad changes in water chemistry.
- **Easy to trend over time**: helpful in groundwater and stormwater monitoring programs.
- **Low-cost field estimation**: conductivity-based TDS can be measured rapidly.

### Limitations
- **Non-specific**: does not identify which dissolved substances are present.
- **Approximate when estimated from conductivity**: conversion factors vary with water chemistry.
- **Insensitive to toxic effects**: a high or low TDS value does not indicate whether water is safe by itself.
- **Can be influenced by natural conditions**: elevated TDS may reflect natural geology rather than contamination.
- **Not a substitute for full chemistry**: major ions, nutrients, and trace constituents are needed for interpretation.

### Interpretation considerations
- TDS should be evaluated with site context, including geology, land use, drainage patterns, and season.
- In stockpile or construction-related monitoring, elevated TDS may indicate leaching from exposed materials, contact with soils, or evaporative concentration in shallow water.
- In stormwater, TDS can rise after first-flush runoff, wash-off of dust and fines, or contact with paved and disturbed surfaces.
