## Definition

**Test Pits** are excavated, non‑structural holes dug into the ground to visually inspect subsurface conditions, collect soil or fill samples, and assess the presence of contaminants, debris, or geotechnical properties. They are a common investigative technique in environmental site assessments, construction quality assurance, and remediation projects.

In the context of clean fill sampling (as per the referenced "Basin 5 Clean Fill Sampling Plan"), test pits are used to characterize fill material for compliance with regulatory standards, such as verifying that imported or on‑site fill meets clean fill criteria before use in a construction or restoration setting.

## How it works

Test pit excavation follows a systematic procedure:

1. **Planning and Permitting** – The sampling plan identifies target locations (often via a grid or random pattern), depth intervals (e.g., 0‑2 ft, 2‑5 ft, etc.), and any required permits (e.g., environmental, utility clearance).

2. **Excavation** – A backhoe, excavator, or similar machinery digs a pit to the target depth (typically 2–10 ft, but deeper for specialized studies). The pit is usually rectangular (e.g., 2 ft × 4 ft) to allow safe access and visual inspection.

3. **Visual and Field Screening** – The exposed sidewalls are examined for:  
   - Soil/fill color, texture, and structure  
   – Visible contamination, debris, or staining  
   – #Foreign materials (e.g., concrete, wood, metal, plastic)  
   – Odor (e.g., petroleum, chemical)  
   – Field screening with an organic vapor analyzer (OVA) or photoionization detector (PID) for volatiles.

4. **Sampling** – Samples are collected from specific horizons or at discrete depths using trowels, augers, or core samplers. Protocols specify:  
   - Number of samples per pit (e.g., one per distinct layer, or one per 2‑ft interval)  
   – Sample volume (e.g., 1‑liter glass jars for VOCs, polyethylene bags for metals)  
   – Chain‑of‑custody documentation.

5. **Backfilling** – After inspection and sampling, the pit is backfilled with the excavated material (or replaced with clean fill if contaminated) and compacted to prevent subsidence. Surface restoration (e.g., seeding, grading) follows.

6. **Documentation** – Field logs record:  
   – Pit dimensions, location (GPS coordinates), depth, weather conditions  
   – Visual observations (photos, sketches)  
   – Sample IDs, depths, and field screening results  
   – Any deviations from the sampling plan.

## Variants

- **Hand‑dug test pits** – Shallow pits (≤ 3 ft) dug manually with shovels, used where machinery access is limited or where disturbance must be minimized (e.g., near buried utilities).  
- **Mechanically excavated test pits** – Using backhoes or excavators for deeper or larger pits, common in large‑scale clean fill verification.  
- **Trench‑style test pits** – Elongated excavations (trenches) for lateral continuity assessment, often used in archaeological or buried infrastructure surveys.  
- **Geotechnical test pits** – Focused on soil density, moisture, and bearing capacity, with field tests (e.g., pocket penetrometer, torvane).  
- **Environmental test pits** – Designed to detect contaminants (e.g., heavy metals, PAHs, VOCs) with stringent decontamination procedures between samples (e.g., trowel cleaning, dedicated sampling equipment).  
- **“Clean fill” test pits** – Sampling strategy for verifying imported or stockpiled fill meets regulatory criteria for reuse, often following a grid‑based statistical approach (e.g., one sample per 500–1000 cubic yards).

## Trade-offs

**Advantages:**

- **Visual confirmation** – Allows direct observation of soil stratigraphy, contamination zones, and debris.  
- **Cost‑effective for shallow depths** – Less expensive than boreholes for depths up to ~10 ft.  
- **Fast turnaround** – Typically one day for excavation, sampling, and backfilling.  
- **Flexible sample collection** – Can collect large‑volume samples and target specific layers.  
- **No drilling fluids** – Avoids potential cross‑contamination from drilling muds.

**Limitations:**

- **Depth restriction** – Typically ≤ 15–20 ft due to excavation safety (e.g., shoring requirements for deeper pits).  
- **Disturbance** – Excavation mixes layers, potentially obscuring subtle contamination patterns.  
- **Safety hazards** – Risk of collapse, utility strikes, or exposure to buried hazards (e.g., gas, chemicals).  
- **Weather‑dependent** – Rain can flood pits, complicate sampling, and cause instability.  
- **Regulatory constraints** – Some jurisdictions restrict pit depth or require permits for excavation.  
- **Less precision** than boreholes for deep or discrete depth‑specific sampling (e.g., 0.5‑ft intervals).  
- **Potential for incomplete characterization** – Pits only capture small, discrete volumes, so spatial variability may be missed (mitigated by adequate spacing per sampling plan).
