## Definition

**Surface water monitoring** is the systematic collection, analysis, and interpretation of physical, chemical, and biological data from water bodies such as rivers, lakes, streams, reservoirs, and stormwater conveyances. It is conducted to assess water quality, detect pollution, ensure regulatory compliance, and inform environmental management decisions. In regulatory contexts (e.g., cleanup sites), surface water monitoring often targets potential contaminant migration from soil or groundwater to adjacent surface waters.

## How it works

Surface water monitoring follows a structured workflow:

1. **Objective setting** – Determine the purpose (e.g., compliance with a permit, assessment of a spill, baseline characterization).
2. **Site selection** – Choose sampling locations that represent the water body’s condition, including upstream/downstream, mixing zones, and sensitive receptors.
3. **Sampling plan** – Specify the number of locations, sampling frequency, field parameters (pH, temperature, conductivity, dissolved oxygen), and analytes (metals, organics, nutrients, bacteria). Plans often follow a Quality Assurance Project Plan (QAPP).
4. **Field collection** – Use techniques such as grab samples (single point in time) or composite samples (integrated over time or depth). Equipment is decontaminated between sites to prevent cross‑contamination.
5. **Sample preservation and transport** – Add preservatives (e.g., acid for metals), seal containers, maintain chain of custody, and deliver to a certified laboratory within holding times.
6. **Laboratory analysis** – Methods vary by analyte (e.g., EPA 600 series for organics, ICP‑MS for metals, SM 2540 for solids). Quality control includes blanks, duplicates, and matrix spikes.
7. **Data evaluation** – Compare results to water quality standards (e.g., California Toxics Rule, Basin Plan objectives), historical data, or background levels.
8. **Reporting** – Summarize findings, note exceedances, and recommend follow‑up actions. Reports often include field logs, analytical reports, chain-of-custody forms, and data validation.

The sources indicate that surface water monitoring can be part of a larger environmental investigation. For example, the *Modesto Stockpiles* report (Dec 2016) involved sampling surface water from stockpiles to assess potential runoff impacts. The *DTSC Letter Response* (2025 GW Monitoring Report) likely integrates surface water monitoring with groundwater monitoring to evaluate contaminant pathways. The *Surface Water Letter* (March 2023) suggests a regulatory response, possibly a voluntary cleanup agreement or enforcement action, requiring surface water assessment.

## Variants

- **Stormwater monitoring** – Sampling runoff from industrial sites, construction zones, or municipal separate storm sewer systems (MS4s). Often event‑based and flow‑weighted.
- **Receiving water monitoring** – Assessing water quality in lakes, rivers, or estuaries that receive discharges. Used for total maximum daily load (TMDL) implementation.
- **Grab sampling** – Single discrete sample collected at a specific time and location. Simple and widely used but may miss episodic events.
- **Composite sampling** – A blend of multiple increments taken over time (time‑composite) or across a cross‑section (depth‑integrated). Provides an average concentration but can mask short‑term peaks.
- **Passive sampling** – Devices (e.g., semipermeable membrane devices, polar organic chemical integrative samplers) that accumulate contaminants over days to weeks, yielding time‑weighted average concentrations.
- **Biological monitoring** – Using macroinvertebrates, fish, or algae as indicators of overall water quality (bioassessment). Often combined with chemical monitoring.

## Trade-offs

| Trade-off | Consideration |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Cost vs. density** | Higher sampling frequency and more analytes increase cost. Sparse sampling may miss critical events (e.g., first flush of a storm). |
| **Representativeness** | A single grab sample may not reflect diurnal or storm‑driven variability. Composite or passive sampling improves representativeness but requires more field effort. |
| **Quality assurance** | Field blanks, duplicate samples, and matrix spikes are essential but add cost. Without them, data may be legally indefensible. |
| **Regulatory acceptance** | Some methods (e.g., EPA‑approved) are preferred by agencies. Using non‑standard methods can delay regulatory decisions. |
| **Seasonal/weather effects** | Dry‑weather vs. wet‑weather conditions drastically alter flow, dilution, and contaminant loading. Monitoring plans must account for both. |
| **Sample complexity** | Certain analytes (e.g., volatile organic compounds) require zero headspace and immediate preservation. Field errors can invalidate results. |
| **Interpretation** | Exceedances of water quality criteria do not always indicate impairment; natural background or analytical interference must be considered. |
