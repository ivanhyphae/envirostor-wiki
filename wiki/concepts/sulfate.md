## Definition

**Sulfate** (SO₄²⁻) is a polyatomic anion consisting of a central sulfur atom bonded to four oxygen atoms in a tetrahedral arrangement. It is a common, naturally occurring species in minerals, soils, water, and biological systems. Sulfate is the fully oxidized form of sulfur and plays essential roles in geochemical cycles, industrial processes, and environmental chemistry.

## How it works

Sulfate ions form when sulfur (typically from sulfides or elemental sulfur) is oxidized. In aqueous environments, sulfate is highly soluble and stable, acting as a major carrier of sulfur in the hydrologic cycle. In biological systems, sulfate serves as a terminal electron acceptor for sulfate‑reducing bacteria under anaerobic conditions, producing hydrogen sulfide (H₂S). In geochemistry, sulfate contributes to the formation of evaporite minerals such as gypsum (CaSO₄·2H₂O) and is a key component of acid rain when combined with nitric acid.

Sulfate concentrations in water are typically measured by ion chromatography, turbidimetry, or gravimetric analysis. Regulatory standards (e.g., US EPA secondary drinking water standard of 250 mg/L) exist to limit taste and laxative effects.

## Variants

- **Sulfate salts** – e.g., sodium sulfate (Na₂SO₄), calcium sulfate (CaSO₄), magnesium sulfate (MgSO₄). These vary in solubility and industrial use.
- **Hydrated forms** – e.g., gypsum (CaSO₄·2H₂O), Epsom salts (MgSO₄·7H₂O).
- **Sulfate esters** – organic compounds containing -OSO₃⁻ groups (e.g., in biological molecules like chondroitin sulfate).
- **Sulfate minerals** – barite (BaSO₄), celestine (SrSO₄), anglesite (PbSO₄).

## Trade-offs

- **Environmental**: Elevated sulfate in water bodies can lead to eutrophication and formation of toxic hydrogen sulfide in sediments. Acid mine drainage releases high sulfate loads, lowering pH and mobilizing metals.
- **Health**: High sulfate concentrations (>600 mg/L) may cause diarrhea and dehydration, especially in infants. Conversely, sulfate is essential for detoxification pathways in the liver.
- **Industrial**: Sulfate is widely used in detergents, paper, and textile manufacturing. However, sulfate discharge is regulated to prevent scaling in pipes and corrosion in concrete.
- **Measurement**: Accurate quantification requires careful sample preservation (cooling, filtration) to avoid precipitation or microbial transformation.
