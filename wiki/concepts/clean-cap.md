## Definition
A **clean cap** is a layer of uncontaminated soil, aggregate, or geosynthetic material placed over contaminated, disturbed, or otherwise unacceptable ground to isolate the underlying media from human contact, ecological receptors, and environmental transport pathways. It is a common closure or interim measure in remediation, landfill closure, and construction projects where leaving contaminated material in place is acceptable provided a functional barrier is established.

## How it works
The clean cap physically separates the underlying material from the surface environment. Implementation typically involves:

1. **Material sourcing** – Clean fill must meet chemical and physical specifications (e.g., concentrations of metals, hydrocarbons, or other contaminants below regulatory thresholds) as defined by site-specific technical memos (e.g., *SR 132 Basin 5 Clean Fill Tech Memo*). Certification of fill origin and testing is often required.

2. **Placement** – The cap is graded to a design thickness (commonly 2–4 feet for soil caps, but varies with land use) and compacted to minimize settlement. A vegetative cover (e.g., grass or native plants) is usually established for erosion control.

3. **Performance monitoring** – The cap is inspected for cracking, erosion, or animal burrowing. Some caps include underlying gas vents or drainage layers to manage leachate or biogas.

4. **Regulatory approval** – Agencies (e.g., DTSC) review and approve the cap design and material certification, as seen in the *DTSC E-mail Approval of Carpenter Shoofly Tech Memo*. The cap becomes a permanent institutional control, restricting excavation and future use.

## Variants
- **Simple soil cover** – Clean native soil or imported fill placed over waste or contaminated soil, often used in interim stabilization.
- **Engineered cap** – A multi‑layered system including a geomembrane, drainage geocomposite, and soil cover; typical for active landfills or high‑risk sites.
- **Vegetated cap** – Incorporates deep‑rooted plants to manage water balance through evapotranspiration, reducing leachate generation.
- **Pavement / hard cap** – Asphalt, concrete, or compacted gravel used in areas designated for parking, roads, or building pads.

## Trade-offs
- **Cost** – Sourcing, testing, and placing certified clean fill can be expensive, especially when large volumes are needed (see *Bent 2 Stockpiles Tech Memo* for stockpile management).
- **Maintenance** – Caps require periodic inspection and repair; erosion or settlement can compromise integrity.
- **Land‑use restrictions** – The cap must remain intact; future excavation or deep foundations are often prohibited, reducing land value.
- **Contaminant mobility** – Caps do not destroy contaminants; they can still migrate laterally or via groundwater upwelling if the barrier is incomplete.
- **Regulatory complexity** – Approval hinges on demonstrating long‑term performance; changes in ownership or adjacent development may require additional oversight.
