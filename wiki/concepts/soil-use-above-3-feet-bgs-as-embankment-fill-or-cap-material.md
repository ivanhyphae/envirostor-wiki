## Definition

**Soil Use Above 3 Feet Bgs As Embankment Fill Or Cap Material** refers to the reuse of excavated soil located **more than 3 feet below ground surface (bgs)** as either:

- **embankment fill**: material placed and compacted to build up grade or support a slope, roadway, berm, or similar earthwork feature; or
- **cap material**: soil placed as a cover layer, commonly over a managed area such as a stockpile, wall footing zone, or other engineered surface.

The phrase is typically used in a geotechnical or environmental construction context to indicate that soil from deeper excavations may be acceptable for reuse, provided it meets project-specific engineering and environmental criteria.

## How it works

The basic idea is that soil excavated from depths greater than 3 feet bgs is evaluated for suitability and, if acceptable, reused on site rather than disposed of.

Typical evaluation steps include:

1. **Characterization of the soil**
   - Soil is reviewed for texture, moisture condition, contamination indicators, debris content, and suitability for compaction.
   - Deeper soils are sometimes presumed to be less affected by surface disturbances, but they still require verification.

2. **Acceptance review**
   - A technical memo, field observation, or e-mail acceptance may document whether the material is allowed for reuse.
   - The acceptance usually depends on whether the soil can function structurally as fill or as a cover/cap layer without creating instability, drainage problems, or environmental risk.

3. **Placement requirements**
   - If used as embankment fill, the soil is placed in lifts and compacted to meet density and moisture criteria.
   - If used as cap material, it is placed to achieve a specified thickness and surface condition, often to isolate underlying material and promote drainage or protect a completed surface.

4. **Quality control**
   - Material is checked during placement for large clods, organics, excess moisture, frozen conditions, or other unsuitable characteristics.
   - Compaction and grading are verified to ensure performance.

In practice, “above 3 feet bgs” functions as a screening threshold. Soil from beyond that depth may be considered more likely to be clean or undisturbed, but the actual decision still depends on project acceptance criteria, geotechnical performance, and any environmental restrictions.

## Variants

Common variants and related implementations include:

- **Embankment fill reuse**
  - Soil is reused to build up slopes, road subgrades, berms, or other structural earthworks.

- **Cap material reuse**
  - Soil is used as a cover layer over a stockpile, excavation, or other surface requiring a protective cap.

- **Select fill reuse**
  - A more restrictive version where only certain soil types are approved, often based on gradation, plasticity, and compaction behavior.

- **Import fill substitute**
  - On-site soil is reused in place of imported borrow material when it meets specifications.

- **Clean soil reuse**
  - Used when the main criterion is that the soil is free of unacceptable contamination or debris.

- **Engineered cover system**
  - In some projects, the cap may be part of a layered system with drainage, separation, or protective layers rather than a simple soil cover.

## Trade-offs

### Advantages
- **Reduces disposal volume** and landfill/export costs.
- **Lowers material import needs**, saving time and money.
- **Supports sustainable reuse** of excavated material.
- **Can be efficient for site balancing**, especially when cut-and-fill volumes are similar.

### Limitations and risks
- **Suitability is not automatic**: deeper soil still needs confirmation for contamination and engineering performance.
- **Variable soil quality** may cause compaction or stability issues.
- **Moisture sensitivity** can make placement difficult, especially for fine-grained soils.
- **Overly coarse, organic, or debris-laden soil** may not perform well as fill or cap.
- **Environmental restrictions** may prohibit reuse even if the soil is structurally acceptable.
- **Thickness and grading constraints** may limit whether material can function as a proper cap.

### Practical considerations
- Verify whether the acceptance applies specifically to a project area or material stockpile.
- Confirm whether the soil is allowed for **structural fill**, **cover material**, or both.
- Check whether any testing, documentation, or field approval is required before reuse.
- Consider drainage and settlement behavior if the soil will support loads or protect underlying material.
