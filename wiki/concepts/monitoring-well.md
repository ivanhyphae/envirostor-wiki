## Monitoring Well

### Definition

A monitoring well is a constructed borehole specifically designed to allow repeated measurement of groundwater levels, collection of representative water quality samples, and evaluation of hydraulic properties (e.g., hydraulic conductivity, gradient) within a defined subsurface zone. Monitoring wells are typically installed in arrays at sites where potential contamination exists or where baseline hydrogeologic conditions must be established, such as at industrial facilities, landfills, brownfields, or transportation infrastructure stockpile areas (e.g., the Caltrans Modesto Stockpiles site). Unlike production wells, monitoring wells are not intended for water supply but rather for observational and regulatory compliance purposes.

### How It Works

A monitoring well functions as a discrete window into the aquifer through the following components and procedures:

- **Installation:** A borehole is drilled to the target depth using appropriate methods (e.g., hollow‑stem auger, sonic drilling). A casing (typically PVC or stainless steel) is lowered into the borehole. A well **screen** of predetermined length is placed across the hydrostratigraphic interval of interest. A filter pack (sand or gravel) is placed around the screen to prevent fine particles from entering the well. Above the screen, several feet of bentonite pellets or grout form a seal to prevent vertical flow along the annulus. The remaining annular space is grouted to the surface to isolate the monitored zone from overlying strata and surface water.

- **Development:** After installation, the well is developed by surging, pumping, or air‑lifting to remove fine sediment and drilling fluids, thereby restoring natural hydraulic conductivity around the screen.

- **Purging and Sampling:** Before sample collection, the well is purged by removing at least three to five casing volumes of water (or until field parameters – pH, specific conductance, turbidity, temperature – stabilize). This ensures that the sample represents in‑situ groundwater rather than stagnant water in the casing. Samples are collected using dedicated bailers, pumps (submersible, peristaltic, or inertial), or passive samplers, then analyzed for the required chemical parameters.

- **Water Level Measurement:** Static water levels are measured using an electric water level sounder or pressure transducer. These data are used to calculate groundwater flow direction and velocity.

- **Ongoing Monitoring:** Following initial baseline sampling, monitoring wells are sampled at defined frequencies (quarterly, semi‑annually, annually) to track changes in water quality and contaminant plume movement. Statistical evaluation of time‑series data (as in the Modesto Stockpiles statistical evaluation report) is used to determine attainment of cleanup standards or to detect degradation.

### Variants

- **Piezometer:** A narrow‑diameter well (e.g., 1‑2 inches) primarily used to measure groundwater pressure (head) but with limited capacity for water sampling. Often constructed with short screens and dedicated to water level measurement.

- **Multilevel Monitoring Well:** A single borehole with multiple screened intervals isolated by packers or separate casing strings, allowing discrete water level and quality measurements from several depths. Useful in investigating vertical concentration gradients.

- **Nested Wells:** Two or more wells at the same surface location drilled to different depths, each with its own casing and screen. Simpler than multilevel systems but require multiple boreholes.

- **Dedicated Sampling Wells:** Larger‑diameter wells (typically 2–4 inches) designed to accommodate submersible pumps for low‑flow sampling, often used at sites where repeated high‑quality samples are needed.

- **Temporary Wells:** Drive‑point wells or soil‑gas monitoring points installed for short‑term investigations and then removed after data collection.

- **Well Clusters:** Multiple wells installed at varying depths and lateral positions to define the three‑dimensional shape of a contaminant plume.

### Trade‑Offs

- **Cost vs. Data Quality:** High‑quality installation (proper filter pack, seal, development) increases cost but is essential for representative samples. Cheap installation may lead to turbid samples, channeling, or cross‑contamination.

- **Screen Length and Placement:** A longer screen may “dilute” contaminant concentrations by mixing water from different depths; a shorter screen provides better vertical resolution but may miss a thin plume zone.

- **Material Compatibility:** Stainless steel and PVC have different adsorption and leaching characteristics. The chosen material must be compatible with the analytes of concern (e.g., metals, VOCs).

- **Well Maintenance:** Over time, biofouling, sediment accumulation, or corrosion can degrade data quality. Wells must be re‑developed occasionally.

- **Disruption to Natural Flow:** Large‑diameter wells can act as preferential flow paths if not properly grouted, potentially short‑circuiting hydraulic gradients.

- **Destruction/Decommissioning:** At the end of their service life, monitoring wells must be properly destroyed (filled with grout or bentonite) to prevent future vertical movement of contaminants. Destruction reports (e.g., S2350-01-02 Well Destruction Report) document that these procedures were followed.
