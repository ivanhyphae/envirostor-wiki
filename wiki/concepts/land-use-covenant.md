## Definition

A **Land Use Covenant** is a legally binding private agreement that restricts, regulates, or prescribes the use of a parcel of land for the benefit of another parcel (the dominant tenement) or for a public purpose. It runs with the land, meaning future owners of the burdened property are bound by its terms, provided certain legal requirements are met. Land use covenants are a subset of real covenants and equitable servitudes, commonly used to enforce neighborhood standards, preserve natural resources, or ensure compliance with development approvals.

## How it works

Land use covenants are created by a deed, contract, or separate document recorded in the land records of the jurisdiction where the property is located. To be enforceable, a covenant typically requires:

1. **Intent**: The original parties must intend the covenant to bind successors in title.
2. **Notice**: The covenant must be recorded or otherwise discoverable by a reasonable purchaser.
3. **Horizontal privity** (for covenants at law): The covenant must arise out of a landlord-tenant relationship or a grantor-grantee relationship.
4. **Vertical privity**: Successors take the same interest as the original party.
5. **Touch and concern the land**: The covenant must affect the use, value, or enjoyment of the land, not merely personal obligations.
6. **Burden/Benefit**: The burdened land is subject to restriction; the benefited land receives the advantage.

Breach of a land use covenant is typically enforced through a lawsuit for injunction, damages, or specific performance. Some jurisdictions allow enforcement by a homeowners' association or a public body if the covenant is tied to a permit or subdivision approval.

## Variants

- **Negative Covenant (Restrictive Covenant)**: Prohibits specific uses (e.g., no commercial activity, no building over a certain height).
- **Affirmative Covenant**: Requires the landowner to perform an act (e.g., maintain a common area, pay maintenance fees).
- **Equitable Servitude**: Enforceable in equity even if legal requirements for a real covenant are not met, often requiring notice and intent.
- **Conservation Easement**: A type of land use covenant dedicating land to conservation purposes, typically held by a government agency or land trust.
- **Covenant Running with the Land at Law vs. Equity**: Legal covenants require privity and touch-and-concern; equitable servitudes are more flexible and enforceable against any subsequent purchaser with notice.

## Trade-offs

| Advantage | Limitation |
|---|---|
| Provides predictable use restrictions for neighbors and communities | Can become obsolete or overly burdensome as neighborhood character changes |
| May increase property values by maintaining standards | May limit property rights and reduce flexibility for owners |
| Enforceable through courts, offering clear remedies | Requires litigation for enforcement, which is costly and time-consuming |
| Can be tailored to specific local conditions | Recording and drafting errors can render a covenant unenforceable |
| Supports long-term planning (e.g., conservation, historic preservation) | May be challenged as an unreasonable restraint on alienation or public policy violations |
