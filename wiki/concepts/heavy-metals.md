## Definition

**Heavy metals** are a loosely defined group of metallic elements and metalloids with relatively high density (typically greater than 4–5 g/cm³) that exhibit potential toxicity or ecotoxicity at low concentrations. In environmental and regulatory contexts, the term commonly refers to elements such as arsenic (As), cadmium (Cd), chromium (Cr), copper (Cu), lead (Pb), mercury (Hg), nickel (Ni), and zinc (Zn). Their presence in soil, water, or construction materials is governed by stringent limits due to their persistence, bioaccumulation potential, and adverse health effects. In civil engineering and earthwork projects, heavy metals are a critical concern for classifying soils as clean fill or contaminated material, as demonstrated in borrow area sampling and clean fill source testing.

## How it works

Heavy metals occur naturally in the Earth's crust but are concentrated through anthropogenic activities such as mining, industrial discharge, and land application of wastes. They exist in various chemical forms (e.g., free ions, oxides, sulfides, or organometallic compounds), and their mobility, bioavailability, and toxicity depend on factors like pH, redox potential, organic matter content, and soil mineralogy.

In practice, heavy metal assessment follows a structured sampling and testing protocol:

1. **Sampling Plan Design**: A borrow area sampling plan (as seen in the "Borrow Area Sampling Plan.pdf" source) defines the number, location, and depth of soil samples needed to characterize the site. This includes statistical methods to ensure representative coverage.
2. **Laboratory Analysis**: Samples are digested (typically using acid extraction per EPA methods) and analyzed via techniques such as inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS) or atomic absorption spectroscopy. The "Hwy 132 Clean Fill Source Testing Exhibit.pdf" depicts a similar process for verifying that a source material meets clean fill criteria.
3. **Comparison to Thresholds**: Results are compared to regulatory action levels. In California, for example, the Department of Toxic Substances Control (DTSC) defines "clean fill" as material with heavy metal concentrations below specific screening levels (e.g., Title 22 California Code of Regulations). Exceedances require classification as hazardous or special waste, with implications for disposal and handling costs.
4. **Risk Assessment and Remediation**: If heavy metal levels exceed thresholds, engineering controls (e.g., capping, soil washing, stabilization) or removal are mandated to prevent groundwater contamination or direct human exposure.

## Variants

- **Essential vs. Non-Essential Heavy Metals**: Some heavy metals (e.g., copper, zinc, iron) are essential micronutrients at low doses but toxic at high concentrations; others (e.g., lead, cadmium, mercury) have no known biological function and are toxic even at trace levels.
- **Regulatory Classification**: Different jurisdictions define "heavy metals" and their thresholds variably. For example, the U.S. EPA defines "characteristic hazardous waste" under 40 CFR 261.24 based on Toxicity Characteristic Leaching Procedure (TCLP) concentrations, while California's DTSC uses Title 22 threshold limits.
- **Matrix Variants**: Testing considers the form of heavy metals (total concentration vs. leachable concentration). TCLP simulates landfill leaching conditions, while total metals analysis gives the full elemental content. Clean fill classification often uses total metals with comparison to background levels or site-specific risk-based criteria.
- **Analytical Variants**: X-ray fluorescence (XRF) provides field-screening, while ICP-MS offers lab-grade precision. Each has trade-offs in detection limits, cost, and turnaround time.

## Trade-offs

- **Cost vs. Accuracy**: Comprehensive heavy metal characterization with lab analysis (ICP-MS) is expensive and time-consuming, especially for large borrow areas or multiple clean fill sources. Field XRF is cheaper but may have higher detection limits and interferences. The sampling plan must balance cost with statistical confidence.
- **Quality vs. Quantity**: Over-sampling ensures robust data but increases project costs; under-sampling risks undetected hot spots and legal liability. The "Borrow Area Sampling Plan.pdf" illustrates the need for a statistically valid number of samples based on the area's heterogeneity.
- **Clean Fill Classification**: Using heavy metal results to classify material as "clean fill" reduces disposal costs but requires strict adherence to regulatory thresholds. If material is found to exceed limits, it may require special handling, adding significant project expense. The "Hwy 132 Clean Fill Source Testing Exhibit.pdf" shows the documentation process for demonstrating compliance.
- **Environmental vs. Human Health**: Heavy metal risk assessment often prioritizes human health (e.g., soil ingestion, dust inhalation) over ecological endpoints, which may require more stringent thresholds. Soils with low human exposure risk (e.g., deep fills) may still pose groundwater threats.
- **Temporal and Spatial Variability**: Heavy metal concentrations can vary significantly across a site and over time (due to erosion or chemical changes), necessitating periodic re-testing for long-term projects.
