## Title 22

**Title 22** refers to the California Code of Regulations, Title 22, Division 4, which sets water quality and public health standards for recycled water use, including groundwater recharge and monitoring. In the context of the source report (“Modesto Stockpiles GW April 2017”), it specifically pertains to groundwater quality evaluation under Title 22 criteria for potential reuse or recharge projects.

### Definition
Title 22 establishes the regulatory framework for the safe use of recycled water in California, administered by the State Water Resources Control Board (SWRCB) and the Department of Public Health (CDPH). It defines treatment levels, constituent concentration limits, monitoring frequencies, and operational requirements for groundwater recharge via surface spreading or direct injection. The regulations apply to projects that use treated municipal wastewater to augment groundwater supplies, including those involving stockpiled materials (as in the Modesto site).

### How it works
The regulatory process under Title 22 requires:

- **Water quality criteria**: Maximum contaminant levels (MCLs) for constituents such as total coliform, nitrogen, dissolved solids, heavy metals, and trace organic compounds (e.g., pharmaceuticals, endocrine disruptors).
- **Treatment requirements**: Recycled water must undergo advanced treatment (e.g., microfiltration, reverse osmosis, advanced oxidation) before groundwater recharge.
- **Monitoring wells**: A network of groundwater monitoring wells (as seen in the Modesto report, e.g., MW-1 through MW-7) is used to sample water quality before and after recharge. Stiff diagrams and other hydrochemical analyses are used to assess plume movement and aquifer impact.
- **Compliance**: If groundwater quality meets Title 22 standards, the site may be approved for recharge or release. Exceedances require corrective actions or alternative disposal.

### Variants
- **Title 22 – Groundwater Recharge Regulations**: Detailed in sections 60320–60320.2, covering spreading basins (surface) and injection wells (subsurface). Includes pathogen reduction requirements and operational monitoring.
- **Title 22 – Direct Potable Reuse (DPR)**: Advanced treatment for direct drinking water supply (still under development in some areas, e.g., California’s DPR regulations are separate but related).
- **Title 22 – Recycled Water Use for Irrigation**: Standards for non‑potable uses (e.g., landscape, agriculture) with lower treatment levels than recharge.

### Trade-offs
- **Cost vs. Safety**: Meeting Title 22 standards requires expensive treatment trains (e.g., RO + UV/AOP). Lower treatment reduces capital cost but may not achieve regulatory approval.
- **Public perception**: Stringent standards increase trust but can delay projects due to high monitoring and reporting burdens.
- **Site‑specific variability**: Contaminant levels in stockpiled materials or ambient groundwater may exceed Title 22 limits, requiring additional remediation or alternative disposal (e.g., discharge to surface water).
