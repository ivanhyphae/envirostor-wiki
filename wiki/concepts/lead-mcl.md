## Overview

**Lead Mcl** (Maximum Contaminant Level) refers to the enforceable regulatory standard for lead in drinking water, established by the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency under the Safe Drinking Water Act. For the Caltrans Modesto Soil Stockpiles remediation project (SR 132, Stanislaus County, CA), the Lead MCL of 15 µg/L (parts per billion) is a key threshold used to assess surface water runoff quality from the stockpile areas. The project involves three soil stockpiles containing elevated metals, and surface water sampling is conducted to determine if runoff poses a risk of exceeding the MCL, triggering regulatory reporting or remedial action.

## Site Record

- **Documented Sampling Event**: Surface water samples were collected on March 22, 2018, in accordance with the *Final Surface Water Sampling and Analysis Plan (SAP)* (Shaw Environmental, Inc., January 2006) and the *Addendum to Surface Water Sampling and Analysis Plan* (February 20, 2013), as reported in the *Surface Water Sampling Report – March 22, 2018* (Project No. S1200-01-01, April 24, 2018).  
- **Sampling Locations**: Surface water samples were collected from locations near Stockpiles 1, 2, and 3, as depicted on the Site Plan, Figure 2 of the report.  
- **Lead Concentration Data**: The source documents do not provide tabulated lead concentrations from the March 2018 sampling event. However, a related technical memorandum (*SR 132 Stockpile 3 BCS Removal Tech Memo*) discusses removal of Stockpile 3, which was characterized by elevated metals including lead. No specific lead concentrations (in mg/kg or µg/L) are reported in the sources provided.  
- **Threshold Reference**: The Lead MCL of 15 µg/L is the applicable drinking water standard, but neither source explicitly states that this was used as a comparison value for surface water. Surface water is not drinking water; MCLs are often used as conservative screening benchmarks in absence of site-specific surface water quality objectives.

- **Compliance**: No exceedances of the Lead MCL were reported in the provided sources. The March 2018 sampling report indicates that surface water results were submitted for review, but no conclusions regarding MCL exceedances are included in the excerpts.
