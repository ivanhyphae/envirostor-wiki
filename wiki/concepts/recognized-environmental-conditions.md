## Definition

**Recognized Environmental Conditions (RECs)** is a term defined by the American Society for Testing and Materials (ASTM) Standard E1527, the industry-standard practice for Phase I Environmental Site Assessments (ESAs). A REC refers to the presence or likely presence of any hazardous substances or petroleum products on a property under conditions that indicate an existing release, a past release, or a material threat of a release into structures on the property or into the ground, groundwater, or surface water of the property. The term is central to identifying environmental liabilities in commercial real estate transactions and is used to inform buyers, lenders, and regulatory agencies about potential contamination risks that may require further investigation or remediation.

## How it works

RECs are identified during a Phase I ESA, a due diligence process that evaluates a property’s historical and current uses to assess the likelihood of contamination. The process follows ASTM E1527-21 (or the applicable version) and involves four key components:

1. **Records Review**: Examination of federal, state, and local regulatory databases (e.g., EPA CERCLIS, state leaking underground storage tank lists) to identify reported spills, releases, or cleanup activities on or near the property.
2. **Site Reconnaissance**: A visual inspection of the property and adjacent areas to observe signs of potential contamination, such as stained soil, distressed vegetation, improper waste storage, or abandoned drums.
3. **Historical Research**Review of historical aerial photographs, fire insurance maps, city directories, and land use records to identify past activities (e.g., industrial operations, gas stations, dry cleaners) that may have involved hazardous substances.
4. **Interviews**: Conversations with current and former property owners, occupants, and local government officials to gather anecdotal evidence of past spills or operations.

If any of these steps reveal evidence or a reasonable suspicion of a release or material threat of a release, the environmental professional (EP) conducting the assessment designates the finding as a REC. The finding must be documented in the Phase I report, along with a rationale and, if necessary, a recommendation for further investigation (e.g., Phase II ESA involving soil or groundwater sampling).

Importantly, the ASTM standard distinguishes RECs from other conditions:
- **Controlled Recognized Environmental Conditions (CRECs)**: A past release that has been addressed to the satisfaction of the regulatory authority but still requires ongoing controls (e.g., engineering barriers, deed restrictions) to prevent exposure.
- **Historical Recognized Environmental Conditions (HRECs)**: A past release that has been remediated to unrestricted use standards, with no remaining contamination or legal controls required.
- **De Minimis Conditions**: Minor, localized releases that do not pose a material risk (e.g., small oil stains from a leaking vehicle), which are excluded from REC classification.

## Variants

- **ASTM E1527-13 vs. E1527-21**: The 2021 revision updated definitions to clarify HRECs and CRECs, emphasizing that CRECs include conditions where residual contamination remains but is managed (e.g., an active groundwater monitoring plan), whereas HRECs require that no further action has been approved with unrestricted use.
- **All Appropriate Inquiries (AAI)**: The U.S. EPA’s rule under the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act (CERCLA) incorporates ASTM E1527 standards but also mandates specific requirements for landowner liability protections, such as continuous property ownership and no prior knowledge of contamination.
- **International Variants**: Non-U.S. jurisdictions (e.g., Canada’s CSA Z768, UK’s BS EN 16030) have similar concepts but may use different terminology, such as “Potential Contamination” or “Contamination Risk,” and may not align perfectly with ASTM’s REC framework.

## Trade-offs

- **Subjectivity**: The determination of a REC relies heavily on the professional judgment of the environmental professional, leading to variability between assessors. Two EPs may disagree on whether a past spill constitutes a REC versus a de minimis condition, affecting transaction costs and risk perception.
- **Cost vs. Certainty**: A Phase I ESA is relatively inexpensive ($2,000–$5,000 for a typical property) but only identifies *potential* risks; it does not confirm contamination. False positives (misidentifying a REC) can trigger unnecessary Phase II investigations, while false negatives can lead to undiscovered liability.
- **Regulatory vs. Transactional Context**: RECs are defined for real estate transactions, not regulatory enforcement. A property may have no RECs but still have undetected contamination that regulators later discover, because Phase I ESAs are limited to visible evidence and database records.
- **Temporal Limitations**: RECs are based on current knowledge and historical records; they do not protect against future releases or newly discovered contamination from past activities that were not documented. The ASTM standard explicitly states that an ESA is not a guarantee of no contamination.
- **Liability Protection**: Complying with AAI requirements (using ASTM E1527) can shield buyers from CERCLA liability for pre-existing contamination, but only if no further releases occur and the buyer exercises appropriate care. A REC finding often leads to renegotiation of property price or contract terms.
