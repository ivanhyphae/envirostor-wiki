## Definition

A **health advisory** is a non‑regulatory, non‑enforceable guideline issued by a public health authority (e.g., the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency) that provides information on the concentration of a contaminant in drinking water at which adverse health effects are not anticipated over a specified exposure period. Health advisories are intended to assist water utilities, regulators, and consumers in making risk‑management decisions when no legally enforceable standard (such as a Maximum Contaminant Level, or MCL) exists or when new health information warrants interim guidance.

## How it works

Health advisories are derived from toxicological and epidemiological data, typically using a risk‑assessment framework similar to that used for enforceable drinking‑water standards. The process includes:

1. **Hazard identification** – determining the contaminant’s potential to cause adverse effects.  
2. **Dose‑response assessment** – establishing the relationship between exposure level and health effect (often using experimental animal data with uncertainty factors).  
3. **Exposure assessment** – estimating typical drinking‑water intake (e.g., 2 liters per day for adults, 1 liter for a 10‑kg child).  
4. **Risk characterization** – calculating a reference dose (RfD) or concentration that yields a negligible risk over the exposure duration.

For non‑carcinogenic effects, the advisory level is often computed as:  
\[
\text{Advisory Level} = \frac{\text{RfD} \times \text{body weight}}{\text{drinking water intake}} \times \text{relative source contribution}
\]  
For carcinogens, a target cancer risk (e.g., 10⁻⁶) is used.

Health advisories are classified by duration:

- **One‑day** – for short‑term accidental releases or spikes.  
- **Ten‑day** – for relatively brief exposures during remediation.  
- **Lifetime** – for long‑term exposure, typically over a 70‑year lifespan.  

These values are published by the EPA and updated as new studies emerge. In the context of the provided sources (e.g., *S9525‑01‑44B Modesto Stockpiles GW June 2013.0613.pdf* and subsequent reports), health advisories would have been used to evaluate groundwater contaminants detected at the Modesto Stockpiles site, comparing measured concentrations to the advisory levels to inform monitoring and potential remediation priorities.

## Variants

Several types of health advisories exist:

- **EPA Drinking Water Health Advisories** – the most widely referenced in the U.S.; includes both individual contaminant advisories and “health advisory levels” for unregulated contaminants (e.g., perchlorate, PFOA/PFOS).  
- **WHO Guidelines for Drinking‑water Quality** – analogous but international; some national authorities adopt or reference these.  
- **State‑specific advisories** – e.g., California’s Public Health Goals (PHGs) or notification levels, which may be more stringent than federal guidelines.  
- **Short‑term vs. lifetime** – duration‑specific numbers as described above.  
- **Cancer vs. non‑cancer** – two distinct derivation pathways, often resulting in different advisory values for the same contaminant.

## Trade‑offs

- **Non‑enforceability** – health advisories are not legally binding; water systems are not required to meet them, which can lead to inconsistent protection across communities.  
- **Precautionary vs. economic impact** – low advisory levels (e.g., for PFAS) reduce health risk but can trigger costly treatment or well closures, raising equity concerns.  
- **Scientific uncertainty** – advisories rely on incomplete data; revisions (e.g., lowering a lifetime advisory based on new toxicity evidence) may cause public confusion and loss of trust.  
- **Temporal lag** – advisories may take years to update, leaving a gap between emerging science and public health guidance.  
- **Communication challenges** – the public and even some water professionals may mistake an advisory for a legal limit, leading to either unnecessary alarm or unwarranted complacency.
