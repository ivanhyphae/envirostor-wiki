## Definition

**Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbons (PAHs)** are a class of organic compounds consisting of two or more fused aromatic rings. They are formed primarily during the incomplete combustion of organic materials such as coal, oil, gas, wood, and waste. PAHs are ubiquitous environmental contaminants and are often found in soil, sediment, and groundwater at industrial sites, former fuel storage areas, and transportation corridors.

In the context of the Modesto soil stockpile remediation efforts referenced in the source documents, PAHs are a key contaminant of concern (COC) requiring characterization, removal, and/or capping to protect human health and the environment.

## How It Works

PAHs are semi-volatile, hydrophobic, and tend to sorb strongly to soil organic matter. Their behavior in the environment depends on molecular weight and structure:

| Property | Low molecular weight (2–3 rings) | High molecular weight (4–6 rings) |
|----------|----------------------------------|----------------------------------|
| Volatility | Higher (semi-volatile) | Low (non-volatile) |
| Water solubility | Low (mg/L range) | Very low (μg/L range) |
| Sorption to soil | Moderate | Strong |
| Bioavailability | Moderate | Low |
| Persistence | Weeks to months | Years to decades |

PAHs migrate primarily via particulate transport (dust, erosion) or dissolution in water with co‑contaminants (e.g., non‑aqueous phase liquids). In soil stockpiles, PAHs remain largely immobile if undisturbed, but can become a risk through inhalation of airborne dust or leaching into groundwater over long periods.

Remediation approaches for PAH-contaminated soils include:
- **Soil removal and off‑site disposal** (e.g., removal action plans)
- **Capping with impermeable layers** to prevent contact and migration
- **Bioremediation** (microbial degradation, often slow for high‑MW PAHs)
- **Thermal treatment** or **chemical oxidation** (effective but costly)

The Modesto stockpile documents describe interim and ultimate containment strategies, including capping and pavement, to control PAH exposure.

## Variants

PAHs include more than 100 individual compounds. Common variants of regulatory and toxicological significance include:

- **Naphthalene** (2 rings) – most volatile, used as a surrogate for lighter PAHs.
- **Acenaphthene, Fluorene, Phenanthrene, Anthracene** (3 rings)
- **Pyrene, Benzo[a]anthracene, Chrysene** (4 rings)
- **Benzo[a]pyrene** (5 rings) – highly carcinogenic, often the benchmark for risk assessment.
- **Dibenz[a,h]anthracene, Indeno[1,2,3-cd]pyrene** (5–6 rings)

These are often grouped as **Total PAHs** (sum of a priority list, e.g., 16 EPA PAHs) for regulatory purposes.

## Trade-offs

- **Persistence vs. Remediation Feasibility**: High‑MW PAHs are very persistent, making complete removal difficult; capping may transfer risk rather than eliminate it.
- **Cost vs. Effectiveness**: Removal and off‑site disposal (typical for stockpiles) is expensive but provides immediate risk reduction; in‑situ methods are cheaper but slower and often leave residual contamination.
- **Health Risk Sensitivity**: Benzo[a]pyrene and other carcinogenic PAHs drive risk calculations; non‑carcinogenic PAHs may be overlooked but still pose ecological threats.
- **Analytical Challenges**: PAH quantification requires careful sampling (avoid volatilization loss) and isomer‑specific analysis; results can vary significantly between laboratories.
