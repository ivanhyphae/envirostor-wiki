## Definition

**Shallow Groundwater Flow To The Southeast** is a hydraulic flow pattern in which the near-surface groundwater table or shallow subsurface water moves generally toward the southeast. In hydrogeologic terms, this indicates that the local hydraulic gradient declines in the southeast direction, so groundwater in the shallow aquifer tends to migrate that way under gravity and pressure differences.

This concept is usually interpreted from field observations such as groundwater-level measurements in monitoring wells, contour maps of potentiometric or water-table elevations, or inferred flow-direction arrows on site hydrogeology figures.

## How it works

Shallow groundwater flow is driven by differences in hydraulic head. Water in the shallow subsurface moves from areas of higher head to lower head, approximately perpendicular to contours of equal water level.

When the regional or site-specific head distribution slopes downward toward the southeast:

1. **Recharge or higher-head zones** exist upgradient, likely to the northwest or west/northwest.
2. **Lower-head zones** lie downgradient to the southeast.
3. Groundwater in the shallow zone follows this gradient, producing a net southeastward flow direction.

The flow direction is controlled by several factors:

- **Aquifer properties**: Hydraulic conductivity, porosity, and saturation influence how easily water moves.
- **Hydraulic gradient**: The steeper the gradient, the stronger the flow tendency.
- **Vertical confinement**: In a shallow unconfined setting, the water table often responds quickly to recharge, pumping, and seasonal change.
- **Local site conditions**: Ditches, drainage features, pumping wells, surface-water bodies, or low-permeability layers can bend or redirect flow.

In practice, “shallow groundwater flow to the southeast” is often a site-specific interpretation rather than a universal regional rule. It describes the observed or modeled direction of groundwater movement in the upper portion of the subsurface, not necessarily deeper aquifer systems.

## Variants

Common variants and related interpretations include:

- **Seasonal variation**: The shallow flow direction may remain generally southeast but shift slightly with recharge, irrigation, or pumping changes.
- **Localized deflection**: Flow may still trend southeast overall while bending around buried utilities, stockpiles, excavation pits, or low-permeability zones.
- **Perched shallow flow**: A shallow perched water layer can flow southeast independently of the deeper water table.
- **Multi-layer flow systems**: The shallow system may flow southeast while deeper groundwater flows in another direction.
- **Site-model representation**: In reports, this may appear as a mapped arrow, a potentiometric surface interpretation, or a groundwater contour trend.

## Trade-offs

Key considerations and limitations include:

- **Direction may be approximate**: “To the southeast” often summarizes a generalized trend, not a perfectly straight path.
- **Temporal variability**: Shallow groundwater directions can change after storms, irrigation events, or pumping cycles.
- **Scale dependence**: A site-wide southeast trend can coexist with small-scale local reversals.
- **Data density matters**: Sparse monitoring wells can produce uncertain flow-direction interpretations.
- **Shallow systems are sensitive**: Because they respond quickly to surface conditions, conclusions may be less stable than for deeper, confined aquifers.

For site assessment, it is important to distinguish between:
- **observed flow direction**, based on measurements, and
- **inferred flow direction**, based on limited contours or conceptual models.
