## Definition

**Background Based Clean Fill Evaluation** is a method for determining whether a proposed soil or borrow source can be used as **clean fill** by comparing its chemistry to **background concentrations** rather than only to generic regulatory screening levels. The approach is used when the source material is soil, native borrow, or stockpiled soil that may contain naturally occurring constituents typical of the regional geologic background.

In practice, the evaluation asks: **Is the material consistent with local background soil quality, and therefore suitable for beneficial reuse as clean fill, without indicating anthropogenic contamination?**

This concept appears in Caltrans-related soil source and clean fill documentation for SR 132 / Basin 5, where borrow areas, stockpiles, and source testing were reviewed for clean fill suitability using sampling results, background comparisons, and project-specific acceptance criteria.

## How it works

Background-based evaluation typically follows these steps:

1. **Define the proposed source area**
   - Identify the borrow pit, excavation, stockpile, or other source area intended for reuse.
   - Document land use, site history, and whether the material is native soil, imported material, or previously stockpiled soil.

2. **Establish a background benchmark**
   - Use one or more of the following:
     - regional background soil data,
     - site-specific native soil samples from unaffected areas,
     - published geologic background values,
     - project-specific background datasets collected under a sampling plan.
   - Background values are intended to represent naturally occurring concentrations in the local area, accounting for geologic variation.

3. **Collect representative samples**
   - Sampling is designed to characterize the material to be reused.
   - Samples are often collected from different depths and subareas to capture variability.
   - A sampling plan may specify number of increments, composites, analytical suites, and QA/QC requirements.

4. **Analyze for constituents of concern**
   - Common analyses include metals, pH, moisture, and other constituents relevant to soil reuse.
   - The analyte list is usually tied to the project risk profile and regulatory requirements.

5. **Compare results to background and acceptance criteria**
   - Results are compared against:
     - local background concentrations,
     - project action levels,
     - regulatory thresholds for clean fill or soil reuse.
   - The key decision point is whether detections are consistent with background and not indicative of a release or contamination source.

6. **Evaluate lines of evidence**
   - Chemical data are interpreted together with:
     - site history,
     - field observations,
     - soil type and geology,
     - absence of impacted land use indicators,
     - consistency across samples.
   - A source may be accepted as clean fill even if some constituents are detected, provided the values are within expected background ranges and meet reuse criteria.

7. **Document the determination**
   - The conclusion is typically recorded in a technical memo, source testing exhibit, or approval email.
   - Documentation states whether the source is acceptable for use as clean fill and any handling, segregation, or placement restrictions.

### Why “background based” matters

A simple pass/fail comparison to a fixed universal threshold can be misleading for soil. Many naturally occurring constituents vary substantially by geology. A background-based method recognizes that some soils legitimately contain higher concentrations of certain metals or other analytes without being contaminated. This is especially important when evaluating native borrow or stockpiled soil from roadway projects.

## Variants

### Site-specific background evaluation
Uses samples from the project area or nearby uncontaminated reference soils as the comparison basis. This is the most defensible approach when local geology is variable.

### Regional or geologic background comparison
Compares results to published background datasets for the broader geologic unit or region. This is useful when site-specific background data are limited.

### Project acceptance-based clean fill evaluation
Instead of a strict background test, some projects use a combination of:
- background comparisons,
- regulatory clean-fill criteria,
- and agency-approved acceptance limits.

### Stockpile re-evaluation
Material that was previously excavated and stockpiled may be retested before reuse. This is common when documentation of the original source is incomplete or when the stockpile has been stored for some time.

### Borrow area technical memo approach
A borrow source is evaluated in a formal memorandum summarizing site history, sampling results, and reuse suitability. This is common for transportation projects needing large soil volumes.

### Alternatives
- **Generic screening-level evaluation**: simpler, but may over-reject naturally elevated soils.
- **Risk-based soil reuse evaluation**: focuses on exposure pathways and end use rather than background alone.
- **Hazardous waste characterization**: a different purpose entirely; used when waste disposal classification is needed rather than clean-fill suitability.

## Trade-offs

### Advantages
- Better reflects natural geochemical variability.
- Reduces unnecessary disposal of usable soil.
- Supports beneficial reuse and project cost savings.
- More defensible for native soils and geologically unusual materials.

### Limitations
- Requires good-quality background data.
- Background values can vary by location, depth, and soil unit.
- Sampling error can cause misclassification if the source is heterogeneous.
- Does not automatically address all reuse concerns, such as physical debris, organics, or non-chemical impacts.
- Regulatory acceptance may depend on agency-specific criteria and documentation quality.

### Important considerations
- **Representativeness**: Samples must reflect the full source area.
- **Comparability**: Background samples should be collected and analyzed using similar methods.
- **Multiple lines of evidence**: Chemistry alone may be insufficient if site history suggests contamination.
- **End use**: Material acceptable as clean fill for one project may not be suitable for another with more restrictive use conditions.
- **Stockpile mixing**: Mixed materials can obscure background signatures and complicate interpretation.
