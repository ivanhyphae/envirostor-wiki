## Definition

**Decommissioning monitoring wells** is the process of permanently taking a groundwater or environmental monitoring well out of service and sealing it so it no longer provides a pathway between the land surface and subsurface aquifers. The goal is to eliminate a potential conduit for cross-contamination, protect groundwater quality, and remove obsolete infrastructure safely and in compliance with applicable regulations.

A decommissioned well is typically filled and sealed with appropriate materials, often after any required sampling, inspection, or documentation has been completed.

## How it works

Decommissioning usually follows a formal procedure:

1. **Assessment and planning**
   - Confirm the well is no longer needed for monitoring, compliance, or remediation.
   - Review construction details such as depth, diameter, casing type, screen interval, and known geologic units.
   - Identify any contamination concerns that affect sealing materials or methods.

2. **Site preparation**
   - Inspect the well for accessibility and structural condition.
   - Remove surface appurtenances such as locks, caps, protective casings, and vault components.
   - In some cases, develop the well or remove accumulated sediment if needed to facilitate sealing.

3. **Selection of abandonment method**
   - Choose sealing materials and placement methods suited to the well construction and hydrogeology.
   - Common sealing materials include bentonite, cement grout, neat cement, or combinations thereof.
   - The seal must block vertical movement of water and contaminants.

4. **Placement of sealing materials**
   - The well is filled from the bottom upward or by tremie pipe/grouting methods to avoid voids.
   - Screened intervals and any permeable zones are sealed carefully.
   - If multiple aquifers or confining layers are present, seals may be placed to isolate them.

5. **Surface completion**
   - The upper portion is sealed and the surface restoration is completed.
   - A visible marker or record may be retained depending on local requirements.

6. **Documentation and reporting**
   - Record the final well status, materials used, depths sealed, and date of abandonment.
   - Submit required notifications to regulators or owners if applicable.

The technical objective is not just to “fill” the hole, but to create a long-term hydraulic barrier that prevents migration of groundwater between strata and prevents surface infiltration.

## Variants

- **Temporary decommissioning / suspension**
  - The well is taken out of service but not permanently sealed. This is not true decommissioning, but sometimes wells are mothballed pending future use.

- **Partial decommissioning**
  - Only a section of the well is sealed, usually when part of the casing is removed or when a well is modified. This may occur during redevelopment or retrofit, but permanent abandonment still requires complete isolation.

- **Full abandonment with cut-and-cap**
  - The casing is cut below grade and sealed, often used when the well is shallow or easy to access.

- **Grout abandonment**
  - Cementitious grout is pumped into the full depth of the borehole or into intervals, common for permanent closure.

- **Bentonite plug systems**
  - Granular bentonite, bentonite chips, or pellets are used as part of the sealing sequence, sometimes alternating with grout or sand where appropriate.

- **Well cluster or network decommissioning**
  - Multiple wells at a site are closed as a group, often after a monitoring network is redesigned or a remediation program ends.

- **Regulatory closure vs. physical abandonment**
  - Physical decommissioning is the field activity; regulatory closure includes approvals, notifications, and record updates.

## Trade-offs

- **Permanent loss of monitoring capacity**
  - Once sealed, the well cannot be reused without drilling a new one.

- **Cost and labor**
  - Proper abandonment can be expensive, especially for deep wells, inaccessible sites, or wells with complex construction.

- **Hydrogeologic complexity**
  - Wells that intersect multiple aquifers or fractures require careful sealing to avoid creating a contamination pathway during abandonment.

- **Material compatibility**
  - Sealing materials must match site conditions; improper choice can lead to shrinkage, cracking, or incomplete seals.

- **Regulatory compliance**
  - Requirements vary by jurisdiction, and failure to follow approved procedures can create legal and environmental liability.

- **Long-term environmental protection**
  - Although costly and sometimes disruptive, proper decommissioning reduces the risk of future contamination and improves site safety.
