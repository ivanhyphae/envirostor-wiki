## Definition

**Stockpile 3** is a designated soil stockpile identified within a Remedial Design Implementation Plan (RDIP) for a contaminated site. It is subject to specific management procedures including confirmation sampling, grading, and eventual off-site transport or on-site treatment. The designation appears in a technical memo regarding *Confirmation Sampling* (approval document) and in a set of RDIP figures that include grading plans and generalized cross-sections for multiple stockpiles (e.g., Stockpile 3 Grading Plan, Figures 3-1 through 3-5). The site is located near Emerald Avenue, with an additional “Emerald Ave OC” (likely organic compound) stockpile referenced in the cross-section figures.

Stockpile 3 is one of several numbered stockpiles (1 through 5) that form part of a larger remediation program, where contaminated soil is excavated, temporarily stockpiled, characterized, and then either treated or removed to meet cleanup standards.

## How it works

The management of Stockpile 3 follows a systematic process:

1. **Excavation and Stockpiling**: Contaminated soil is excavated and placed in designated stockpile areas according to the RDIP grading plans (e.g., Figure 3-3 *Stockpile Grading Plan 3*). These plans specify the physical dimensions, slopes, and containment measures (e.g., liners, covers) to prevent migration of contaminants.

2. **Confirmation Sampling**: After stockpile formation, a confirmation sampling program is implemented. The technical memo “Stockpile 3 Confirmation Sampling Tech Memo Approval” (source document) indicates that a formal approval process governs the sampling strategy—likely including:
   - Number and location of samples (e.g., grid or depth‑based sampling).
   - Analytical parameters (target contaminants).
   - Acceptance criteria (concentrations below action levels).
   - QA/QC procedures.

3. **Data Evaluation**: Analytical results are compared to cleanup goals. If exceedances occur, the stockpile may require additional sampling, blending, or treatment.

4. **Transportation and Disposal**: The RDIP includes a *Soil Transportation Plan* (Figure 5) that outlines how soil from Stockpile 3 (and others) is to be moved—either to an off‑site disposal facility or to an on‑site treatment area. The *Generalized Cross‑Section* (Figure 4B) for the Emerald Ave OC stockpile suggests that different contaminant types may be handled separately.

5. **Final Closure**: Once approved, the stockpile area can be graded and closed per the RDIP, potentially including final cap installation or revegetation.

## Variants

Stockpile 3 is explicitly part of a suite of stockpiles at the same site. The RDIP figures identify:

- **Stockpile 1, 2, 3, 4, 5** – each with its own grading plan (Figures 3‑1 through 3‑5). These likely differ in size, contaminant profile, and proximity to sensitive receptors.
- **Emerald Ave OC Stockpile** – a separate stockpile for organic compounds (PCBs, VOCs, etc.), shown in a generalized cross-section (Figure 4B). This indicates that Stockpile 3 may be dedicated to different contaminants (e.g., metals, inorganics).
- **Temporary vs. permanent stockpiles** – some may be interim storage before treatment, while others may be capped in place.

Variants can also be defined by regulatory status (e.g., RCRA vs. non‑RCRA) and by physical configuration (e.g., under canopy, lined, bermed). 

## Trade-offs

- **Sampling density vs. cost**: Adequate confirmation sampling provides statistical confidence that contaminants have been removed or treated, but each additional sample increases time and analytical costs. The tech memo approval likely balances these factors.
- **Timing**: Confirmation sampling and re‑analysis can delay site closure. Stockpile 3’s schedule must be coordinated with other stockpiles and overall remediation milestones.
- **Environmental risk**: A stockpile with incomplete characterization may leave residual contamination in place, posing long‑term risk. Conversely, overly conservative sampling may unnecessarily increase duration and expense.
- **Containment vs. mobilization**: Stockpile 3 may be protected by liners and covers to prevent leaching, but these measures increase cost and must be maintained until final disposition.
- **Transportation logistics**: The Soil Transportation Plan must account for the volume and type of material in Stockpile 3, potentially affecting haul routes, disposal facility capacity, and emissions.
