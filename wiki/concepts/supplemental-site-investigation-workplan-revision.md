## Definition

A **Supplemental Site Investigation Workplan Revision** is a formal update to an already approved or submitted Site Investigation Workplan used to add, refine, or correct investigation activities after new information becomes available. It is typically prepared when prior review comments, field conditions, data gaps, or regulatory feedback indicate that the original workplan needs modification before additional site investigation work proceeds.

In practice, the revision document explains:
- what parts of the original workplan are changing,
- why the changes are needed,
- how the revised tasks will be implemented, and
- whether the changes affect sampling locations, analytical methods, field procedures, reporting, or schedules.

## How it works

A supplemental revision is usually developed during an active regulatory review or after initial field mobilization when the project team identifies a need to adjust the investigation strategy. Common triggers include:
- agency comments on the original workplan,
- unexpected site access limitations,
- field-observed conditions that differ from assumptions,
- discovery of additional areas of concern,
- refinement of conceptual site model understanding, or
- requests to clarify methods, quality assurance, or data handling.

The revision process generally follows these steps:

1. **Review of comments or new information**  
   The project team evaluates regulator comments, prior investigation results, and any site-specific changes.

2. **Identification of required modifications**  
   The team determines which sections of the workplan need revision, such as:
   - investigation objectives,
   - boring or sampling locations,
   - sample depths,
   - target analytes,
   - field screening methods,
   - waste handling procedures,
   - health and safety provisions, or
   - reporting deliverables.

3. **Preparation of the supplemental revision**  
   The revised document usually references the original workplan and clearly distinguishes new text from existing content. It may include:
   - response-to-comments tables,
   - marked revisions or redline text,
   - updated figures or tables,
   - revised schedules, and
   - justifications for each change.

4. **Regulatory submission and approval**  
   The revision is submitted to the overseeing agency for review. Work may be paused or limited until the revision is accepted, especially if the changes affect the scope or method of investigation.

5. **Implementation in the field**  
   Once approved, the revised procedures become the operative instructions for the supplemental site investigation.

This concept is closely tied to Site Investigation, Workplan, Regulatory Review, and Response to Comments workflows. In environmental projects, the revision is often used to maintain alignment between field activities and regulatory expectations without having to replace the entire original workplan.

## Variants

Common variants or related document types include:

- **Amended workplan**  
  A broader rewrite of the original site investigation plan, often used when substantial changes are needed.

- **Addendum**  
  A short supplemental document that adds limited new information or procedures without fully revising the base workplan.

- **Response to comments with embedded revisions**  
  A submission that answers agency comments and incorporates revised text in the same package.

- **Field change memorandum**  
  A less formal record of minor field modifications made during implementation.

- **Supplemental investigation plan**  
  A separate plan for additional work not fully covered by the original scope.

- **Iterative revision under agency review**  
  Multiple rounds of updates may occur if the regulator requests further clarification or scope changes.

## Trade-offs

Key considerations when using a supplemental revision include:

- **Precision vs. delay**  
  Revising the workplan improves technical accuracy, but it can delay field work while the agency reviews the changes.

- **Flexibility vs. procedural burden**  
  Supplemental revisions allow the investigation to adapt to real site conditions, but they add documentation and approval steps.

- **Clarity vs. document complexity**  
  A good revision makes changes explicit, but repeated revisions can make it harder to track the controlling scope.

- **Regulatory alignment vs. operational efficiency**  
  Formal revisions help ensure compliance and defensibility, though they may be less efficient than making minor field adjustments informally.

- **Scope control vs. discovery-driven investigation**  
  Supplemental revisions are especially useful in phased investigations where new findings require expanded sampling or altered methods, but frequent revisions can indicate an underdeveloped initial conceptual model.
