## Definition

**Natural Background Concentrations (NBCs)** are the levels of a chemical substance that occur in environmental media due to naturally occurring geologic, hydrologic, or biological processes, rather than human activity. In contaminated site and groundwater contexts, NBCs are used to distinguish naturally present constituents from site-related contamination when evaluating data, setting cleanup goals, or defining the extent of an impact.

NBCs can apply to groundwater, soil, sediment, surface water, and sometimes air, but they are most commonly discussed in relation to groundwater constituents such as arsenic, iron, manganese, sulfate, chloride, uranium, and other elements that may be present because of local geology or geochemical conditions.

## How it works

Determining natural background concentrations is usually a site-specific process.

1. **Identify the relevant medium and constituents**  
   The investigator selects the environmental medium of concern, such as groundwater in a borrow area or surrounding aquifer, and identifies constituents that may be naturally occurring or potentially site-related.

2. **Establish a background dataset**  
   Samples are collected from locations believed to be unaffected by the source area. These may include:
   - upgradient wells,
   - nearby reference wells,
   - deeper or shallow intervals outside the influence of the site,
   - regional or historical datasets if they are demonstrably representative.

3. **Screen for representativeness**  
   Background points are evaluated to confirm they reflect natural conditions and are not influenced by:
   - nearby releases,
   - legacy contamination,
   - anthropogenic recharge,
   - water treatment discharges,
   - roadway runoff,
   - agricultural inputs.

4. **Characterize variability**  
   Natural systems are variable. Concentrations may vary with:
   - lithology,
   - redox conditions,
   - pH,
   - seasonal recharge,
   - depth,
   - well construction,
   - geochemical mixing.

   Because of this, NBCs are not usually a single fixed number derived from one sample. They are commonly summarized using statistics such as:
   - range,
   - mean,
   - median,
   - upper tolerance limits,
   - percentile-based thresholds.

5. **Compare site data to background**  
   Site samples are compared against the background distribution to determine whether an observed concentration is likely within natural variability or elevated above expected background. This comparison may support:
   - plume delineation,
   - risk assessment,
   - remedy selection,
   - cleanup standard development,
   - determination of whether exceedances are naturally occurring.

6. **Document the basis for decisions**  
   A credible NBC determination requires clear documentation of:
   - sampling locations,
   - analytical methods,
   - quality assurance/quality control,
   - statistical treatment,
   - hydrogeologic rationale,
   - exclusion of influenced samples.

In borrow-area or excavation settings, NBCs are especially important because soils and groundwater exposed during construction may appear “impacted” simply because native materials naturally contain elevated constituent levels.

## Variants

Common variants and related approaches include:

- **Background concentration**  
  A general term for concentrations representative of ambient or unaffected conditions.

- **Ambient background**  
  Concentrations in the surrounding environment under existing conditions, sometimes including widespread human influence if truly unavoidable and not attributable to the site.

- **Regional background**  
  A broader background estimate derived from an area outside the site, often used when local background data are limited.

- **Upgradient background**  
  Background based on samples collected hydraulically upgradient from a source area.

- **Geochemical background**  
  Emphasizes concentrations controlled by natural geochemistry, such as mineral dissolution or reductive mobilization.

- **Upper tolerance limit / statistical background threshold**  
  A statistical criterion used to define a concentration above which values are unlikely to be part of the background population.

- **Site-specific natural background concentration**  
  A formally established NBC for a given site and medium, often used in regulatory decision-making.

## Trade-offs

Key considerations and limitations include:

- **Natural does not mean low**  
  Some naturally occurring constituents can exceed regulatory criteria or drinking water standards without any human source.

- **Background is hard to define cleanly**  
  Truly unaffected locations may be rare, especially in developed or industrialized areas.

- **Spatial heterogeneity**  
  Geology can vary over short distances, so background can differ significantly across a site.

- **Temporal variability**  
  Seasonal changes, groundwater flow shifts, and geochemical dynamics can change concentrations over time.

- **Sampling bias**  
  Poorly chosen background wells or reference samples can misrepresent natural conditions.

- **Statistical uncertainty**  
  Small datasets may not capture the true range of natural variability, while overbroad datasets may mix unlike hydrogeologic settings.

- **Regulatory implications**  
  If a constituent is classified as natural background, it may not be treated as site contamination; however, this determination usually requires strong technical support and may not override all regulatory obligations.

- **Intersection with anthropogenic diffuse sources**  
  In some settings, widespread non-site human influence can make it difficult to separate “natural” from “ambient” conditions.
