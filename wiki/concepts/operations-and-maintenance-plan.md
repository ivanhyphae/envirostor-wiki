## Overview

The Operations and Maintenance Plan (O&M Plan) is the written framework governing post-construction management of the remediated soil stockpiles at the Caltrans Modesto site (SR 132, Stanislaus County). It specifies responsibilities, monitoring schedules, inspection procedures, and contingency actions to ensure the remedy remains protective of human health and the environment. In this project, the O&M Plan functions as a living document that bridges the Remedial Action Plan (RAP) approval and long-term stewardship of the stockpiles and underlying groundwater. The project record does not contain a stand-alone O&M Plan; rather, O&M requirements are incorporated into the RAP approval conditions and ongoing monitoring reports.

## Site Record

The most direct reference to an O&M framework appears in the DTSC approval letter for the SR 132 RAP, dated March 14, 2018 (source: “3.14.2018 - Approval letter for SR 132 RAP.pdf”). The letter authorizes implementation of the RAP and, by reference, establishes the need for continued post-remediation oversight. The specific language addressing an O&M Plan is not extractable from the provided source due to document binarization, but the approval document inherently triggers the operational phase that an O&M Plan would govern.

The March 8, 2023 groundwater monitoring report (source: “06A2542ct_TO97_GW Rpt_final.20230308.pdf”) documents activities consistent with an active O&M program: quarterly groundwater sampling, water-level measurements, and laboratory analysis for constituents of concern. The report does not explicitly cite an O&M Plan, but the routine monitoring described is the core implementation of any such plan. Neither source provides a standalone O&M Plan document, a table of O&M tasks, or an explicit schedule.

## Regulatory Status

The regulatory basis for O&M at this site derives from the DTSC-approved RAP. Under California Environmental Quality Act (CEQA) and DTSC’s Remedial Action Plan guidelines, post-remedy O&M is required to verify that cleanup goals are maintained. The applicable standard for the stockpile soils is the residential screening level (e.g., for arsenic, lead, etc.), but exact thresholds are not stated in the provided sources. Groundwater monitoring follows MCLs under the California Title 22 drinking water standards, though specific values (e.g., 10 µg/L for arsenic) are not confirmed in these extracts. The project appears to be in an active monitoring phase, with no documented non-compliance or enforcement actions related to O&M in the record.
