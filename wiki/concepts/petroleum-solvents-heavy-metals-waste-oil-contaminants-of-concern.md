## Definition

**Petroleum solvents, heavy metals, and waste oil contaminants of concern** are the groups of potentially hazardous constituents commonly evaluated in soil, fill, sediment, and waste oil during environmental site assessment and source testing. In the context of clean fill or imported fill verification, these contaminants are checked to determine whether a material is suitable for reuse or disposal without creating a risk to human health or the environment.

In practice, the phrase refers to three broad categories:

- **Petroleum solvents**: volatile and semi-volatile organic chemicals derived from petroleum or used in fuel/industrial formulations, often including gasoline-range compounds, diesel-range compounds, and related aromatic hydrocarbons.
- **Heavy metals**: inorganic elements that may be toxic at elevated concentrations, such as lead, arsenic, cadmium, chromium, mercury, nickel, and selenium.
- **Waste oil contaminants**: compounds associated with used or waste oil, including petroleum hydrocarbons, degradation products, and in some cases additives or combustion-related byproducts.

These are “contaminants of concern” because they may indicate impacted soil or liquid waste rather than uncontaminated fill material.

## How it works

Environmental testing programs identify these contaminants through sampling and laboratory analysis. The workflow is typically:

1. **Sample collection**
   - Soil, stockpile, sediment, or waste oil samples are collected from representative locations.
   - Sampling plans are designed to capture likely contamination from past industrial use, vehicle activity, spills, storage, or disposal.

2. **Laboratory analysis**
   - **Petroleum solvents** are often analyzed by gas chromatography/mass spectrometry or similar methods for volatile organic compounds, extractable petroleum hydrocarbons, and petroleum-related fractions.
   - **Heavy metals** are analyzed using elemental methods such as ICP-based techniques after digestion of the sample.
   - **Waste oil contaminants** may be evaluated through total petroleum hydrocarbons, oil and grease, or other hydrocarbon fraction testing, sometimes supplemented by specific volatile or semi-volatile compounds.

3. **Comparison to criteria**
   - Results are compared against regulatory thresholds, screening levels, or project-specific acceptance criteria.
   - The purpose is to determine whether the material qualifies as clean fill, requires special handling, or must be managed as contaminated waste.

4. **Interpretation**
   - Elevated petroleum compounds can suggest fuel spills, vehicle maintenance waste, or industrial hydrocarbon releases.
   - Elevated metals may indicate construction debris, industrial fill, slag, paint residues, batteries, or historical land use impacts.
   - Waste oil indicators often imply improper disposal, machinery runoff, or mixed waste streams.

The concept is especially important in fill-source verification, where the receiving party needs assurance that imported material will not introduce contaminated soil or create liabilities for future excavation and disposal.

## Variants

Common variants or related classes of contaminants include:

- **Petroleum hydrocarbons**
  - Gasoline-range organics
  - Diesel-range organics
  - Motor oil / heavier hydrocarbon fractions
- **Petroleum solvents**
  - Aromatic solvents such as benzene, toluene, ethylbenzene, and xylenes
  - Mineral spirits or other refinery-derived solvent mixtures
- **Heavy metals**
  - Lead
  - Arsenic
  - Cadmium
  - Chromium
  - Mercury
  - Nickel
  - Copper, zinc, and others depending on project criteria
- **Waste oil-related analytes**
  - Oil and grease
  - Total petroleum hydrocarbons
  - Semi-volatile residues
  - Additive-related metals such as lead, zinc, or barium in some legacy oils

In site assessment practice, these may be grouped differently depending on the regulatory framework, the source material, and the intended land use.

## Trade-offs

Key considerations and limitations include:

- **Broad screening vs. specificity**
  - Testing for broad contaminant classes is efficient, but it may not identify the exact source or toxicity profile.
  - More targeted analyses provide detail but cost more and take longer.

- **Representative sampling**
  - Contamination is often highly variable across a site or stockpile.
  - Limited sampling can miss localized hot spots.

- **Detection limits and thresholds**
  - Analytical methods must be sensitive enough to compare against relevant criteria.
  - A result below detection does not always mean the material is pristine; it may simply be below the method limit.

- **Matrix effects**
  - Fine-grained, organic-rich, or oily materials can complicate analysis and interpretation.
  - Metals may bind strongly to soil, while petroleum compounds may volatilize or degrade over time.

- **Regulatory context**
  - What counts as a contaminant of concern depends on jurisdiction, project specifications, and intended reuse.
  - Material acceptable for one use may be unsuitable for another.

- **Risk management**
  - Conservative screening protects against liability, but can classify otherwise reusable material as waste.
  - Less conservative approaches may reduce disposal costs but increase environmental and legal risk.
