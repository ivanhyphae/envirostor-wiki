## Definition

**Dissolved metals and inorganic stormwater analysis** is the laboratory and regulatory evaluation of the metal fraction and other inorganic constituents present in stormwater runoff after the sample has been filtered to measure the **dissolved** portion, rather than total recoverable material. It is used to determine whether runoff from sites such as stockpiles, construction areas, industrial yards, or drainage outlets contains pollutants that could affect receiving waters or violate permit limits.

In practice, this analysis usually includes dissolved metals such as **copper, lead, zinc, nickel, cadmium, chromium, and iron**, along with other inorganic water-quality parameters such as **pH, conductivity, turbidity, chloride, sulfate, hardness, alkalinity, and nutrients** when required by the monitoring program. It is a common component of stormwater compliance work and environmental characterization, including the type of sampling documented in the provided stormwater reports.

## How it works

Stormwater analysis for dissolved metals and inorganics follows a sequence designed to capture the chemistry of runoff during rain events:

1. **Sample collection during storm events**
   - Runoff is collected from a drainage point, outfall, or representative flow path during a qualifying precipitation event.
   - Samples are often taken as **grab samples** or **composite samples**, depending on the permit or study design.
   - Field conditions such as antecedent dry period, rainfall intensity, and discharge location matter because they affect contaminant mobilization.

2. **Field filtration for dissolved metals**
   - To measure the dissolved fraction, the sample is filtered, typically through a **0.45 µm filter** in the field or shortly after collection.
   - The filtrate represents the operationally defined dissolved phase.
   - The unfiltered sample would be used for **total recoverable metals**, which includes metals bound to particulates.

3. **Preservation and transport**
   - Filtered samples for metals are acid-preserved, commonly with nitric acid, and kept chilled.
   - Proper preservation is critical because metals can adsorb to container walls, precipitate, or change oxidation state.

4. **Laboratory analysis**
   - Dissolved metals are typically measured by **ICP-MS**, **ICP-OES**, or **GFAA** depending on detection limits and program requirements.
   - Inorganic analytes are measured using methods appropriate to each parameter, such as ion chromatography for anions or electrode-based methods for pH and conductivity.
   - Analytical results are reported against method detection limits, reporting limits, and any applicable benchmark thresholds.

5. **Data interpretation**
   - Results are compared with site-specific background, stormwater benchmarks, water-quality objectives, or permit action levels.
   - Dissolved metals are especially important for ecological risk because dissolved forms are often more bioavailable than particle-associated forms.
   - Inorganic chemistry also helps explain metal behavior. For example:
     - **Lower pH** can increase metal solubility.
     - **Higher hardness** can reduce toxicity of some metals.
     - **Suspended sediment** can indicate whether metals are primarily particulate-bound.

6. **Regulatory and source assessment use**
   - Results support stormwater sampling, permit compliance, trend analysis, and identification of potential contaminant sources such as exposed soils, stockpiles, metal-bearing materials, or wash areas.
   - Repeated sampling across storm events helps determine whether observations are event-driven or persistent.

## Variants

Common variants and related implementations include:

- **Dissolved metals analysis**
  - Focuses only on the filtered fraction of metals.
  - Used when speciation or bioavailability is important.

- **Total recoverable metals analysis**
  - Measures metals in unfiltered samples after digestion.
  - Captures both dissolved and particle-associated metals.

- **Suspended/particulate-associated metals**
  - Derived indirectly by comparing total recoverable and dissolved results.
  - Useful for distinguishing sediment-bound contamination.

- **Stormwater benchmark monitoring**
  - Analytical results are compared to regulatory or guidance benchmarks rather than full water-quality standards.
  - Common in industrial stormwater compliance programs.

- **Receiving-water screening**
  - Applies stormwater inorganic and metals data to assess potential impacts on surface waters.
  - Often includes hardness-adjusted or pH-dependent interpretation.

- **Event-based grab sampling**
  - Captures a single point in time during runoff.
  - Simpler, but may miss concentration changes across the event.

- **Composite/event-integrated sampling**
  - Combines multiple aliquots over time or flow.
  - Better for estimating average event concentration.

- **Targeted metals panels**
  - Only selected metals are analyzed based on likely site sources.
  - Common at industrial stockpiles or facilities with known metal-bearing materials.

## Trade-offs

Key considerations and limitations include:

- **Dissolved is operational, not absolute**
  - The dissolved fraction is defined by filtration method and pore size, so it is a practical laboratory category rather than a fundamental chemical state.

- **Sampling timing strongly affects results**
  - First-flush effects, short storms, and changing runoff intensity can cause large concentration swings.
  - A single grab sample may not represent the entire event.

- **Filtering can alter results**
  - Field filtration reduces particulate contamination, but improper technique can introduce contamination, loss, or bias.

- **Detection limits matter**
  - Some metals occur at very low concentrations in stormwater; whether a lab can quantify them depends on the method and sample matrix.

- **Matrix effects can interfere**
  - Turbid, saline, or highly mineralized stormwater can complicate analysis and interpretation.

- **Dissolved metals are more directly relevant to toxicity**
  - This is an advantage for ecological assessment, but it also means dissolved results may appear more concerning than total results in some settings.

- **Inorganic context is essential**
  - Metals cannot be interpreted well in isolation. pH, hardness, conductivity, and turbidity help explain whether metals are mobilized, dissolved, or particle-bound.

- **Compliance thresholds vary**
  - Different permits, jurisdictions, and receiving waters may use different benchmarks, action levels, or hardness-adjusted criteria.

- **Source attribution is inferential**
  - Elevated dissolved metals may indicate source materials, but they do not alone prove a specific source without site observations and supporting data.
