## Cadmium

### Definition
Cadmium is a chemical element with symbol Cd and atomic number 48. It is a soft, bluish-white transition metal that occurs naturally in the Earth’s crust, almost always in association with zinc ores. Cadmium is a by‑product of zinc refining and is used in a range of industrial applications including nickel‑cadmium batteries, electroplating, pigments, plastic stabilizers, and photovoltaic cells. It is classified as a human carcinogen and accumulates in the environment, where it poses risks to human health and ecosystems.

### How it works
#### Chemistry and occurrence
Cadmium belongs to group 12 of the periodic table, sharing many chemical properties with zinc and mercury. Its most stable oxidation state is +2, and it forms inorganic compounds such as cadmium oxide (CdO), cadmium sulfide (CdS), and cadmium chloride (CdCl₂). The metal is resistant to corrosion and is often plated onto steel and other metals. In nature, cadmium is never found in pure form; it is concentrated in zinc‑bearing minerals like sphalerite. It is also introduced into soils through phosphate fertilizers that contain trace amounts of the element and through atmospheric deposition from industrial emissions.

#### Toxicity and environmental fate
Cadmium and its compounds are highly toxic. Once absorbed, cadmium accumulates in the kidneys and liver, with a biological half‑life of 10–30 years in humans. Chronic exposure leads to renal tubular dysfunction, impaired bone mineralization (itai‑itai disease), and pulmonary carcinomas. In the environment, cadmium is mobile under acidic conditions and can leach into groundwater. It binds strongly to organic matter in soil but is taken up by plants, entering the food chain. Regulatory agencies (e.g., USEPA, DTSC) set strict cleanup standards for cadmium‑contaminated soil, often on the order of 0.5–1 mg/kg for residential use. Remediation of cadmium‑contaminated soil stockpiles — such as those associated with the Caltrans Modesto Stockpile project — typically involves stabilization, soil washing, excavation and off‑site disposal, or capping to prevent migration.

#### Compounds and applications
- **Cadmium sulfide (CdS)** – a yellow pigment (cadmium yellow) used in paints, plastics, and ceramics; also a semiconductor in photoresistors and solar cells.
- **Cadmium telluride (CdTe)** – a thin‑film photovoltaic material.
- **Cadmium hydroxide (Cd(OH)₂)** – used in nickel‑cadmium battery electrodes.
- **Cadmium stearate** – a stabilizer in PVC, now largely replaced in consumer goods due to toxicity concerns.

### Trade-offs
#### Advantages
- Excellent corrosion resistance, making cadmium electroplating valuable for aerospace and military components.
- High electrochemical potential yields reliable rechargeable Ni‑Cd batteries (long cycle life, good performance at low temperatures).
- Unique optical and electronic properties enable specialized semiconductors (CdS, CdTe) and phosphors.

#### Limitations and risks
- **Toxicity** – cadmium is a known carcinogen and nephrotoxin; poisoning can occur from inhalation of fumes (e.g., during welding or smelting) or ingestion of contaminated food or water.
- **Environmental persistence** – cadmium does not degrade and bioaccumulates; even low‑level contamination can require costly long‑term remediation.
- **Regulatory restrictions** – many uses are banned or heavily regulated under RoHS, REACH, and the USEPA. Recycling of Ni‑Cd batteries is mandatory in many jurisdictions.
- **Remediation challenges** – for large soil stockpiles (e.g., those at Caltrans Modesto), treating cadmium‑contaminated material is expensive and often involves removal or encapsulation rather than in‑situ destruction.
