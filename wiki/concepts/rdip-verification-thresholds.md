## Definition

**Rdip Verification Thresholds** are the decision criteria used in the RDIP confirmation/verification process to determine whether soil or stockpile sampling results are sufficiently below a regulatory screening level to support closure, reduced investigation, or a variance request without further action. In practice, they define how far below the applicable cleanup or screening benchmark analytical results must be before a stockpile or excavation can be verified as acceptable under RDIP-based sampling plans.

In the source memos, these thresholds are applied to stockpile and wall-sampling results for Caltrans projects to show that remaining soil does not exceed the relevant environmental criteria and that confirmatory sampling supports the proposed disposition.

## How it works

Rdip verification thresholds function as a compliance decision rule layered on top of a site-specific sampling program:

1. **Select the applicable benchmark**
   The project team identifies the governing screening or cleanup threshold for the chemical of concern, based on the project’s regulatory context and the approved RDIP approach.

2. **Collect confirmation samples**
   Samples are taken from stockpiles, excavation sidewalls, or other representative locations, depending on the physical setting and the memo’s sampling plan. The memos show this used for:
   - stockpile confirmation sampling,
   - MSE wall sampling,
   - stockpile removal verification.

3. **Compare results to the verification threshold**
   Laboratory results are compared against the threshold. The key question is not only whether a result is below the limit, but whether it is below the limit by enough margin to support a verification conclusion under the RDIP framework.

4. **Apply the decision rule**
   If results are consistently below the threshold, the stockpile or sampled material is treated as verified for the intended disposition. If results approach or exceed the threshold, the material may require additional sampling, removal, segregation, or a revised disposition strategy.

5. **Document the basis for closure or variance**
   The results and comparison logic are recorded in a technical memo, which becomes part of the project record supporting an RDIP variance or other regulatory approval.

In the project memos, verification thresholds are used as an operational control: they translate laboratory data into a yes/no determination for whether the stockpile meets the criteria for release, reuse, or acceptance.

## Variants

Common variants and related implementations include:

- **Stockpile verification thresholds**  
  Used when discrete piles of excavated soil are sampled before reuse, removal, or disposal.

- **Wall-sampling verification thresholds**  
  Used for excavation sidewalls or structural interfaces, such as in the Stockpile 1 MSE Wall Sampling Tech Memo context, where confirmation samples demonstrate that remaining material meets the required criteria.

- **Confirmation sampling thresholds**  
  Used after removal work to verify that residual material is acceptable, as reflected in the Stockpile 3 Confirmation Sampling Tech Memo Approval.

- **Project-specific RDIP variance thresholds**  
  These are threshold values or decision criteria embedded in a formal variance package, such as the Caltrans Modesto Soil Stcokpiles RDIP Variance, which tailor the general RDIP process to a specific site and material stream.

- **Removal-completion thresholds**
  In some projects, the threshold is framed as a completion criterion: once sample results are below the threshold, removal is considered sufficient and no further excavation is needed.

## Trade-offs

Key considerations include:

- **Protectiveness vs. practicality**  
  Lower thresholds provide greater conservatism but can trigger more excavation, more sampling, and higher disposal costs.

- **Representativeness of sampling**
  Thresholds are only as reliable as the sampling design. Poorly distributed samples can miss localized contamination, especially in heterogeneous stockpiles.

- **Statistical interpretation**
  A single result below threshold may not be enough if the project requires a pattern of confirmatory results. Some approvals rely on all samples, or on a specified percentile or average, depending on the RDIP package.

- **Regulatory acceptance**
  Verification thresholds must match the approved project methodology; otherwise the data may not be acceptable for closure or variance support.

- **Material management constraints**
  Stockpile handling, weathering, mixing, and segregation can affect whether a threshold comparison is meaningful at the time of sampling.

- **Documentation burden**
  Because thresholds are used to justify disposition decisions, the technical record must clearly show the sampling locations, analytical methods, and comparison logic.
