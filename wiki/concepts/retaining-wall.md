## Definition

A **retaining wall** is a structure designed to hold back soil, rock, or other materials laterally, preventing them from sliding or collapsing under their own weight and any applied surcharges. Retaining walls are commonly used in civil and geotechnical engineering for slope stabilization, excavation support, roadway embankments, bridge abutments, and containment of contaminated materials. Their function relies on resisting the lateral earth pressure exerted by the retained material, while transferring loads safely to the foundation and subgrade.

## How it works

Retaining walls primarily resist two types of loading: **lateral earth pressure** (active, at-rest, or passive) and **hydrostatic pressure** from groundwater. The wall must be stable against:

- **Sliding** along its base.
- **Overturning** about its toe.
- **Bearing failure** of the foundation soil.
- **Global instability** (deep-seated slope failure).

Critical design elements include:

- **Drainage** – Weep holes, granular backfill, or drainage blankets reduce water buildup behind the wall, minimizing hydrostatic pressures and frost heave.
- **Backfill material** – Free-draining granular soils are preferred to limit water retention and improve stability.
- **Reinforcement** – Some wall types incorporate steel or geosynthetic reinforcement to increase tensile capacity (e.g., mechanically stabilized earth walls).
- **Foundation preparation** – Proper compaction and bearing capacity are essential.

## Variants

Several common types of retaining walls exist, each suited to different height ranges, site conditions, and cost constraints.

- **Gravity walls** – Massive concrete or stone walls that rely on their own weight to resist lateral forces. Simple and durable, but material-intensive.
- **Cantilever walls** – Reinforced concrete walls with a base slab; the stem and base act as a cantilever. Efficient for moderate heights (3–10 m).
- **Sheet pile walls** – Interlocking steel, vinyl, or timber piles driven into the ground. Used for temporary shoring or in soft soils. Often require tiebacks or anchors for additional stability.
- **Mechanically Stabilized Earth (MSE) walls** – A composite structure of granular backfill reinforced with metal strips or geosynthetic layers, faced with precast panels or wrapped fabric. Common in highway projects and bridge abutments. The Stockpile 1 MSE Wall Sampling Tech Memo details construction quality control for such walls, including material sampling and compaction testing.
- **Anchored walls** – A thin concrete or shotcrete facing tied back to anchors drilled into stable soil or rock. Suitable for deep excavations.
- **Counterfort / Buttress walls** – Cantilever walls with intermittent vertical ribs on the back (counterfort) or front (buttress) to resist higher loads.

## Trade-offs

The choice of retaining wall involves balancing several factors:

| Consideration | Advantages | Limitations |
|---|---|---|
| **Cost** | Gravity and MSE walls are often economical for moderate heights; sheet piles can be reused. | Cantilever and anchored walls require more reinforcement and skilled labor. |
| **Space** | MSE and gravity walls need wide base footprints; sheet piles require minimal space. | Anchored walls need clear zones for tiebacks. |
| **Aesthetics** | MSE panels and architectural finishes can improve appearance. | Steel sheet piles may be visually intrusive. |
| **Height** | Anchored and counterfort walls can exceed 15 m. | Gravity walls become impractical at large heights due to material volume. |
| **Maintenance** | Drainage must be periodically inspected; weep holes can clog. | Land use covenants and annual inspections (as in 2025 - RE_ Reminder - Annual Inspection Report.pdf) ensure long-term performance, especially for containment projects. |
| **Long‑term durability** | Concrete and MSE walls have proven service lives > 50 years with proper design and drainage. | Corrosion of steel reinforcement in MSE walls may reduce lifespan; require monitoring. |
| **Environmental** | Contaminated soils can be safely contained behind retaining walls, as seen in the SR 132 RAP where DTSC approved a containment remedy using retaining walls and bridge abutments. | Remedy relies on continued operation and maintenance; contaminant remains in place indefinitely. |

Key limitations include the need for adequate foundation soils, the potential for differential settlement, and the requirement for ongoing inspection and repair of drainage features. In containment applications, a land use covenant restricts future excavation to avoid exposure.
