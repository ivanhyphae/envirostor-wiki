## Definition

**Hydrogeology Southeast Groundwater Flow** refers to the study and characterization of groundwater movement in the **southeast portion of a site or hydrogeologic area**, with emphasis on how water levels, hydraulic gradients, aquifer properties, and boundary conditions control flow direction and rate. In practice, it is usually a **site-specific groundwater flow interpretation** used in hydrogeologic investigations, monitoring programs, and remediation planning.

At a contaminated site, such as the one documented in the source report, southeast groundwater flow analysis helps determine whether groundwater in the southeast area is moving toward or away from potential source areas, receptors, or monitoring points.

## How it works

Groundwater flow in the southeast area is governed by the same principles as groundwater flow elsewhere, but the local geology and hydraulic conditions determine the actual pattern:

1. **Hydraulic head differences**
   - Groundwater moves from areas of **higher hydraulic head** to **lower hydraulic head**.
   - Water levels measured in monitoring wells across the southeast area are compared to establish the **hydraulic gradient**.

2. **Aquifer properties**
   - The rate and ease of flow depend on the **hydraulic conductivity** of the geologic materials.
   - Coarse sand and gravel transmit water more readily than silts or clays.
   - Vertical layering can cause the southeast groundwater flow to differ by depth.

3. **Flow direction**
   - Potentiometric surface maps are constructed from well water-level data.
   - Contour patterns indicate the groundwater flow direction, which is generally **perpendicular to contour lines** and toward lower head.

4. **Local controls**
   - Surface water features, recharge from irrigation or precipitation, pumping wells, buried utilities, and low-permeability layers can all alter flow in the southeast area.
   - In site investigations, engineered features such as stockpiles, paved areas, or drainage controls may also affect infiltration and shallow groundwater response.

5. **Monitoring and interpretation**
   - Groundwater elevation data are collected periodically from a network of wells.
   - Seasonal changes, pumping cycles, and storm recharge are considered when interpreting whether southeast groundwater flow is stable, transient, or vertically stratified.

6. **Use in site assessment**
   - The resulting flow interpretation is used to predict **migration pathways** for dissolved contaminants.
   - It also supports decisions about where to place additional wells, how to define a plume boundary, and whether corrective action is needed.

## Variants

Common variants or related implementations include:

- **Shallow southeast groundwater flow**  
  Flow in the upper unconfined zone, often most responsive to recharge and surface conditions.

- **Deep southeast groundwater flow**  
  Flow in a deeper confined or semi-confined zone, typically less affected by short-term surface changes.

- **Seasonal groundwater flow interpretation**  
  Flow direction and gradient are evaluated during wet and dry seasons to capture temporal variability.

- **Three-dimensional flow analysis**  
  Uses multiple well depths to distinguish horizontal flow from vertical gradients.

- **Conceptual site model-based flow interpretation**  
  Integrates geology, hydrology, and source/receptor locations to explain why southeast flow behaves as observed.

- **Numerical groundwater flow modeling**  
  Simulates southeast groundwater flow using software-based models rather than relying only on field measurements.

## Trade-offs

Key considerations and limitations include:

- **Sparse well coverage**
  - If few wells exist in the southeast area, flow direction may be uncertain or oversimplified.

- **Temporal variability**
  - Groundwater levels can change after rainfall, pumping, or seasonal recharge, so a single snapshot may not represent long-term conditions.

- **Vertical complexity**
  - Flow may differ by depth; a shallow map can be misleading if deeper zones behave differently.

- **Heterogeneous geology**
  - Layers of varying permeability can cause local deviations from the regional flow direction.

- **Interpretive uncertainty**
  - Potentiometric maps are conceptual tools; errors in surveying, casing elevations, or sampling timing can affect the result.

- **Site-specific relevance**
  - “Southeast groundwater flow” is not a general regional hydrogeologic class; it is usually a location-based interpretation that must be tied to the specific site setting.
