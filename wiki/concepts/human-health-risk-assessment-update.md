## Definition

A **Human Health Risk Assessment Update** is a revised evaluation of potential adverse health effects to people from exposure to site-related chemicals, physical agents, or other stressors. It updates a prior Human Health Risk Assessment (HHRA) using newly available information such as additional sampling data, revised exposure assumptions, updated toxicity values, corrected analytical results, or changes in site conditions.

In remediation and environmental review contexts, an update is typically used to determine whether earlier conclusions about risk still apply, whether cleanup goals remain protective, or whether additional investigation or action is needed. It is commonly paired with a Site Screening Investigation or other remedial investigation work to incorporate new field data into the risk evaluation.

## How it works

A human health risk assessment update follows the same basic framework as an original HHRA, but it revisits the inputs and calculations with current information.

### 1. Identify the scope of the update
The assessor first determines what changed since the prior risk evaluation. Common triggers include:

- new soil, groundwater, sediment, or air data
- changes in detected contaminants or concentrations
- revised site boundaries or land use
- updated exposure scenarios
- new toxicity reference values or cancer slope factors
- corrections to prior assumptions or data quality issues

### 2. Reassess exposure pathways
The update examines whether receptors could still be exposed through the same pathways considered previously, such as:

- incidental ingestion of soil or dust
- dermal contact with soil or water
- inhalation of particulates or vapors
- ingestion of groundwater
- food-chain transfer, where relevant

The update may confirm that a pathway remains complete, become incomplete, or require a different quantitative treatment.

### 3. Apply updated exposure assumptions
Exposure assumptions are recalculated using current site and receptor information. This may include:

- residential, commercial, construction, or industrial land use
- adult versus child receptor groups
- exposure frequency and duration
- soil adherence rates and inhalation factors
- groundwater use assumptions
- averaging time for carcinogenic and noncarcinogenic effects

If a site is changing from one land use scenario to another, the risk update often has major consequences for the resulting risk estimates.

### 4. Use current toxicity criteria
Chemical concentrations are translated into risk using toxicity values from accepted regulatory sources. An update may incorporate:

- revised cancer potency factors
- updated reference doses or reference concentrations
- agency-specific screening levels
- new chemical-specific guidance

Because toxicity values evolve over time, an older HHRA can become obsolete even if site concentrations do not change.

### 5. Recalculate risk and hazard
For each chemical and pathway, the assessor estimates:

- **Cancer risk**, usually expressed as incremental lifetime cancer risk
- **Noncancer hazard**, usually expressed as a hazard quotient or hazard index

Results are then compared with applicable regulatory benchmarks or project-specific decision criteria.

### 6. Compare against cleanup or decision thresholds
The updated risk estimates are used to evaluate whether the site remains acceptable or whether further response is needed. Depending on the program, this may inform:

- remedial design
- excavation or capping decisions
- soil management plans
- institutional controls
- monitoring requirements
- No Further Action determinations

### 7. Document assumptions, revisions, and conclusions
A key purpose of the update is traceability. The report should clearly identify:

- what data were added or revised
- what methods changed
- what assumptions were retained
- how the update affects prior conclusions

This is especially important when an update is used to support regulatory closure or modify a remedy.

## Variants

Common variants of a human health risk assessment update include:

- **Focused update**: revises only one component, such as a single exposure medium or one receptor scenario.
- **Full HHRA revision**: re-runs the entire assessment with new data and updated toxicity values.
- **Screening-level update**: a simplified recalculation to determine whether a detailed reassessment is needed.
- **Cumulative risk update**: incorporates multiple contaminants and pathways to update total risk or hazard.
- **Site-specific remedial update**: used to support remedy modification, closure, or confirmation sampling decisions.
- **Occupational or construction risk update**: evaluates short-term worker exposures during excavation or other intrusive work.
- **Alternative receptor scenario**: applies a different future-use assumption, such as industrial instead of residential exposure.

In practice, these updates may be embedded in broader Risk Assessment or Remedial Investigation documents rather than issued as stand-alone reports.

## Trade-offs

### Benefits
- Reflects the most current site conditions and toxicological information
- Can reduce unnecessary remediation if prior estimates were conservative
- Can identify newly relevant risks if conditions have worsened or assumptions changed
- Supports defensible regulatory decision-making with transparent documentation

### Limitations
- Results depend heavily on data quality and representativeness
- Small changes in assumptions can produce large changes in calculated risk
- Updates may not resolve uncertainty if sampling remains sparse or spatially biased
- Regulatory acceptability can vary by jurisdiction and program
- Comparing old and new assessments can be difficult if methods, receptors, or toxicity values have changed substantially

### Practical considerations
- Consistency with prior methodologies matters for comparability
- Analytical detection limits can affect whether low-level contaminants are counted
- Temporal changes, such as seasonal groundwater variation, may require conservative interpretation
- Updates should distinguish between actual risk change and methodological change
