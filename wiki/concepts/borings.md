## Definition

A **boring** is a narrow, cylindrical hole advanced into the subsurface using a drilling or augering technique to obtain representative soil, rock, or groundwater samples for geotechnical, environmental, or construction‑related testing. In the context of borrow area sampling, soil stockpile characterization, and clean fill verification, borings are the primary intrusive method to collect material for laboratory analysis, ensuring that the extracted soil meets specified quality, contamination, or compaction standards before use in engineered fill or earthwork.

## How it works

Borings are performed by advancing a hollow drill rod or auger into the ground. The process generally follows these steps:

1. **Site preparation and layout** – A grid or targeted locations (e.g., per a Borrow Area Sampling Plan or Fenceline Workplan) are staked out based on project specifications.  
2. **Drilling** – A powered drill rig rotates a cutting tool into the subsurface. Common methods include:  
   - **Hollow‑stem auger (HSA)** – Screw‑type flights cut through soil; sample collection may occur through the hollow center using a split‑spoon or tube sampler.  
   - **Rotary drilling** – Uses a rotating bit and drilling fluid (mud, air, or foam) to penetrate more competent materials or bedrock.  
3. **Sampling** – At specified depths, a sampler (e.g., split‑spoon, thin‑wall tube, or piston sampler) is driven or pressed into the soil ahead of the drill rod. The recovered core or soil plug is removed, visually described, and placed in airtight containers for transport to the laboratory.  
4. **Field logging** – Each boring is logged (depth, soil type, color, moisture, odor) and photographed. The boring log becomes a permanent record.  
5. **Backfill and abandonment** – After sampling, boreholes are sealed with bentonite grout or cuttings to restore the ground surface and prevent cross‑contamination.

The number and depth of borings are determined by the scale of the project, the variability of the soil, and regulatory requirements. For example, a borrow area might require borings at rates such as one per 10,000 cubic yards, while stockpile or fenceline workplans may specify a minimum number of borings per defined area or volume.

## Variants

- **Continuous sampling borings** – Auger cuttings or core samples are collected at close intervals (e.g., every 5 feet) to provide a detailed subsurface profile.  
- **Split‑spoon (SPT) borings** – The Standard Penetration Test (SPT) is conducted by counting hammer blows needed to advance a split‑spoon sampler, giving a measure of soil density and strength.  
- **Solid‑stem auger borings** – Used for shallow, relatively homogeneous soils where continuous core is not required; spoil is recovered from the auger flights.  
- **Rotary (rock) coring borings** – A diamond‑tipped bit recovers intact rock core for geotechnical evaluation or contaminant assessment.  
- **Sonic (vibratory) borings** – High‑frequency vibration advances a drill string with minimal disturbance, ideal for collecting intact soil samples in sensitive environmental investigations.  

## Trade-offs

- **Cost vs. coverage** – More borings yield higher spatial resolution but increase drilling and laboratory expenses. A balance must be struck based on project risk and regulatory minimums.  
- **Sample disturbance** – The method of advancement can alter the soil’s natural structure and moisture content, potentially affecting laboratory test results. Thin‑wall tubes (undisturbed samples) reduce disturbance but are slower and more expensive than split‑spoon (disturbed) sampling.  
- **Access and mobility** – Drill rigs require relatively level ground; rocky, steep, or confined sites may limit boring feasibility or require specialized equipment (e.g., portable or track‑mounted rigs).  
- **Regulatory compliance** – Borings performed in areas of suspected contamination must follow strict decontamination procedures between holes to avoid cross‑contamination, and all soil cuttings must be properly managed.  
- **Temporal limitations** – In cold climates, frozen ground can slow or prevent boring advancement; in loose sands, borehole collapse may occur before sampling is completed.
