## Fenceline Sampling

### Definition
Fenceline sampling is an environmental monitoring methodology in which air quality, soil, or groundwater samples are collected at the perimeter (fenceline) of a contaminated site or industrial facility to assess off-site migration of pollutants. It is commonly used to evaluate fugitive emissions, dust, or vapor intrusion from soil stockpiles, landfills, Superfund sites, or active industrial operations. The approach provides a direct measurement of contaminant concentrations at the boundary where exposures to nearby communities or ecosystems may occur.

### How it works
Fenceline sampling typically involves deploying monitoring stations or collecting discrete samples along the property boundary of a site. The specific method depends on the medium being assessed:

- **Air sampling**: Active or passive samplers (e.g., sorbent tubes, canisters, or passive diffusion samplers) are placed at multiple points along the fenceline, often at breathing height (1.5–2 meters). Sampling may be time-integrated (e.g., 24-hour or weekly) or real-time using continuous monitors. Samples are analyzed for volatile organic compounds (VOCs), particulate matter, heavy metals, or other site-specific contaminants. Wind direction and meteorological data are recorded simultaneously to correlate concentration spikes with source activities.

- **Soil sampling**: Soil cores are collected at intervals along the fenceline, typically at depths of 0–15 cm (surface) and sometimes deeper to assess subsurface migration. Samples are composited or analyzed individually for contaminants such as metals, hydrocarbons, or pesticides. This variant is used when soil stockpiles or erosion control is a concern.

- **Groundwater sampling**: Monitoring wells installed along the downgradient fenceline are sampled to detect dissolved contaminants migrating from a source (e.g., a stockpile or landfill). Sampling frequency may follow regulatory protocols (e.g., quarterly or semi-annually).

Data from fenceline sampling are compared to regulatory action levels (e.g., US EPA National Ambient Air Quality Standards or cleanup levels) to determine if corrective actions are needed. Statistical methods—such as upper confidence limits on the mean or comparison to background—are applied to account for natural variability.

### Variants
- **Real-time fenceline monitoring**: Uses continuous analyzers (e.g., photoionization detectors, FTIR, or mass spectrometers) to provide instantaneous data, enabling rapid response to exceedances.
- **Passive fenceline sampling**: Employs passive diffusion samplers (e.g., Radiello tubes or OVM badges) that require no power and are deployed for days to weeks, offering a cost-effective means of time-averaged exposure assessment.
- **Municipal landfills**: The U.S. EPA requires fenceline monitoring for non-methane organic compounds at large municipal solid waste landfills (NSPS/EG 40 CFR Part 60), using surface emission monitoring or fenceline sondes.
- **Superfund or RCRA corrective action**: Fenceline sampling is used as part of vapor intrusion investigations or to verify containment of soil stockpiles (e.g., at Brownfields sites).
- **Odor monitoring**: A specialized variant using electronic noses (e-noses) or olfactometry panels to track nuisance odors from landfills or wastewater treatment plants.

### Trade-offs
- **Pros**:
  - Direct measure of off-site migration.
  - Can provide early warning of contaminant release.
  - Supports compliance with environmental regulations.
  - Cost-effective compared to full dispersion modeling (though modeling is often used in conjunction).

- **Cons and limitations**:
  - **Spatial representation**: Single-point fenceline samples may miss localized plumes if monitor placement is inadequate, especially under variable wind conditions.
  - **Temporal coverage**: Discrete grab samples may not characterize peak events; continuous monitoring increases costs.
  - **Interpretation challenges**: Background concentrations from adjacent sources can confound results.
  - **Regulatory thresholds**: Action levels may not consider cumulative or long-term low-level exposure.
  - **Weather dependency**: Rain, wind direction, and temperature can affect sampling quality and representativeness.
  - **Implementation cost**: Continuous real-time systems and laboratory analysis for multiple contaminants can be expensive.
