## Definition

Clean Fill Suitability Evaluation is the process of determining whether excavated material is acceptable for reuse as clean fill, based on site-specific sampling, laboratory testing, and comparison to applicable regulatory or project criteria. The goal is to confirm that the material is sufficiently free of contamination and other undesirable constituents so it can be placed in a borrow area, embankment, or other fill location without creating unacceptable environmental or geotechnical risk.

In practice, the evaluation asks two core questions:

1. Is the material chemically suitable for reuse as clean fill?
2. Is the material physically suitable for the intended placement and compaction requirements?

The source documents indicate that this evaluation is performed through a borrow area sampling plan and then summarized in a technical memorandum for approval of the results.

## How it works

Clean fill suitability evaluation typically follows a staged workflow:

### 1. Define the material source and intended reuse
The first step is to identify the source area, the anticipated excavation limits, and the proposed reuse location. This includes understanding whether the material will be used for general fill, engineered fill, or a specific borrow area.

The Borrow Area Sampling Plan establishes how the source material will be characterized before approval.

### 2. Develop a sampling strategy
A sampling plan is prepared to collect representative samples from the material source. The plan usually addresses:

- sampling locations and spacing
- depth intervals
- number of samples
- field logging and compositing approach, if any
- laboratory analytical suite
- QA/QC requirements
- chain-of-custody procedures

The sampling design is intended to capture the spatial variability of the material so the evaluation is defensible.

### 3. Collect field samples
Field crews collect soil or fill samples from the borrow or excavation area. Samples are documented with:

- location coordinates or stationing
- depth
- soil description
- observations of staining, odor, debris, or unusual material
- sample identification numbers

Field observations matter because apparent contamination or unsuitable physical material may disqualify material even before lab results are reviewed.

### 4. Perform laboratory testing
Laboratory analysis is used to assess whether the material meets clean fill criteria. The exact tests depend on the project and regulatory framework, but commonly include:

- metals
- petroleum hydrocarbons
- volatile or semi-volatile organic compounds
- other site-specific contaminants of concern
- moisture or gradation-related tests when physical suitability is relevant

The results are then compared against cleanup levels, screening criteria, or project-specific fill acceptance thresholds.

### 5. Compare results to acceptance criteria
The laboratory and field data are evaluated against the standards established for the project. Clean fill suitability is generally confirmed when:

- contaminants are below applicable thresholds
- no prohibited waste characteristics are present
- no unacceptable debris, organic material, or construction waste is included
- the material is workable and can be compacted or placed as intended

If results exceed criteria, the material may be rejected, segregated, or managed under a different disposal or reuse pathway.

### 6. Document the conclusion
The final evaluation is typically captured in a technical memorandum. The memo summarizes:

- background and purpose
- sampling approach
- analytical results
- comparison to criteria
- conclusion on suitability
- recommended approval or restrictions

The Borrow Area Results Tech Memo Approval indicates formal review and approval of these results, which is the administrative step that authorizes reuse if the material is deemed acceptable.

## Variants

Clean fill suitability evaluation can take several forms depending on project needs:

### Borrow-area screening
A targeted evaluation of a planned borrow source to determine whether material can be excavated and reused elsewhere.

### Construction reuse approval
Evaluation of on-site or imported fill material for placement in embankments, backfill, or grading.

### Environmental clearance evaluation
A more contamination-focused review where the main purpose is to confirm the material does not pose an environmental liability.

### Geotechnical suitability evaluation
A fill-quality review emphasizing physical properties such as grain size, moisture content, plasticity, organics, and compaction behavior.

### Composite sampling vs. discrete sampling
- **Discrete sampling** captures conditions at individual locations and depths.
- **Composite sampling** combines multiple increments to reduce analysis costs but can mask localized contamination.

### Screening-level vs. confirmatory evaluation
- **Screening-level** evaluations use limited sampling to determine whether further review is needed.
- **Confirmatory** evaluations are more exhaustive and used to support final approval.

## Trade-offs

### Representativeness vs. cost
More sampling improves confidence but increases time and laboratory cost. Too little sampling risks missing localized contamination or unsuitable zones.

### Environmental caution vs. material reuse efficiency
Strict acceptance criteria protect the environment but may reduce the amount of material that can be reused, increasing disposal or import needs.

### Discrete data vs. composited data
Discrete samples provide better spatial resolution. Composite samples are cheaper but may dilute high-concentration hotspots.

### Chemical suitability vs. physical suitability
Material can be chemically clean but still unsuitable for fill if it contains too much organics, clay, debris, or excessive moisture.

### Regulatory consistency vs. project-specific criteria
Some projects rely on general regulatory thresholds, while others use owner- or agency-specific acceptance criteria. This can complicate cross-project comparisons.

### Approval formality
Formal memo approval provides traceability and accountability, but can add schedule delay if revisions or additional sampling are required.
