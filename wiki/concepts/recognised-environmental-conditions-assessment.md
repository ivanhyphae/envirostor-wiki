## Definition

Recognised Environmental Conditions Assessment is a due-diligence investigation used to identify environmental conditions that are known, observable, or reasonably suspected to be present at a property and that could indicate a release of hazardous substances, petroleum, or other contaminants. In practice, the assessment is typically performed to support property transactions, redevelopment, financing, or regulatory decision-making by evaluating evidence of current or historical environmental impacts.

The core purpose is to determine whether there are indicators of environmental liability or contamination risk before a project proceeds. This often includes reviewing site history, current and former uses, visible conditions, regulatory records, and available analytical data.

## How it works

A Recognised Environmental Conditions Assessment generally follows a structured environmental review process:

1. **Desktop review**
   - Examine historical and current aerial photographs, fire insurance maps, land records, title documents, zoning information, and regulatory databases.
   - Identify past uses that may have involved fuel storage, waste handling, industrial operations, fill placement, staining, dumping, or other contamination-generating activities.

2. **Site reconnaissance**
   - Visit the property to observe physical conditions.
   - Look for obvious indicators such as distressed vegetation, stained soils, drums, vents, sumps, fill material, discharge points, fill stockpiles, or evidence of unauthorized disposal.

3. **Interviews and records review**
   - Speak with owners, operators, tenants, and adjacent property representatives when available.
   - Review permits, closure reports, prior environmental studies, remediation documents, and agency correspondence.

4. **Assessment of recognised conditions**
   - Evaluate whether observed or documented conditions constitute a recognised environmental condition, or whether they are merely potential concerns requiring further investigation.
   - Consider whether contamination may have migrated from adjacent properties or off-site sources.

5. **Follow-up recommendations**
   - If conditions warrant, recommend sampling, delineation, risk evaluation, or remedial action.
   - If no significant concerns are identified, document the basis for that conclusion.

A practical example is a clean fill or borrow material review for a project such as a highway improvement. Source testing and documentation are used to verify that imported fill does not introduce contamination and to confirm that the material is suitable for the intended use.

## Variants

Recognised Environmental Conditions Assessment can appear in several forms:

- **Phase I Environmental Site Assessment**
  - A formal property due-diligence study that commonly serves as the main framework for identifying recognised environmental conditions.

- **Environmental compliance due diligence**
  - Focuses on permits, operational history, waste handling, and regulatory compliance rather than transaction risk alone.

- **Fill source testing or source approval**
  - Evaluates material proposed for import, such as clean fill, to ensure it meets project and regulatory requirements.

- **Limited-scope reconnaissance**
  - A narrower assessment for a specific issue, such as suspected dumping, staining, or drainage impacts.

- **Expanded subsurface investigation**
  - Adds soil, groundwater, sediment, or vapour sampling where surface observations suggest a recognised condition may be present.

Alternatives or closely related approaches include Phase I Environmental Site Assessment, Phase II Environmental Site Assessment, and environmental due diligence.

## Trade-offs

Key considerations include:

- **Depth versus cost**
  - More comprehensive reviews reduce uncertainty but require more time and expense.

- **Non-intrusive versus intrusive methods**
  - A reconnaissance-based assessment is faster and less disruptive, but it may miss contamination that is not visible at the surface.

- **Historical uncertainty**
  - Older sites often have incomplete records, making it difficult to distinguish between benign conditions and actual environmental liabilities.

- **False positives and false negatives**
  - Visible staining or fill material may suggest a problem even when contamination is absent, while buried impacts may remain undetected without sampling.

- **Project timing**
  - Early assessment can prevent delays, but late discovery of concerns can force redesign, remediation, or regulatory review.

- **Regulatory and legal reliance**
  - The usefulness of the assessment depends on whether it is prepared and documented in a way that supports the intended purpose, such as financing, acquisition, or construction approval.
