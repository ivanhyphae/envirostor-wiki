## Definition

**Depth Discrete Composite Sampling (DDCS)** is a sampling approach in which material is collected from **specific, discrete depth intervals** and then **combined into a single composite sample** for analysis. The method is used when decision-making depends on understanding subsurface quality or contamination over a defined vertical interval, while reducing the number of laboratory analyses required.

In clean fill and stockpile characterization plans, DDCS is typically used to verify whether material at different depths within a fill area, excavation, or stockpile is suitable for reuse or meets project cleanup criteria. The “discrete” part means samples are collected from individual depth slices rather than from a blended continuous core, and the “composite” part means selected aliquots from those slices are mixed together before laboratory testing.

## How it works

DDCS is usually applied in a structured sequence:

1. **Define the sampling interval**
   - The target depth range is divided into one or more **discrete depth increments**.
   - The increments are selected to represent vertical changes in material quality, potential contamination, or stratigraphy.

2. **Collect discrete samples by depth**
   - A field team advances sampling equipment to a specified depth.
   - Separate material is recovered from each chosen depth interval.
   - The samples remain identifiable by depth until compositing.

3. **Evaluate or combine aliquots**
   - Equal or proportionate portions from the discrete depth samples are combined into one composite sample.
   - The composite may represent:
     - a single vertical profile,
     - a specified fill lift,
     - or a decision unit spanning multiple depth intervals.

4. **Analyze the composite**
   - The composite sample is submitted for laboratory analysis.
   - Results are used to infer whether the represented depth interval is acceptable for the project objective, such as clean fill verification.

5. **Compare to action criteria**
   - Analytical results are compared with the project’s screening levels, cleanup criteria, or reuse thresholds.
   - If results exceed criteria, the project may require additional discrete sampling, reclassification of material, segregation, or disposal.

### Practical purpose

DDCS is useful when a project needs to balance two goals:
- **represent vertical variability accurately**, and
- **limit the number of laboratory analyses**.

In the source sampling plans, this type of sampling is used in the context of clean fill verification for areas such as excavations, basin fills, shoofly areas, and stockpiles.

## Variants

Common variants and closely related implementations include:

- **Single-depth discrete sampling**
  - Separate samples are collected at each depth and analyzed individually.
  - This provides the most vertical resolution but increases analytical cost.

- **Depth interval composite sampling**
  - Multiple depths within a defined interval are combined into one sample.
  - This is the closest general form of DDCS.

- **Incremental depth composite sampling**
  - Equal increments are taken across a depth interval, often to reduce bias from localized hotspots.

- **Stockpile depth composite sampling**
  - Samples are taken from different depths within a stockpile face or test pit and composited.
  - Useful when material is layered or when surface conditions may not represent deeper material.

- **Decision-unit compositing**
  - Samples from several points within a defined management unit are composited, sometimes across both horizontal and vertical dimensions.

### Alternatives

- **Discrete interval sampling**
  - No compositing; each depth sample is analyzed separately.
  - Better for identifying depth-specific contamination.

- **Grab sampling**
  - A single sample from one location/depth.
  - Simpler, but less representative.

- **Systematic grid sampling**
  - Samples collected on a spatial grid, often with or without compositing.
  - Better for areal characterization than depth-focused assessment.

- **Composite surface sampling**
  - Material from a surface layer only is composited; not depth-focused.

## Trade-offs

### Advantages

- **Lower analytical cost**
  - Fewer lab samples are needed than if each depth interval were analyzed separately.

- **Improved representativeness over a single grab**
  - Multiple depths are incorporated, reducing reliance on one sample.

- **Useful for vertical characterization**
  - Helps assess whether material varies with depth and whether a fill or stockpile is uniformly suitable.

- **Efficient for large clean-fill decisions**
  - Appropriate when the goal is to confirm overall compliance rather than map every contaminant hotspot.

### Limitations

- **Loss of depth-specific information**
  - Once samples are composited, it is harder to identify which depth contributed to an elevated result.

- **Potential dilution of hotspots**
  - A contaminated layer can be masked by cleaner material from adjacent depths.

- **Requires careful sample planning**
  - Composite design must be defensible: equal mass, consistent depth increments, and clear decision units.

- **Not ideal for heterogeneous material**
  - If conditions vary sharply with depth, discrete analysis may be necessary.

- **Interpretation depends on the purpose**
  - DDCS may be appropriate for clean fill verification but insufficient when regulatory or remedial decisions require precise contaminant location.

### Key considerations

- Define depth intervals before field work starts.
- Keep compositing rules consistent and documented.
- Preserve chain-of-custody and field records showing which depths contributed to the composite.
- Use more discrete sampling where uncertainty is high or where contamination is expected to vary vertically.
- Ensure the method aligns with the project’s Sampling Plan, Composite Sampling, and cleanup objectives.
