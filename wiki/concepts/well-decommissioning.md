## Definition

**Well decommissioning** (also referred to as well abandonment or well plugging) is the process of permanently sealing a well that is no longer in active use. The objective is to prevent the migration of fluids between subsurface formations, protect groundwater resources, eliminate physical hazards at the surface, and comply with regulatory requirements. The process applies to water wells, oil and gas wells, geothermal wells, and monitoring wells, each with specific technical standards.

## How it works

Well decommissioning involves several sequential steps tailored to the well’s construction, depth, and geological setting:

1. **Pre‑abandonment assessment** – Review of well records, geophysical logs, and integrity tests (e.g., cement bond log, pressure tests) to identify construction details and potential pathways for fluid migration.

2. **Well preparation** – Removal of downhole equipment (pumps, tubing, packers) and surface infrastructure (wellhead, casing stubs). The wellbore is cleaned of debris, scale, and hydrocarbons if present.

3. **Zonal isolation** – Perforations, open-hole intervals, and permeable zones are isolated using mechanical plugs (e.g., bridge plugs) or by placing cement across each interval. In many jurisdictions, the casing must be cut and removed at a specified depth, or the annulus must be sealed.

4. **Cement plugging** – Portland cement or specially blended cement is placed at designated intervals (e.g., above and below freshwater zones, at the base of the well, and at the surface). The cement volume is calculated to achieve a minimum height (e.g., 100 ft above the uppermost permeable zone) and is often verified by tagging or pressure testing.

5. **Surface abandonment** – The remaining casing is cut below grade (typically 3–5 feet), a steel plate or cement cap is installed, and the site is backfilled, graded, and revegetated. For water wells, a bentonite seal may replace cement in the upper portion to avoid fracturing.

6. **Documentation** – Reports detailing plugging depths, cement volumes, and post‑abandonment monitoring (e.g., static water level, water quality samples) are submitted to the regulatory authority.

## Variants

- **Water well decommissioning** – Emphasizes sealing of the borehole to prevent surface contamination and inter‑aquifer flow. Often requires removal of the entire casing and placement of a bentonite‑cement grout from bottom to top.

- **Oil and gas well abandonment** – More complex due to multiple casing strings, hydrocarbon zones, and high‑pressure formations. May involve cutting and pulling casing, setting cement plugs across each producing zone, and installing a surface cement cap after cutting the wellhead.

- **Monitoring well decommissioning** – Usually shallower; involves removing the screen and filter pack, then filling the borehole with a low‑permeability grout (cement‑bentonite) under pressure to ensure a continuous seal.

- **Geothermal well decommissioning** – Must address high temperatures and potential scaling. Cement formulations with additives for thermal stability are used, and the well may be left with a surface seal that allows future re‑entry if needed.

- **Temporary versus permanent abandonment** – In some industries, a well can be temporarily abandoned (e.g., for economic reasons) with a retrievable surface plug, but permanent abandonment requires non‑retrievable cement seals.

## Trade‑offs

| Consideration | Description |
|---------------|-------------|
| **Cost** | Full decommissioning can be expensive (tens to hundreds of thousands of dollars) depending on depth, number of casing strings, and regulatory requirements. Cutting corners may lead to future liabilities. |
| **Regulatory compliance** | Failure to meet minimum plugging depths, cement quality standards, or documentation can result in fines and forced remediation. |
| **Long‑term integrity** | Cement degradation (e.g., from carbonation, sulfate attack, or thermal cycling) can create leakage pathways over decades. Use of additives (fly ash, latex) may improve durability but increase cost. |
| **Surface restoration** | In sensitive environments, backfill settlement may require repeated revegetation or erosion control. |
| **Re‑entry potential** | Permanent abandonment usually eliminates the possibility of future use. For wells that may be reactivated (e.g., geothermal), a different abandonment method (e.g., retrievable packer) may be chosen, but is less secure. |
| **Groundwater protection** | Inadequate sealing of the annular space can connect freshwater and saline aquifers. Proper grouting is critical, but placement in a wet borehole can cause dilution or channeling. |
