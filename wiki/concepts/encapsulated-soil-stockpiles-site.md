## Definition

An **Encapsulated Soil Stockpiles Site** is a contaminated-site management area where excavated soil is temporarily or permanently placed in engineered stockpiles and then **encapsulated** so the soil remains isolated from rainwater, runoff, erosion, and direct human or ecological contact. The purpose is to prevent contaminants in the soil from migrating into groundwater, surface water, or air while remediation or disposal decisions are being made.

In practice, the term usually refers to a site-specific remedy or interim control used at a contaminated site, especially where soil cannot be immediately removed offsite. The stockpiles are managed as a contained, monitored unit rather than as open piles of soil.

## How it works

Encapsulation relies on physical containment and water management. A typical encapsulated soil stockpile system includes:

- **Prepared base layer**: A compacted pad, asphalt, concrete, or low-permeability liner placed beneath the stockpile to prevent downward migration.
- **Side containment**: Berms, walls, sandbags, or engineered barriers to retain the pile and prevent lateral spread.
- **Cover system**: Plastic sheeting, geotextile, soil cover, or a multi-layer cap to limit infiltration and reduce wind dispersion.
- **Stormwater controls**: Diversion berms, drainage swales, and grading to keep clean runoff away from the pile and prevent contaminated runoff from leaving the area.
- **Inspection and maintenance**: Regular checks for tears, settling, erosion, ponding, or damaged containment features.
- **Sampling and monitoring**: Soil, runoff, and sometimes groundwater monitoring to confirm the stockpile is not causing releases.

The operational sequence is generally:

1. **Excavation** of contaminated soil from the impacted area.
2. **Placement into the stockpile footprint** on the prepared containment surface.
3. **Encapsulation** with cover materials and perimeter controls.
4. **Monitoring and maintenance** over the active period.
5. **Final disposition** by treatment, reuse, or offsite disposal, or conversion to a more permanent remedy if approved.

Encapsulation is often used when soil excavation is necessary to address an immediate hazard, but final disposition is delayed by permitting, transport logistics, or additional characterization needs.

## Variants

Common variants include:

- **Temporary stockpile encapsulation**: Short-term containment during active remediation.
- **Permanent or long-term encapsulation**: Stockpiles left in place as part of a remedial action, with engineered caps and institutional controls.
- **Onsite reuse stockpiles**: Cleaned or treated soil held temporarily before being reused onsite.
- **Hazardous-waste stockpiles**: More stringent containment for soil with higher contaminant concentrations or regulated hazardous characteristics.
- **Indoor or covered stockpiles**: Used where weather exposure must be minimized or space is constrained.
- **Inert encapsulation**: Stockpiles of relatively low-risk soil isolated primarily for erosion and dust control rather than leachate containment.

Alternatives to encapsulated stockpiling include offsite disposal, direct soil treatment, thermal treatment, stabilization/solidification, or leaving soil in place under an engineered cap.

## Trade-offs

Key trade-offs include:

- **Speed vs. permanence**: Encapsulation is often fast to deploy, but it may be only an interim measure.
- **Containment reliability**: It reduces exposure risk, but performance depends on maintenance and integrity of liners, covers, and drainage controls.
- **Space requirements**: Stockpiles need substantial onsite area, which may be limited.
- **Weather sensitivity**: Heavy rain, wind, or flooding can stress containment systems.
- **Monitoring burden**: Requires routine inspections and sometimes sampling to verify continued effectiveness.
- **Long-term liability**: Leaving contaminated soil onsite can create ongoing management obligations.
- **Regulatory complexity**: The remedy may need to comply with waste handling, stormwater, air quality, and site cleanup requirements.

Encapsulation is best understood as a containment strategy rather than a treatment technology: it controls exposure and migration, but it does not necessarily destroy contaminants.
