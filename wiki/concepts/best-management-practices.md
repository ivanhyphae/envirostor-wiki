## Best Management Practices

### Definition
Best Management Practices (BMPs) are a set of operational, structural, and managerial techniques, schedules of activities, and prohibitions designed to prevent or reduce the discharge of pollutants and adverse environmental impacts from diffuse sources. BMPs are most commonly applied to stormwater management, erosion and sediment control, agricultural runoff, and industrial or construction site operations. They aim to maintain or restore water quality, protect aquatic ecosystems, and comply with regulatory requirements such as National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System (NPDES) permits.

### How It Works
BMPs function by addressing pollutant loads and hydrologic alteration at the source or during conveyance and treatment. The technical framework involves:

- **Identification of potential pollutants** (sediment, nutrients, heavy metals, bacteria, oil/grease) and the pathways by which they reach receiving waters.
- **Selection of appropriate BMPs** based on site-specific factors: soil type, slope, rainfall frequency, drainage area, land use, regulatory targets, and receiving-water sensitivity.
- **Implementation** through design, construction, inspection, and maintenance protocols. For example, a stormwater detention basin is designed to capture runoff and allow sedimentation of particulates before outlet release.
- **Performance monitoring** to evaluate removal efficiency and adjust operations. Common parameters include total suspended solids reduction, nutrient attenuation, and flow volume control.

BMPs can be categorized by function:
- **Source Control** – prevent pollutant generation (e.g., street sweeping, fertilizer management, spill prevention plans).
- **Treatment Control** – remove pollutants after entrainment (e.g., bioretention cells, sand filters, constructed wetlands).
- **Flow Control** – manage runoff volume and velocity to reduce erosion and flooding (e.g., check dams, grassed swales, permeable pavement).

The selection and combination of BMPs into a “treatment train” is standard practice, as no single BMP fully addresses all conditions. Regulatory documents (like surface water quality letters) often mandate specific BMP families (stabilized construction entrances, silt fences, temporary seeding) for construction sites and urban developments.

### Variants
BMPs are not monolithic; they vary by sector, medium, and regulatory context:

- **Structural BMPs**: Physical engineered systems (e.g., stormwater ponds, rain gardens, underground detention vaults, rock check dams).
- **Non-structural BMPs**: Policy, education, and behavioral practices (e.g., employee training, site-specific erosion control plans, land-use restrictions, composting by-laws).
- **Agricultural BMPs**: Conservation tillage, cover crops, riparian buffers, nutrient management plans, irrigation schedules.
- **Industrial BMPs**: Secondary containment for chemical storage, drip pans, spill kits, equipment washing pads.
- **Construction BMPs**: Silt fences, sediment basins, inlet protection, stockpile covers, stabilization following grading.
- **Permanent vs. Temporary**: Permanent BMPs (e.g., constructed wetlands) remain in place; temporary BMPs (e.g., straw wattles) are retired after construction completion.
- **Region-Specific Guidelines**: In surface water quality permits, jurisdictions may require “enhanced” BMPs for sensitive waters (e.g., 100% sediment control vs. 80% baseline).

### Trade-offs
Implementing BMPs involves balancing cost, effectiveness, maintenance, and unintended consequences:

- **Efficacy vs. Space**: High-efficiency structural BMPs often require large footprints (wetlands, ponds), which may be unavailable in dense urban areas. Underground systems offer space savings but at higher capital cost.
- **Maintenance Burden**: BMPs require routine inspection, sediment removal, vegetation management, and equipment replacement. Neglected BMPs can become pollutant sources (e.g., clogged sediment basin releasing concentrated flows).
- **Cost Trade-offs**: Non-structural BMPs (education, policies) have lower upfront costs but variable adoption rates; structural BMPs are expensive to install but provide consistent removal if maintained.
- **Biological Consequences**: Wetland BMPs may create mosquito habitat; some vegetated systems can export nutrients if not harvested (e.g., high nitrogen from decaying plant matter).
- **Regulatory Risk**: Permitting agencies may require BMPs with high removal rates for TSS or phosphorus, even if site conditions marginalize those benefits (e.g., high groundwater limiting infiltration BMPs). Over-reliance on a single BMP can lead to permit violations if it fails.
- **Climate Adaptation**: BMP sizing based on historical rainfall is increasingly inadequate under climate change; over-designer storms cause bypass and erosion, reducing overall effectiveness.
