## Chromium

### Overview
Chromium is a metal contaminant of potential concern at the Caltrans Modesto Soil Stockpiles site (SR 99 and SR 132, Stanislaus County). It is present in the stockpile fill materials, which originated from excavations of former FMC Corporation evaporation ponds that received liquid processing waste from the 1950s to 1970s. The stockpiles (~121,000 cubic yards across Stockpiles 1, 2, and 3) include native soil and pond tailings placed in the early 1960s for the future SR 132 alignment. Chromium is classified as a Title 22 (CAM 17) metal under California hazardous waste criteria and is subject to Cal/OSHA regulation as chromium VI (T8 CCR §1532.2). At this site, chromium has been detected in soil at concentrations up to 26 mg/kg but was not identified as a chemical of potential concern (COPC) in the 2007 Human Health Risk Assessment (HHRA); barium and PAHs were the primary COPCs. No groundwater exceedances for chromium have been documented in the available record.

**Soil concentrations – 2012 Supplemental Investigation (Fenceline Workplan)**

A soil sample collected on June 21, 2012 (Client ID not fully legible in source) and analyzed by Advanced Technology Laboratories (ATL Work Order B2G0509/B2G0389) reported:

- Total chromium: **21 mg/kg** (EPA 6020 / ICP-MS; PQL 10 mg/kg, MDL 3.1 mg/kg) — detected above the reporting limit.
- STLC (WET) leachate test: **Chromium not detected** (< 1.0 mg/L by EPA 6010B; PQL 1.0 mg/L, MDL 0.03 mg/L), indicating low leachability under WET conditions.

This 2012 sample was part of the supplemental site investigation under Contract 06A1580, Task Order 44. The workplan (dated September 18, 2012) included 29 fenceline borings (5-ft depth), 22 perimeter borings (3-ft depth), and 5 cadmium-focused borings (20-ft depth) — the cadmium borings targeted Stockpiles 2 and 3 based on elevated cadmium detections from 2004, not chromium.

**Groundwater monitoring**

Eight monitoring wells (MW-1 through MW-8) were installed adjacent to the stockpiles in 2006. Two groundwater sampling events were conducted. The 2012 workplan states: *"The results of recent sampling indicates that the groundwater meets drinking water standards for those constituents analyzed."*

A 138-page groundwater monitoring report dated September 2012 (source document S9525-06-44A) covers chromium and other metals in groundwater, but full extraction of numerical results was not possible from the available source. No chromium exceedances in groundwater are documented in the provided record.

**Surface water sampling – March 2018**

Surface water samples were collected from drainage features adjacent to the stockpiles (Blocks 1–4) in March 2018. Analytical results for total chromium exist in the source (S1200-01-01) but numerical values could not be extracted from the encrypted/compressed data.

**Spatial distribution**

Chromium concentrations in soil do not show a strong spatial pattern specific to chromium alone. The stockpile material is described as *"layered, poorly graded sand and silty sand similar to underlying native alluvial deposits of the Modesto Formation."* Maximum stockpile fill thickness is approximately 25 feet. First groundwater is at 30–40 feet below grade.

### Regulatory status

**Applicable standards:**
- California MCL for total chromium (drinking water): 50 µg/L
- California MCL for hexavalent chromium (Cr(VI)): 10 µg/L
- Cal/OSHA PEL-TWA for chromium VI: 5 µg/m³ (T8 CCR §1532.2)
- California Human Health Screening Level (CHHSL) for chromium (commercial/industrial): not documented in these sources; HHRA did not identify chromium as a COPC
- Title 22 (CAM 17) hazardous waste threshold for chromium: applicable for waste classification; STLC results for chromium were non-detect (<1.0 mg/L)

**Regulatory agencies:**
- Lead oversight: Department of Toxic Substances Control (DTSC)
- Cooperating agency: Central Valley Regional Water Quality Control Board (RWQCB)
- Responsible party: California Department of Transportation (Caltrans), District 6

**Compliance status:**
- Soils: Chromium concentrations in stockpile soils (11–26 mg/kg) are typical of background for the region and were not identified as a COPC in the 2007 HHRA. DTSC's December 17, 2009 letter found that the stockpiles *"as currently managed by Caltrans on Caltrans ROW do not pose a risk to human health"* for workers, trespassers, and adjacent residents. The 2007 HHRA evaluated metals (notably barium) and PAHs, not chromium.
- Groundwater: No documented exceedances. The 2012 workplan states groundwater meets drinking water standards.
- DTSC directed Caltrans to continue stockpile management and maintain the existing groundwater monitoring system.

### Open items

1. **Chromium speciation**: The available sources report only total chromium. Hexavalent chromium (Cr(VI)) was not separately analyzed in the 2004 PSI or the 2012 supplemental investigation. Given the lower MCL for Cr(VI) (10 µg/L vs. 50 µg/L total), speciation data may be needed to fully assess groundwater compliance.

2. **Surface water data**: The March 2018 surface water sampling results for chromium could not be extracted from source documents. These results should be reviewed for exceedances.

3. **Post-2018 monitoring**: No monitoring data beyond March 2018 were provided in the source materials. Current site status is not documented.

4. **Groundwater-surface water pathway**: The potential for chromium migration from stockpile soils to groundwater and then to surface water (Modesto Irrigation District Lateral #4 crosses beneath Stockpile 3) has not been characterized in the available record.

5. **Remedial action triggers**: No corrective actions (soil removal, capping, treatment) specific to chromium have been documented in the provided sources. The stockpiles remain in place pending future construction of the SR 99/132 interchange.
