## Definition

Groundwater Monitoring Event September 2013 refers to a scheduled groundwater sampling and/or measurement event conducted in September 2013 for the Modesto Stockpiles site. In environmental monitoring practice, a groundwater monitoring event is a time-specific field investigation used to collect groundwater-level data and water-quality samples from designated wells to evaluate site conditions, track contaminant behavior, and support regulatory or remedial decision-making.

Based on the source file name, this event is associated with the September 2013 monitoring round for the Modesto Stockpiles groundwater program.

## How it works

A groundwater monitoring event typically follows a standard field and laboratory workflow:

1. **Preparation**
   - Select monitoring wells included in the event.
   - Review prior sampling history, site conditions, and any event-specific instructions.
   - Prepare sampling equipment, containers, field forms, calibration materials, and quality assurance supplies.

2. **Field mobilization**
   - Visit the site and locate each monitoring well.
   - Inspect well condition, integrity, labeling, and accessibility.

3. **Water-level measurement**
   - Measure static groundwater depth in each well, usually relative to a surveyed measuring point.
   - Use the water levels to estimate groundwater elevation and, when multiple wells are measured, infer flow direction.

4. **Well purging and sampling**
   - Remove standing water from the well if required by the sampling protocol.
   - Collect groundwater samples using approved low-flow, bailer, or pump methods.
   - Record field parameters such as pH, temperature, conductivity, dissolved oxygen, turbidity, and oxidation-reduction potential where applicable.

5. **Sample handling**
   - Preserve samples according to analyte-specific requirements.
   - Maintain chain-of-custody documentation.
   - Ship samples to an accredited laboratory for analysis.

6. **Laboratory analysis and reporting**
   - Analyze samples for site-specific constituents of concern.
   - Compare results to historical trends, background conditions, and applicable cleanup criteria.
   - Summarize findings in a monitoring report or event memo.

In this context, the September 2013 event would have generated a dataset representing groundwater conditions at that point in time for the Modesto Stockpiles site.

## Variants

Groundwater monitoring events can vary by objective and method:

- **Water-level-only event**: Measures groundwater elevations without collecting chemistry samples.
- **Full sampling event**: Includes both water-level measurements and laboratory samples.
- **Low-flow sampling event**: Uses controlled pumping to minimize disturbance and turbidity.
- **Conventional purge-and-sample event**: Uses well purging prior to sample collection.
- **Compliance monitoring event**: Conducted to satisfy permit or regulatory requirements.
- **Remedial performance monitoring event**: Used to evaluate whether cleanup actions are reducing contaminant concentrations.
- **Trend monitoring event**: Repeated sampling round focused on long-term changes rather than immediate compliance.

The September 2013 event is best understood as one round within an ongoing site monitoring program rather than a unique monitoring technology.

## Trade-offs

Key considerations and limitations of groundwater monitoring events include:

- **Temporal representativeness**: A single event captures conditions only at one point in time; groundwater quality can vary seasonally or after rainfall.
- **Well-network dependence**: Conclusions are limited by the number, location, and depth of the monitoring wells.
- **Sampling disturbance**: Purging and sampling can alter chemistry, especially in low-yield wells or highly turbid conditions.
- **Analytical constraints**: Laboratory detection limits and analyte selection affect what can be concluded from the data.
- **Field variability**: Changes in temperature, barometric pressure, or well recovery can influence measured water levels and sample quality.
- **Interpretive uncertainty**: Contaminant plume behavior is inferred from sparse points, so interpolation between wells introduces uncertainty.

Despite these limitations, recurring monitoring events are valuable because they provide consistent, comparable data over time.
