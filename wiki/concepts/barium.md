# Barium

## Definition

Barium (Ba) is a soft, silvery-white alkaline earth metal that is highly reactive and never found in its elemental form in nature. It is the 56th element on the periodic table, with an atomic weight of 137.33. In environmental and construction contexts, barium primarily occurs as a trace constituent in soil and groundwater, often associated with naturally occurring minerals such as barite (barium sulfate, BaSO₄) or as a byproduct of industrial activities.

## How it works

Barium is naturally present in many geologic materials, including soil and rock, where it can leach into groundwater under certain pH and redox conditions. Its mobility in the subsurface depends on:
- **Solubility controls**: Barium sulfate is very insoluble (Ksp ≈ 1.1×10⁻¹⁰), which typically limits barium concentrations in sulfate-rich groundwater. Barium can also precipitate as barium carbonate.
- **Sorption**: Barium readily adsorbs to clay minerals and organic matter, reducing its mobility.
- **Competition**: In high-calcium or high-iron environments, barium may be displaced or co-precipitated.

In environmental monitoring, barium is analyzed as a dissolved metal in groundwater samples (typically by ICP-MS or ICP-OES) with detection limits in the low µg/L range. The California Maximum Contaminant Level (MCL) for barium in drinking water is 1,000 µg/L (1 mg/L), based on cardiovascular toxicity.

## Variants

- **Soluble barium compounds**: Barium chloride (BaCl₂) and barium nitrate (Ba(NO₃)₂) are highly soluble and toxic if ingested; used in industrial processes and pyrotechnics.
- **Insoluble barium compounds**: Barium sulfate (barite) is extremely insoluble and used as a weighting agent in drilling fluids and as a radiographic contrast agent due to its low toxicity.
- **Barium carbonate (BaCO₃)**: Moderately soluble (13 mg/L at 20°C), used in ceramics, glass, and rodenticides.
- **Barium in soil**: Naturally occurring background concentrations in California soils typically range from 50 to 600 mg/kg, but may be elevated in areas with barite deposits or industrial contamination.

## Trade-offs

- **Toxicity vs. detectability**: While barium can cause cardiac and neuromuscular effects at elevated levels, its relatively high MCL (1 mg/L) means that low-level exceedances are uncommon. However, in sulfate-poor groundwater, barium can become more mobile and problematic.
- **Analytical interference**: Barium can be confused with other elements (e.g., strontium, calcium) in spectroscopic analysis if not properly corrected.
- **Remediation**: Barium is difficult to remove from groundwater because its solubility is controlled by sulfate availability—adding sulfate can precipitate barite, but may cause other issues (e.g., sulfate-reducing bacteria).
- **Natural background**: High natural barium can result in exceedances of background thresholds without anthropogenic contamination, complicating liability assessments in cleanup projects.
