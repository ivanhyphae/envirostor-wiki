## Definition

Site Specific Primary Chemicals Of Concern are the specific contaminants identified for a given site as the main chemicals requiring evaluation, monitoring, and/or cleanup. In environmental investigation and remediation documents, this term usually refers to the subset of compounds that are most important at that location because they are known or suspected to be present at elevated concentrations, are associated with the site’s historical activities, or drive risk-based decisions.

In the Caltrans materials provided, the concept appears in the context of a stockpile / groundwater monitoring project, where site-specific contaminants are selected from the broader universe of possible analytes and treated as the primary drivers for assessment. In practice, these are often the constituents used to focus sampling programs, compare against cleanup criteria, and determine whether additional corrective action is needed.

## How it works

The designation of site-specific primary chemicals of concern typically follows a structured environmental decision process:

1. **Identify site history and potential releases**  
   Past land use, material handling, stockpiling, fueling, maintenance, or industrial operations are reviewed to infer which chemicals may be present.

2. **Review available analytical data**  
   Soil, groundwater, soil vapor, or waste characterization results are screened to determine which compounds are actually detected and at what concentrations.

3. **Compare to regulatory thresholds**  
   Detections are compared with applicable standards, screening levels, or risk-based criteria. Constituents that exceed or approach relevant limits often become primary chemicals of concern.

4. **Select the key site drivers**  
   The list is narrowed to the contaminants most relevant to the site. These are the chemicals most likely to:
   - pose a human health or ecological risk,
   - migrate in groundwater or soil gas,
   - require treatment or long-term monitoring,
   - determine the remedial strategy.

5. **Use them to guide monitoring and remediation**  
   Once identified, the primary chemicals of concern become the focus of:
   - sampling plans,
   - laboratory analytical suites,
   - trend analysis,
   - exposure pathway evaluation,
   - remedy selection and performance monitoring.

In a groundwater monitoring context, this approach avoids treating every possible chemical as equally important. Instead, the investigation centers on the contaminants that are most site-relevant and most likely to influence regulatory or engineering decisions.

## Variants

Common variants and related terms include:

- **Chemicals of Concern (COCs)** — the general term for contaminants that matter at a site.
- **Primary Chemicals of Concern** — the main contaminants that drive risk or remediation.
- **Site-Specific COCs** — emphasizes that the list is unique to the site rather than generic.
- **Contaminants of Potential Concern (COPCs)** — broader screening set used before narrowing to primary constituents.
- **Primary Contaminants / Indicator Constituents** — sometimes used when a smaller set of chemicals is selected to represent site conditions.
- **Analyte list** — the laboratory test panel; broader than the actual chemicals of concern.

In some projects, the primary chemicals may change over time as more data are collected. For example, initial screening may identify a broad set of COPCs, while later investigations define a smaller set of site-specific primary chemicals of concern.

## Trade-offs

Key considerations and limitations include:

- **Narrowing the list improves focus but can miss emerging issues**  
  Concentrating only on the main chemicals may overlook less obvious contaminants, degradation products, or co-contaminants.

- **Site specificity reduces over-monitoring**  
  A tailored list avoids unnecessary sampling and analysis of irrelevant compounds, saving cost and time.

- **The designation depends on data quality**  
  Poor sampling design, limited detections, or incomplete site history can lead to an incomplete or biased list.

- **Regulatory relevance can change**  
  Cleanup standards, toxicity information, and exposure assumptions may evolve, affecting which chemicals remain “primary.”

- **Hydrogeologic behavior matters**  
  A chemical with a low concentration may still be important if it is highly mobile, persistent, or toxic.

- **Long-term monitoring may require a stable indicator set**  
  Primary chemicals are often chosen for consistency across events, but that can reduce flexibility if site conditions change.
