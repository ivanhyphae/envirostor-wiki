## Definition

**Stormwater sampling** is the collection and analysis of runoff from rain or snowmelt to determine its physical, chemical, and sometimes biological characteristics. It is used to evaluate the quality of water flowing from roads, construction sites, industrial areas, stockpiles, parking lots, and other land surfaces into drains, creeks, rivers, or treatment systems.

Stormwater sampling is a core part of stormwater management and water quality monitoring. It helps identify pollutants, assess compliance with discharge requirements, and guide pollution-prevention practices.

## How it works

Stormwater sampling typically follows a runoff event. Because stormwater is intermittent and highly variable, sampling is designed to capture water during an actual flow event rather than from a standing body of water.

Common steps include:

1. **Site selection**
   - Identify discharge points, outfalls, inlets, or drainage channels.
   - Prioritize locations with likely pollutant sources, such as construction stormwater, industrial yards, or material stockpiles.

2. **Event monitoring**
   - Track rainfall and runoff conditions so sampling occurs during or soon after a storm.
   - Many programs specify event thresholds, such as a minimum rainfall amount or a minimum dry period before sampling.

3. **Sample collection**
   - Collect water manually or with automated samplers.
   - Samples may be grab samples taken at a single point in time, or composite samples built from multiple aliquots over the course of a storm event.
   - Field staff may also measure flow, pH, temperature, conductivity, turbidity, and other in-situ parameters.

4. **Preservation and chain of custody**
   - Samples are preserved according to analyte-specific requirements, often by cooling and using appropriate containers.
   - Documentation tracks who collected the sample, when, where, and how it was handled.

5. **Laboratory analysis**
   - Samples are analyzed for target constituents such as suspended solids, nutrients, metals, oil and grease, hydrocarbons, bacteria, or specific industrial compounds.
   - Results are compared with permit limits, benchmark values, background levels, or site action levels.

6. **Interpretation and response**
   - Results are used to identify pollutant sources, evaluate best management practices, and determine whether corrective actions are needed.
   - Repeated sampling over multiple storm events is often necessary because concentrations can vary widely from one storm to another.

Technical considerations include:
- **First flush effects**, where the beginning of runoff may contain disproportionately high pollutant concentrations.
- **Flow variability**, since pollutant mass depends on both concentration and discharge volume.
- **Timing sensitivity**, because delays can miss peak runoff conditions.
- **Representativeness**, since one sample may not characterize an entire storm event or season.

## Variants

Common variants and implementations include:

- **Grab sampling**
  - A single sample collected at one moment in time.
  - Useful for capturing the first flush or a specific discharge condition.

- **Composite sampling**
  - Multiple subsamples collected over time or proportional to flow and combined into one sample.
  - Better for characterizing average event quality.

- **Flow-weighted sampling**
  - Samples collected in proportion to runoff volume.
  - Helps estimate pollutant loads more accurately.

- **Time-weighted sampling**
  - Samples collected at fixed time intervals during a storm.
  - Simpler than flow-weighted methods but can be less representative when flow changes rapidly.

- **Manual sampling**
  - Field personnel collect samples directly.
  - Flexible, but labor-intensive and may miss short-lived peaks.

- **Automated sampling**
  - Programmable samplers collect samples without continuous on-site presence.
  - Useful for remote sites or rapidly changing storm events.

- **Dry-weather screening**
  - Sampling conducted during no-rain conditions to detect illicit discharges, groundwater intrusion, or persistent drainage issues.
  - Not strictly stormwater sampling, but often used in the same monitoring programs.

- **Permit-driven monitoring**
  - Sampling required under NPDES or similar regulatory programs.
  - Focuses on compliance and documented environmental performance.

## Trade-offs

Stormwater sampling has several important trade-offs:

- **Event specificity vs. practicality**
  - The most informative samples are collected during actual storm events, but storms are unpredictable and may occur outside normal work hours.

- **Grab vs. composite**
  - Grab samples can capture peak conditions but may not represent the whole event.
  - Composite samples provide a broader picture but can dilute short-term spikes.

- **Accuracy vs. cost**
  - Automated, flow-weighted systems are more representative but require equipment, calibration, maintenance, and power.
  - Manual sampling is less expensive but more labor-intensive and less reliable for fast-changing events.

- **Representativeness vs. site constraints**
  - Ideal sampling points are often difficult to access, unsafe during storms, or hydraulically challenging.

- **Concentration vs. load**
  - Concentration data alone may underestimate impact if runoff volumes are large.
  - Load estimation requires flow measurement, which adds complexity.

- **Short-term variability**
  - Stormwater quality can change quickly within a single event and across seasons.
  - Multiple events are often needed to draw robust conclusions.

- **Chain-of-custody and contamination risk**
  - Field sampling must control for contamination, especially when analyzing trace metals, hydrocarbons, or bacteria.
