## Definition

An **Underground Storage Tank** (UST) is a tank and any underground piping connected to the tank that has at least 10 percent of its combined volume (including pipes) beneath the surface of the ground. USTs are primarily used to store regulated substances such as petroleum products (e.g., gasoline, diesel, heating oil), hazardous chemicals, or wastes. They are governed by strict environmental regulations to prevent leaks that could contaminate soil and groundwater.

## How It Works

USTs are designed as closed systems consisting of several key components:

- **Tank body**: Typically made of steel (with or without cathodic protection), fiberglass-reinforced plastic (FRP), or a composite material. Steel tanks require corrosion protection (e.g., coatings, sacrificial anodes, or impressed current). FRP tanks are inherently corrosion-resistant.
- **Piping**: Connected to the tank for product fill, withdrawal, venting, and monitoring. Piping must be double-walled or protected from leaks.
- **Overfill prevention**: Devices such as automatic shut-off valves or alarm systems that stop filling when the tank reaches 90% capacity to prevent spills.
- **Leak detection**: Required under regulations (e.g., US EPA 40 CFR Part 280). Methods include:
  - **Automatic tank gauging**: Continuous monitoring of product level and temperature to detect small changes in volume.
  - **Interstitial monitoring**: Detection of leaks between the primary and secondary walls of double-walled tanks.
  - **Vapor monitoring**: Sensors in the tank cavity that detect vapors from leaked hydrocarbons.
  - **Groundwater monitoring**: Wells around the tank site to sample for contaminants.
- **Spill containment**: A spill bucket or containment sump around the fill pipe to capture small spills during delivery.
- **Release detection**: Must be performed monthly (for petroleum USTs) or more frequently, depending on the system.

A UST system is buried in an excavated pit, backfilled with sand or gravel, and covered with soil or pavement. Vent pipes extend above ground to equalize pressure during filling and emptying. The tank’s structural integrity is periodically tested (e.g., tightness testing) or monitored through corrosion prevention systems.

## Variants

- **Single-walled USTs**: Older tanks with only one layer of steel or FRP. No built-in leak containment; rely on external leak detection. Largely phased out in many jurisdictions due to higher leak risk.
- **Double-walled USTs**: A tank within a tank (two concentric layers). The interstitial space between walls is monitored for leaks. Offers secondary containment.
- **Compartmentalized USTs**: A single tank divided into multiple compartments for storing different products (e.g., regular and premium gasoline). Each compartment has independent piping and monitoring.
- **Underground storage tank systems with cathodic protection**: Steel tanks coated and equipped with sacrificial anodes (zinc or magnesium) or impressed current systems to prevent corrosion. Common for older installations that have been retrofitted.
- **Fiberglass USTs**: Non-metallic, corrosion-resistant tanks. Common in new installations, especially for petroleum products.
- **Hazardous substance USTs**: Designed to store hazardous chemicals (e.g., solvents, acids) rather than petroleum. Subject to more stringent leak detection and containment requirements under RCRA Subtitle C.
- **Abandoned or closed-in-place USTs**: Tanks that have been emptied, cleaned, and filled with inert material (e.g., sand or foam) or left in place under regulatory closure to avoid excavation costs. These no longer hold product but may still pose environmental risks if not properly sealed.

## Trade-offs

| Aspect | Considerations |
|--------|---------------|
| **Cost** | UST installation is expensive (excavation, tank, piping, monitoring equipment, regulatory fees). Double-walled and fiberglass tanks have higher upfront costs but lower long-term maintenance. |
| **Environmental risk** | Leaks from USTs can cause severe soil and groundwater contamination, leading to costly remediation and liability. Double-walled tanks with interstitial monitoring greatly reduce but do not eliminate risk. |
| **Regulatory burden** | UST owners must comply with federal, state, and local regulations (e.g., EPA 40 CFR Part 280, state UST programs). This includes registration, periodic testing, recordkeeping, and financial responsibility (insurance or bond) for cleanup costs. |
| **Lifecycle** | Steel USTs typically last 20–30 years with proper corrosion protection; fiberglass tanks may last 30–50 years. Older single-walled tanks have shorter useful lives and higher failure rates. |
| **Space and accessibility** | USTs are buried and require excavation for major repairs or removal. Aboveground storage tanks (ASTs) are often easier to inspect and maintain but take up surface space and have different regulatory requirements. |
| **Flexibility** | USTs are permanent fixtures – changing product type or capacity requires major work. Compartmentalization provides limited flexibility. |
| **Monitoring** | Automated leak detection systems require power and periodic calibration. Manual methods (e.g., tank gauging) are less reliable but lower cost. |

A key trade-off is between initial investment and long-term operational liability: a cheap single-walled UST may save money upfront but expose the owner to enormous cleanup costs if a leak occurs. Double-walled or fiberglass systems with robust monitoring are recommended for high-risk sites (e.g., near drinking water wells).
