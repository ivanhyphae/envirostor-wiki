## Dissolved Metals

### Definition
Dissolved metals are metallic elements that are present in aqueous solution, typically defined as those that pass through a 0.45‑micron filter. In environmental chemistry, this operational definition distinguishes them from **particulate metals** (those retained on the filter) and **total metals** (the sum of dissolved and particulate fractions). Dissolved metals are often the most bioavailable and mobile form in aquatic systems, making them a key measure in groundwater and surface water quality assessments.

### How it works
The determination of dissolved metals starts with field filtration: a water sample is passed through a 0.45‑µm membrane filter immediately upon collection, and the filtrate is preserved (commonly with nitric acid) to keep metals in solution. In the laboratory, the filtrate is analyzed using techniques such as inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP‑MS) or atomic absorption spectroscopy (AAS). The result represents the concentration of metal ions and soluble complexes, excluding metals bound to suspended solids or colloids larger than 0.45 µm.

This operational definition is important because metal speciation (e.g., free ion > hydrolysis species > organic complexes) determines toxicity and transport behavior. For example, dissolved copper (Cu²⁺) is more toxic to aquatic organisms than copper bound to organic matter, even though both may pass a 0.45‑µm filter. Regulatory criteria (e.g., USEPA water quality criteria) often use dissolved metal concentrations because they correlate better with bioaccumulation and ecological effects than total metals.

In groundwater monitoring, such as the Caltrans Modesto Stockpiles investigations (reports S9800‑01‑17 and S9800‑01‑17A), dissolved metals are measured to assess the mobility of contaminants from stockpiled materials into the aquifer. Sample collection follows strict protocols to avoid contamination and to preserve in‑situ redox conditions (e.g., using low‑flow sampling). Typical parameters include dissolved arsenic, chromium, lead, nickel, and zinc.

### Variants
- **Total recoverable metals** – sample is acid‑digested to dissolve all metal-containing phases; this includes both dissolved and particulate fractions.
- **Toxicologically relevant dissolved metals** – some methods (e.g., diffusive gradients in thin films, DGT) measure the labile fraction considered bioavailable, potentially refining the operational 0.45‑µm cutoff.
- **Field‑filtered vs. lab‑filtered** – filtration may occur on‑site (to avoid changes during transport) or later in the laboratory; differences can arise due to precipitation or adsorption.
- **Speciated dissolved metals** – advanced analyses (ion‑selective electrodes, equilibrium modeling) separate dissolved metals into individual species (e.g., free ion, carbonate complexes).

### Trade-offs
- **Bioavailability relevance**: Dissolved metals are a better proxy for toxicity than total metals, but the 0.45‑µm cutoff may include some colloidal metals that are not truly bioavailableholistic.
- **Operational definition**: The filter pore size is arbitrary; smaller colloids (<0.45 µm) can still carry toxic metals (e.g., iron‑oxyhydroxide nanoparticles). Conversely, some truly dissolved organic‑metal complexes may be filtered out if the organic matter is large.
- **Sample integrity**: Filtration must be done quickly; delays can cause sorption to container walls or precipitation. Redox‑sensitive metals (e.g., iron, manganese) may change speciation after exposure to air, requiring field preservation.
- **Cost and time**: Dissolved metals analysis requires separate field equipment (filters, syringes, dedicated preservatives) and often duplicate samples for quality control, increasing project costs compared to total metals analysis.
