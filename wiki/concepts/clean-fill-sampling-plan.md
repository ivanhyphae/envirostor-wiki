## Definition

A **Clean Fill Sampling Plan** (CFSP) is a documented, site-specific protocol for collecting and analyzing soil or other earthen materials to verify that they meet regulatory criteria for classification as "clean fill" — meaning they are free from contamination at levels that would require special handling, disposal, or treatment. The plan defines sampling locations, depths, frequencies, analytical parameters, and quality control procedures used to demonstrate that material is suitable for reuse as fill without posing a risk to human health or the environment.

Clean fill sampling plans are typically required by environmental agencies when soil is excavated from a site (e.g., during construction, remediation, or basin maintenance) and is intended for beneficial reuse, such as backfill, grading, or off-site transfer to another location. The plan establishes the scientific basis for confirming that the material is not impacted by hazardous substances, petroleum hydrocarbons, heavy metals, or other regulated contaminants above predetermined action levels.

## How it works

A CFSP follows a systematic process to characterize the quality of earthen materials. The key components include:

1. **Site Characterization and Background Review**: Prior to sampling, the plan reviews historical site use, previous environmental investigations, and existing analytical data to identify potential contamination sources (e.g., former industrial operations, historical spills, or waste disposal). This informs the selection of target analytes and establishes background reference levels.

2. **Sampling Strategy and Design**: The plan specifies the number, location, and depth of sampling points based on the volume of material and the degree of suspected contamination. Common approaches include:
   - **Grid sampling**: Dividing the area into a grid and collecting samples at node points to ensure spatial coverage.
   - **Composite sampling**: Combining multiple subsamples (typically 3–5) from a defined area into a single sample for analysis, reducing cost while maintaining representativeness.
   - **Random sampling**: Using statistical methods to select unbiased sampling locations.
   - **Depth-stratified sampling**: Collecting samples at different depths (e.g., 0–2 ft, 2–5 ft, >5 ft) to account for vertical variability.

3. **Analytical Parameters**: The plan lists the chemical analytes to be tested, which commonly include:
   - Metals (e.g., arsenic, lead, chromium, cadmium, mercury)
   - Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs)
   - Petroleum hydrocarbon fractions (e.g., TPH-d, TPH-gasoline range)
   - Volatile organic compounds (VOCs)
   - Polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs)
   - Pesticides and herbicides
   - pH, conductivity, and other general chemistry parameters

4. **Analytical Methods and Detection Limits**: Specific laboratory methods (e.g., EPA Method 8260 for VOCs, Method 6010 for metals) and required detection limits are defined to ensure data quality and comparability with regulatory standards.

5. **Quality Assurance/Quality Control (QA/QC)**: The plan includes:
   - Field duplicates (to assess sampling precision)
   - Trip blanks and field blanks (to check for cross-contamination)
   - Matrix spikes (to evaluate analytical accuracy)
   - Surrogate recoveries (for organic analyses)
   - Chain-of-custody documentation

6. **Data Evaluation Criteria**: Results are compared against established clean fill criteria (often listed by state regulations, such as California's PRC Section 1600 standards or EPA regional screening levels). If all samples meet these criteria, the material is deemed clean fill. If some exceedances occur, the plan may outline a "hotspot" designation, exclusion of contaminated zones, or trigger for additional investigation.

7. **Reporting**: The plan concludes with requirements for a sampling report that documents field activities, analytical results, statistical analysis, and a certification that the material qualifies as clean fill.

## Variants

While the core principles remain consistent, clean fill sampling plans can vary based on jurisdiction and project context:

- **Regulatory-specific plans**: Many states and countries publish prescriptive guidance — e.g., California's Water Boards' "Clean Fill Sampling Plan Guidance," Washington State Ecology's "Clean Fill Sampling Protocol," or UK Environment Agency's "Land Quality / Clean Fill" standards. These may dictate specific sampling densities, target analytes, and cleanup thresholds.

- **Risk-based plans**: Instead of a generic "clean fill" threshold, some plans use site-specific risk assessment to derive acceptable levels of contaminants based on the intended end use (e.g., residential vs. industrial fill). This allows greater flexibility in borderline contamination cases.

- **Volumetric composite plans**: For large-volume projects (e.g., hundreds of thousands of cubic yards), plans may reduce the number of discrete samples and rely on composite sampling from stockpiles or active excavation faces to achieve statistical representativeness with fewer analyses.

- **Stockpile-based plans**: When material is excavated and placed in temporary stockpiles, sampling is performed on each stockpile (e.g., one composite per 1,000–5,000 cubic yards). These plans often include procedures for managing stockpile mixing and segregation.

- **Continuous or iterative plans**: In some remediation settings, sampling occurs as excavation progresses, allowing real-time classification of material as clean or impacted. This is common in highway construction or channel dredging projects.

## Trade-offs

Key considerations when developing or implementing a clean fill sampling plan include:

- **Cost vs. confidence**: Higher sampling density and more comprehensive analytical suites increase confidence in classification but significantly raise costs. A plan must balance the risk of undetected contamination against budget constraints.

- **Temporal variability**: Contaminant concentrations can change over time due to weathering, leaching, biological activity, or mixing. A plan designed for a single sampling event may not capture long-term conditions, requiring periodic resampling.

- **Representativeness**: Composite samples reduce cost but can mask "hotspots" — discrete areas of high contamination that, when averaged with cleaner material, may incorrectly pass the clean fill criteria. Discrete samples are more sensitive but more expensive.

- **Regulatory acceptance**: Not all jurisdictions accept the same sampling density or data quality. A plan that satisfies one regulator may be rejected by another. Early consultation with regulatory agencies is essential to avoid rework.

- **Interpretation of "background"**: Distinguishing naturally elevated levels of metals (e.g., arsenic in certain geological formations) from anthropogenic contamination requires careful comparison to site background data and may necessitate separate criteria for natural vs. human-caused chemicals.

- **Integration with site closure**: Clean fill classification is often a prerequisite for site closure or completion of remediation. Failure to properly design the sampling plan can delay project timelines and incur additional investigation costs.
