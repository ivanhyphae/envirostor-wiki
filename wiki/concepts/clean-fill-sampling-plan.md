## Definition

A **Clean Fill Sampling Plan** is a project-specific technical plan that defines how imported soil or other fill material will be sampled, tested, documented, and approved before it is used on a site. Its purpose is to demonstrate that the material meets the applicable definition of **clean fill** and is suitable for unrestricted or specified reuse without creating unacceptable environmental risk.

In practice, a clean fill sampling plan is used to support decisions about whether off-site material can be accepted for use in construction, grading, backfill, or restoration activities. It typically specifies the source of the material, sampling locations and frequency, analytical test methods, field screening procedures, documentation requirements, and acceptance criteria. In the source examples, the plans were submitted to and approved by DTSC as part of site-specific cleanup or construction planning for the Carpenter Shoofly and Basin 5 projects.

## How it works

A clean fill sampling plan converts a regulatory or project requirement into a step-by-step quality assurance procedure for imported fill. The plan generally follows these steps:

1. **Characterize the source area**
   - Identify where the fill is coming from.
   - Review historical land use, prior investigations, and available environmental data.
   - Determine whether the source area has potential contamination concerns.

2. **Define the material to be evaluated**
   - Specify what counts as candidate clean fill, such as native soil, excavated soil, or screened borrow material.
   - Exclude materials that are obviously unsuitable, such as debris, asphalt, concrete, trash, or stained soil, unless the plan expressly allows them under limited conditions.

3. **Establish sampling strategy**
   - Set the number of samples based on volume, heterogeneity, and source history.
   - Specify whether samples will be collected by grid, judgmental targeting, truckload composite, stockpile sampling, or trench excavation face sampling.
   - Define depth intervals or stockpile locations to ensure the sample represents the full material mass.

4. **Specify analytical testing**
   - Require laboratory testing for contaminants relevant to the site and the intended reuse.
   - Typical analytes may include metals, petroleum hydrocarbons, VOCs, SVOCs, pesticides, PCBs, or other site-specific constituents.
   - Include field observations and screening, such as visual inspection, odor checks, photoionization detector use, or colorimetry where appropriate.

5. **Compare results to acceptance criteria**
   - Evaluate analytical results against project-specific clean fill thresholds or applicable regulatory screening levels.
   - Determine whether the fill is acceptable, conditionally acceptable with restrictions, or rejected.

6. **Document and approve**
   - Maintain chain-of-custody, laboratory reports, field logs, sample location maps, and waste disposition records.
   - Submit the plan or results to the oversight agency when required.
   - In the cited project files, DTSC approval e-mails indicate that the sampling plans or supporting technical memoranda were reviewed and accepted prior to implementation.

7. **Implement acceptance controls**
   - Use the approved plan during import.
   - Inspect loads, segregate suspect material, and reject nonconforming loads.
   - Maintain records showing that placed fill complied with the approved criteria.

The core function of the plan is representativeness: it must provide enough data to reasonably conclude that the entire candidate fill volume is clean, not just a few individual samples.

## Variants

Common variants and related implementations include:

- **Imported clean fill sampling plan**
  - Used when soil is brought to a site from an off-site borrow source.
  - Focuses on source characterization and acceptance of incoming loads.

- **Stockpile sampling plan**
  - Used when material has already been excavated and stockpiled.
  - Samples are collected from a defined stockpile geometry to represent the stockpile as a whole.

- **Source-area sampling plan**
  - Used to qualify material directly from a borrow pit, staging area, or excavation area before transport.
  - Often paired with pre-approval of the source area.

- **Project-wide clean fill management plan**
  - Broader than sampling alone.
  - Includes procedures for screening, manifesting, transportation, placement, recordkeeping, and contingency actions.

- **Conditional clean fill acceptance criteria**
  - Allows reuse only if specific analytes are below thresholds and field observations show no signs of contamination.
  - May include restrictions on depth of placement or location of use.

- **Alternative verification approaches**
  - Some projects use a combination of prior environmental characterization, supplier documentation, and limited confirmation sampling rather than full sampling of every load.

The source documents suggest site-specific implementations for the Carpenter Shoofly area and Basin 5, likely reflecting different source histories, project footprints, and oversight requirements.

## Trade-offs

Key considerations and limitations include:

- **Representativeness vs. practicality**
  - More sampling improves confidence but increases cost, schedule, and laboratory burden.
  - Too little sampling may miss localized contamination.

- **Source history uncertainty**
  - Even apparently clean borrow sources can contain localized impacts from past uses, imported material, fill debris, or spills.
  - Historic records and field inspection reduce but do not eliminate uncertainty.

- **Project-specific criteria**
  - Clean fill thresholds are often tailored to the site and regulatory context.
  - A material acceptable for one project may not qualify for another.

- **Field heterogeneity**
  - Soil can vary substantially across short distances or within stockpiles.
  - Sampling plans must address spatial variability to avoid false confidence.

- **Regulatory oversight**
  - Agency review can add time but improves defensibility and documentation quality.
  - The approved plan becomes the controlling framework; deviations may require re-approval.

- **Operational impacts**
  - Load rejection, re-handling, and resampling can disrupt construction logistics.
  - Strong screening procedures reduce downstream problems.

- **Incomplete detection of all hazards**
  - Standard analytical panels may not capture every possible contaminant.
  - The plan is only as good as the contaminant list and conceptual site understanding behind it.
