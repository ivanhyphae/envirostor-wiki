## Definition

**Stockpile 2 MSE Wall Footing Sampling** refers to the planned or completed sampling of the footing material associated with the Stockpile 2 mechanically stabilized earth (MSE ) wall for construction quality control and/or geotechnical verification. In practice, this means obtaining representative samples from the wall footing area—typically concrete footing material, foundation support material, or adjacent prepared foundation materials—so that the material can be tested against project specifications before, during, or after construction.

The concept is used in the context of an MSE retaining structure, where footing and foundation conditions are critical to wall performance, alignment, and long-term stability.

## How it works

Footing sampling for an MSE wall generally follows a quality assurance workflow:

1. **Identify the sampling location**
   - The footing segment is selected based on stationing, lift, pour sequence, or inspection requirements.
   - For a structure like Stockpile 2 MSE Wall, the sampling point is tied to the wall footing zone rather than the reinforced backfill itself.

2. **Collect representative samples**
   - Samples may be taken from the footing concrete, base preparation, or supporting material depending on the purpose of the memo or inspection plan.
   - Sampling is performed so that the material represents the actual constructed condition at the specified wall location.

3. **Document field conditions**
   - Inspectors record location, date, project segment, and any observable issues such as segregation, moisture condition, irregular subgrade, or deviations from design geometry.
   - Documentation is important because footing performance depends heavily on proper support conditions.

4. **Perform laboratory or field testing**
   - Concrete footing samples may be evaluated for strength, composition, or compliance with mix design.
   - Foundation or base material samples may be tested for gradation, compaction, moisture content, or suitability as support for the wall system.

5. **Compare results to project criteria**
   - Results are checked against contract specifications, design requirements, and acceptance thresholds.
   - If sampling indicates nonconformance, corrective action may include repair, replacement, rework, or further investigation.

In an MSE wall system, footing sampling matters because the footing helps transfer loads, maintain wall geometry, and support the facing system. Any deficiency in footing materials or foundation support can affect settlement, tilt, drainage, and overall structural behavior.

## Variants

Common variants of MSE wall footing sampling include:

- **Concrete footing sampling**
  - Sampling of cast-in-place footing concrete for strength or mix compliance.

- **Foundation subgrade sampling**
  - Sampling of the prepared soil or aggregate foundation beneath the footing to confirm adequacy before placement.

- **Base course or leveling pad sampling**
  - If the wall uses a leveling pad or working platform, that material may be sampled instead of, or in addition to, the footing itself.

- **Construction quality assurance sampling**
  - Sampling performed as part of routine inspection and acceptance.

- **Investigative or corrective sampling**
  - Sampling performed after a concern arises, such as visible cracking, settlement, or dimensional nonconformance.

- **Alternative verification methods**
  - In some cases, nondestructive testing, survey verification, or density testing may supplement or replace direct sampling depending on the material and specification.

## Trade-offs

Key considerations and limitations include:

- **Representative sampling is difficult**
  - A small sample may not capture variability across the entire footing or foundation zone.

- **Sampling can be intrusive**
  - Removing material for testing may slightly disturb the footing or supporting area, especially if done after placement.

- **Timing matters**
  - Sampling too early may miss later curing or settlement issues; sampling too late may delay corrective action.

- **Laboratory testing adds time and cost**
  - More thorough verification improves confidence but increases project duration and expense.

- **Limited diagnostic scope**
  - A footing sample can confirm material properties, but it may not reveal all structural or geometric issues affecting the wall.

- **Specification dependence**
  - Acceptance criteria depend on the project’s construction documents; the same sample may be acceptable on one project and rejected on another.
