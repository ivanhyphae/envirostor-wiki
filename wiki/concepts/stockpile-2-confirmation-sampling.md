## Definition

**Stockpile 2 Confirmation Sampling** is a verification sampling process used to confirm the quality or composition of material in a stockpile before it is accepted, processed, or used in decision-making. In practice, it is a secondary or follow-up sampling step intended to validate the results of an initial stockpile assessment and reduce the risk of relying on unrepresentative or biased samples.

The concept is most commonly associated with stockpile management in mining, bulk material handling, and similar operations where material is heterogeneous and difficult to sample perfectly. The “2” typically indicates a second confirmation stage, a second stockpile, or a specific confirmation sampling campaign in an approved technical memo or project workflow.

## How it works

Confirmation sampling is designed to answer a simple question: **does the stockpile as a whole match the expected characteristics suggested by prior sampling or model estimates?**

A typical workflow is:

1. **Define the sampling objective**
   - Confirm grade, moisture, contamination, size distribution, or other material properties.
   - Specify whether the result is for acceptance, reconciliation, blending, or release.

2. **Design the sampling pattern**
   - Select sample locations across the stockpile surface, access points, or discharge points.
   - Ensure coverage of spatial variability, since stockpiles are rarely uniform.
   - Use a plan that minimizes bias from segregation, layering, and particle-size effects.

3. **Collect confirmation samples**
   - Obtain increments from predetermined points or intervals.
   - Combine increments into composites if the goal is to characterize the stockpile as a whole.
   - Maintain chain of custody and clear labeling.

4. **Prepare and analyze samples**
   - Reduce, split, dry, crush, or otherwise prepare the material according to the target analyte.
   - Analyze using the appropriate laboratory or field method.

5. **Compare against expected values**
   - Compare results with prior stockpile estimates, reconciliation targets, or specification limits.
   - Evaluate whether observed differences fall within acceptable tolerance.

6. **Decide on disposition**
   - Accept the stockpile, reclassify it, reblend it, re-sample it, or reject it.
   - Document the reasoning and any limitations of the sampling campaign.

The technical challenge is that stockpiles are inherently difficult to sample representatively. Material can segregate by particle size, density, or moisture during stacking and reclaiming. For that reason, confirmation sampling is usually treated as an evidence-based check rather than an exact measurement of the entire stockpile.

## Variants

Common variants and related implementations include:

- **Surface confirmation sampling**
  - Samples are taken from accessible surface points.
  - Useful when the stockpile cannot be penetrated easily, but can be biased by surface segregation.

- **Trench or pit sampling**
  - Material is exposed by digging trenches or pits into the stockpile.
  - Can improve representativeness compared with surface-only sampling, but is more invasive.

- **Incremental composite sampling**
  - Multiple small increments are collected across the stockpile and combined.
  - Often preferred when the goal is to estimate the average composition of the stockpile.

- **Discharge sampling**
  - Samples are collected as the stockpile is reclaimed or discharged.
  - Often more representative than static sampling because material flow can be made more systematic.

- **Duplicate or replicate confirmation sampling**
  - Additional samples are taken to check precision and reduce uncertainty.
  - Useful when the first sampling set is inconclusive or near decision thresholds.

- **Grade-control confirmation sampling**
  - Used in mining contexts to confirm ore classification, feed quality, or blend assumptions.

- **Specification compliance sampling**
  - Used to verify that the stockpile meets contractual or processing requirements.

## Trade-offs

Key trade-offs and limitations include:

- **Representativeness vs. practicality**
  - More representative sampling usually requires more effort, access, and disruption.
  - Easier sampling methods are often less reliable because of segregation and spatial variability.

- **Cost vs. confidence**
  - Additional samples improve confidence but increase labor, analytical, and operational costs.

- **Speed vs. rigor**
  - Rapid field checks can support quick decisions, but laboratory confirmation is usually more defensible.

- **Sampling bias risk**
  - Static stockpiles are especially vulnerable to bias if samples are taken only from accessible or visually distinct areas.

- **Heterogeneity**
  - Large stockpiles may vary internally enough that a small number of samples cannot fully characterize them.

- **Decision thresholds**
  - Confirmation sampling is most useful when the result is near a cutoff value; however, uncertainty can still leave some decisions ambiguous.

- **Operational disruption**
  - Accessing the stockpile for better sampling may interfere with production, reclaim, or safety constraints.
