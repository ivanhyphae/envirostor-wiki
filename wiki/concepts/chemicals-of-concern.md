## Definition

**Chemicals of Concern (COCs)** are substances that, due to their toxicity, persistence, bioaccumulation potential, or adverse environmental and human health effects, are subject to regulatory monitoring, reporting, or management actions. In the context of stormwater and water quality monitoring, COCs typically include heavy metals, pesticides, industrial chemicals, and other pollutants that exceed baseline concentrations or trigger specific regulatory thresholds. The term is operationalized in monitoring programs to focus on constituents that pose the greatest risk to receiving water bodies and beneficial uses.

## How it works

Identification and monitoring of COCs follows a systematic, risk-based process:

1. **Screening and Selection**: COCs are initially identified based on historical data, land use activities (e.g., industrial sites, highways, agricultural runoff), and regulatory lists (e.g., Clean Water Act priority pollutants, California Toxics Rule). For example, in the referenced Caltrans stormwater monitoring reports, COCs are selected from the "List of 303(d) Impaired Waterbodies" and Total Maximum Daily Load (TMDL) programs.

2. **Sample Collection and Analysis**: Water, sediment, or biota samples are collected at designated monitoring stations (e.g., compliance points, receiving water bodies) using standard protocols (e.g., EPA Methods). Parameters include:
   - Metals (e.g., copper, lead, zinc, mercury)
   - Organic compounds (e.g., PCBs, PAHs, pesticides)
   - Nutrients (e.g., nitrogen, phosphorus) though not always classified as COCs
   - Sediment toxicity indicators

3. **Benchmarking**: Measured concentrations are compared to established water quality criteria (e.g., California Water Quality Objectives, Aquatic Life Criteria). Exceedances trigger further evaluation or corrective actions.

4. **Reporting and Adaptive Management**: Results are compiled in monitoring reports (e.g., GND Water Monitoring Reports from 2012) that document trends, exceedances, and compliance status. If COC levels rise, management practices (e.g., stormwater best management practices, source control) are adjusted.

## Variants

- **Regulatory COCs**: Defined by government agencies (e.g., US EPA, California State Water Resources Control Board) under laws like the Clean Water Act. These are fixed lists updated periodically.
- **Site-Specific COCs**: Identified through site investigations (e.g., Caltrans stormwater SAP Addendum S9525-06-44A) based on local land use, historical spills, or TMDLs. Tailored to specific outfalls or waterbodies.
- **Emerging COCs**: Contaminants of emerging concern (e.g., PFAS, pharmaceuticals, microplastics) that are not yet regulated but are increasingly monitored.
- **Chronic vs. Acute COCs**: Differentiated by exposure duration. Chronic COCs (e.g., low-level metals) affect long-term ecosystem health; acute COCs (e.g., pesticide spills) cause immediate toxicity.

## Trade-offs

- **Comprehensiveness vs. Cost**: Monitoring a broad suite of COCs is expensive and analytically complex. Focusing on a few priority COCs (e.g., copper, zinc) may miss emerging threats but is more feasible for routine programs.
- **False Positives/Negatives**: Analytical methods for trace-level COCs can yield false positives or miss low-concentration pollutants. Quality assurance/quality control (QA/QC) is critical but adds cost.
- **Regulatory Lag**: Lists of regulated COCs often fail to keep pace with new scientific findings (e.g., PFAS); site-specific monitoring can fill gaps but lacks enforcement power.
- **Spatial/Temporal Variability**: COC concentrations vary with storm events, seasonal flows, and land use changes. Discrete grab samples may not capture worst-case conditions; composite sampling or continuous monitoring is more accurate but resource-intensive.
- **Risk Communication**: Communicating COC data to the public and decision-makers requires careful interpretation—exceedances do not always imply immediate harm, but non-exceedances may undervalue cumulative risks.
