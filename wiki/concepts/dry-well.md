## Definition

A **dry well** is a subsurface structure designed to manage stormwater runoff by allowing water to infiltrate into the surrounding soil. It typically consists of a perforated chamber, pit, or trench filled with aggregate (e.g., gravel), which temporarily stores water and promotes percolation into the ground. Dry wells are a type of Best Management Practice for stormwater management, commonly used to reduce runoff volume, mitigate flooding, and recharge groundwater.

## How it works

Dry wells operate on the principle of infiltration: they capture water from impervious surfaces (e.g., roofs, driveways, parking lots) and direct it into a subsurface storage area. The water is conveyed via pipes or downspouts into the dry well, where it is temporarily held. Over time, the stored water seeps through the perforated walls or base of the structure into the surrounding soil. Key components include:

- **Inlet structure**: Conveys water from the source into the dry well, often with a filter or debris screen to prevent clogging.
- **Storage chamber**: A void space (e.g., a prefabricated plastic crate or a gravel-filled pit) that holds water during storm events. The volume is sized based on the drainage area and local rainfall intensity.
- **Infiltration zone**: The surrounding soil, which must have adequate permeability (typically, a saturated hydraulic conductivity of at least 0.5 inches per hour) to ensure effective drainage. A geotextile fabric may line the excavation to prevent soil migration into the aggregate.
- **Overflow mechanism**: A high-flow bypass or overflow pipe to route excess water during extreme storms, preventing surface ponding or structural failure.

The system relies on gravity flow; water percolates downward until it reaches the water table or is fully absorbed. Design calculations often follow the Darcy's Law equation: infiltration rate = hydraulic conductivity × hydraulic gradient × flow area.

## Variants

Several design variants exist, tailored to site conditions and regulatory requirements:

- **Gravel-filled dry well**: A simple excavation filled with clean, crushed stone or gravel. The void ratio (typically 30–40% of total volume) determines storage capacity.
- **Chamber-based dry well**: Uses a prefabricated plastic or concrete chamber (e.g., a large perforated pipe or vault) that provides a higher void ratio (up to 95%) than gravel, reducing the required footprint.
- **Trench dry well**: A linear, shallow trench filled with aggregate, often combined with perforated pipe for distribution. Used for linear runoff sources (e.g., along driveways).
- **Dry well with pretreatment**: Incorporates a sediment trap, catch basin, or vegetated filter strip to remove debris and sediment before water enters the main infiltration area, prolonging lifespan.

## Trade-offs

Key considerations when implementing dry wells:

- **Advantages**:
  - Reduces stormwater runoff volume and peak flow, aiding in flood control.
  - Recharges local groundwater, supporting baseflow in streams.
  - Requires minimal surface area, making it suitable for urban or space-constrained sites.
  - Low maintenance after initial installation, if properly sited.

- **Disadvantages**:
  - Clogging risk: Sediment, organic matter, and fine particles can reduce infiltration over time, requiring periodic cleaning or replacement of filter media.
  - Soil dependency: Performance is highly sensitive to soil type; clay-rich or low-permeability soils may render a dry well ineffective.
  - Groundwater contamination: Concentrated infiltration of polluted runoff (e.g., from industrial areas) can degrade groundwater quality without adequate pretreatment.
  - Structural failure: Improper sizing or soil settlement can lead to collapse, particularly in frost-prone regions where freeze-thaw cycles cause damage.
  - Regulatory constraints: Many jurisdictions require setbacks from buildings, property lines, and wells to avoid water intrusion or contamination.
