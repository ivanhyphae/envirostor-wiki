## Sampling Plan

### Definition
A **sampling plan** is a structured, documented strategy that specifies the number, location, method, and frequency of samples to be collected from a population or material stream, along with acceptance criteria or analytical protocols. It ensures that collected data are representative, defensible, and sufficient to meet defined objectives (e.g., quality control, contamination assessment, or process verification).

### How it works
A sampling plan operates through a series of predefined steps:

1. **Objective Definition**: The plan first clarifies the goal—whether to estimate a mean, detect nonconformance, or characterize spatial variability. This drives all subsequent choices.

2. **Population Delineation**: The material or area of interest is divided into logical sampling units (e.g., strata, grids, or lot increments). In environmental contexts, this may involve layering by depth, soil type, or contamination history (as in the Carpenter Shoofly Area Clean Fill Sampling Plan).

3. **Sample Size Determination**: Statistical formulas (e.g., based on variance, desired precision, and confidence level) or regulatory guidelines set the number of samples. For example, a workplan may require one sample per 500 m³ of fill material.

4. **Sampling Method Selection**: Two broad categories exist:
   - **Probability sampling** (random, stratified, systematic grids): ensures statistical validity.
   - **Judgmental sampling**: targets suspected hotspots (common in site investigations like the Stockpile 2 BCS Foundation Spoil Workplan).

5. **Analytical Protocols**: Specifies laboratory methods, detection limits, and quality assurance procedures (e.g., field duplicates, blanks, and spikes).

6. **Acceptance Criteria**: Defines threshold values for “pass/fail” decisions. For example, clean fill must have contaminant concentrations below action levels per the Carpenter plan.

7. **Documentation**: The plan records field forms, chain-of-custody, and data to enable traceability and future audits.

### Variants
- **Simple Random Sampling**: Every item has equal selection probability. Least efficient for heterogeneous populations.
- **Stratified Sampling**: Population divided into homogeneous strata; samples allocated proportionally or optimally. Common in the Stockpile 2 workplan for different spoil layers.
- **Systematic Grid Sampling**: Samples at regular intervals (e.g., 10 m × 10 m grid). Useful for spatial contamination mapping.
- **Composite Sampling**: Multiple physical samples are combined into a single analytical sample to reduce cost and estimate averages (e.g., for soil fill verification).
- **Sequential Sampling**: Sampling occurs in stages, with interim decisions on whether to continue or stop; often used in quality control lot acceptance.
- **Double-Stage (Two-Phase) Sampling**: A coarse first stage screens for contamination, followed by detailed second-stage analysis on selected samples (explicit in the Carpenter plan for clean fill verification).

### Trade-offs
- **Cost vs. Precision**: More samples improve accuracy but increase analytical and labor expense. A plan must balance budget constraints with statistical rigor.
- **Representativeness vs. Practicality**: Probability-based plans yield defensible data but may miss localized anomalies; judgmental plans efficiently find contamination but are difficult to generalize.
- **Time Constraints**: Complex plans (e.g., stratified sequential sampling) require longer execution, while simplified plans (e.g., composite sampling) provide faster turnaround.
- **Regulatory Acceptance**: Some agencies mandate specific plans (e.g., ASTM D5650 or EPA protocols) that may not align perfectly with site-specific needs.
- **Composite vs. Discrete**: Composite sampling reduces cost but obscures high-value outliers or spatial patterns; discrete samples preserve variability but are more expensive.
- **Quality Assurance Overhead**: The Carpenter plan explicitly notes that field quality control (duplicates, blanks) consumes 10–15% of samples, adding cost but ensuring data integrity.
