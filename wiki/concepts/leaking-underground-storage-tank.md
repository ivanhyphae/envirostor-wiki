## Definition

A **Leaking Underground Storage Tank (LUST)** is an underground storage tank (UST) system from which any amount of regulated substance (typically petroleum or hazardous chemicals) has leaked, spilled, or otherwise been released into the surrounding soil or groundwater. LUSTs represent a widespread environmental liability, as USTs are commonly used to store fuel at gas stations, industrial facilities, farms, and residential properties. Regulatory agencies (e.g., the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency under RCRA Subtitle I and analogous state programs) mandate detection, reporting, and remediation of releases to protect human health and the environment.

## How it works

A LUST arises when there is a failure in the primary containment system of a UST. Leaks may originate from:

- **Corrosion** – steel tanks (unprotected or with degraded coating) develop pinholes or larger breaches.
- **Structural stress** – ground movement, heavy traffic, or improper backfilling can crack fiberglass-reinforced plastic (FRP) tanks.
- **Piping, fitting, or sump failures** – flexible connectors, shear valves, and sump seals are common leak points, accounting for many confirmed releases.
- **Overfills or spills** – during fuel delivery, excess fuel not captured by spill containment can enter the soil through loose fittings or improper drainage.
- **Improper installation or abandonment** – tanks missing cathodic protection, lacking leak detection, or abandoned without proper closure can corrode and leak over time.

Once released, the liquid (typically gasoline, diesel, or heating oil) migrates downward through soil under gravity. The lighter hydrocarbon fractions volatilize into soil gas, while heavier components can form a light non-aqueous phase liquid (LNAPL) layer floating on the water table if groundwater is present. Dissolved-phase constituents (e.g., benzene, toluene, ethylbenzene, xylene – BTEX) contaminate groundwater and can create a plume that spreads downgradient.

Detection occurs through:

- **Interstitial monitoring** – double-walled tanks or piping with sensors that detect liquid or vapor between walls.
- **Automatic tank gauging (ATG)** – continuous measurement of product level; a small loss rate over time indicates a leak.
- **Inventory reconciliation** – manual comparison of deliveries, sales, and stick gauge measurements; often insufficient when slow leaks occur.
- **Vapor monitoring** – wells installed in the tank backfill detect hydrocarbon vapors.
- **Groundwater monitoring wells** – installed after a confirmed release to assess extent and concentration of contamination.

State regulations typically require owners/operators to report a suspected release within 24 hours and to initiate corrective action (site assessment, source removal, and cleanup) under oversight of the regulatory agency.

## Variants

LUSTs are primarily defined by the regulated substance and the environmental setting, leading to several variants:

- **Petroleum LUSTs** – the most common; gasoline, diesel, kerosene, and heating oil. Remediation focuses on BTEX and (for gasoline) MTBE or other oxygenates.
- **Hazardous substance LUSTs** – less frequent but more complex; chemicals like solvents, acids, or pesticides stored in USTs. Different cleanup standards apply under CERCLA or state hazardous waste programs.
- **Heating oil tanks** – often excluded from federal UST regulations (e.g., if <1,100 gallons), but many states regulate them. Leaks can go undetected for years because inventory tracking is absent.
- **Abandoned or orphaned LUSTs** – tanks left in place without a responsible party; state funds (e.g., California’s Underground Storage Tank Cleanup Fund) may pay for remediation.
- **LUSTs in sensitive receptors** – sites near drinking water wells, wetlands, or basements may require expedited action and alternative remediation strategies (e.g., soil vapor extraction vs. excavation).

## Trade-offs

- **Detection vs. cost** – double-walled tanks and automatic monitoring are highly effective but expensive; older unprotected steel tanks are cheap but prone to failure. Many states have mandated tighter leak detection for new installations, but legacy systems remain.
- **Remediation technology choice** – excavation and off-site disposal is fast but costly and disruptive; in situ bioremediation, air sparging, or chemical oxidation can be less invasive but take years to achieve cleanup goals.
- **Liability and reporting** – early reporting triggers regulatory oversight and cleanup costs but minimizes future spread and legal penalties; delaying reporting can result in criminal charges and third-party lawsuits.
- **Closure vs. long-term monitoring** – tank closures (removal or abandonment in place) eliminate the source but require verification of no remaining contamination; continued groundwater monitoring may be required for decades if dissolved-phase plumes persist.
- **State vs. federal jurisdiction** – federal rules (RCRA Subtitle I) set minimum standards, but states can adopt stricter criteria. This creates variation in cleanup standards (e.g., residential vs. industrial land use) and funding availability.
