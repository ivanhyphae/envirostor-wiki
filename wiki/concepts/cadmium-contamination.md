## Definition

**Cadmium contamination** is the presence of cadmium in soil, dust, water, sediment, air, or biological material at concentrations high enough to pose environmental or health risk. Cadmium is a toxic heavy metal that can accumulate in the environment and in living organisms. In site investigations, contamination is typically identified through sampling and laboratory analysis, then compared against applicable cleanup, screening, or risk-based standards.

In the provided source context, cadmium appears as one of the contaminants of concern in a soil stockpile/fenceline monitoring setting, where the goal is to determine whether site materials are contributing metals to surrounding soil or dust and whether additional management or remediation is needed.

## How it works

Cadmium contamination occurs when cadmium is introduced, mobilized, or redistributed into an environmental medium.

### Common sources
- Mining and smelting activities
- Battery manufacture and disposal
- Metal plating and industrial emissions
- Phosphate fertilizers and some agricultural inputs
- Landfill leachate, wastewater sludge, and waste stockpiles
- Weathering of cadmium-bearing materials

### Environmental behavior
Cadmium is relatively mobile compared with some other metals, especially under acidic conditions. Its behavior depends on:
- **Soil pH**: lower pH generally increases cadmium solubility and mobility
- **Organic matter content**: can bind cadmium and reduce immediate bioavailability
- **Redox conditions**: may affect speciation and transport
- **Particle size and dust generation**: contaminated fines can move offsite by wind or runoff
- **Water chemistry**: affects dissolution and sorption

### Exposure pathways
Cadmium contamination becomes a concern because it can enter exposure routes such as:
- **Ingestion of contaminated soil or dust**
- **Inhalation of airborne particulates**
- **Consumption of contaminated crops, water, or animal products**
- **Direct contact with contaminated media**, with indirect exposure through hand-to-mouth transfer

### Health and ecological significance
Cadmium is persistent and can accumulate over time. Human exposure is associated with:
- Kidney damage
- Bone effects
- Respiratory effects after inhalation
- Increased risk from long-term exposure

Ecologically, cadmium can impair plant growth, soil microbial activity, and aquatic life, depending on concentration and bioavailability.

### Site assessment and monitoring
Typical technical steps include:
1. **Sampling design**: collect soil, dust, water, or stockpile samples, often including upgradient/downgradient or fenceline locations.
2. **Laboratory analysis**: measure total cadmium, and sometimes extractable or bioavailable fractions.
3. **Data comparison**: compare results to background levels, screening values, or site-specific action levels.
4. **Interpretation**: determine whether contamination is localized, widespread, surface-associated, or migrating offsite.
5. **Response actions**: controls may include covering stockpiles, dust suppression, removal, stabilization, or monitoring.

## Variants

Cadmium contamination may be described or assessed in several forms:

- **Soil contamination**: cadmium in topsoil, subsoil, or stockpile material
- **Dust contamination**: cadmium-bearing particulate matter that can migrate by wind
- **Water contamination**: dissolved or suspended cadmium in groundwater, surface water, or leachate
- **Sediment contamination**: cadmium accumulated in deposited particles
- **Plant uptake contamination**: cadmium transferred into crops or vegetation
- **Mixed-metal contamination**: cadmium occurring with other metals such as lead, arsenic, zinc, or copper

Analytical and regulatory variants include:
- **Total cadmium analysis**
- **Leachable/extractable cadmium testing**
- **Bioavailability-focused assessment**
- **Background-adjusted evaluation**
- **Risk-based cleanup assessment**

## Trade-offs

Managing cadmium contamination involves several important trade-offs:

- **Accuracy vs. cost**: More sampling and finer spatial resolution improve confidence but increase cost and time.
- **Total concentration vs. bioavailability**: Total cadmium is easy to measure, but not all measured cadmium is equally mobile or bioavailable.
- **Short-term control vs. long-term remediation**: Dust suppression and capping can reduce exposure quickly, while excavation or treatment may provide more permanent reduction.
- **Containment vs. removal**: Leaving contaminated material in place may be less disruptive, but requires ongoing monitoring and institutional controls.
- **Site-specific variability**: Cadmium mobility can change with soil chemistry, making one-time results less predictive than repeated monitoring.
- **Co-contaminant complexity**: Cadmium often occurs with other metals, so remediation must address combined risks rather than cadmium alone.

Key limitations in interpretation:
- Background concentrations may already vary naturally.
- Surface and subsurface contamination may differ substantially.
- Particle transport can create offsite impacts even when average site concentrations are modest.
- A single sample may not represent heterogeneous stockpile material or fenceline conditions.
