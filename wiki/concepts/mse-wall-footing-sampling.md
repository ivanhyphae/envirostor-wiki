## Definition

MSE Wall Footing Sampling refers to the systematic collection and testing of soil, aggregate, or concrete materials used in the foundation (footing) of a Mechanically Stabilized Earth (MSE) wall. The process ensures that the footing materials meet specified engineering properties (e.g., compaction, gradation, strength, durability) to support the wall’s structural integrity and long-term performance. Sampling is typically conducted on stockpiles or in situ during construction as part of a quality assurance/quality control (QA/QC) plan.

## How it works

MSE wall footings are constructed from select granular fill, reinforced concrete, or stabilized earth. Sampling involves the following steps:

1. **Identification of sampling locations** – Based on the stockpile or construction layout, representative samples are obtained from the top, middle, and bottom of stockpiles, or from designated grid points in the footing area.

2. **Sample collection methods** – For granular materials, a split-tube sampler, shovel, or auger is used to collect a minimum mass dictated by ASTM or project specifications. Concrete samples are taken from fresh mix per ASTM C172.

3. **Frequency and acceptance criteria** – The sampling plan specifies the number of samples per volume of material (e.g., one sample per 500 cubic yards) and acceptance limits for moisture, density, grain size, and soundness. Materials failing acceptance may require retesting or removal.

4. **Laboratory testing** – Tests include standard Proctor compaction, sieve analysis, Atterberg limits, California Bearing Ratio (CBR), and, for concrete, slump, compressive strength, and air content.

5. **Documentation and approval** – Results are recorded and submitted to the project engineer or owner for acceptance. The acceptance email in the sources indicates that the sampling plan was approved for specific stockpiles (Stockpiles 1 and 2) for MSE wall footing construction.

## Variants

- **Stockpile sampling** – Material is sampled before placement; common for large volumes of granular backfill.
- **In situ footing sampling** – Samples taken from compacted layers or fresh concrete during placement.
- **Destructive vs. non-destructive testing** – In situ density testing (nuclear gauge, sand cone) versus core sampling for concrete.
- **Frequency-based vs. random sampling** – Some plans use fixed intervals; others use probabilistic methods.

## Trade-offs

- **Stockpile sampling provides pre-placement control** but may miss segregation during transport and placement. In situ sampling is more representative of as-built conditions.
- **Increased sampling frequency** improves confidence but raises cost and schedule impacts.
- **Destructive testing** (e.g., concrete cores) damages the footing and requires patching.
- **Acceptance criteria** that are too stringent can cause unnecessary rejections and delays; too lax can compromise structural safety.
- **Sample handling** – Improper transport or storage can alter material properties (e.g., moisture loss).
