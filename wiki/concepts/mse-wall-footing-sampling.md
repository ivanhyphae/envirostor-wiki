## Definition

**MSE Wall Footing Sampling** is the planned inspection and material-testing of the footing concrete placed beneath a Mechanically Stabilized Earth Wall (MSE wall). The purpose is to verify that the wall footing material meets project requirements before the footing is accepted for use as part of the wall foundation system.

In the acceptance records provided, footing sampling was treated as a **quality assurance / quality control** step associated with wall construction, with sampling plans and acceptance emails used to document approval of the proposed sampling approach for specific wall areas or stockpiles.

## How it works

MSE wall footing sampling typically follows a sequence like this:

1. **Sampling plan is proposed**
   - The contractor or project team submits a sampling plan or technical memo describing:
     - what will be sampled,
     - where samples will be taken,
     - how many samples are required,
     - and what tests or acceptance criteria apply.
   - In the provided documents, this was communicated through sampling-plan emails and a technical memo for wall footing sampling.

2. **Acceptance is requested and documented**
   - The plan is reviewed by the accepting authority, often the owner, engineer, or agency representative.
   - Acceptance is then issued by email or memo, establishing that the proposed sampling method is approved.

3. **Samples are collected**
   - Samples are taken from the wall footing material at designated locations.
   - Depending on the project specification, this may involve:
     - concrete cylinder samples,
     - drill cores,
     - or other representative footing material samples.

4. **Laboratory or field testing is performed**
   - Samples are tested to confirm compliance with strength, durability, or composition requirements.
   - For concrete footings, this often means compressive strength and related acceptance checks.

5. **Results are reviewed for acceptance**
   - Test results are compared against project specifications.
   - If results meet criteria, the footing is accepted.
   - If not, corrective action may be required before the wall can proceed.

6. **Variance handling may be required**
   - If the planned sampling approach deviates from the original contract requirements, a variance or exception request may be submitted.
   - The RDIP variance acceptance email indicates that at least some sampling-related actions required formal approval outside the base plan.

The key function of footing sampling is to provide objective evidence that the foundation element supporting the MSE wall is adequate and consistent with design assumptions.

## Variants

Common variants or closely related implementations include:

- **Stockpile-specific sampling plans**
  - Sampling may be organized by stockpile, wall segment, or construction phase.
  - The source material references sampling plans for **Stockpiles 1 and 2**, suggesting project segmentation by material source or work area.

- **Technical-memo-based sampling**
  - Instead of a standalone testing protocol, the sampling method may be explained in a **technical memo** and then accepted by email.

- **Variance-based sampling**
  - If actual field conditions differ from the contract or original plan, a formal **variance request** may be used to obtain approval.
  - This is common when field constraints, access, or schedule affect how samples can be taken.

- **Owner/agency acceptance workflows**
  - Some projects require explicit acceptance emails before sampling begins.
  - Others may rely on stamped submittals, field directives, or inspection hold points.

- **Alternative foundation verification methods**
  - In some contexts, footing adequacy may be verified with additional geotechnical review, structural inspection, or non-destructive evaluation rather than extensive destructive sampling.

## Trade-offs

### Advantages
- Provides direct evidence that footing material meets specification.
- Reduces risk of hidden foundation defects under the MSE wall.
- Creates a documented acceptance trail for construction records.
- Supports quality assurance and dispute resolution.

### Limitations
- Sampling is only representative; it may not capture all localized defects.
- Destructive sampling can damage the footing if not planned carefully.
- Requires coordination, delay, and documentation effort.
- Acceptance depends on clear test criteria; ambiguous criteria weaken the value of sampling.
- Variances can complicate project controls if the approved method differs from standard requirements.

### Practical considerations
- Sampling locations should be chosen to be representative and accessible.
- The acceptance authority must confirm whether the plan satisfies contract requirements.
- Chain-of-custody and test documentation matter if results are later challenged.
- Sampling for one wall segment or stockpile may not be automatically transferable to another segment unless explicitly approved.
