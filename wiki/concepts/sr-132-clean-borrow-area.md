## Definition

The **SR 132 Clean Borrow Area** is a designated source area for importing **clean fill material** used on the SR 132 project, specifically to support site grading, basin construction, and other earthwork needs where uncontaminated soil is required. In practice, a clean borrow area is a permitted or approved offsite or onsite source of native soil that has been characterized to ensure it does not contain hazardous waste, contaminated media, or other unsuitable materials.

For SR 132, the clean borrow area appears to have been established and documented through a technical memo and associated sampling plan approvals to confirm that the material could be accepted for use as clean fill in project features such as clean fill embankments and basin work.

## How it works

A clean borrow area is used when a project needs large volumes of soil that meet environmental and engineering criteria. The process generally includes:

1. **Identification of the source area**
   - A borrow source is selected based on soil availability, compatibility with design needs, and access/logistics.
   - For SR 132, the area was evaluated in conjunction with project earthwork and basin fill requirements.

2. **Sampling and characterization**
   - Soil from the proposed borrow area is sampled under a formal plan.
   - Samples are tested for indicators of contamination and other relevant parameters.
   - The sampling plan is typically reviewed and approved before material is moved.

3. **Approval for clean fill use**
   - If the analytical results meet the project’s clean fill criteria, the material can be designated as acceptable borrow.
   - The approval e-mail for the SR 132 Basin 5 Clean Fill Sampling Plan indicates that the sampling and approval workflow was part of the acceptance process.

4. **Excavation and transport**
   - Approved clean soil is excavated, stockpiled if needed, and transported to the receiving area.
   - Care is taken to avoid commingling with unsuitable material.

5. **Placement and compaction**
   - The fill is placed in accordance with geotechnical specifications, often in lifts and compacted to target density.
   - In basin applications, the material may be used to form embankments, subgrades, or fill volumes needed for drainage and stormwater features.

6. **Documentation and compliance**
   - Records of sampling, analytical results, approvals, and source tracking are maintained.
   - This is important for environmental compliance and for verifying that the receiving area was constructed using approved material.

In the SR 132 context, the clean borrow area is not just a stockpile location; it is part of a controlled materials management process used to ensure that imported fill meets both engineering and environmental requirements.

## Variants

Common variants of a clean borrow area or clean fill source include:

- **Onsite clean borrow**
  - Material excavated from one part of the project and reused elsewhere on the same project.
  - Often preferred when soil quality and logistics allow.

- **Offsite clean borrow**
  - Material imported from an external source after testing and approval.
  - Used when onsite material is insufficient.

- **Temporary stockpile**
  - Approved soil stored temporarily before placement.
  - May require segregation, erosion control, and tracking to preserve “clean” status.

- **Clean fill sampling plan**
  - The formal plan that defines how the soil will be tested and accepted.
  - This is a procedural variant rather than a physical source type, but it is central to how the source is qualified.

- **RDIP variance / stockpile variance**
  - Some Caltrans soil stockpiles require a variance or special approval pathway, especially when reused material is managed under specific redevelopment or reuse rules.
  - The Caltrans Modesto soil stockpiles RDIP variance source suggests a related regulatory or administrative mechanism for approving soil reuse/stockpiling.

## Trade-offs

Using a clean borrow area offers important benefits, but it also has constraints:

### Advantages
- **Environmental risk reduction**
  - Sampling and approval reduce the chance of importing contaminated soil.
- **Regulatory clarity**
  - Documentation supports compliance and agency oversight.
- **Project efficiency**
  - Approved borrow can reduce the need to dispose of excavated soil offsite and can supply needed fill materials.
- **Engineering suitability**
  - Soil can be screened for properties relevant to compaction and constructability.

### Limitations and considerations
- **Testing cost and schedule**
  - Sampling, laboratory analysis, and review can delay excavation or placement.
- **Volume uncertainty**
  - A source may not yield enough acceptable material for the full project need.
- **Segregation requirements**
  - Once approved, material must be protected from mixing with unapproved soil.
- **Reclassification risk**
  - Disturbance, storage conditions, or commingling can complicate clean status.
- **Administrative burden**
  - Tracking approvals, source limits, and receiving locations adds documentation overhead.
- **Transport impacts**
  - If the source is offsite, hauling increases cost, emissions, and traffic impacts.
