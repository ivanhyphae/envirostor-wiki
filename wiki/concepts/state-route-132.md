# State Route 132

## Definition
**State Route 132 (SR 132)** is a California state highway that extends approximately 35 miles from Interstate 580 near Tracy eastward to State Route 99 in Modesto. The corridor is functionally important for regional freight movement, linking the San Joaquin Valley with the San Francisco Bay Area. Since the early 2000s, portions of SR 132 have been the subject of extensive environmental investigation and remediation due to historical industrial activities, soil stockpiling, and groundwater contamination associated with Caltrans maintenance operations and adjacent land use.

## How it works
The environmental remediation program along SR 132 is managed by the California Department of Transportation (Caltrans) under oversight from the Department of Toxic Substances Control (DTSC). The work addresses multiple discrete sites—often referred to as “stockpiles” or “basins”—that contain contaminated fill, construction spoils, and legacy waste.

**Key components of the remediation program:**

- **Stockpile Identification and Classification** – Several distinct stockpiles exist along the corridor, including “Basin 5 Clean Fill,” “Stockpile 2,” and “BCS Foundation Spoil.” Each has been characterized through soil and groundwater sampling. *(Source: SR 132 Basin 5 Clean Fill Tech Memo; Stockpile 2 BCS Removal Tech Memo)*

- **Risk Assessment** – A Site Screening and Human Health Risk Assessment (SSI/HHRA) was conducted to evaluate potential exposure pathways for nearby residents, workers, and sensitive receptors. This assessment informed cleanup targets. *(Source: Final SSI and HHRA 2013)*

- **Groundwater Monitoring** – A network of monitoring wells has been installed along the stockpile areas. Quarterly and annual groundwater reports track contaminant concentrations (e.g., volatile organic compounds, metals, petroleum hydrocarbons) and document trends. *(Source: Modesto Stockpiles July 2012 Groundwater Report; 2025 GW Monitoring Report DTSC Response)*

- **Removal and Remedial Actions** – Contaminated soil from stockpiles has been excavated and disposed of under removal work plans. The process includes confirmation sampling to verify that remaining soil meets residential or commercial cleanup standards. *(Source: Acceptance Letter Draft Interim RACR; Stockpile 2 BCS Removal Tech Memo)*

- **Regulatory Submittals** – Progress is documented through documents such as the Interim Remedial Action Completion Report (RACR), which DTSC reviews and accepts once cleanup objectives are met. *(Source: Acceptance Letter Draft Interim RACR)*

## Variants
While the term “State Route 132” generally refers to the highway corridor, the remediation sites are grouped into distinct operational variants based on location and source of contamination:

- **Basin 5 Clean Fill Stockpile** – A designated area where relatively clean fill material was historically placed, but later found to contain low-level contaminants requiring management.
- **Stockpile 2 (BCS Foundation Spoil)** – A larger accumulation of spoils from foundation excavations, often with higher concentrations of contaminants such as arsenic and lead.
- **Modesto Stockpile Complex** – A collection of smaller stockpiles near the SR ‑99 interchange, addressed collectively in groundwater reports.
- **Central Valley Stockpile Sites** – Similar but geographically separate stockpiles elsewhere along SR 132; some remain under active investigation.

No alternative highway alignments or operational variants (e.g., expressway vs. freeway) are described in the source materials; the focus is entirely on the environmental management program.

## Trade-offs

The remediation of SR 132 involves several trade-offs and considerations:

| Trade-off | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Cost vs. Schedule** | Full excavation and off‑site disposal is expensive and logistically complex, requiring road closures and traffic management. Faster removal can increase off‑site disposal costs. |
| **Health Protection vs. Land Use** | Cleanup levels are set to protect future residential use in some areas, but if the land remains industrial, a less stringent standard could be applied, reducing costs. |
| **Groundwater vs. Soil** | Contamination in groundwater may require long‑term monitoring or active treatment, while soil removal addresses immediate risks but does not guarantee groundwater improvement. |
| **Public Perception** | The history of contamination and years of remediation create public skepticism; transparent reporting (through RACRs and monitoring reports) is necessary but time‑consuming. |
| **Regulatory Complexity** | Multiple agencies (Caltrans, DTSC, Regional Water Board) must coordinate. Differing cleanup standards for soil vs. groundwater can lead to incremental decisions rather than a single comprehensive remedy. |
| **Remediation Limits** | Some contaminants (e.g., dissolved metals in groundwater) may be naturally occurring or infeasible to fully remove, leading to residual risk and indefinite monitoring. |
