## Definition

A threshold screening level is a concentration value (typically in mg/kg for soil or µg/L for water) used in environmental risk assessment and remediation to determine whether a contaminant poses a potential unacceptable risk and thus warrants further investigation or active management. It serves as an initial, conservative benchmark: concentrations below the screening level are considered “safe” for the intended land use (e.g., residential, industrial), while concentrations above trigger more detailed assessment, such as site-specific risk evaluation or remediation planning. In practice, screening levels are derived from toxicological reference values, exposure assumptions, and fate‑and‑transport models, and are often codified in regulatory guidance.

## How It Works

Threshold screening levels are established through a four‑step process:

1. **Hazard Identification & Toxicity Assessment** – For each contaminant of concern, an acceptable daily intake (e.g., reference dose, RfD) or cancer slope factor is selected from authoritative sources (e.g., USEPA IRIS, ATSDR).

2. **Exposure Scenario Definition** – The intended land use (residential, commercial, construction worker) determines the exposure pathways (ingestion, inhalation, dermal contact) and exposure parameters (e.g., body weight, soil ingestion rate, exposure duration).

3. **Screening Level Calculation** – Using standard risk equations (e.g., from USEPA’s Regional Screening Levels methodology), a maximum contaminant concentration is back‑calculated that, if not exceeded, results in a hazard index ≤ 1 or lifetime cancer risk ≤ 10⁻⁶. The calculation incorporates default assumptions (e.g., 50% bioavailability, standard soil‑to‑air volatilization factors).

4. **Comparison with Measured Concentrations** – Soil or groundwater sample results are compared directly to the threshold screening level. If all results are below the level, no further action is required (the site is considered “clean” for that contaminant). If any result exceeds the level, a more detailed, site‑specific risk assessment or remediation may be triggered.

In the context of construction projects (e.g., SR 132 Stockpile, Carpenter Road Shoofly), materials that are stockpiled or excavated are tested against threshold screening levels to classify them as “clean fill” or as requiring special handling (e.g., disposal at a permitted facility).

## Variants

- **Soil Screening Levels (SSLs)** – Developed by USEPA for volatiles and semi‑volatiles in soil, often used in CERCLA and RCRA investigations.
- **Risk‑Based Screening Levels (RBSLs)** – State‑specific values (e.g., in Washington State’s Model Toxics Control Act) that incorporate local environmental conditions and land‑use assumptions.
- **Generic vs. Site‑Specific** – Generic screening levels are pre‑calculated using conservative defaults; site‑specific levels are derived using actual site characteristics (e.g., soil type, depth to groundwater) and may be higher or lower.
- **Direct‑Exposure vs. Leaching** – Direct‑exposure screening levels protect human receptors on‑site, whereas leaching screening levels protect groundwater via soil‑to‑water transport.

## Trade‑Offs

- **Conservatism vs. Cost** – Generic threshold screening levels are intentionally conservative (protective of a hypersensitive hypothetical receptor), which can lead to “false positives” – clean materials being incorrectly designated as contaminated, driving unnecessary testing or disposal costs.
- **Uncertainty** – Screening levels rely on default parameters (e.g., 50% bioavailability) that may not reflect actual conditions, potentially over‑ or under‑estimating risk.
- **Regulatory Inconsistency** – Different jurisdictions or programs (federal vs. state) may use different screening levels for the same contaminant, complicating multi‑state projects.
- **Risk Communication** – Exceeding a screening level is not a direct measure of harm; it only triggers further evaluation. Stakeholders may misinterpret exceedances as indicating an immediate health threat.
- **Limited Coverage** – Screening levels exist only for a subset of contaminants (commonly ~100 chemicals). Emerging contaminants (e.g., PFAS, 1,4‑dioxane) may lack derived levels, requiring alternative approaches.
