## Definition

**Clean Cap Criteria** are the acceptance criteria used to determine whether a capped environmental feature—such as a soil stockpile, excavation area, wall footing zone, or similar remedial feature—has been prepared and sampled sufficiently to be considered “clean” for closure, reuse, or the next construction/remedial step.

In practice, the criteria define:
- what material or area must be sampled,
- how many samples are required,
- what analytical results or field observations are acceptable, and
- what approvals are needed before the feature can be documented as meeting the clean standard.

The concept appears in remedial construction documentation and agency approval e-mails, where a technical memo proposes the criteria and the regulator confirms acceptance.

## How it works

Clean cap criteria are usually established during remedial design or construction quality assurance and then implemented in the field through sampling and documentation.

A typical process is:

1. **Define the capped unit**
   - Identify the specific stockpile, footing, shoofly, or other area to be evaluated.
   - Delineate its boundaries and depth or thickness.

2. **Specify sampling requirements**
   - Determine whether sampling is required at the surface, along the base, at intervals through the material, or at discrete locations.
   - For stockpiles, this often means collecting samples from multiple locations to represent the whole pile.
   - For footing-related areas, sampling may target disturbed soil or excavated subgrade.

3. **Establish analytical and/or field acceptance thresholds**
   - Results must demonstrate that the material meets project-specific cleanup levels or clean fill criteria.
   - Field observations may also matter, such as absence of staining, debris, odors, or visibly impacted soil.
   - The criteria can be either numerical laboratory thresholds, visual standards, or both.

4. **Document compliance**
   - A technical memorandum summarizes the sampling plan, methods, results, and conclusion.
   - If the results satisfy the criteria, the memo states that the stockpile or area is acceptable as a clean cap or clean material layer.

5. **Obtain agency concurrence**
   - In the source material, acceptance is confirmed by e-mail from the regulatory agency, indicating that the proposed criteria or the technical memo was approved.
   - This agency concurrence is important because it converts a project proposal into an accepted compliance basis.

The main function of clean cap criteria is to create an objective decision rule for whether material can remain in place or be incorporated into a final configuration without further remedial action.

## Variants

Clean cap criteria can vary by project and by the type of feature being evaluated.

### By project element
- **Stockpile clean cap criteria**: used to classify excavated soil piles before reuse, disposal, or temporary cover.
- **Wall footing sampling criteria**: used to evaluate soil or bedding material beneath or adjacent to structural footings.
- **Shoofly or temporary work area criteria**: used for temporary construction reroutes or work platforms.
- **Remedial design implementation criteria**: broader criteria used across multiple features in a cleanup area.

### By basis of acceptance
- **Chemical criteria**: concentrations must be below cleanup standards, background levels, or risk-based thresholds.
- **Physical criteria**: material must meet texture, debris, moisture, or placement requirements.
- **Combined criteria**: both chemical and physical conditions must be satisfied.

### By approval status
- **Proposed criteria**: presented in a technical memo for review.
- **Accepted criteria**: formally approved by the agency, often via e-mail concurrence.
- **Field-adjusted criteria**: modified during construction if unexpected site conditions require a change.

### By documentation style
- **Memo-based criteria**: embedded in a technical memorandum.
- **E-mail acceptance**: approval documented in correspondence rather than a signed report.
- **Variance-based criteria**: established through an approved variance request when standard requirements cannot be met exactly.

## Trade-offs

### Strengths
- Provides a clear, auditable decision standard.
- Reduces ambiguity in the field.
- Supports regulatory compliance and construction sequencing.
- Helps distinguish acceptable clean material from impacted material.

### Limitations
- Criteria can be site-specific and not transferable without review.
- If sampling density is too low, accepted material may still contain localized contamination.
- If criteria are too strict, they can increase cost, delay work, or require unnecessary excavation.
- If approval is only captured in e-mail, the administrative record must be carefully preserved to avoid later disputes.

### Considerations
- The sampling design must be representative of the entire unit.
- Analytical methods and detection limits must be appropriate for the target criteria.
- The criteria should align with the project’s conceptual site model and remedial objectives.
- Acceptance often depends on both the technical memo and regulator concurrence, not just laboratory data.
