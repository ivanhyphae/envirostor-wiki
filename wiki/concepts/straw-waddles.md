## Definition

**Straw waddles** (also called straw wattles, straw rolls, or erosion control wattles) are cylindrical, tube-shaped erosion and sediment control devices made of compressed straw (often wheat or rice straw) encased in a biodegradable netting, such as jute, coir, or synthetic mesh. They are used on construction sites, slopes, and disturbed soil areas to slow runoff, capture sediment, and promote revegetation.

## How it works

Straw waddles function primarily through three mechanisms:

- **Runoff velocity reduction:** The cylindrical shape creates a permeable barrier that slows overland flow, causing sediment particles to settle out of suspension.
- **Sediment capture:** As water passes through the straw matrix, fine soil particles are filtered out and accumulate behind the waddle.
- **Water infiltration:** By ponding water behind the waddle, the device increases infiltration time, reducing erosion and promoting soil moisture for plant growth.

Installation typically involves placing the waddle in shallow trenches (2–4 inches deep) cut perpendicular to the slope, staking it with wooden stakes at regular intervals (typically 3–4 feet), and slightly overlapping ends to prevent bypass flow. Proper keying into the soil prevents undercutting.

## Variants

- **Biodegradable vs. non-biodegradable netting:** Some waddles use jute or coir netting that decomposes over months; others use synthetic polypropylene mesh (non-biodegradable) for longer service life but require removal.
- **Straw type:** Rice straw is common because it has fewer weed seeds; wheat straw may be used regionally. Coir (coconut fiber) waddles are denser, heavier, and more durable in high‑flow channels.
- **Size:** Diameters range from 4 to 12 inches; lengths are typically 10–25 feet. Larger diameters are used for concentrated flows or steep slopes.
- **Weighted waddles:** Some variants include an internal sand or gravel core to increase stability in high‑velocity runoff.

## Trade-offs

| Advantage | Limitation |
|-----------|------------|
| Effective at reducing concentrated flow erosion on slopes | Less effective in channels with high flow velocities unless anchored heavily |
| Biodegradable options reduce removal labor | Straw may introduce weed seeds (opt for certified weed‑free) |
| Flexible and easy to contour to terrain | Installation labor is higher than for sediment fences |
| Can be left to decompose on site | Non‑biodegradable netting must be removed and disposed of |
| Supports vegetation establishment by trapping moisture and seed | Degradation rate can be too fast in wet climates (6–12 months) |

Key considerations: Waddles are not intended for perimeter sediment control on large flat areas (where silt fences are better). They require regular inspection after storms to ensure no bypass erosion has occurred.
