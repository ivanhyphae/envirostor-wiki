## Underground Storage Tanks

### Definition

An underground storage tank (UST) is any tank and its associated piping that has at least 10 percent of its volume below the surface of the ground. USTs are commonly used to store regulated substances such as petroleum products (gasoline, diesel, heating oil), hazardous chemicals, or waste liquids. In regulatory contexts, the term often refers specifically to tanks subject to federal and state leak prevention, monitoring, and closure requirements.

### How it works

A UST system consists of the tank itself, piping, and ancillary equipment (e.g., pumps, spill containment, overfill protection). Tanks are usually cylindrical and made of steel or fiberglass-reinforced plastic. They are installed below grade, often in excavated pits backfilled with sand or gravel for support and leak detection access.

The key operational components are:

- **Spill prevention**: Catch basins or spill buckets at the fill pipe to contain drips.
- **Overfill prevention**: Automatic shutoff valves or audible/visual alarms that stop flow when the tank reaches a preset level.
- **Corrosion protection**: Cathodic protection (sacrificial anodes or impressed current) for steel tanks and piping, or the use of non-corrodible materials.
- **Leak detection**: Continuous monitoring via interstitial monitoring (double-walled tanks), automatic tank gauging, vapor monitoring, groundwater monitoring, or statistical inventory reconciliation.

When a UST is in active service, product is delivered through a fill pipe, stored, and withdrawn through a submerged pump or suction system. Pressure or vacuum sensors, along with electronic line leak detectors, provide early warning of releases.

### Variants

- **Steel tanks**: Traditional, require corrosion protection and may be single- or double-walled.
- **Fiberglass tanks**: Non-corrodible, inherently more resistant to leaks, often double-walled.
- **Double-walled tanks**: The inner wall contains the substance; the interstitial space is monitored for leaks.
- **Underground piping**: May be flexible or rigid, with secondary containment or monitoring wells.
- **Cathodically protected tanks**: Steel tanks with anodes or impressed current to prevent rust.
- **Decommissioned or closed-in-place tanks**: Filled with an inert material (e.g., sand or foam) and left in place when no longer used, with proper documentation.

### Trade-offs

| Consideration | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|---------------|------------|---------------|
| **Environmental risk** | Properly managed USTs safely store large volumes of fuel underground, reducing fire and explosion risk compared to aboveground storage. | Leaks can cause soil and groundwater contamination, posing long-term liability and cleanup costs. |
| **Space utilization** | Saves surface area for other uses (e.g., parking lots, buildings). | Difficult to inspect; leaks may go undetected for long periods. |
| **Regulation** | Federal (EPA 40 CFR 280/281) and state rules provide clear compliance standards. | Expensive to install, upgrade, and maintain; requires recordkeeping, testing, and third-party inspections. |
| **Lifespan** | Typically 20–30 years with proper maintenance. | Corrosion and structural failure can occur earlier if protection systems fail. |
| **Closure** | Can be removed or closed in place with proper procedures. | Removal is costly; closed-in-place tanks must be permanently filled and documented, which may complicate future land use. |
