## Definition

**Sr 132** refers to a collection of soil borrow areas, stockpiles, and clean fill source locations associated with California State Route 132 (SR‑132) construction projects. These sites are subject to environmental sampling and monitoring plans (SMPs) to verify that earth materials meet regulatory standards for reuse as clean fill or structural backfill. In the provided documents, Sr 132 is used as a project identifier for borrow area sampling, stockpile reclamation, and clean fill source testing under Caltrans oversight.

## How it works

The management of Sr 132 borrow areas and stockpiles follows a standardized workflow:

1. **Sampling Plan Development** – A Borrow Area Sampling Plan specifies the frequency, methods, and analytes for testing soil from designated sources along SR‑132. This plan must be approved by regulatory agencies (e.g., via the Final SMP Approval E‑mail).

2. **Stockpile Characterization** – Materials excavated and stockpiled (as seen in the *Caltrans Modesto Stockpile RDIP*) are evaluated for chemical and physical properties. The Reclamation and Disposal Implementation Plan (RDIP) outlines criteria for classifying stockpiled soil as clean or contaminated.

3. **Clean Fill Source Testing** – The *Hwy 132 Clean Fill Source Testing Exhibit* demonstrates that a specific source meets the definition of "clean fill" – typically meaning no hazardous constituents or only trace levels below action thresholds. Testing includes analyses for metals, volatile organic compounds (VOCs), semi‑volatile organic compounds (SVOCs), and petroleum hydrocarbons.

4. **Regulatory Approval** – The Sampling and Monitoring Plan (SMP) is submitted to oversight bodies (e.g., California Department of Toxic Substances Control or local water boards). Final approval, as indicated in the e‑mail, allows the project to proceed with material movement or reuse.

5. **Documentation and Tracking** – Each load from an approved borrow area or stockpile is accompanied by a chain‑of‑custody and laboratory results, ensuring that only approved materials are used as fill.

## Variants

- **Borrow Area vs. Stockpile** – A borrow area is a source site from which soil is excavated; a stockpile is a temporary storage of excavated material. Both require separate sampling plans, though criteria may overlap.
- **On‑site vs. Off‑site Sources** – Sr 132 may include both on‑corridor borrow areas (within the highway right‑of‑way) and off‑site commercial sources that supply fill.
- **Clean Fill vs. Beneficial Reuse** – Materials that do not meet clean fill criteria may still be used for other purposes (e.g., structural fill, landscaping) under a different regulatory framework (e.g., a waste discharge requirements order).

## Trade-offs

- **Cost of Testing** – Comprehensive sampling (chemical, physical, radiological) can be expensive; balancing the number of samples with acceptable confidence is a recurring challenge.
- **Time Delays** – Approval of the SMP and testing results may delay construction schedules, especially if regulatory review cycles are long.
- **Environmental Compliance vs. Construction Needs** – Strict testing ensures protection of groundwater and soil quality but can restrict the volume of usable fill, forcing contractors to seek alternative sources.
- **Stockpile Management** – Large stockpiles require erosion control, dust suppression, and periodic retesting if stored for extended periods.
