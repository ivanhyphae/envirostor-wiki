## Definition

A **Remedial Action Completion Report (RACR)** is a formal document that demonstrates the successful implementation and completion of a selected remedial action at a contaminated site. It provides a comprehensive summary of the remedial activities performed, verifies that cleanup objectives have been met, and supports regulatory closure or transition to long-term monitoring. The RACR serves as the primary record for confirming that the remedy is operating as intended, that all performance standards are satisfied, and that site conditions are consistent with the remedial action objectives (RAOs) defined in the decision document (e.g., Record of Decision, Corrective Action Plan).

## How it works

A RACR is typically prepared after the remedial action construction phase is complete and any necessary performance testing or verification sampling has been conducted. The report follows a standardized structure, often aligned with regulatory guidance (e.g., US EPA’s Superfund program, state cleanup programs, or RCRA corrective action). Key steps in its development include:

1. **Documentation of Remedial Construction**  
   The RACR describes the as-built condition of all remedial components (e.g., extraction wells, treatment systems, caps, slurry walls, in-situ treatment zones). It includes construction logs, materials certifications, as-built drawings, and deviations from the original design.

2. **Verification of Remedial Action Objectives**  
   Site‑specific RAOs are restated, and the report presents data demonstrating that each objective is met. This typically involves:
   - Comparison of post-remediation contaminant concentrations to cleanup standards.
   - Evaluation of engineered system performance (e.g., groundwater extraction rates, treatment efficiencies).
   - Confirmation that institutional controls (e.g., deed restrictions, access limitations) are in place.

3. **Quality Assurance/Quality Control (QA/QC) Summary**  
   The RACR includes a discussion of sampling and analytical QA/QC measures, data validation results, and any deviations from the approved Quality Assurance Project Plan.

4. **Compliance with Regulatory Milestones**  
   The report confirms that all permit conditions, consent decree requirements, or state‑issued orders have been satisfied. It may also include a schedule for future operations, maintenance, and monitoring (OMM) if the remedy requires long‑term stewardship.

5. **Certification and Approval**  
   Professional certification (e.g., by a licensed engineer or geologist) accompanies the RACR. The document is submitted to the lead regulatory agency for review and approval. Upon acceptance, the site may transition to **Operation & Maintenance (O&M)** status or be closed with a **No Further Action** determination.

## Variants

While the core purpose of a RACR is consistent across programs, several variants exist:

- **Interim RACR** – Used when remedial actions are implemented in phases. An interim report documents completion of a distinct operable unit or construction cell, even though the entire site remedy is not yet finished. The source document (Draft Interim RACR_ App D-G.pdf) is an example.
- **Final RACR** – Covers the entire remedial action for a site, leading to regulatory closure or long‑term O&M.
- **Remedial Action Report (RAR)** – A common synonym in RCRA corrective action and some state programs; often identical in scope to a RACR.
- **Construction Completion Report (CCR)** – Applied in US EPA Superfund after "Construction Completion" is attained. The CCR is a predecessor to the RACR but focuses on physical construction rather than performance verification. A RACR may be required after the CCR to confirm remedy effectiveness.
- **Corrective Action Completion Report (CACR)** – Used in RCRA corrective action; functionally equivalent but specific to the Resource Conservation and Recovery Act framework.

## Trade-offs

- **Timing vs. Certainty** – Issuing a RACR too early (e.g., before sufficient post-construction monitoring data are collected) risks overlooking residual contamination that may require additional action. Delaying the report increases costs and delays site closure.
- **Regulatory Acceptance** – Different regulatory authorities have varying requirements for the level of detail and the number of confirmatory samples. A RACR that satisfies one program may not meet the standards of another (e.g., state vs. federal oversight).
- **Data Representativeness** – The report’s conclusions are only as robust as the verification data. Limited spatial or temporal sampling can lead to false confidence; overly conservative sampling plans drive up costs.
- **Institutional Controls Burden** – When active remediation ends, the RACR must describe how long‑term controls (e.g., restrictions on groundwater use, zoning limits) will be maintained. The burden of perpetual monitoring and enforcement may be transferred to future landowners or local agencies.
- **Professional Liability** – The certifying professional bears significant liability if the report later proves incorrect. This may lead to conservative interpretations and additional data collection to reduce risk.
