## Overview

The FMC Evaporation Pond is a former industrial waste containment feature located within a 4.3-acre parcel in Stanislaus County, California that was historically owned by the Food Machinery and Chemical Corporation (FMC). The pond was situated in the southernmost corner of the parcel. FMC and its predecessors used the site for chemical manufacturing, processing barium sulfate, strontium sulfate, and other mineral ores. The evaporation pond received process tailings and waste from these operations.

The pond is the primary source of contamination at the Caltrans Modesto Soil Stockpiles site, which consists of excess native soils and pond tailings generated during construction of State Route 99 north of Kansas Avenue in the early 1960s. When Caltrans excavated the highway segment, it traversed a portion of the FMC parcel and encountered the former evaporation pond materials.

## Site Record

The FMC Evaporation Pond is documented in the November 29, 2012 letter from DTSC to Caltrans regarding groundwater monitoring reports. The letter states that the soil stockpiles "consist of excess native soils and pond tailings that were generated when Caltrans constructed a segment of SR 99 north of Kansas Avenue in the early 1960's" and that "excavating the segment traversed a portion of a 4.3-acre parcel purchased from Food Machinery and Chemical Corporation (FMC). An evaporation pond was located in the southernmost corner of the parcel."

The primary chemicals of concern (COCs) associated with the pond are **barium**, **lead**, and **strontium**. These are identified in the DTSC November 29, 2012 letter as the chemicals of concern for the site.

Groundwater monitoring wells MW-9 and MW-10 were installed in June 2012 to monitor groundwater quality south of Kansas Avenue, in the vicinity of the former pond. MW-9 is located south of Kansas Avenue and west of SR 99; MW-10 is located south of Kansas Avenue and east of SR 99. These wells supplement existing monitoring network for assessing COC migration.

The groundwater monitoring reports reviewed by DTSC cover May, June, and July 2012 sampling events. The reports were prepared by Geocon Consultants and dated November 2012. DTSC concurred with the findings of these reports via letter dated November 29, 2012.

## Regulatory Status

The site is overseen jointly by the **Department of Toxic Substances Control (DTSC)** and the **Regional Water Quality Control Board, Central Valley Region (RWQCB)** . DTSC is the lead regulatory agency for the soil stockpile remediation. The November 29, 2012 letter from DTSC Senior Engineering Geologist Randy S. Adams confirmed DTSC's concurrence with the groundwater monitoring reports.

The DTSC Brownfields and Environmental Restoration Program is the specific program managing the oversight. The applicable regulatory standards for the COCs (barium, lead, strontium) are not specified in the reviewed source documents, but groundwater monitoring is conducted to assess compliance with applicable water quality objectives.

The December 2012 DTSC letter requested that "future groundwater monitoring reports be completed as groundwater data becomes available," indicating ongoing monitoring obligations under the regulatory framework.
