## Definition

**Maximum Contaminant Levels (MCLs)** are enforceable regulatory limits set by the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) on the amount of a specific substance allowed in public drinking water supplies. They are established under the Safe Drinking Water Act (SDWA) to protect human health from both chronic and acute adverse effects while accounting for the feasibility of detection and treatment. In the context of groundwater monitoring at sites such as the Modesto Stockpiles, MCLs serve as benchmark concentrations for evaluating water quality and determining the need for remediation.

## How it works

MCLs are derived from **Maximum Contaminant Level Goals (MCLGs)**, which are non‑enforceable health‑based targets set at levels that pose no known or anticipated risk, with an adequate margin of safety. The EPA then sets the MCL as close to the MCLG as feasible, where *feasible* means achievable with the best available treatment technology, taking cost into account. The process involves:

1. **Health effects assessment** – toxicological studies determine the dose‑response relationship for each contaminant.
2. **Exposure analysis** – typical drinking water ingestion rates are used to calculate the concentration that would result in an acceptable lifetime risk (e.g., 10⁻⁶ cancer risk for carcinogens).
3. **Technical feasibility** – treatment technologies are evaluated for their ability to reliably reduce contaminant concentrations to the target level.
4. **Cost‑benefit analysis** – economic impacts on water systems are considered to avoid undue financial burden while still achieving significant public health protection.

Once promulgated, public water systems must monitor regularly and ensure that the concentration of each regulated contaminant does not exceed its MCL. Exceedances trigger public notification and, if persistent, enforcement actions (e.g., fines, mandatory treatment upgrades). For unregulated contaminants, the EPA may issue **Health Advisories** (non‑enforceable) to guide local action.

## Variants

- **Primary MCLs** – apply to contaminants that pose direct health risks (e.g., arsenic, lead, nitrate). These are enforceable standards.
- **Secondary MCLs (SMCLs)** – non‑enforceable aesthetic guidelines for substances that affect taste, odor, or appearance (e.g., chloride, iron, manganese). Water suppliers are encouraged, but not required, to meet them.
- **State‑specific MCLs** – some states adopt more stringent limits than the federal MCLs (e.g., California’s Proposition 65 MCLs for certain chemicals).
- **Operational MCLs** – internal trigger levels used by water utilities or remediation projects (like those at a stockpile site) to manage treatment processes before a violation occurs.
- **Site‑specific screening levels** – variations used in groundwater monitoring programs (e.g., at the Modesto Stockpiles) that may reference MCLs as cleanup goals but can be adjusted based on local hydrogeology and land use.

## Trade-offs

- **Health vs. cost** – setting MCLs too low may require expensive treatment that small water systems cannot afford, leading to compliance failures and, paradoxically, higher population exposure. Too high undermines health protection.
- **Feasibility vs. ideal safety** – MCLs are often higher than MCLGs because complete removal of a contaminant is economically or technically impractical; a small residual risk is accepted.
- **Detection limits** – analytical methods must be able to reliably measure concentrations at or below the MCL. For some contaminants (e.g., perfluorinated chemicals), current MCLs may be near or below practical quantitation limits.
- **Emerging contaminants** – the rulemaking process is slow; many substances (e.g., PFAS, NDMA) remain unregulated or governed only by health advisories, leaving gaps in protection.
- **Temporal variability** – MCLs are static limits, but contaminant concentrations in groundwater can fluctuate seasonally or due to remediation activities, requiring careful sampling design to avoid false compliance assessments.
