## Definition

**Dissolved oxygen (DO)** is the amount of gaseous oxygen (O₂) dissolved in a liquid, typically water. It is a critical parameter in aquatic chemistry, water quality assessment, and environmental monitoring. DO is usually expressed in milligrams per liter (mg/L) or as a percentage of saturation relative to the equilibrium concentration at a given temperature, pressure, and salinity.

In groundwater systems, dissolved oxygen levels indicate the redox state of the aquifer. Low DO (<1 mg/L) suggests anaerobic conditions, while higher DO (>5 mg/L) indicates aerobic environments.

## How it works

### Factors controlling DO concentration
The solubility of oxygen in water follows Henry’s law: the concentration of dissolved gas is proportional to its partial pressure in the atmosphere. Key variables include:

- **Temperature**: Oxygen solubility decreases as temperature rises. For example, at 0 °C freshwater can hold about 14.6 mg/L, while at 25 °C it holds about 8.3 mg/L.
- **Salinity**: Higher salinity reduces oxygen solubility (the “salting‑out” effect).
- **Atmospheric pressure**: Lower pressure (e.g., at high altitude) reduces the partial pressure of oxygen, lowering DO.
- **Biological activity**: Photosynthesis by algae and plants produces oxygen, while respiration and decomposition consume it.
- **Turbulence and aeration**: Mixing from water flow, waves, or artificial aeration increases the rate of oxygen transfer from the atmosphere.

### Measurement methods
DO can be measured in the field or laboratory using:

- **Electrochemical (Clark‑type) sensors**: A membrane‑covered electrode reduces oxygen at the cathode, producing a current proportional to DO.
- **Optical (luminescence) sensors**: An oxygen‑sensitive dye is excited by a light pulse; oxygen quenches the fluorescence, and the decay time correlates with DO.
- **Winkler titration**: A chemical method (azide modification) where manganese(II) is oxidized by DO in basic solution, then titrated with thiosulfate. This is the reference standard.

### Importance in environmental monitoring
DO is a primary indicator of water quality. In groundwater investigations—such as those for Caltrans Modesto stockpiles—DO helps delineate contamination plumes, assess natural attenuation, and design remediation strategies. Low DO often coincides with elevated dissolved iron, manganese, or sulfide, signaling reducing conditions.

## Variants

### Oxygen saturation (% saturation)
The fraction of DO relative to the theoretical maximum solubility under ambient conditions. Saturation below 100% indicates net oxygen consumption; supersaturation (>100%) can occur during intense photosynthesis or below waterfalls.

### Biochemical oxygen demand (BOD)
The amount of oxygen consumed by microorganisms while decomposing organic matter under aerobic conditions at a specified temperature (usually 20 °C) over a set period (commonly 5 days, BOD₅). It is an indirect measure of biodegradable organic pollution.

### Chemical oxygen demand (COD)
A similar measure that uses a strong chemical oxidant (dichromate) to oxidize organic matter. COD is generally higher than BOD because it oxidizes compounds that are not biologically degradable.

### Dissolved oxygen in groundwater
In aquifers, DO typically declines with depth and residence time. Shallow, recently recharged groundwater may be oxygenated; deeper or confined aquifers often become anoxic. The DO gradient helps map flow paths and redox zones.

## Trade‑offs

### Sensor accuracy and maintenance
Electrochemical sensors require calibration and membrane replacement; optical sensors are more stable but more expensive. Both are sensitive to fouling by biofilms or silt, which is common in groundwater samples.

### Field vs. laboratory measurement
DO should be measured immediately in the field because oxygen can be rapidly consumed or gained during sample handling and transport. This is especially critical for groundwater, where low DO values are easily altered by contact with air.

### Interpretation challenges
DO alone does not reveal the specific electron donors or acceptors present. Coupling DO with other parameters (e.g., pH, Eh, nitrate, sulfate, iron) is necessary to understand biogeochemical processes. Supersaturation in surface waters may be transient and not reflect long‑term conditions.
