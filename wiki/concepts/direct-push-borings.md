## Definition

**Direct Push Borings** are a subsurface exploration technique where a hollow steel rod or sampler is advanced into the ground by hydraulically pushing, hammering, or vibrating it without the use of a rotary drilling rig or circulating drilling fluids. The method relies on static force and/or percussion, typically applied by a small, truck- or track-mounted percussion hammer or hydraulic ram, to penetrate soil and collect continuous or discrete soil cores. It is widely used for geotechnical investigations, environmental site assessments, and borrow area evaluations, especially in unconsolidated or soft-to-medium earth materials.

## How it works

Direct push boring involves advancing a steel sampling tube (often with a disposable or reusable liner) into subsurface soils using direct mechanical force. The process is as follows:

1. **Setup**: A direct push rig, typically a small, mobile unit (e.g., Geoprobe® system), positions the probe at the target location. The rig’s hydraulic hammer or push mechanism is aligned vertically.

2. **Advancement**: The sampler is forced into the ground by repeated percussive blows or a steady hydraulic push. In most systems, the rate of penetration is roughly 1–2 inches per blow, depending on soil density and texture. For harder layers or gravel, both pushing and hammering may be used.

3. **Sampling**: Inside the outer sampling tube, a thin-walled liner or piston sampler collects a continuous soil column as the tool advances. In soft clays or sands, the sample is retained via internal friction or a spring-loaded retention system.

4. **Retrieval**: After reaching the desired depth (up to 30–40 feet in typical geotechnical work, sometimes deeper with specialized equipment), the sampler is retracted by reversing the hydraulic system or by lifting with a winch. The liner containing the soil core is removed and sealed for transport to a laboratory.

5. **Data collection**: Logging on the rig records penetration resistance (blow counts per foot), depth, and soil type changes. Collected cores are then analyzed for grain size, moisture content, shear strength, and chemical contaminants as needed.

This method is distinct from rotary drilling, which uses a rotating bit and circulating mud to cut and remove material, or auger drilling, which uses a helical screw to extract spoil.

## Variants

Several direct push systems exist, differing in driving mechanism and sample collection:

- **Hydraulic Push Systems**: Use a static hydraulic ram to advance a solid or hollow rod. Ideal for soft, homogenous soils (clays, silts) where minimal disturbance is desired.
- **Percussion-Driven Systems**: Use a reciprocating hammer (e.g., hammer drill) to drive the sampler. Better suited for compact or gravelly soils where pushing alone fails.
- **Vibratory Systems**: Employ a high-frequency vibrator to liquefy surrounding soil and reduce friction, enabling deeper penetration in dense sands or cohesive soils.
- **Continuous Sampling Probes**: Collect uninterrupted soil cores typically 4–5 feet in length per drive. The most common variant in geotechnical work.
- **Discrete (Split-Spoon) Samplers**: Collect short (2 ft or less) samples at specific depths, often used in standard penetration test (SPT) method when combined with manual hammering.
- **Environmental Probes**: Small-diameter push probes (e.g., 1–2 inch) used to collect soil gas, groundwater samples, or for installing monitoring wells without creating large boreholes.

Alternatives to direct push boring include:

- Rotary Drilling with hollow-stem augers or mud rotary systems
- Auger Drilling (continuous flight augers or bucket augers)
- Cone Penetration Testing (CPT), which also uses a static push but measures tip resistance and sleeve friction rather than collecting physical cores

## Trade-offs

| Advantage | Limitation |
|-----------|------------|
| **Speed**: Often 2–5 times faster than rotary drilling in appropriate soils | **Depth restriction**: Typically limited to 30–40 ft in most soils; can reach deeper with specialized equipment but rarely exceeds 100 ft |
| **Minimal waste**: No drilling fluids or cuttings need disposal; equipment is compact | **Soil limitations**: Ineffective in gravel, cobbles, boulders, hard rock, or heavily cemented layers |
| **Low disturbance**: Less disturbance to site surface and soil structure compared to traditional drilling | **Sample quality**: Cores can be disturbed by hammering or may experience smearing or compression |
| **Cost-effective**: Lower mobilization and equipment costs for shallow investigations | **No soil strength data**: Does not directly measure in-situ strength (unlike CPT or SPT); strength must be inferred from lab tests on recovered cores |
| **Environmental safety**: Preferred for contaminated sites as it generates less potentially hazardous debris | **Limited casing**: Difficulty maintaining hole stability in loose sands; requires temporary casing or grouting in caving conditions |
| **Accessibility**: Can be deployed in tight spaces or hard-to-reach areas | **Equipment wear**: High push forces can damage rods or tip cones, especially in gravel |

Key considerations: Direct push borings are not suitable for deep bedrock investigations or highly heterogeneous soils with large obstructions. In borrow area evaluations (such as those described in the source technical memo), they are ideal for characterizing shallow alluvial or glacial deposits where rapid, continuous sampling is needed, but coarser soils may require alternative methods like power augering or backhoe excavations.
