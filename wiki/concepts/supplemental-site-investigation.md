## Definition

Supplemental Site Investigation (SSI) is a targeted follow-up investigation performed after an initial site characterization, usually when existing data are insufficient to fully define the type, extent, or significance of contamination at a site. It is typically designed to resolve specific uncertainties identified during earlier sampling, to refine the conceptual site model, and to support decisions about risk assessment, corrective action, or closure.

An SSI is not a general exploratory survey; it is a focused program of additional field work and analysis intended to answer outstanding technical questions about environmental conditions, contaminant distribution, and potential exposure pathways.

## How it works

A Supplemental Site Investigation generally follows an initial site assessment or remedial investigation and is structured around the data gaps that remain. The process usually includes the following steps:

1. **Identify data needs**
   - Review prior investigation results.
   - Determine what is still unknown, such as:
     - lateral and vertical extent of contamination,
     - source areas,
     - contaminant concentrations in specific media,
     - migration pathways,
     - conditions needed for human health or ecological risk evaluation.

2. **Develop focused sampling objectives**
   - Define the specific questions the SSI must answer.
   - Select analytes, media, locations, and depths based on prior findings.
   - Ensure the sampling design is targeted rather than broad.

3. **Collect additional field data**
   - Perform soil, groundwater, soil gas, sediment, surface water, or other media sampling as needed.
   - Use techniques appropriate to the site, such as borings, monitoring wells, test pits, grab samples, or composite sampling.
   - In some cases, field screening tools or geophysical methods may be used to improve spatial understanding.

4. **Analyze and interpret results**
   - Compare new analytical results with prior data.
   - Map contaminant concentrations and assess whether the new data define the affected area.
   - Update the conceptual site model to reflect revised understanding of sources, transport, receptors, and exposure routes.

5. **Support risk and remedy decisions**
   - Use the findings to update a human health risk assessment or ecological evaluation.
   - Determine whether concentrations warrant remedial action, additional monitoring, or no further action.
   - In some projects, SSI results are used to support regulatory closure or to refine cleanup boundaries.

In practice, an SSI is often iterative: one round of supplemental investigation may reduce uncertainty but still reveal additional questions, leading to further targeted sampling.

## Variants

Common variants and closely related implementations include:

- **Focused SSI**: Investigation limited to a small area or a specific contaminant concern, often used when the main issue is already understood but boundary conditions are not.
- **Delineation sampling**: A form of SSI aimed specifically at defining the horizontal and vertical extent of contamination.
- **Data gap investigation**: An SSI framed around filling explicit missing information needed for decision-making.
- **Risk-driven SSI**: Supplemental sampling designed mainly to support or update a human health risk assessment.
- **Remedial design investigation**: A follow-on investigation used to gather engineering data needed to design cleanup actions.
- **Alternative names**: Depending on jurisdiction or program, similar work may be called a supplemental investigation, additional investigation, focused investigation, or step-out sampling.

## Trade-offs

Key considerations and limitations include:

- **Better decision quality vs. added cost**
  - SSI improves confidence in site conclusions, but it adds field, laboratory, and reporting expenses.

- **Targeted efficiency vs. incomplete coverage**
  - Because SSI is focused, it can efficiently answer specific questions.
  - However, narrow sampling may miss unrecognized hotspots or pathways if the conceptual model is incomplete.

- **Faster closure vs. iterative delay**
  - Well-designed SSI can shorten the path to closure by resolving uncertainties.
  - Poorly scoped SSI may simply postpone decisions and lead to repeated rounds of sampling.

- **Refined risk estimates vs. false precision**
  - More data can improve a human health risk assessment, but site variability may still limit certainty.
  - Dense sampling is not always sufficient to eliminate uncertainty in heterogeneous media.

- **Regulatory alignment**
  - SSI must be aligned with agency expectations, especially when used to support cleanup decisions.
  - Inadequate documentation of methods, QA/QC, or sampling rationale can reduce its usefulness.

- **Conceptual model dependence**
  - The value of SSI depends heavily on the quality of the existing conceptual site model.
  - If the original model is flawed, supplemental sampling may not address the real problem.
