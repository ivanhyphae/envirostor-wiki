## CEQA (California Environmental Quality Act)

### Definition
CEQA (California Environmental Quality Act) is a state law (California Public Resources Code §§ 21000–21189.70) that requires public agencies to identify and mitigate the significant environmental impacts of proposed projects before approval. CEQA applies to discretionary projects undertaken, funded, or permitted by state or local government agencies. It mandates disclosure of environmental effects, public participation, and adoption of feasible mitigation measures or project alternatives that reduce significant environmental harm.

### How it works
CEQA compliance follows a multi-step process:

1. **Initial Study**: The lead agency (the public agency approving or carrying out the project) conducts a preliminary review to determine whether the project may have a significant effect on the environment. The study evaluates impacts on air quality, water resources, traffic, noise, biological resources, cultural resources, land use, and other environmental factors.

2. **Exemptions**: If a project is categorically exempt under CEQA Guidelines (e.g., infill development, minor alterations to existing facilities) or subject to a statutory exemption, no further environmental review is required. A Notice of Exemption (NOE) may be filed.

3. **Negative Declaration (ND) / Mitigated Negative Declaration (MND)**: If the initial study finds no significant impacts, the agency prepares a Negative Declaration. If mitigation measures reduce all significant impacts to less-than-significant levels, a Mitigated Negative Declaration is prepared. Both require a 20–30 day public review period.

4. **Environmental Impact Report (EIR)**: If the initial study identifies one or more significant impacts that cannot be fully mitigated, the agency must prepare an EIR. The EIR includes:
   - Project description
   - Environmental setting (baseline conditions)
   - Significant environmental impacts
   - Mitigation measures
   - Project alternatives (e.g., no-project alternative, reduced-density alternative)
   - Cumulative impacts
   - Growth-inducing effects
   - Irreversible environmental changes

5. **Public Review and Hearings**: Draft EIRs undergo a 45–60 day public comment period. The lead agency must respond to all substantive comments in writing. A Final EIR is certified by the agency’s decision-making body.

6. **Findings and Statement of Overriding Considerations**: After certifying the EIR, the agency must adopt findings for each significant impact: (a) changes have been made to avoid the impact, (b) mitigation measures have been adopted, or (c) specific economic, social, or other considerations make mitigation infeasible. If significant unavoidable impacts remain, the agency may approve the project only by adopting a Statement of Overriding Considerations (SOC), which explains why the benefits outweigh the harm.

7. **Mitigation Monitoring and Reporting Program (MMRP)**: For projects with adopted mitigation, the agency must ensure implementation through a monitoring and reporting plan.

CEQA review typically takes 6–18 months for EIR-level projects, though complex projects can take longer. The lead agency must circulate a Notice of Preparation (NOP) to responsible agencies and the public early in the process.

### Variants
CEQA is not a single procedure but has several variants depending on project type and context:

- **Tiered EIR**: Used when a programmatic EIR is prepared for a broad plan (e.g., general plan update), followed by subsequent project-level EIRs that tier off the programmatic analysis, focusing on site-specific impacts.

- **Program EIR**: Assesses the cumulative environmental effects of a series of related actions (e.g., a regional transportation plan). Subsequent projects within the program may rely on the program EIR if they are consistent and have no new significant impacts.

- **Supplemental EIR (SEIR)**: Prepared for new significant impacts or substantial changes to a project after certification of the original EIR.

- **Recirculated Draft EIR**: Required if significant new information arises after the draft EIR is published, necessitating a new public review period.

- **Functional Equivalent Documents**: Certain projects (e.g., oil and gas drilling permits, coastal development permits, regulatory approvals under the CEQA Guidelines’ “certified regulatory program” provisions) may use a different environmental document that serves as a functional equivalent of an EIR. However, these are limited and must meet CEQA’s substantive standards.

- **Expedited CEQA for Housing**: Under recent legislation (e.g., AB 1440, SB 35), some affordable housing projects may qualify for shortened review, such as a “streamlined” environmental review through a “related project” exemption or a limited Initial Study.

### Trade-offs
- **Public Participation vs. Efficiency**: CEQA provides robust public input opportunities, but the process can be time-consuming and costly. Delays often arise from litigation, which can challenge environmental documents for procedural or substantive errors.

- **Comprehensive Analysis vs. Project Feasibility**: The law requires thorough analysis of all significant effects, but mitigation measures can dramatically alter project design or add costs. The SOC mechanism allows approval of projects with unavoidable impacts, but political pressure can lead to under-estimation of impacts.

- **Mitigation vs. Alternatives**: The “no project” alternative is always required, but meaningful alternatives (e.g., reduced scale, different location) may be infeasible due to zoning, cost, or property control. Agencies sometimes narrow alternatives to avoid conflict with project objectives.

- **Baseline Determination**: CEQA uses “existing conditions” (actual physical conditions at the time of the Initial Study) as the baseline, not future conditions under the General Plan. This creates tension for long-range planning projects where the baseline may not reflect future growth.

- **Cumulative Impact Complexity**: Assessing combined effects of multiple projects is difficult and often relies on “list-based” or “projection-based” methods. Inconsistent data across jurisdictions weakens cumulative analysis.

- **Litigation Risk**: CEQA lawsuits (“CEQA lawsuits” are common) can delay projects for years. The same law that protects environmental values can also be used to block desirable projects (e.g., clean energy, affordable housing) for procedural reasons unrelated to environmental merit.

- **Functional Equivalent Limitations**: While alternatives to EIRs exist, they are difficult to achieve in practice because they must meet CEQA’s strict requirements for analysis, public notice, and mitigation. Most agencies default to EIRs to avoid legal risk.
