## Nitrate Contamination

### Definition
Nitrate contamination refers to the presence of elevated concentrations of nitrate (NO₃⁻) in groundwater or surface water, typically exceeding regulatory drinking water standards (e.g., the U.S. EPA Maximum Contaminant Level of 10 mg/L as nitrogen). It is a widespread form of nonpoint-source pollution, primarily linked to agricultural and industrial activities, where excess nitrogen from fertilizers, manure, or wastewater infiltrates the subsurface and accumulates in aquifers.

### How it works
Nitrate contamination results from the natural biogeochemical nitrogen cycle interacting with anthropogenic nitrogen inputs. Key processes include:

- **Nitrogen Sources:** Synthetic fertilizers, animal manure, septic system leachate, and atmospheric deposition introduce nitrogen compounds (e.g., ammonium, urea) into the soil.
- **Nitrification:** In aerobic soil conditions, ammonium (NH₄⁺) is oxidized by bacteria (e.g., *Nitrosomonas*, *Nitrobacter*) to nitrate (NO₃⁻), a highly soluble and mobile anion.
- **Leaching:** Because nitrate does not adsorb strongly to soil particles (unlike ammonium), water percolating through the vadose zone carries nitrate downward, bypassing root uptake zones and entering groundwater. This is accelerated in coarse-textured soils or under irrigation/rainfall.
- **Transport:** Once in groundwater, nitrate moves with advection and dispersion, forming plumes that can persist for decades. In reducing (anoxic) conditions, denitrification can convert nitrate to harmless nitrogen gas (N₂), but this is often limited by low organic carbon or oxygen-limiting conditions.

Contamination is often seasonal, peaking after fertilizer application or intense rainfall. Monitoring data from reports like S9525-01-44B (Modesto Stockpiles, June 2013) and S9800-01-17 (June 2014) indicate that nitrate levels in groundwater near stockpile sites can exceed background levels due to leaching from decomposing organic matter.

### Variants
- **Agricultural Nitrate Contamination:** The most common form, from synthetic fertilizer and manure application on croplands (e.g., corn, wheat, vegetables).
- **Industrial Nitrate Contamination:** From fertilizer manufacturing, explosives, or metal finishing operations.
- **Sewage/Septic Nitrate Contamination:** From failed septic systems or wastewater treatment plant effluent, often co-occurring with pathogens or trace organics.
- **Natural Nitrate Contamination:** Rare, from weathering of nitrogen-bearing rocks or biological nitrogen fixation in certain soils (e.g., playa lakes).
- **Stockpile/Stockyard Contamination:** A variant seen in Modesto, CA (reports above), where nitrate leaches from stockpiled organic materials (e.g., compost, manure), creating localized plumes.

### Trade-offs
- **Human Health vs. Agricultural Productivity:** Reducing nitrate contamination often requires limiting fertilizer use, which can reduce crop yields unless precision agriculture or improved irrigation is adopted.
- **Regulatory Compliance vs. Economic Cost:** Meeting the 10 mg/L standard may require expensive remediation (e.g., reverse osmosis, ion exchange, denitrification bioreactors) or providing bottled water, burdensome for small communities.
- **Detection vs. Remediation:** Nitrate plumes are difficult to delineate due to high mobility and mixing; even after source control, legacy contamination persists for decades in aquifers.
- **Denitrification Potential:** While denitrification is a natural remedy, it can produce nitrous oxide (N₂O), a potent greenhouse gas, and may be limited by oxygen or carbon availability.
- **Monitoring Frequency:** Sporadic sampling (e.g., quarterly in above reports) can miss peak contamination events during wet seasons or after fertilizer applications.
