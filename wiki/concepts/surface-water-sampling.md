## Surface Water Sampling

### Definition

Surface water sampling is the systematic collection, preservation, and analysis of water from open bodies such as rivers, lakes, streams, estuaries, and reservoirs to assess their physical, chemical, and biological characteristics. It is a core activity in environmental monitoring, regulatory compliance, and water quality management, often performed to evaluate pollution, ecosystem health, and suitability for human use.

### How it works

Surface water sampling follows a structured workflow to ensure representativeness and reliability of data.

**1. Sampling design** – The first step defines the objectives (e.g., baseline monitoring, spill response, trend analysis) and determines the number and location of sampling sites. Grid, random, or stratified designs are used, with reference sites chosen upstream of potential contamination sources.

**2. Equipment selection** – Common tools include:
- *Grab samplers* (e.g., Van Dorn, Kemmerer) for discrete depth samples.
- *Depth-integrating samplers* (e.g., DH-81, USGS-adapted) that collect a composite sample across the water column.
- *Automated samplers* (e.g., ISCO units) for time- or flow-based sequential samples.

**3. Sample collection** – Technicians collect water using pre-cleaned containers, avoiding contamination from boat motors, hands, or airborne particles. For volatile constituents, samples are collected without headspace and filled to the brim. Depth-specific samples may be taken at stratified layers (epilimnion, metalimnion, hypolimnion) in lakes.

**4. Field measurements** – *In situ* parameters such as temperature, dissolved oxygen, pH, conductivity, and turbidity are measured immediately using calibrated portable meters.

**5. Preservation and transport** – Samples are preserved according to analyte stability (e.g., acidification for metals, ice cooling for nutrients) and transported in coolers. Chain-of-custody forms accompany every batch to document handling.

**6. Laboratory analysis** – Samples are analyzed using standardized methods (e.g., EPA, ISO) for nutrients, metals, organic compounds, pathogens, or toxicity.

**7. Data validation and reporting** – Results are compared to quality control (QC) samples (field blanks, duplicates) and assessed against regulatory standards.

### Variants

- **Grab sampling** – Single discrete sample at a specific time and depth; suitable for stable conditions or targeted analyses.
- **Composite sampling** – Mixture of multiple grab samples over time or space; reduces variance and cost for continuous parameters like suspended solids.
- **Depth-integrated sampling** – Collects water from the entire water column at a single point using specialized samplers; ideal for mean concentrations in rivers.
- **Passive sampling** – Uses diffusion-controlled devices (e.g., polar organic chemical integrative samplers) to accumulate analytes over hours to weeks; provides time-weighted average concentrations.
- **Remote / automated sampling** – Deployed from boats, drones, or fixed stations with programmable pumps; allows high-frequency monitoring with reduced manual effort.
- **Stormwater / event sampling** – Triggered by rainfall or flow thresholds to capture first-flush or extreme conditions; often uses automatic samplers with rain gauges.
- **Biomonitoring** – Collects organisms (e.g., macroinvertebrates, fish) as bioindicators; water itself is not the sole sample, but the approach complements chemical sampling.

### Trade-offs

| Factor | Consideration |
|--------|---------------|
| **Representativeness** | Grab samples capture a snapshot; temporal and spatial variability may be missed without multiple samples or composite strategies. |
| **Cost** | Automated samplers and sophisticated analyses (e.g., trace metals, organics) increase expense; manual grab sampling is cheaper but labor-intensive. |
| **Contamination risk** | Field handling, equipment, and containers can introduce bias; rigorous blanks and clean protocols are essential. |
| **Storage limitations** | Many parameters degrade rapidly (e.g., orthophosphate within hours, volatile organics in days); preservation and prompt analysis are critical. |
| **Regulatory compliance** | Different agencies (e.g., USEPA, state environmental departments) have specific protocols for sampling depth, container type, and holding times; deviation can invalidate data. |
| **Safety** | Sampling in fast-moving rivers, near industrial outfalls, or in remote areas poses physical and chemical hazards. |
