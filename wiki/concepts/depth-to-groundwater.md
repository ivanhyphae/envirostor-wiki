## Definition

**Depth to groundwater** (DTG) is the vertical distance from the ground surface to the water table of an unconfined aquifer, or to the piezometric surface of a confined aquifer. It is typically measured in feet or meters below ground surface (bgs) and represents the depth at which water will first be encountered when excavating or drilling a borehole. This measurement is a critical hydrogeologic parameter for evaluating groundwater availability, assessing contamination risks, and designing foundations, excavations, or dewatering systems.

## How it works

Depth to groundwater is determined through direct measurement in monitoring wells, piezometers, or boreholes. The process involves installing a well casing with a screened interval that intercepts the water table, then measuring the distance from a fixed reference point (typically the top of casing or ground surface) to the water level using an electronic water level meter, steel tape, or pressure transducer. The measurement is corrected for the reference point elevation to obtain the depth below ground surface.

The water table fluctuates seasonally due to precipitation, evapotranspiration, groundwater pumping, and recharge from surface water bodies. DTG is often reported as an average or range over a monitoring period, with seasonal highs typically occurring in late winter/spring and lows in late summer/fall. For example, in the Modesto Stockpiles monitoring program (2013 and 2017 reports), depth to groundwater was measured at multiple monitoring wells (MW-1 through MW-8) to track changes over time and assess potential impacts from nearby operations.

## Variants

- **Static water level**: The water level in a well that is not influenced by pumping or nearby extraction, representing natural conditions.
- **Pumping water level**: The water level during active pumping, which is lower than static due to drawdown.
- **Depth to water table**: Specific to unconfined aquifers where the water table is the upper surface of the saturated zone.
- **Depth to potentiometric surface**: For confined aquifers, the level at which water rises in a well due to artesian pressure, which may be above or below the top of the aquifer.
- **Apparent water table**: The water level measured in a well that may be affected by vertical gradients or perched aquifers, not necessarily representing the regional water table.

## Trade-offs

**Measurement variability**: DTG can vary significantly over short distances due to heterogeneity in aquifer properties, topography, recharge patterns, and pumping influences. Single well measurements may not represent regional conditions.

**Seasonal and temporal changes**: DTG fluctuates with seasonal recharge and drought cycles. A single measurement can be misleading if not contextualized with historical data. For groundwater investigations, multiple measurements over time are required to characterize long-term trends.

**Well construction effects**: Improper well construction (e.g., short screens, seal failures, or wellbore storage) can yield DTG readings that do not reflect actual aquifer conditions. The screened interval must be properly placed to intercept the water table.

**Data reporting conventions**: DTG may be reported relative to ground surface or top of casing, requiring accurate elevation surveys to convert between reference frames. Incorrect reference can introduce errors of several feet.

**Contamination interpretation**: Shallow DTG (e.g., less than 10 feet bgs) increases the vulnerability of groundwater to surface contamination from spills, leaks, or agricultural runoff. Conversely, deeper DTG may indicate lower immediate risk but could mask contamination that has migrated vertically.
