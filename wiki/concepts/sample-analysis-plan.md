## Definition

A **Sample Analysis Plan** (SAP) is a formal document that specifies the procedures, methods, quality control measures, and data handling steps for collecting, preserving, transporting, and analyzing environmental samples. It serves as the operational blueprint for a field sampling event, ensuring that data generated are representative, defensible, and comparable to regulatory or project-specific criteria. SAPs are commonly used in environmental compliance monitoring, site characterization, and remediation projects—such as the surface water sampling of soil stockpiles described in the referenced Modesto Stockpile reports (raw/S1200-01-01 Modesto Stockpiles Jan 2018 SW Sampling_02.18.pdf, raw/S1200-01-01 Modesto Stockpiles March 2018 SW Sampling_04.18.pdf).

## How it works

A typical SAP is developed before field work begins and includes the following key sections:

1. **Project Background & Objectives** – States why sampling is being performed (e.g., regulatory compliance, assessment of contamination) and defines data quality objectives (DQOs).

2. **Sampling Design** – Describes the number, locations (often guided by a site map), and frequency of samples. In the Modesto stockpile examples, surface water samples are collected from stormwater runoff around stockpiles.

3. **Field Procedures** – Details sample collection methods (grab vs. composite), equipment (e.g., dippers, bottles), decontamination protocols, and field measurements (pH, temperature, conductivity).

4. **Sample Handling & Preservation** – Specifies container types, preservatives, holding times, and temperature requirements. For typical surface water analysis, this may include H₂SO₄ or HNO₃ for metals.

5. **Analytical Methods** – Lists the laboratory tests to be performed (e.g., total suspended solids, heavy metals, pH) and cites standard methods (EPA, ASTM, SM).

6. **Quality Assurance/Quality Control (QA/QC)** – Defines field blanks, trip blanks, duplicates, matrix spikes, and laboratory control samples. Acceptance criteria for precision and accuracy are included.

7. **Data Management** – Outlines chain-of-custody procedures, data validation, and reporting formats.

The plan is reviewed and approved by project managers, regulators, and the analytical laboratory before execution.

## Variants

- **SWDP (Storm Water Pollution Prevention Plan) Sampling Plans** – Often required under NPDES permits for industrial sites (e.g., stockpiles). Focus on stormwater runoff.
- **QAPP (Quality Assurance Project Plan)** – A broader document that encompasses the SAP but also includes project management and assessment elements.
- **SAP for Specific Media** – Variants exist for soil, groundwater, surface water, air, or tissue sampling, each with unique handling and analysis requirements.
- **Adaptive SAP** – A flexible plan that allows modifications based on field observations, common in long-term monitoring programs.

## Trade-offs

- **Cost vs. Data Quality** – More rigorous QA/QC increases cost but improves confidence in results; a minimal SAP may lead to unusable data.
- **Standardization vs. Site‑Specificity** – Generic SAPs are easier to implement but may miss unique site conditions (e.g., high clay content in stockpile runoff). Customization improves relevance but requires more development time.
- **Timeliness** – Detailed SAPs take time to prepare and approve; emergency response sampling often uses abbreviated plans that trade off some defensibility.
- **Laboratory Capacity** – The SAP must align with the analytical laboratory’s capabilities and turnaround times. Unrealistic requirements can cause delays or sample exceedances.
