## Definition

**Soil Placement Below 3 Feet Bgs In Containment Zones** refers to the placement of soil material at depths greater than 3 feet below ground surface (bgs) within an engineered containment or exclusion zone, typically adjacent to or beneath structural components associated with a containment system.

In the context of the provided source material, the concept is associated with the **Stockpile 2 wall footing** design and an **email acceptance** of that technical memo. The key issue is whether soil placed below the 3-foot bgs threshold within the containment zone is acceptable from an engineering, geotechnical, or environmental-control standpoint.

## How it works

Containment zones are designed to control the movement of soil, water, and potentially impacted material by using physical barriers, footings, walls, or liners. When soil is placed below 3 feet bgs in these zones, the design must account for several factors:

- **Structural support:** Soil below a wall footing or containment barrier affects bearing capacity and settlement behavior.
- **Hydraulic control:** Deeper soil placement can influence seepage, drainage, and pore-water pressure.
- **Containment integrity:** Material placed within the containment zone must not compromise the barrier’s ability to isolate the stockpile or subsurface area.
- **Construction sequencing:** Deep placement may require excavation, compaction, and verification before the containment structure is completed.

The technical memo on the **Stockpile 2 wall footing** likely addresses how the footing interacts with the underlying soil and whether the placement below 3 feet bgs is acceptable under the proposed design. The email acceptance indicates that the memo’s approach was reviewed and accepted, implying that the placement was considered consistent with the project’s containment and structural requirements.

In practice, approval of below-3-foot placement usually depends on:

1. **Geotechnical suitability** of the native or imported soil.
2. **Compliance with containment design criteria**.
3. **Adequate compaction and grading**.
4. **Protection of the wall footing** against undermining or differential settlement.
5. **No adverse impact on contaminated material isolation**, if applicable.

## Variants

Common variants or related implementations include:

- **Soil placement above 3 feet bgs:** Shallower placement that may be simpler to construct and verify.
- **Structural backfill below footing level:** Engineered fill placed beneath or around a footing to achieve grade and support.
- **Containment-zone fill:** Soil used to build up or recontour an isolated area within an engineered barrier system.
- **Imported clean fill vs. site soil:** Depending on contamination controls, the placed soil may need to be clean, screened, or otherwise approved.
- **Compacted structural fill:** Fill placed in controlled lifts to support walls or containment structures.
- **Alternative barrier systems:** Such as liners, cutoff walls, or other engineered containment approaches that reduce dependence on soil placement depth.

## Trade-offs

Key considerations and limitations include:

- **Settlement risk:** Deeper fill can settle over time if not properly compacted or if underlying soils are weak.
- **Constructability:** Working below 3 feet bgs may require shoring, dewatering, or additional excavation support.
- **Containment performance:** Poorly controlled placement can create preferential pathways for water or contaminant migration.
- **Inspection difficulty:** Subsurface fill is harder to inspect and verify after installation.
- **Cost:** Deeper excavation and engineered placement generally increase cost and schedule.
- **Regulatory or design constraints:** Projects may impose depth limits or approval requirements for material placement within containment zones.
- **Footing interaction:** The wall footing must remain stable and not be undermined by excavation or fill placement practices.
