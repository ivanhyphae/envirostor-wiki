## Definition

**Cleanup Levels** are the numerical target concentrations for contaminants of concern (COCs) that must be achieved at a remediation site to demonstrate that the cleanup is complete and the site is safe for its intended use. In the context of the *Stockpile 2 Confirmation Sampling Tech Memo* (the primary source), cleanup levels represent the specific chemical thresholds used to evaluate whether material in a stockpile has been successfully treated or removed to meet regulatory or project‑specific standards. They serve as benchmarks for confirmation sampling, where collected samples are compared against these levels to verify compliance.

## How it works

Cleanup levels are typically set during the planning phase of a remediation project based on risk assessments, regulatory guidelines, and the planned future land use. For a stockpile remediation, the process often involves the following steps:

1. **Establishment of criteria:** Cleanup levels are derived from applicable federal, state, or local regulations (e.g., residential vs. industrial soil standards). They may also consider background concentrations or site‑specific risk calculations.
2. **Sampling plan design:** A confirmation sampling plan is designed to collect a statistically representative number of samples from the stockpile after treatment or removal. The plan specifies sampling locations, depth intervals, and analytical methods.
3. **Laboratory analysis:** Samples are analyzed for the targeted COCs (e.g., metals, petroleum hydrocarbons, pesticides). Results are compared directly to the cleanup levels.
4. **Decision making:** If all sample results are at or below the cleanup levels, the stockpile is considered remediated. If any sample exceeds a level, additional treatment or removal may be required, followed by re‑sampling.

In the *Stockpile 2 Confirmation Sampling Tech Memo*, cleanup levels are presumably predefined thresholds that the confirmation sampling results must meet to obtain regulatory approval for closure of the stockpile.

## Variants

While the source does not enumerate variants explicitly, cleanup levels in practice vary by:

- **Regulatory jurisdiction:** Different agencies (e.g., EPA, state environmental departments) publish their own standard cleanup levels for common contaminants.
- **Land use:** Residential cleanup levels are generally more stringent than industrial/commercial levels.
- **Media:** Soil cleanup levels differ from groundwater or air cleanup levels.
- **Risk‑based vs. fixed:** Some cleanup levels are fixed numeric standards; others are calculated using site‑specific risk assessment models.
- **Timeframe:** Cleanup levels may be interim (during active remediation) or final (post‑remediation).

The memo’s context (stockpile confirmation) likely applies soil‑specific cleanup levels, possibly for a limited set of COCs.

## Trade-offs

- **Stringency vs. feasibility:** More stringent cleanup levels reduce long‑term risk but may increase remediation cost, time, and technical difficulty.
- **Uncertainty:** Confirmation sampling can only test a finite number of points; cleanup levels might be achieved on average while isolated hotspots remain, posing trade‑offs between sampling density and confidence.
- **Regulatory acceptance:** Using risk‑based cleanup levels can be more flexible but requires extensive documentation and may be contested by regulators or stakeholders.
- **Stockpile heterogeneity:** A stockpile’s variable composition means that a single set of cleanup levels may not capture all contaminant distributions, leading to either over‑ or under‑treatment of portions of the material.

The memo likely addresses trade‑offs in sample number and statistical power needed to demonstrate compliance with the chosen cleanup levels.
