## Definition

A **Surface Water Sampling Network Revision** is a formal update to an established network of surface-water monitoring stations, sampling locations, schedules, or analytical targets. The purpose of a revision is to better match the monitoring network to current data needs, hydrologic conditions, regulatory objectives, or site accessibility constraints.

In practice, a revision may add, remove, relocate, consolidate, or change the sampling frequency of stations on rivers, streams, lakes, reservoirs, or other surface waters. It may also adjust which parameters are collected at each location.

## How it works

A surface water sampling network revision typically follows these steps:

1. **Review of existing network performance**
   - Evaluate whether current stations are still representative of the water body or watershed.
   - Identify redundancy, gaps in spatial coverage, or stations with poor accessibility or low data value.
   - Review data quality, consistency, and trend usefulness.

2. **Identification of drivers for change**
   Common reasons include:
   - new regulatory requirements,
   - updated hydrogeologic or watershed understanding,
   - changes in land use or discharge sources,
   - infrastructure changes,
   - safety or access issues,
   - resource constraints,
   - need to better detect seasonal or event-driven variability.

3. **Proposed network changes**
   Changes may include:
   - adding new monitoring locations upstream/downstream of key features,
   - discontinuing low-value stations,
   - relocating a station to improve representativeness,
   - changing sampling frequency,
   - revising analyte lists or field measurements,
   - modifying quality assurance requirements.

4. **Comparison to historical data**
   Because revisions can affect trend continuity, agencies often compare proposed locations and methods against prior stations to determine whether data can be bridged or whether a new baseline is needed.

5. **Approval and implementation**
   The revised network is documented, approved, and then implemented in field operations, data systems, and reporting workflows.

6. **Ongoing evaluation**
   After implementation, the network is periodically reassessed to confirm that it remains fit for purpose.

Technically, the revision is a **design and governance process**, not just a field change. Its goal is to maintain a monitoring system that is spatially representative, statistically useful, and operationally feasible.

## Variants

Common variants of a surface water sampling network revision include:

- **Station addition**
  - New site added to capture a missing tributary, outfall influence, or upstream/downstream gradient.

- **Station deletion or consolidation**
  - One or more stations removed when coverage is redundant or data value is low.

- **Station relocation**
  - Sampling point moved to a more stable cross-section, safer access point, or better representative reach.

- **Frequency revision**
  - Routine monthly sampling changed to seasonal, quarterly, event-based, or continuous monitoring.

- **Parameter revision**
  - Analytical suite expanded or reduced, such as adding nutrients, metals, or suspended sediment.

- **Network densification**
  - More stations added in a localized area to resolve spatial variability.

- **Network simplification**
  - Fewer stations retained to focus on priority waters or to reduce cost.

- **Program-specific implementation**
  - Revisions may be driven by a Monitoring Program objective, a Water Quality Assessment, or a Regulatory Compliance requirement.

## Trade-offs

Key trade-offs in revising a surface water sampling network include:

- **Continuity vs. improvement**
  - New locations or methods may improve representativeness, but they can interrupt long-term trend records.

- **Coverage vs. cost**
  - More stations improve spatial resolution, but increase labor, laboratory, and logistics costs.

- **Frequency vs. practicality**
  - More frequent sampling better captures short-term variability, but can be difficult to sustain.

- **Representativeness vs. accessibility**
  - The best hydrologic location may be unsafe or difficult to sample consistently.

- **Consistency vs. flexibility**
  - Stable methods aid trend analysis, but revisions may be needed when conditions change.

- **Detection of extremes vs. routine monitoring**
  - Fixed-interval sampling may miss storm-driven pulses, while event-based sampling is harder to standardize.

A well-designed revision balances these factors so the network remains scientifically defensible and operationally manageable.
