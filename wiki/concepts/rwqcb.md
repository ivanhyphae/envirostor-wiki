## Definition

**Rwqcb** is an acronym for **Regional Water Quality Control Board**, a regulatory agency within the California Environmental Protection Agency (CalEPA) responsible for protecting water quality in a designated geographic region. In the context of the Modesto Soil Stockpiles project (project number S9525-06-44), the RWQCB (likely the Central Valley RWQCB) oversees groundwater statistical evaluations, removal action completion reports, and workplan approvals in coordination with the Department of Toxic Substances Control (DTSC). The concept appears in regulatory correspondence, workplan responses, and acceptance letters related to fenceline soil stockpile remediation and bentonite soil treatment.

## How it works

The RWQCB’s involvement in a remediation project follows a structured, multi-step regulatory process:

1. **Workplan Review** – A responsible party submits a fenceline workplan describing how soil stockpiles will be assessed and managed. The RWQCB (often alongside DTSC) reviews the plan for compliance with water quality standards and may request revisions. For the Modesto project, this is documented in *7083_S9525-06-44 Modesto Soil Stockpiles Fenceline Workplan*.

2. **Statistical Evaluation Approval** – The RWQCB evaluates groundwater sampling data and statistical analyses to determine whether contaminant levels meet cleanup goals. An *Approval of GW Statistical Evaluation* letter confirms acceptance of the statistical methods and results, as seen in the source *Approval of GW Statistical Evaluation (900259).pdf*.

3. **Technical Memo Acceptance** – For specific activities such as the "Bent 2 Soils" (bentonite soil treatment), the RWQCB reviews and accepts technical memoranda via e-mail or formal correspondence, as shown in *E-mail Acceptance of Bent 2 Soils Tech Memo.pdf*.

4. **Completion Report Sign-Off** – After removal actions are finished, a Final Removal Action Completion Report is submitted. The RWQCB issues an *Acceptance Letter* (e.g., *Acceptance Letter_Final Removal Action Completion Report_Modesto Soil Stockpiles (900259).pdf*) confirming that the remediation meets regulatory requirements.

5. **Response to Comments** – The RWQCB may provide feedback on workplan comments, requiring the responsible party to respond. The document *7084_S9525-06-44 Response to DTSC Workplan Comments.0912.pdf* illustrates this iterative approval process.

Throughout, the RWQCB ensures that soil and groundwater protection standards are enforced, often cross-referencing water quality objectives under the California Water Code and the Porter-Cologne Water Quality Control Act.

## Variants

There are **nine Regional Water Quality Control Boards** in California, each with jurisdiction over a specific watershed or region:

| Region | Name | Coverage Area |
|--------|------|---------------|
| 1 | North Coast RWQCB | Northwestern CA |
| 2 | San Francisco Bay RWQCB | Bay Area |
| 3 | Central Coast RWQCB | Central coast |
| 4 | Los Angeles RWQCB | Los Angeles & Ventura |
| 5 | Central Valley RWQCB | Sacramento & San Joaquin Valleys (including Modesto) |
| 6 | Lahontan RWQCB | Eastern Sierra, Lake Tahoe |
| 7 | Colorado River Basin RWQCB | Southeastern CA |
| 8 | Santa Ana RWQCB | Orange, Riverside, San Bernardino |
| 9 | San Diego RWQCB | San Diego & Imperial |

The Modesto Soil Stockpiles project falls under Region 5 (Central Valley RWQCB). Each regional board operates similarly but may have specific basin plans and local water quality objectives.

## Trade-offs

- **Regulatory Rigor vs. Project Speed** – RWQCB oversight ensures thorough protection of water resources but can lengthen project timelines through multiple review rounds, comment responses, and formal approvals.
- **Coordination Complexity** – Projects often involve dual oversight by both RWQCB and DTSC (and sometimes local agencies), leading to potential duplication or conflicting requirements. The Modesto documents show separate approval streams for groundwater statistics (RWQCB) and removal action completion (DTSC).
- **Cost** – Compliance with RWQCB conditions (e.g., extra sampling, statistical analyses, technical memoranda) adds direct costs and delays, which can be significant for large soil stockpile operations.
- **Public Trust** – Independent regulatory review by the RWQCB bolsters community confidence in remediation outcomes, but the bureaucratic process can frustrate property owners and developers seeking rapid site closure.
