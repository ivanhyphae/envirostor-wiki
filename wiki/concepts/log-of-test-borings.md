## Definition

A **Log of Test Borings** (LOTB) is a structured geological record that documents the subsurface conditions encountered during a test boring (or borehole) investigation. It systematically describes the types, depths, thicknesses, and characteristics of soil, rock, and groundwater strata encountered, along with field test results, sampling data, and drilling observations. LOTBs are fundamental deliverables of geotechnical site investigations, used for foundation design, slope stability analysis, and environmental assessments.

## How it works

A test boring is performed using a drill rig (e.g., hollow‑stem auger, rotary wash, or sonic drilling). As drilling progresses, a field engineer or geologist logs each change in subsurface material at the depth it is encountered. The log typically records:

- **Boring identification** – location, coordinates, ground elevation, date, drilling method.
- **Depth scale** – vertical scale (e.g., 1 inch = 5 feet) showing depths from ground surface.
- **Material description** – Unified Soil Classification System (USCS) symbols, colour, moisture condition, consistency/density, plasticity, and secondary constituents (gravel, cobbles, organics).
- **Stratigraphy** – boundaries between distinct soil or rock layers, often shown as solid or dashed lines.
- **Field tests** – Standard Penetration Test (SPT) N‑values, pocket penetrometer readings, vane shear tests, etc.
- **Groundwater** – depth at which water is first encountered, and any subsequent piezometric measurements.
- **Sampling information** – type of sample (disturbed, undisturbed), sample number, recovery length, and core loss.
- **Remarks** – drilling difficulties, caving, artesian conditions, odours, or visual observations.

The log is drawn on a standardized form (often A‑size) with columns for depth, USCS symbol, material description, sample type, field test data, and remarks. Computer‑aided drafting (CAD) or specialized geotechnical software (gINT, HoleBASE) is commonly used to produce neat, reproducible logs.

## Variants

Several variations of test boring logs exist depending on the investigation purpose and local practice:

- **Soil Boring Log** – focused on soil layers, SPT N‑values, and moisture content. Most common for shallow foundations and earthworks.
- **Rock Core Log** – detailed description of rock core (RQD, fracture frequency, weathering grade, hardness) retrieved from rotary coring.
- **Environmental Boring Log** – adds field screening (PID readings, olfactory notes) for contamination assessment; often includes lithologic logs for monitoring well installations.
- **Cone Penetration Test (CPT) Log** – continuous profiles of cone tip resistance and sleeve friction, plotted as depth vs. measured parameters. Not a traditional boring log but serves a similar purpose.
- **Test Pit Log** – used when excavations are dug by backhoe; records vertical soil profile exposed in the pit walls.

## Trade-offs

- **Detail vs. practicality** – Very detailed logs provide maximum subsurface information but slow down field operations and increase cost. Simplified logs may miss key stratigraphic changes.
- **Subjectivity** – Material descriptions rely on the logger’s experience; inconsistencies can arise between different loggers. Standardized USCS and ASTM procedures reduce but do not eliminate variation.
- **Vertical resolution** – Sampling intervals (typically every 1.5–3 m) may miss thin critical layers (e.g., sand seams in clay). Continuous sampling (e.g., Shelby tubes, sonic core) improves resolution but is more expensive.
- **Disturbance** – SPT and split‑spoon sampling can remould fine‑grained soils, affecting subsequent laboratory test results. Undisturbed samples are required for accurate strength parameters but are costly and slower to obtain.
- **Groundwater readings** – Water levels measured during drilling may not represent long‑term static levels due to drilling fluid, artesian conditions, or delayed recovery. Standpipe piezometers are needed for accurate readings.
- **Interpretation required** – A log alone is not a substitute for a geotechnical report; the data must be interpreted by a qualified geotechnical engineer to derive engineering properties.
