## Manganese

### Definition
Manganese (Mn) is a chemical element with atomic number 25. It is a hard, brittle, silvery-grey transition metal that occurs naturally in many minerals and is essential to both biological life and industrial processes. Manganese is the twelfth most abundant element in Earth’s crust, typically found in combination with iron, oxygen, or sulfur.

### How it works
Manganese exists in multiple oxidation states (from −3 to +7), with +2, +4, and +7 being the most common in nature. This redox versatility underpins its roles:

- **Geochemical cycling**: Manganese is mobilized in reducing environments (e.g., groundwater) as soluble Mn²⁺ and precipitated as MnO₂ or Mn(III) oxyhydroxides under oxidizing conditions. This cycle influences the transport of trace metals and organic matter.
- **Industrial catalyst**: Manganese dioxide (MnO₂) acts as a catalyst in the production of oxygen and chlorine, and as a depolarizer in dry-cell batteries. Manganese oxide is also used in steelmaking to remove sulfur and oxygen.
- **Biological role**: Manganese is a cofactor for several enzymes, including superoxide dismutase (Mn‑SOD) which protects cells from oxidative damage, and arginase involved in the urea cycle. It is an essential trace element for humans, but excessive exposure can lead to neurotoxicity.
- **Stainless steel production**: Manganese improves strength, workability, and corrosion resistance by deoxidizing and desulfurizing molten steel. Most manganese (approx. 90%) is consumed by the steel industry.

### Variants
- **Oxidation states**: Mn²⁺ (most stable in aqueous solution), Mn³⁺ (unstable, disproportionates), Mn⁴⁺ (as MnO₂, a common ore), Mn⁷⁺ (as permanganate, a strong oxidizer like KMnO₄).
- **Minerals and ores**: Pyrolusite (MnO₂), rhodochrosite (MnCO₃), braunite (Mn²⁺Mn³⁺₆SiO₁₂), and manganite (MnO(OH)).
- **Alloys**: Ferromanganese (high‑carbon, medium‑carbon, low‑carbon) and silicomanganese are the primary industrial forms used in steelmaking.
- **Organometallic compounds**: Manganese methylcyclopentadienyl tricarbonyl (MMT) is an antiknock additive in some gasoline formulations (phased out in many regions due to toxicity).

### Trade-offs
- **Essential vs. toxic**: Manganese deficiency is rare but can impair growth and metabolism; chronic inhalation of manganese dust or fumes (e.g., in mining or welding) causes manganism, a Parkinson’s‑like neurological disease.
- **Environmental mobility**: Soluble Mn²⁺ can contaminate groundwater under acidic or reducing conditions; oxidation to particulate MnO₂ can sequester other metals but also clog drainage.
- **Iron‑manganese interplay**: Often co‑occurs with iron; manganese is harder to remove than iron from water because its oxidation kinetics are slower and require a higher pH or catalyst.
- **Economic considerations**: Manganese ore grades vary; low‑grade ores need beneficiation. Demand is closely tied to the steel industry, with recycling of steel providing a secondary source of manganese.
