## Definition

California Maximum Contaminant Levels (MCLs) are legally enforceable numeric standards that specify the highest allowable concentration of a contaminant in water delivered to any user of a public water system in California. Established under the California Safe Drinking Water Act (SDWA), these standards are set by the State Water Resources Control Board (SWRCB) and often exceed the stringency of federal MCLs established by the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA). MCLs apply to all regulated drinking water contaminants—including chemical, microbiological, radiological, and physical agents—and serve as the principal regulatory tool for protecting public health from waterborne hazards.

## How it works

MCLs are derived through a multi‑step process that balances health risk reduction with technical and economic feasibility:

1. **Health‑based goal setting:** The Office of Environmental Health Hazard Assessment (OEHHA) calculates a Public Health Goal (PHG) for each contaminant—the concentration that poses no significant health risk over a lifetime of exposure, assuming a margin of safety. PHGs are non‑enforceable advisory levels.

2. **Feasibility assessment:** The SWRCB evaluates analytical detection limits, available treatment technologies, and the costs of achieving various concentrations. An MCL must be set as close to the PHG as is “technologically and economically feasible,” considering the contaminant’s occurrence in California water supplies.

3. **Rulemaking process:** Proposed MCLs undergo public comment, peer review, and adoption as regulations (Title 22, California Code of Regulations). The final MCL is enforceable 60 days after publication.

4. **Compliance and monitoring:** Public water systems must monitor for each regulated contaminant at prescribed frequencies. Any result exceeding the MCL triggers a violation, requiring public notification and corrective actions (e.g., treatment upgrades, source blending, or replacing the supply). System operators report MCL exceedances to the SWRCB and local health agencies.

MCLs apply only to community water systems and non‑transient non‑community water systems (e.g., schools with their own wells). Private domestic wells are not directly regulated under the SDWA, though county health departments may set local limits.

## Variants

California’s drinking water standards include several categories that operate alongside MCLs:

- **Primary MCLs:** Health‑based, enforceable limits for contaminants that pose acute or chronic health risks (e.g., arsenic, nitrate, lead, coliform bacteria). Exceeding a primary MCL is a direct violation that must be remedied.

- **Secondary MCLs:** Enforceable limits for aesthetic or organoleptic properties (taste, odor, color). While not directly health‑based, secondary MCLs improve consumer acceptance and identify potential corrosion or distribution system issues.

- **Maximum Contaminant Level Goals (MCLGs):** Non‑enforceable health‑based concentrations identical to PHGs for California‑specific contaminants. Federal MCLGs are analogous but may differ.

- **Notification Levels:** Non‑enforceable thresholds for unregulated contaminants that, when exceeded, require the water system to report to the SWRCB. Notification levels often precede formal MCL adoption when new health data emerge.

- **Response Levels:** Immediate action levels for short‑term exposure to unregulated contaminants (e.g., perfluoroalkyl substances). These are not MCLs but trigger rapid protective measures.

## Trade-offs

Setting MCLs involves inherent tension between public health protection and practical constraints:

- **Health vs. cost:** Lower MCLs reduce long‑term health risks but increase compliance expenditures for treatment, monitoring, and infrastructure. Small water systems may struggle to afford advanced filtration, leading to rate increases or consolidation.

- **Detection capability:** Very low MCLs can approach the practical quantitation limit of analytical methods, raising false‑positive rates and controversy over what constitutes a “detectable” concentration.

- **Feasibility gap:** Some contaminants (e.g., hexavalent chromium, perchlorate) have PHGs far below achievable detection levels. California has adopted or adjusted MCLs for these based on treatment feasibility and economic impacts, sometimes provoking litigation.

- **Federal‑state harmonization:** California can set MCLs stricter than federal limits, but this creates inconsistent requirements for systems operating across state lines. The federal Safe Drinking Water Act prohibits states from adopting less stringent MCLs than EPA’s national primary standards.

- **Emerging contaminants:** The rulemaking process is lengthy (often 5–10 years per contaminant). Notification levels provide interim guidance but lack enforcement teeth, leaving some communities exposed while standards develop.

- **Private well gap:** Over 1 million Californians rely on private wells not subject to MCLs. Voluntary testing and local ordinances are inconsistent, leading to disparities in water quality among neighboring properties.
