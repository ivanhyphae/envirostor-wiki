## Definition

The **Clean Fill Sampling Plan For Basin 5** is a project-specific environmental sampling and verification plan prepared for the SR 132 Basin 5 area to confirm that proposed or imported fill material qualifies as **clean fill** before placement. In practice, the plan establishes how fill will be screened, sampled, analyzed, documented, and accepted for use at Basin 5 so that the material does not introduce unacceptable contamination to the site.

This type of plan is typically used during earthwork or grading activities where excavated or imported soils must meet regulatory or project criteria for reuse or disposal. The Basin 5 plan is a formal control document rather than a general policy; it applies to the Basin 5 work area and its associated fill acceptance process.

## How it works

A clean fill sampling plan generally works as a **quality assurance / environmental compliance workflow**:

1. **Define the fill source or acceptance area**
   - The plan identifies the material stream to be evaluated, such as imported borrow, reused site soil, or stockpiled fill intended for Basin 5 placement.
   - It also defines the receiving area and the conditions under which material may be accepted.

2. **Establish acceptance criteria**
   - The plan sets the project’s clean fill standard, usually requiring material to be free of contamination and to meet applicable environmental and engineering requirements.
   - Criteria often address visible contamination, debris, petroleum impacts, industrial waste, asbestos-containing material, and other prohibited constituents.

3. **Field screening and inspection**
   - Incoming or proposed fill is visually inspected for staining, odors, debris, trash, construction waste, and other indicators of contamination.
   - If the plan requires it, field screening methods may be used to supplement visual review.

4. **Sampling design**
   - The plan specifies how many samples to collect, where to collect them, and at what frequency.
   - Sampling may be based on:
     - volume of material,
     - number of truckloads,
     - source area size,
     - or observed heterogeneity.
   - The goal is to obtain a representative characterization of the material.

5. **Laboratory analysis**
   - Collected samples are submitted to a laboratory for analysis of contaminants relevant to the project’s clean fill criteria.
   - The analyte list is usually chosen to reflect likely contamination risks for the source material and site setting.

6. **Evaluation against criteria**
   - Analytical results are compared with the plan’s acceptance thresholds.
   - Material that meets the criteria may be approved for Basin 5 use.
   - Material that fails may be rejected, segregated, resampled, or managed under a different disposal or reuse path.

7. **Documentation and approval**
   - The plan typically requires records of sample locations, dates, chain of custody, analytical reports, and acceptance determinations.
   - Approval may be documented by project environmental staff or the responsible agency representative before material is placed.

8. **Construction controls**
   - Accepted fill is tracked during placement to maintain traceability.
   - Nonconforming material is kept out of the Basin 5 work area or isolated for further evaluation.

The approval e-mail source indicates that the sampling plan was formally reviewed and approved, which suggests the plan was intended to be implemented as an authorized project procedure rather than an informal field practice.

## Variants

Possible variants of a clean fill sampling plan include:

- **Source-characterization plan**
  - Focuses on confirming whether a candidate borrow source is suitable before any transport begins.

- **Load-by-load screening plan**
  - Relies primarily on visual inspection and documentation for each truckload, with sampling used as confirmation or in response to concerns.

- **Stockpile sampling plan**
  - Samples material after it has been stockpiled, often used when material is excavated and temporarily stored before reuse.

- **Incremental composite sampling**
  - Combines multiple increments into composite samples to characterize larger volumes more efficiently.

- **Discrete sampling plan**
  - Uses individual samples from specific locations or depths to detect localized contamination that a composite might mask.

- **Regulatory-driven vs. project-driven clean fill criteria**
  - Some plans are built around state or local definitions of clean fill.
  - Others add project-specific restrictions based on site sensitivity, stormwater requirements, or agency commitments.

For Basin 5, the exact variant depends on how the source material was obtained and what environmental concerns were considered in the approved plan.

## Trade-offs

Key trade-offs and considerations in a clean fill sampling plan include:

- **Representativeness vs. cost**
  - More samples provide better confidence that the fill is clean, but increase time and laboratory expense.

- **Composite vs. discrete sampling**
  - Composites are efficient and reduce analytical burden.
  - Discrete samples better detect localized contamination but are more resource-intensive.

- **Speed vs. certainty**
  - Visual screening and documentation can move construction quickly.
  - Laboratory confirmation provides higher certainty but can delay material acceptance.

- **Source control vs. receiving control**
  - Tight control at the source reduces risk before transport.
  - Tight control at the receiving site protects Basin 5 but may require more field oversight.

- **Flexibility vs. compliance**
  - Flexible acceptance procedures help construction progress.
  - Strict criteria reduce environmental risk and support defensible compliance records.

- **Site-specific assumptions**
  - The plan is only as reliable as the assumptions about source history, contamination potential, and intended use of the fill.
  - If those assumptions change, the sampling plan may need revision.

Practical limitations often include heterogeneous soil conditions, inaccessible sampling locations, weather impacts, and the possibility that contamination is present but not captured by the selected sampling density.
