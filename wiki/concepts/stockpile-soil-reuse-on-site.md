## Definition

**Stockpile soil reuse on site** is the practice of excavating soil, temporarily storing it in designated stockpiles, and later reusing that same soil elsewhere within the project footprint rather than exporting it off site and importing new fill. It is commonly used when excavated native soil is suitable, with or without processing, for backfill, grading, wall construction support, or other approved construction uses.

In regulated projects, on-site reuse is typically controlled by an approved workplan, sampling plan, or variance/approval letter that establishes where the soil came from, how it was characterized, where it may be used, and what construction controls apply.

## How it works

The process generally follows these steps:

1. **Excavate and segregate material**
   - Soil is removed from a defined source area and separated from other materials.
   - Different stockpiles may be created for different source zones or handling histories.

2. **Characterize the stockpiled soil**
   - The stockpile is sampled and analyzed to confirm it meets the reuse criteria for the intended location.
   - Sampling may be performed for stockpiles intended for use in MSE wall backfill, wall footing areas, spoils reuse, or other specific civil works.

3. **Obtain approval for the reuse**
   - Reuse is often contingent on acceptance by the project engineer, environmental consultant, or regulator.
   - The provided sources indicate formal approvals and acceptances via e-mail and letters for reuse-related technical memoranda and workplans, including stockpiles associated with wall footing sampling, MSE wall sampling, spoils workplans, and a variance request.

4. **Reuse the soil in approved locations**
   - The soil is placed where the approval allows it, such as within structural fill, wall backfill, or grading fill.
   - Placement usually requires construction quality control, including moisture conditioning, compaction, lift thickness control, and confirmation that no disallowed material is introduced.

5. **Document closure**
   - Records typically show where the stockpile originated, sampling results, approval status, and final placement location.
   - This documentation is important for environmental compliance and as-built construction records.

In practice, on-site reuse helps balance cut-and-fill quantities and reduce hauling. However, the reuse decision is not simply based on volume; it depends on engineering suitability, contamination status, and whether the receiving area can accept the material under the governing approval.

## Variants

Common variants and implementations include:

- **Unrestricted clean-soil reuse**
  - Soil that has been characterized as suitable for general reuse with minimal restrictions.

- **Controlled reuse under a technical memo or workplan**
  - Soil is reused only in specified construction elements after sampling and written acceptance.
  - The sources suggest this approach for stockpiles linked to wall footing and MSE wall construction.

- **Spoils reuse**
  - Excavated spoils are reused on site after evaluation, rather than being disposed of.
  - This is often used when the spoil material is native soil and meets project criteria.

- **Variance-based reuse**
  - A formal variance request is used when the proposed reuse differs from the baseline plan or needs special approval.
  - The source set includes an approval e-mail for a variance request, indicating this pathway is used when standard procedures are not sufficient.

- **Engineered fill reuse**
  - Soil is reused as engineered structural fill after meeting gradation, moisture, and compaction requirements.
  - This is common in retaining wall construction and site grading.

## Trade-offs

Key considerations include:

- **Cost savings vs. control burden**
  - Reuse can reduce export/import trucking, disposal fees, and borrow material costs.
  - It also adds sampling, documentation, and approval overhead.

- **Environmental benefit vs. regulatory complexity**
  - On-site reuse reduces material hauling and associated emissions.
  - But it can require more stringent characterization and compliance tracking.

- **Schedule efficiency vs. risk of delays**
  - If stockpiles are approved for reuse, construction can proceed with available material.
  - If sampling or approval is incomplete, stockpiles may sit idle and delay follow-on work.

- **Material suitability**
  - Not all excavated soil is appropriate for reuse.
  - Constraints may include moisture content, oversized particles, debris, organics, or contamination concerns.

- **Project footprint constraints**
  - Stockpiles require space, access control, and protection from erosion, runoff, and cross-contamination.
  - Limited site space can make temporary storage difficult.

- **Traceability**
  - Once soil is mixed or moved without controls, it becomes harder to prove provenance and compliance.
  - Segregation and labeling are therefore important.
