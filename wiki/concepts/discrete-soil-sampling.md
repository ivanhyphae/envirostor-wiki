## Discrete Soil Sampling

### Definition
Discrete soil sampling is a method of collecting individual soil samples from specific locations and depths within a site, where each sample is analyzed separately to characterize the soil properties or contaminant distribution at that exact point. Unlike composite sampling, which combines multiple subsamples into a single analysis, discrete sampling preserves the spatial variability of soil characteristics.

### How it works
1. **Sampling grid design**: A systematic or judgmental grid is established over the area of interest. Sample locations are selected based on regulatory requirements, statistical design, or site history.
2. **Sample collection**: At each designated point, a discrete sample is collected from a specific depth interval (e.g., 0–6 inches, 6–12 inches) using hand tools (trowel, auger) or mechanical equipment (direct push, split-spoon sampler).
3. **Field handling**: Each sample is placed in its own container, labeled with a unique ID, and stored according to preservation requirements (e.g., cool on ice for volatile analyses).
4. **Laboratory analysis**: Each discrete sample is analyzed independently, resulting in a concentration value for that specific location and depth.
5. **Data interpretation**: Results are mapped to show spatial patterns, identify hot spots, or calculate area-weighted averages.

### Variants
- **Grab sample**: A single discrete sample taken at one point and time; often used for screening or compliance.
- **Split-spoon sample**: A discrete sample collected from a specific interval during drilling, retaining original stratification.
- **Depth-discrete sample**: Samples taken from multiple discrete depths within the same borehole, each analyzed separately.
- **Field duplicate**: A second discrete sample collected at the same location to assess sampling precision.
- **Co-located sample**: Two or more discrete samples collected in close proximity (e.g., 2 feet apart) for statistical evaluation.

### Trade-offs
| Advantage | Disadvantage |
|-----------|--------------|
| Preserves spatial variability; identifies hot spots and small-scale heterogeneity | Higher analytical costs per location compared to composite sampling |
| Provides defensible data for risk assessment and remedial decisions | More labor-intensive field effort (more samples, labeling, shipping) |
| Allows calculation of exceedance frequency and statistical confidence | May require more total sample volume for some analyses |
| Can be combined with compositing for certain objectives | Data interpretation requires geostatistical analysis if grid is irregular |

**Key considerations**:
- Regulatory driver: Many cleanup standards require discrete samples to demonstrate compliance at each point.
- Media heterogeneity: Discrete sampling is preferred when contamination is expected to be unevenly distributed (e.g., NAPL, debris-filled soil).
- Cost: Budget often limits the number of discrete samples; compositing may be used for low-risk areas.
