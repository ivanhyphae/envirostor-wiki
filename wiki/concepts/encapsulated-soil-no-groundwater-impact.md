## Definition

**Encapsulated Soil No Groundwater Impact** is a groundwater risk classification used for materials or waste managed in a way that prevents contact with surrounding soil and eliminates or materially reduces the potential for leachate to migrate into groundwater. In practice, it describes a contained, engineered placement where the material is isolated from the subsurface and has been evaluated to show **no measurable adverse impact on groundwater quality** under the approved assessment conditions.

This concept is typically applied in environmental compliance, waste management, and site remediation contexts where a statistical or technical evaluation demonstrates that the encapsulated material does not cause groundwater exceedances or detectably change groundwater conditions.

## How it works

The concept relies on two linked controls:

1. **Physical encapsulation**
   - The material is placed within an engineered barrier system, such as a liner, cap, vault, cell, container, or other containment structure.
   - The containment prevents direct contact between the material and surrounding soil and limits infiltration of water.
   - The design is intended to isolate the material from pathways that would otherwise allow leaching to groundwater.

2. **Groundwater impact evaluation**
   - Monitoring data and statistical analysis are used to determine whether the containment is effective.
   - The evaluation typically compares groundwater quality data upgradient and downgradient, or against background and compliance criteria.
   - A “no groundwater impact” conclusion means the data do not show a statistically significant or regulatory-relevant increase attributable to the encapsulated material.

In an approval context, this usually means the site or disposal action has been reviewed and accepted on the basis that:
- the material remains contained,
- infiltration and migration pathways are controlled,
- groundwater monitoring does not indicate adverse impact,
- and the statistical evaluation supports the conclusion.

The technical basis may include:
- hydrogeologic characterization,
- contaminant fate and transport considerations,
- groundwater monitoring well data,
- background correction,
- statistical tests for compliance,
- and engineering design review of the containment system.

## Variants

Common variants or closely related implementations include:

- **Encapsulated waste cell**
  - Waste is placed in a lined or otherwise isolated cell, often with a cap and leachate control system.

- **Contained soil stockpile**
  - Soil is stored in an engineered enclosure or on an impermeable pad with runoff and infiltration controls.

- **Solidified/stabilized encapsulation**
  - Contaminated soil is mixed with binding agents and then placed in a containment structure to reduce leaching.

- **Vault or containerized disposal**
  - Material is placed in sealed containers, drums, or concrete vaults for long-term isolation.

- **Capped in-place management**
  - Contaminated material remains on site but is covered and isolated to prevent exposure and infiltration.

- **No-impact demonstrated by monitoring**
  - A regulatory or statistical demonstration showing the contained material has not affected groundwater, even if the exact engineering configuration varies.

Alternatives include:
- excavation and off-site disposal,
- treatment to reduce contaminant mobility,
- monitored natural attenuation,
- or active pump-and-treat remediation when groundwater impact is present.

## Trade-offs

Key trade-offs and considerations include:

- **Long-term maintenance**
  - Encapsulation depends on the integrity of barriers over time. Caps, liners, and drains may fail if not maintained.

- **Monitoring burden**
  - “No groundwater impact” must usually be demonstrated and periodically revalidated through groundwater monitoring and statistical review.

- **Residual risk**
  - Even well-designed containment may not eliminate risk entirely; it mainly reduces exposure and migration potential.

- **Site-specific performance**
  - Effectiveness depends on soil type, hydrogeology, depth to groundwater, rainfall, contaminant properties, and engineering quality.

- **Regulatory acceptance**
  - Approval often requires robust documentation and may be contingent on institutional controls, inspections, and contingency actions.

- **Cost vs. permanence**
  - Encapsulation can be less expensive than excavation or treatment, but it may impose long-term stewardship obligations.

- **Uncertainty in statistics**
  - A “no impact” conclusion is based on available data and statistical thresholds; it does not prove absolute absence of any contaminant movement.
