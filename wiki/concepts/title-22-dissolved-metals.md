## Definition

**Title 22 dissolved metals** are dissolved-phase metal concentrations measured in water samples and evaluated against California’s **Title 22 drinking-water standards** or related regulatory benchmarks. In the context of the provided Caltrans Modesto Stockpiles groundwater and stormwater monitoring reports, the term refers to laboratory results for metals analyzed on filtered samples and compared to state drinking-water criteria or project-specific screening thresholds.

In practical use, “dissolved metals” indicates the fraction of metals that passes through a filter before analysis, representing the dissolved/aqueous portion rather than the total metal content in the water column. These results are commonly used to assess whether groundwater or stormwater may pose a concern for drinking-water quality, receptor protection, or compliance evaluation.

## How it works

Dissolved metals monitoring typically follows this workflow:

1. **Sample collection**
   - Groundwater or stormwater is collected from monitoring points such as wells, piezometers, or runoff sampling locations.
   - Field protocols are used to minimize contamination and preserve sample integrity.

2. **Filtration**
   - The sample is filtered, usually through a 0.45 µm filter or equivalent, to separate the dissolved fraction.
   - This is the key distinction between dissolved metals and total metals.

3. **Laboratory analysis**
   - The filtered sample is analyzed for target metals using standard instrumental methods.
   - Common analytes in environmental monitoring include aluminum, antimony, arsenic, barium, cadmium, chromium, copper, iron, lead, manganese, mercury, nickel, selenium, silver, thallium, and zinc, depending on the project and permit requirements.

4. **Comparison to standards**
   - Results are compared with **Title 22** drinking-water limits, Maximum Contaminant Levels (MCLs), or other applicable criteria.
   - If a result is below the reporting limit, it is generally reported as non-detect or below detection/quantitation threshold.
   - If a result exceeds a criterion, it may trigger further evaluation, reporting, or corrective action.

5. **Interpretation in context**
   - Dissolved metals are interpreted alongside field parameters such as pH, turbidity, conductivity, oxidation-reduction potential, and groundwater elevation.
   - Geochemical conditions strongly influence whether metals remain dissolved, precipitate, or adsorb to sediments.

In the Modesto Stockpiles reports, dissolved-metals data appear as part of recurring groundwater and stormwater sampling campaigns spanning 2012–2016, suggesting a monitoring program designed to evaluate whether stockpile-related runoff or seepage affected water quality.

## Variants

Common variants and related implementations include:

- **Dissolved metals in groundwater**
  - Used to assess plume behavior, attenuation, and compliance near storage or construction sites.
  - Often paired with field and laboratory results from monitoring wells.

- **Dissolved metals in stormwater**
  - Used to characterize runoff from exposed soils, stockpiles, paved surfaces, and drainage controls.
  - Important for understanding mobilization during rain events.

- **Total recoverable metals**
  - Measures metals after acid digestion of an unfiltered sample.
  - Includes both dissolved and particulate-associated metals.
  - Not the same as dissolved metals and may yield higher values.

- **Filtered metals by regulatory program**
  - Some permits or guidance documents specify filtered samples when the dissolved fraction is the metric of interest.
  - Others require both dissolved and total analyses for comparison.

- **Title 22-based screening**
  - A project may compare dissolved metals results against drinking-water standards, even if the water is not used for potable supply, as a conservative benchmark.

## Trade-offs

Key limitations and considerations include:

- **Dissolved does not mean harmless**
  - Dissolved metals are more mobile and bioavailable than particulate-bound metals, but toxicity depends on species, concentration, and exposure route.

- **Filtration can affect results**
  - Small particulates, colloids, or precipitates may pass through or be retained depending on field conditions and filter handling.
  - This can make dissolved results sensitive to sampling technique.

- **Standards may not match site conditions**
  - Title 22 drinking-water standards are protective benchmarks, but they may not be directly tailored to stormwater, industrial runoff, or non-potable groundwater settings.

- **Geochemistry matters**
  - pH, redox state, salinity, and hardness can strongly influence dissolved-metal concentrations.
  - A low dissolved-metal result in one event does not guarantee stability over time.

- **Detection limits and qualifiers**
  - Laboratory reporting limits, method detection limits, and data qualifiers can complicate trend analysis.
  - Non-detects should be interpreted with the associated detection threshold.

- **Total vs dissolved comparisons**
  - Dissolved-metal results are not directly comparable to unfiltered total metals results without accounting for methodology differences.
