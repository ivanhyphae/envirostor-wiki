## Definition

**Organochlorine Pesticide and Metals Testing** is an analytical environmental testing process used to detect and quantify:

- **Organochlorine pesticides**: a class of persistent chlorinated organic compounds historically used as insecticides, such as DDT and related breakdown products.
- **Metals**: inorganic elements of concern in environmental media, often including both naturally occurring and anthropogenic contaminants.

In environmental investigation and remediation contexts, this testing is commonly performed on **soil, sediment, and sometimes water** to determine whether contaminants are present at concentrations that may pose ecological or human-health risks.

## How it works

This type of testing typically combines two different analytical workflows because organochlorine pesticides and metals require different chemistry and instrumentation.

### 1. Sample collection and handling
Samples are collected from the area of interest using approved field methods and placed in appropriate containers for laboratory analysis. Proper chain-of-custody, preservation, and holding-time requirements are important to ensure data validity.

### 2. Organochlorine pesticide analysis
Organochlorine pesticides are generally extracted from the environmental matrix using organic solvents. The extract is then cleaned up to reduce interference and analyzed by gas chromatography-based methods, often coupled with an electron capture detector or mass spectrometry.

Common analytical features:
- Target compounds are highly chlorinated and semi-volatile.
- Detection is sensitive to trace concentrations.
- Breakdown products may be measured alongside parent pesticides, depending on the method.

### 3. Metals analysis
Metals are typically measured after digestion of the solid sample in strong acids to bring the elements into solution. The digestate is then analyzed by instrumental methods such as:
- Inductively coupled plasma optical emission spectrometry (ICP-OES)
- Inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS)
- Atomic absorption methods

This provides concentrations for individual metals in the sample.

### 4. Data interpretation
Results are compared with:
- Project-specific cleanup levels
- Screening thresholds
- Regulatory criteria
- Background concentrations, where applicable

The combination of pesticide and metals testing helps characterize whether contamination is limited to organic residues, inorganic constituents, or both.

## Variants

Common variants of organochlorine pesticide and metals testing include:

- **Soil testing**: used for contaminated sites, borrow areas, construction areas, and remediation verification.
- **Sediment testing**: used in aquatic or wetland settings.
- **Groundwater or surface water testing**: less common for organochlorines due to their low solubility, but may be used in site assessments.
- **Targeted compound lists**: projects may analyze a standard pesticide suite or a site-specific list.
- **Total metals vs. dissolved metals**: depends on whether the sample is solid media or water.
- **Screening vs. confirmatory analysis**: initial testing may be broader, with follow-up confirmatory sampling if elevated results are found.

Alternatives or complementary approaches include:
- General contaminant characterization
- Pesticide analysis
- Metals analysis
- Broader environmental site assessment

## Trade-offs

Key considerations and limitations include:

- **Different analytical methods are required**: organochlorines and metals cannot usually be assessed with a single instrument or preparation step.
- **Sample heterogeneity**: contaminant concentrations can vary significantly across a site, especially in fill, borrow, or disturbed areas.
- **Detection limits matter**: results near regulatory thresholds may require more sensitive methods or re-sampling.
- **Matrix interference**: soil type, organic content, moisture, and other matrix effects can complicate extraction or digestion.
- **Persistence and legacy contamination**: organochlorines may remain in the environment long after use, so detections often reflect historical application rather than current activity.
- **Interpretation depends on context**: elevated metals may be naturally occurring, imported fill-related, or industrial in origin.
- **Cost and turnaround time**: adding both pesticide and metals packages increases analytical scope and expense.
