## Definition

The National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) is a foundational U.S. environmental law enacted in 1970 that requires federal agencies to assess the environmental effects of proposed actions before making decisions. NEPA establishes a framework for integrating environmental considerations into federal planning and decision-making, mandating that agencies prepare detailed analyses of the potential impacts of major federal actions significantly affecting the quality of the human environment. The Act applies to projects that involve federal funding, permits, approvals, or are carried out by federal agencies. An example of a NEPA document is the *Approval letter for SR 132 RAP* (March 14, 2018), which authorized a Restoration Action Plan (RAP) after completing the NEPA review process.

## How it works

NEPA’s procedural requirements are triggered whenever a federal agency proposes an action (e.g., building a highway, issuing a permit, funding a project). The process unfolds in three tiers:

1. **Categorical Exclusion (CE)** — If the action is one of a list of activities that normally have no significant individual or cumulative effect on the environment (e.g., routine maintenance, minor repairs), the agency may categorically exclude it from further NEPA review. No detailed document is required.

2. **Environmental Assessment (EA)** — If the action does not qualify for a CE, the agency prepares a concise EA to determine whether the action will have significant environmental effects. The EA includes:
   - Description of the proposed action and alternatives
   - Analysis of environmental impacts
   - Public involvement (varies by agency)
   - A finding: either a **Finding of No Significant Impact (FONSI)** or a need for an Environmental Impact Statement.

3. **Environmental Impact Statement (EIS)** — If the EA finds significant impacts, a full EIS is required. The EIS process involves:
   - **Scoping** – Public and agency input defining the range of issues
   - **Draft EIS** – Detailed analysis of impacts, alternatives, and mitigation measures, released for public comment
   - **Final EIS** – Responds to comments and presents the agency’s preferred alternative
   - **Record of Decision (ROD)** – The agency’s final decision, including mitigation commitments.

The *Approval letter for SR 132 RAP* represents the conclusion of a NEPA review that ultimately required a RAP to mitigate unavoidable environmental impacts. The letter likely reflects the agency’s decision (e.g., ROD or FONSI with a mitigation plan) after completing the appropriate NEPA documentation.

## Variants

NEPA itself is a single statute, but its implementation varies across federal agencies and project types:

- **Agency-specific NEPA procedures** – Each federal agency (e.g., FHWA, USFS, EPA) has its own regulations and guidance for implementing NEPA, including tailored CE lists and mitigation requirements.
- **Programmatic NEPA documents** – Agencies sometimes prepare a single EIS or EA covering a group of similar actions (e.g., a nationwide program for bridge replacements) to reduce redundancy.
- **Supplemental EIS** – If new information or changed circumstances arise after a ROD, the agency may prepare a supplemental EIS to reassess impacts.
- **Environmental Assessment with a FONSI** – The most common outcome; the agency concludes no significant impact and moves forward without a full EIS.
- **Mitigated FONSI** – A FONSI that includes specific mitigation measures (like a RAP) to keep impacts below the significance threshold. The *SR 132 RAP* approval falls under this variant.

## Trade-offs

- **Time and cost** – NEPA review can be lengthy and expensive, particularly for EIS-level projects. Delays can affect project schedules and budgets.
- **Public participation** – NEPA mandates public involvement, which improves transparency and can incorporate local knowledge but also opens the process to litigation and extended timelines.
- **Flexibility vs. rigidity** – The categorical exclusion and EA pathways allow streamlined review for minor actions, but the statutory language leaves room for agency discretion, sometimes leading to inconsistent application.
- **Mitigation effectiveness** – Mitigation measures (e.g., restoration action plans) may be required to reduce impacts, but their monitoring and enforcement are not always robust, potentially undermining environmental protection.
- **Litigation risk** – Inadequate NEPA analysis is a common basis for lawsuits, forcing agencies to revisit decisions or redo documents. This can improve environmental outcomes but adds uncertainty.
