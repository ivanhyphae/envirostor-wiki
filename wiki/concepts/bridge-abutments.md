## Definition

**Bridge abutments** are the substructures located at each end of a bridge span that support the superstructure (deck, girders, etc.) and retain the adjacent earth fill (approach embankment). They transfer vertical loads from the bridge and lateral loads from soil pressure, temperature changes, and braking forces into the foundation. In the context of the *SR 132 RAP approval letter* (dated 3/14/2018), the abutments were designed per AASHTO LRFD specifications to accommodate a specific site geometry, seismic criteria, and geotechnical conditions, as documented in that plan approval.

## How it works

Bridge abutments perform two primary functions:

1. **Load bearing**: They support the bridge superstructure at its ends. The abutment includes a **bridge seat** (a horizontal ledge) on which the girder ends rest, often equipped with bearing pads or expansion bearings.
2. **Earth retention**: The back face of the abutment retains the approach fill, preventing soil collapse under the roadway. This requires the abutment to resist active or at-rest earth pressure, surcharge loads from traffic, and hydrostatic pressure.

Structurally, abutments typically consist of:
- **Stem wall**: vertical or battered wall that holds back earth.
- **Backwall** (optional): a shorter extension above the bridge seat that prevents soil from spilling onto the seat.
- **Footing** or **pile cap**: distributes loads to the ground or piles.
- **Wingwalls** (often integral): flared extensions that retain fill along the approach slopes.

The abutment interacts with the superstructure via bearings (fixed or expansion) and with the foundation through direct bearing or deep foundations. Design must account for stability modes: overturning, sliding, bearing capacity failure, and settlement.

## Variants

- **Gravity abutments**: massive concrete or masonry walls relying on self-weight for stability. Common for small spans and low heights.
- **Cantilever abutments**: reinforced concrete T-shaped walls; stem acts as a cantilever from the footing. Most common in modern bridges.
- **Full-height abutments** (or “stub abutments”): tall walls extending from foundation to deck; typical for deep fills.
- **Spill-through abutments** (or “open abutments”): columns or piles spaced apart, allowing fill to “spill through” – used where tall abutments would be uneconomical.
- **Integral abutments**: monolithic connection between abutment and superstructure (no expansion joint). The abutment moves with the bridge due to thermal expansion; requires flexible piles or sliding foundation. Reduces maintenance but increases soil-structure interaction.
- **Semi-integral abutments**: hybrid; superstructure is integral with backwall but abutment stem is separate and moves on bearings.

The SR 132 RAP approval letter likely specifies one of these variants based on the project’s geotechnical report and hydraulic constraints.

## Trade-offs

- **Cost vs. maintenance**: Integral abutments eliminate expansion joints and bearings, reducing long-term maintenance, but require more detailed analysis of cyclic soil pressures and may limit span lengths.
- **Height vs. stability**: Tall gravity abutments become very massive; cantilever abutments are more economical for heights > 5 m but require careful reinforcement detailing.
- **Seismic performance**: In seismic zones, abutments must be ductile. Integral abutments can accommodate thermal and seismic movements, while full-height gravity walls may be vulnerable to overturning.
- **Hydraulic impact**: Abutments encroach on waterway cross-section, causing scour (local erosion around foundations). The SR 132 RAP likely includes scour countermeasures.
- **Construction complexity**: Spill-through abutments involve staged fill placement; integral abutments require careful phasing to avoid locked-in stresses.
