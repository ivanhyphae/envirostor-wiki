# Total Petroleum Hydrocarbons Diesel (TPH-D)

## Definition
Total Petroleum Hydrocarbons Diesel (TPH-D) refers to the specific measurement of hydrocarbon compounds within the diesel-range organic fraction (typically C10–C28 or C10–C40 depending on the analytical method) that are quantified as part of petroleum contamination assessments. TPH-D is a subset of the broader Total Petroleum Hydrocarbons (TPH) category, which encompasses all petroleum-based hydrocarbons present in environmental samples (soil, water, sediment). The diesel range specifically targets the carbon chain lengths characteristic of diesel fuel, heating oil, and other middle-distillate petroleum products. Regulatory standards for TPH-D are often established through site-specific cleanup levels or generic screening values, such as those included in a Site Management Plan (SMP) approval process.

## How it works
TPH-D measurement relies on analytical chemistry methods designed to extract, separate, and quantify hydrocarbons from environmental media. The most common approach is gas chromatography with flame ionization detection (GC-FID), after sample preparation. The process proceeds as follows:

1. **Sample Extraction**: Soil or water samples are extracted using a solvent (e.g., methylene chloride or hexane/acetone) to dissolve the petroleum hydrocarbons. Extraction methods include sonication, Soxhlet extraction, or accelerated solvent extraction (ASE).

2. **Cleanup (if needed)**: Extracts may be purified using silica gel, alumina, or other sorbents to remove polar interferences (e.g., biogenic organic matter), isolating the non-polar hydrocarbon fraction.

3. **Gas Chromatography**: The extract is injected into a GC column (e.g., capillary column with non-polar stationary phase). The column separates hydrocarbons by boiling point/volatility, with lighter compounds eluting earlier.

4. **Detection and Quantification**: The FID detects carbon-containing compounds as they elute. The total area under the chromatogram between specific retention time windows (calibrated to diesel-range standards) is integrated and compared against a calibration curve of known diesel concentrations. Results are expressed as mg/kg (soil) or mg/L (water).

5. **Regulatory Context**: In an SMP approval, TPH-D is typically compared to risk-based screening levels (RBSLs) or cleanup standards established by the regulatory agency. Exceedances trigger additional investigation, remediation, or monitoring requirements. The SMP defines specific action levels, sampling protocols, and reporting procedures.

## Variants
Multiple analytical methods and reporting conventions exist for TPH-D:

- **Method 8015 (EPA)**: A widely used GC method for non-halogenated volatile and semi-volatile organics. TPH-D is quantified using the diesel-range calibration mixture (e.g., C10–C28 or C10–C40).

- **Method 8270 (EPA)**: GC/MS (mass spectrometry) method that can identify and quantify individual compounds within the diesel range, but is less common for bulk TPH-D because it is more expensive and complex.

- **TPH-CWG (Canada-Wide Standard)**: Defines TPH fractions: F1 (C6–C10), F2 (C10–C16), F3 (C16–C34), F4 (C34–C50). TPH-D typically corresponds to F2/F3 fractions, though exact carbon ranges vary by jurisdiction.

- **TPH-GRO vs. TPH-D**: Distinguishes between gasoline-range organics (C6–C10) and diesel-range organics (C10–C28 or C10–C40). Some methods use separate calibration standards for each.

- **Soil and Water Standards**: TPH-D cleanup levels differ for soil vs. groundwater. Soil standards may consider land use (residential, industrial) and depth to groundwater. Water standards may be based on aquatic toxicity or drinking water limits.

- **Total vs. Resolved/Unresolved Fractions**: Some assessments differentiate between the resolved peaks (individual identifiable compounds like n-alkanes, PAHs) and the unresolved complex mixture (UCM) of branched/cyclic hydrocarbons. This distinction helps in source identification and toxicity evaluation.

## Trade-offs
Key trade-offs and limitations when using TPH-D in environmental assessments:

- **Analytical Variability**: TPH-D results can vary significantly between laboratories due to differences in extraction efficiency, calibration standards (e.g., using diesel #2 vs. other reference fuels), integration software settings, and degree of silica gel cleanup. This undermines comparability across studies.

- **Matrix Interference**: Natural organic matter (humic acids, plant waxes) in soil can co-extract and elute in the diesel range, causing false positives or overestimation. Proper cleanup (e.g., silica gel, alumina) is essential but not always performed consistently.

- **Non-Specific Toxicity**: TPH-D is a bulk parameter; it does not identify the specific hydrocarbon compounds responsible for toxicity. Two sites with equal TPH-D concentrations may pose different risks if they contain different proportions of toxic compounds (e.g., polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons, benzene, toluene). This limits risk assessment accuracy.

- **Regulatory Inconsistency**: Different jurisdictions use different carbon ranges and action levels (e.g., C10–C28 vs. C10–C40), making cross-site or cross-border comparisons difficult. SMP approvals may reference site-specific standards that deviate from generic benchmarks.

- **Cost and Turnaround**: Detailed TPH-D analysis using GC-FID with cleanup is moderately expensive (typically $100–300 per sample) and takes 5–10 business days. Cheaper field screening methods (e.g., immunoassay, infrared) are less accurate but faster.

- **Risk-Based vs. Generic Standards**: SMP approvals that rely on generic TPH-D cleanup levels (e.g., 10 mg/kg for residential soil) may be overly conservative for non-toxic diesel fractions, leading to unnecessary remediation costs. Conversely, they may be insufficient for sites with highly toxic hydrocarbon blends.

- **Weathering Alteration**: As diesel fuel weathers (evaporates, biodegrades, dissolves), the hydrocarbon profile shifts (lighter components lost), potentially causing TPH-D measured years after a spill to underestimate original contamination or misrepresent residual risk.
