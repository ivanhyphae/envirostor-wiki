## Definition

Recs High Medium Low Risk Classification is a practical screening framework used to categorize a fill source, material source, or similar environmental/earthwork input into qualitative risk tiers: **high**, **medium**, or **low**. The classification is generally based on the amount and quality of investigation performed on the source material, the observed or analytical evidence for contamination, and the likelihood that the material will meet the receiving project’s environmental acceptance criteria.

In the context of source testing exhibits for clean fill, the classification helps determine whether a source is suitable for unrestricted use, needs additional verification, or should be treated as a potential concern requiring more controls or rejection.

## How it works

The classification is typically a **decision-support tool** built from several lines of evidence:

1. **Source history review**
   - What the material is, where it came from, and how the site or borrow area was used.
   - Past industrial, agricultural, commercial, or waste-related uses can increase risk.

2. **Field inspection and reconnaissance**
   - Visual evidence such as staining, odors, debris, buried waste, fill heterogeneity, or stressed vegetation.
   - Presence of obvious anthropogenic material often raises the risk tier.

3. **Sampling and laboratory testing**
   - Soil or fill samples are analyzed for target contaminants relevant to the receiving project.
   - Results are compared to acceptance thresholds, background levels, or regulatory screening criteria.

4. **Data quality and coverage**
   - More sampling locations, deeper interval coverage, and better spatial representation reduce uncertainty.
   - Sparse or inconsistent data generally increases risk because hidden contamination may remain undetected.

5. **Comparative evaluation**
   - Results are interpreted against project-specific standards.
   - Materials that are consistently clean and well-documented are usually assigned lower risk.

A typical interpretation is:

- **Low risk**
  - Source is well characterized.
  - No obvious concern from site history, observations, or testing.
  - Analytical results are consistently below relevant thresholds.
  - Material is likely acceptable with minimal additional review.

- **Medium risk**
  - Some uncertainty remains.
  - Limited sampling, incomplete history, or minor anomalies may exist.
  - Additional confirmation, spot checks, or restrictions may be needed before acceptance.

- **High risk**
  - Significant uncertainty or clear evidence of contamination exists.
  - Historical use, field observations, or analytical results suggest the material may not qualify as clean fill.
  - Often requires rejection, segregation, further investigation, or management as suspect material.

In practice, the framework is often used to support a go/no-go decision for accepting fill from a particular source, especially for large infrastructure projects where imported material must meet environmental criteria.

## Variants

Common variants of this kind of classification include:

- **Three-tier qualitative ratings**
  - High / Medium / Low risk is the simplest and most common form.

- **Color-coded matrices**
  - Risk tiers are mapped to colors such as red, yellow, and green for quick review.

- **Probability-consequence matrices**
  - Risk is derived from both the likelihood of contamination and the potential impact if contamination is present.

- **Source-specific acceptance categories**
  - Instead of generic risk terms, projects may use labels such as “approved,” “conditionally approved,” or “not approved.”

- **Regulatory or project-specific scoring systems**
  - Some programs convert site history, visual observations, and lab results into a numeric score that is then translated into a risk tier.

- **Clean fill screening frameworks**
  - A specialized variant focused on whether imported material qualifies as clean fill under project criteria.

## Trade-offs

### Advantages
- **Simple to communicate**
  - High/medium/low is easy for engineers, environmental staff, and contractors to understand.
- **Fast triage**
  - Helps prioritize which sources need more testing or scrutiny.
- **Supports consistency**
  - Creates a repeatable way to compare multiple candidate sources.
- **Useful for project control**
  - Reduces the chance of accepting unsuitable material.

### Limitations
- **Coarse granularity**
  - Three tiers may oversimplify complex site conditions.
- **Dependent on data quality**
  - A low-risk label is only as reliable as the underlying investigation.
- **Subjectivity**
  - Different reviewers may weigh history, observations, and test results differently.
- **Not a substitute for compliance**
  - The classification is an aid to decision-making, not a regulatory determination by itself.
- **False reassurance**
  - A source can appear low risk if contamination is localized and sampling is insufficient.

### Practical considerations
- Risk tier should be tied to documented criteria, not informal judgment.
- The classification should be revisited if new information emerges.
- For heterogeneous fill, localized hot spots can make broad source-level ratings misleading.
- Thresholds should be defined in advance to avoid inconsistent decisions.
