## Definition

Geocon Groundwater Sampling Methods are the field and laboratory procedures used to collect representative groundwater samples from a monitoring well, piezometer, or other groundwater access point for geochemical, hydrogeological, and environmental analysis. In practice, the term usually refers to sampling approaches applied during a groundwater monitoring program at a specific site or project, such as a stockpile or remediation area, where results are used to evaluate water quality, contaminant mobility, and potential impacts to surrounding groundwater.

These methods are designed to obtain water that accurately reflects in situ conditions, while minimizing disturbance, cross-contamination, and changes in chemistry during collection, purging, transfer, preservation, and transport.

## How it works

Groundwater sampling generally follows a sequence of steps:

1. **Well inspection and preparation**
   - Verify the well identification, construction details, and sampling history.
   - Inspect the casing, cap, lock, and surrounding area for damage or contamination.
   - Measure water level to determine depth to groundwater and calculate purge volume if needed.

2. **Equipment setup**
   - Select appropriate sampling equipment based on well depth, diameter, target analytes, and required detection limits.
   - Common equipment includes low-flow pumps, bladder pumps, peristaltic pumps, bailers, dedicated pumps, tubing, flow cells, and field meters.
   - Decontaminate non-dedicated equipment between wells to prevent cross-contamination.

3. **Purging or stabilization**
   - Many groundwater methods require removing stagnant water from the well before collecting the sample.
   - Traditional purging removes several well volumes.
   - Low-flow methods minimize drawdown and pump at a reduced rate while monitoring stabilization parameters.
   - Field parameters typically measured include pH, specific conductance, temperature, dissolved oxygen, oxidation-reduction potential, and turbidity.
   - Sampling is usually started once these indicators stabilize.

4. **Sample collection**
   - Collect groundwater directly into the appropriate containers.
   - Use containers and preservatives matched to the analytes of interest.
   - For volatile compounds, minimize agitation and headspace.
   - For dissolved metals or filtered samples, filtration may be performed in the field using a properly sized inline filter.
   - Record sample time, flow conditions, stabilization data, and any anomalies.

5. **Preservation and transport**
   - Preserve samples by cooling, acidification, or other analyte-specific techniques.
   - Maintain chain-of-custody documentation.
   - Ship to the laboratory under required holding-time and temperature conditions.

6. **Quality assurance and quality control**
   - Use field blanks, equipment rinsate blanks, trip blanks, duplicates, and matrix-specific QC samples as required by the sampling plan.
   - Review data against acceptance criteria and flag any results influenced by turbidity, low purge efficiency, or unstable field parameters.

The underlying goal is to preserve groundwater chemistry as closely as possible from the aquifer to the analytical instrument. Sampling method choice strongly affects representativeness, especially for analytes sensitive to aeration, oxidation, volatilization, adsorption, or suspended solids.

## Variants

Common variants and implementations include:

- **Low-flow sampling**
  - Uses a pump installed in the well at a slow rate.
  - Aims to reduce turbidity and disturbance.
  - Often preferred for dissolved metals and VOCs when a stable sample is required.

- **Conventional purge-and-sample**
  - Removes a set number of well volumes before sampling.
  - Simpler and widely used, but may generate more turbidity and waste water.

- **No-purge or minimal-purge sampling**
  - Collects samples with little or no purging under defined conditions.
  - Used in some monitoring programs where groundwater is known to be relatively stable or where well hydraulics support it.

- **Bailer sampling**
  - Uses a cylindrical device lowered into the well to retrieve water.
  - Low equipment cost, but can increase turbulence and alter volatile or dissolved constituents.

- **Dedicated sampling systems**
  - Permanently installed pumps or devices assigned to one well.
  - Reduce cross-contamination and setup time.

- **Passive sampling**
  - Relies on diffusion or equilibrium with the formation water.
  - Examples include diffusion bags, passive diffusion samplers, and equilibrium-based devices.
  - Useful where low-disturbance sampling is important.

- **Filtered vs. unfiltered sampling**
  - Filtered samples target the dissolved fraction.
  - Unfiltered samples represent total recoverable concentrations and may be affected by sediment.

- **Depth-discrete sampling**
  - Collects groundwater from specific depths within the saturated zone.
  - Useful where contaminant concentrations vary vertically.

## Trade-offs

Key trade-offs and limitations include:

- **Representativeness vs. disturbance**
  - Aggressive purging can remove formation water but may also stir up sediment and change geochemistry.
  - Low-flow methods better preserve in situ conditions but require more time and careful stabilization monitoring.

- **Data comparability**
  - Different sampling methods can yield different concentrations, especially for turbidity-sensitive analytes such as metals.
  - Results may not be directly comparable across campaigns unless methods are consistent.

- **Speed vs. quality**
  - Bailers and conventional purge methods are fast and simple.
  - More controlled approaches usually produce higher-quality data but require more equipment and oversight.

- **Cost vs. reliability**
  - Dedicated pumps, field meters, and passive systems can improve consistency but increase setup cost.
  - Simpler methods lower cost but may increase variability and risk of contamination.

- **Waste generation**
  - Traditional purging can generate significant purge water requiring handling and disposal.
  - Low-flow and passive methods typically reduce waste volume.

- **Sensitivity to analyte type**
  - VOCs are sensitive to aeration and agitation.
  - Metals can be influenced by pH shifts, oxidation, and turbidity.
  - Nutrients, major ions, and general water quality parameters may be less sensitive but still require good field practice.

- **Well condition dependency**
  - Poorly constructed, damaged, or low-yield wells may not be suitable for all methods.
  - Silted or low-recharge wells can make stabilization difficult and may compromise sample quality.
