## Definition

**No Unacceptable Human Health Risk** is a risk-management conclusion used in environmental cleanup and site closure decisions. It means that, based on the available data, the remaining contamination at a site does **not** pose a human health risk that is considered unacceptable under the applicable regulatory framework and exposure assumptions.

In practice, this does **not** mean there is zero risk. It means the risk is low enough, or sufficiently controlled, that regulators can accept the site condition for its current or intended use. The determination is typically based on a comparison of measured or modeled contaminant concentrations against health-based screening levels, exposure scenarios, and institutional or engineering controls.

## How it works

A no-unacceptable-risk conclusion is usually developed through a structured risk evaluation:

1. **Identify contaminants of concern**  
   Site sampling identifies chemicals that remain in soil, groundwater, dust, or other media.

2. **Define exposure pathways**  
   Evaluators consider how people could come into contact with contamination, such as:
   - incidental soil ingestion
   - inhalation of dust or vapors
   - dermal contact
   - groundwater ingestion, if applicable

3. **Select receptor scenarios**  
   The analysis uses assumed land uses and receptor groups, such as:
   - construction workers
   - maintenance workers
   - future industrial users
   - adjacent residents, if relevant

4. **Compare concentrations to risk-based criteria**  
   Measured concentrations are compared to regulatory screening levels or used in quantitative risk calculations. If estimated exposure falls below accepted risk thresholds, the site may be concluded to present no unacceptable human health risk.

5. **Account for controls and site conditions**  
   If contamination remains in place, conclusions often depend on:
   - pavement or soil cover
   - deed restrictions or land-use limitations
   - operation and maintenance requirements
   - monitoring and inspection programs

6. **Document the basis for the conclusion**  
   The final decision is supported by the site conceptual model, analytical results, exposure assumptions, and any required controls.

In the source document, this type of conclusion appears in the context of a contaminated stockpile remediation project, where the remedy and site configuration are intended to ensure residual material does not create unacceptable exposure to people.

## Variants

Common variants of this concept include:

- **No significant risk**: a similar but not identical regulatory phrase, often used in different jurisdictions or legal contexts.
- **Risk-based closure**: closure based on meeting health-risk criteria rather than complete contaminant removal.
- **Controlled risk / managed risk**: contamination remains, but exposure is prevented or limited by institutional or engineering controls.
- **Residential vs. industrial risk conclusions**: the acceptable risk level depends on the assumed land use.
- **Human health risk vs. ecological risk**: a site may pose no unacceptable human health risk but still require ecological evaluation.

## Trade-offs

Key considerations and limitations include:

- **Not equivalent to zero contamination**  
  Residual contaminants may remain onsite; the conclusion only addresses acceptability of risk.

- **Depends on assumptions**  
  The result can change if land use, exposure duration, or future site conditions change.

- **Requires controls to stay effective**  
  If caps, pavement, or restrictions fail, risk may increase.

- **May not address all receptors**  
  A conclusion for workers may not apply to the general public, children, or nearby residents.

- **Uncertainty in sampling and modeling**  
  Limited data, heterogeneous contamination, or conservative assumptions can affect confidence in the conclusion.

- **Regulatory specificity**  
  What counts as “unacceptable” depends on the governing program, cleanup standard, and site-specific risk assessment method.
