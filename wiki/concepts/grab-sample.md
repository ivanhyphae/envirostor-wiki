## Definition
A **Grab Sample** is a discrete environmental sample collected at a single point in time and space, representing conditions at that specific moment. In the context of soil and stockpile sampling (as referenced in raw/S1200-01-01 Modesto Stockpiles Oct 2016 SW Sampling_12.16.pdf), a grab sample is typically taken from a distinct location within a stockpile (e.g., surface or shallow depth) without compositing or homogenization across multiple points, providing a snapshot of contaminant concentrations or material characteristics.

## How it works
Grab sampling involves collecting a single aliquot or specimen from a targeted medium (soil, water, air) using standardized tools and procedures. For soil stockpiles, the process (as inferred from the source document, a PDF containing encrypted and technical stream data) likely includes:
- **Selection**: Choosing a representative point on the stockpile surface or depth interval.
- **Collection**: Using a shovel, auger, or corer to extract a fixed volume (e.g., 500 g or 1 kg) of material.
- **Preservation**: Placing the sample in a clean, labeled container (e.g., glass jar or plastic bag) with appropriate preservation (e.g., cooling on ice for volatile analytes).
- **Documentation**: Recording GPS coordinates, depth, date, and visual observations.
- **Analysis**: Submitting the single sample to a laboratory for chemical or physical testing.

In the source PDF, grab samples were used for soil stockpile characterization (Modesto, CA, October 2016), likely to assess metals, hydrocarbons, or other contaminants without compositing, enabling detection of localized hot spots or variability.

## Variants
Variants of grab samples include:
- **Surface Grab Sample**: Collected from the top 0–2 inches of a stockpile; used for surface contamination assessment.
- **Depth Grab Sample**: Taken from a specific depth interval (e.g., 2–4 ft) using direct-push or hand-auger methods.
- **Discrete Grab Sample**: A single point sample without compositing; the standard definition.
- **Field Duplicate**: A second grab sample taken at the same location in the same manner to assess sampling precision.
- **Integrated Grab Sample**: Though rare, a grab can be integrated by collecting over a short period (e.g., 15-minute flow-weighted sample in water sampling) to approximate time-weighted average.
- **Soil Vapor Grab Sample**: Using a syringe or canister to capture vapor from a soil gas probe at one point in time.

## Trade-offs
Trade-offs and limitations of grab samples relative to composite or continuous sampling methods:
- **Advantages**: Simpler to collect, lower cost, higher spatial resolution (detects local heterogeneity), minimal equipment.
- **Disadvantages**:
  - **Poor temporal representativeness**: Captures only one moment; may miss diurnal, tidal, or event-driven variations.
  - **High variability**: Single point may not reflect stockpile average; requires many replicates for statistical confidence.
  - **Loss of precision**: For non-homogeneous materials (e.g., stockpiles with layered contamination), one grab can be misleading.
  - **Limited volume**: Insufficient for low-concentration analyses requiring large masses (e.g., dioxins).
- **Regulatory context**: In soil sampling for stockpile characterization (e.g., the Modesto project from the source), grab samples are often used for initial screening or hot-spot identification, but composite samples may be required for mean concentration estimates under regulatory guidance (e.g., EPA methods for hazardous waste or RCRA).
