## Definition

A **Containment Alternative** is a remedial strategy for contaminated material (e.g., soil, sediment, stockpiles) that relies on engineered barriers and controls to isolate contaminants *in place*, rather than removing or treating the material. In the context of Caltrans Modesto Stockpile remediation documents (RAP and RDIP), containment alternatives include interim and ultimate cover/cap systems designed to prevent direct contact, wind erosion, stormwater infiltration, and migration of contaminants from stockpiled material. These alternatives are typically evaluated when excavation and off‑site disposal are infeasible due to cost, regulatory constraints, or physical site limitations.

## How it works

A containment alternative functions by creating one or more engineered barriers that interrupt the exposure pathways between contaminated media and the environment (humans, groundwater, surface water, air). The primary components include:

- **Low‑permeability cap** – a layer of compacted clay, geomembrane (e.g., HDPE), or asphalt that reduces infiltration of precipitation and prevents upward movement of contaminants.
- **Drainage layer** – a granular or geosynthetic layer that conveys percolating water away from the cap to a collection or downgradient management system.
- **Gas venting layer** – a permeable layer (often placed beneath the cap) that allows volatile organic compounds (VOCs) or methane to be passively or actively vented.
- **Vegetative cover or soil layer** – a final cover of clean soil (typically 2–3 ft) that supports vegetation and provides a separation from the cap, prevents erosion, and meets aesthetic or land‑use requirements.
- **Monitoring system** – wells, lysimeters, and gas probes placed around the containment area to verify that the barrier remains effective and that no unacceptable contaminant migration is occurring.

The specific design is influenced by the nature of the stockpiled material, site hydrogeology, climate, and the intended future use of the land. In the Modesto Stockpile RDIP, figures show cap plans for both interim and ultimate scenarios, with cross‑sections detailing layer thicknesses and materials.

## Variants

- **Interim Containment Alternative** – a temporary cap (often a geomembrane or asphalt) installed to control exposure until a final remedy is selected or implemented. Typically designed for a service life of 5–15 years and requires periodic inspection and maintenance.

- **Ultimate Containment Alternative** – a permanent cap meeting RCRA Subtitle D or equivalent criteria, designed for a service life of 30+ years. Includes a low‑permeability layer, drainage system, and thick soil cover. Often paired with land‑use restrictions (e.g., no residential development).

- **Asphalt/Concrete Cap** – a rigid cap used when the stockpile area is to be converted to paved surfaces (e.g., parking lot, road base). Provides immediate exposure control and may serve as the final surface.

- **Vegetated Cover (Earthen Cap)** – a cap relying on a thick soil layer and native vegetation to control erosion and evapotranspiration, with or without an underlying geomembrane. Common for remote stockpiles where landscape blending is required.

- **Modified Containment (Engineered Barrier)** – may include cement‑stabilized soil, grouting, or sidewall barriers (e.g., slurry walls) to control lateral migration, used when the stockpile is in contact with groundwater.

## Trade-offs

| Consideration | Containment Alternative | Excavation & Off‑site Disposal |
|---------------|------------------------|-------------------------------|
| **Cost** | Lower upfront cost; lower long‑term O&M obligation (if well‑designed). | High immediate cost (transportation, disposal fees). |
| **Permanence** | Long‑term liability; cap may degrade over decades; institutional controls needed. | Removes contaminant source; no future liability. |
| **Land use** | Restricts future use (e.g., no penetrating structures, no residential development). | Land can be re‑used without restrictions. |
| **Regulatory acceptance** | Often acceptable for low‑toxicity, low‑mobility wastes; may require EPA or state approval. | More universally accepted, but may be discouraged by some agencies to avoid waste transfer. |
| **Environmental impact** | Lower transportation emissions; material remains in place. | High carbon footprint from hauling; potential for secondary contamination at disposal site. |
| **Monitoring** | Requires ongoing groundwater, gas, and cap integrity monitoring. | Monitoring typically ends after removal confirmation. |
| **Implementation time** | Faster to implement (weeks to months). | Longer due to excavation, transportation, and disposal. |

Key limitations of containment include the need for perpetual institutional controls, potential for cap failure (e.g., cracking, erosion), and the difficulty of confirming that containment is fully effective over the long term.
