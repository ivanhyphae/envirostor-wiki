## Definition

**Depth Discrete Composite Sampling For Clean Borrow Evaluation** is a geotechnical and environmental sampling approach used to assess whether a proposed or existing **clean borrow area** contains material suitable for use as fill, embankment, or construction borrow without unacceptable contamination or geotechnical variability.

The method combines two ideas:

1. **Depth discrete sampling** — samples are collected from specific, known depth intervals rather than as a single mixed profile.
2. **Composite sampling** — material from multiple locations, depth intervals, or increments is combined into one analytical sample to represent a broader volume of material.

In the context of clean borrow evaluation, this approach is used to characterize the vertical and spatial variability of soil or borrow material efficiently while still maintaining enough resolution to identify contamination, lithologic changes, moisture variation, or unsuitable strata.

## How it works

A depth discrete composite sampling program is typically designed around the expected borrow stratigraphy and the project’s acceptance criteria.

### 1. Define the borrow interval
The candidate borrow area is divided into:
- horizontal sampling locations, and
- vertical depth intervals of interest.

Depth intervals may correspond to changes in soil type, groundwater influence, overburden thickness, or anticipated excavation lifts.

### 2. Collect discrete increments by depth
At each location, material is collected from a specified depth range using direct-push tooling, test pits, augering, or excavation sampling.

The key feature is that the sample is **depth discrete**:
- each increment is tied to a specific depth window,
- deeper and shallower materials are not blended unless explicitly intended.

This allows the evaluator to detect vertical changes that a fully mixed sample would obscure.

### 3. Composite increments when appropriate
Material from several discrete increments may then be combined into a single composite sample for analysis. This is usually done when the goal is to represent the average quality of a borrow volume rather than to test every increment separately.

Common compositing schemes include:
- combining increments from a single lift across one location,
- combining increments from multiple nearby borings or test pits,
- combining increments from a homogeneous depth band across the site.

The composite should be designed so it still reflects the unit being evaluated. If high-risk intervals are present, they may be sampled separately rather than composited.

### 4. Analyze for suitability
The composite sample is analyzed for parameters relevant to clean borrow acceptance, which may include:
- chemistry or contamination indicators,
- grain size / gradation,
- moisture content,
- organics,
- fines content,
- plasticity,
- other project-specific criteria.

Results are compared to the project’s clean borrow criteria and any regulatory thresholds.

### 5. Interpret by volume and depth
Because each composite represents a defined depth interval and spatial area, the results support decisions such as:
- whether the borrow area is acceptable as clean fill,
- which depth zones are acceptable,
- whether selective stripping or segregation is required,
- how much suitable material is available.

## Variants

Several practical variants are used depending on project goals:

### Depth-discrete discrete sampling
Each depth interval is analyzed separately, with no compositing.  
Use when high resolution is needed or when contamination is suspected.

### Spatial composite sampling
Samples from multiple nearby points at the same depth interval are composited to represent a larger lateral area.  
Use when the borrow material is believed to be relatively uniform.

### Depth composite sampling
Multiple increments from different depths within a defined lift are composited.  
Use when the borrow is managed by excavation lifts and vertical variability is modest.

### Stratigraphic-unit compositing
Samples are composited only within a single geologic unit or soil horizon.  
This preserves interpretability while reducing the number of analyses.

### Incremental sampling approach
A systematic set of small increments is collected across a decision unit and combined into a single representative sample.  
This is more common in environmental characterization, but the logic overlaps with clean borrow evaluation.

### Discrete plus confirmatory sampling
Composite samples are used for screening, while discrete samples are retained or analyzed separately to confirm suspicious zones.  
This is a common risk-management hybrid.

## Trade-offs

### Advantages
- **More representative than single-point sampling** for heterogeneous borrow sources.
- **More efficient than analyzing every increment separately**.
- **Preserves depth information**, which is critical when contamination or unsuitable material varies vertically.
- **Supports volume-based acceptance decisions** for excavation planning.

### Limitations
- **Dilution of hotspots:** contaminated or unsuitable material can be masked by cleaner material in the composite.
- **Loss of resolution:** compositing reduces the ability to identify exactly which increment caused a failure.
- **Design sensitivity:** the value of the results depends heavily on how the composite unit is defined.
- **Not ideal for highly variable sites:** strong lateral or vertical heterogeneity may require more discrete sampling.
- **Potential regulatory constraints:** some programs or agencies may require discrete confirmation samples rather than composites.

### Practical considerations
- Keep compositing boundaries aligned with **geology, excavation lifts, or decision units**.
- Do not composite across materials with clearly different properties unless the project explicitly allows it.
- Document exactly which increments contributed to each composite sample.
- Use field observations to decide when a zone should be sampled separately.
- Maintain chain-of-custody and sample traceability for every increment contributing to a composite.
