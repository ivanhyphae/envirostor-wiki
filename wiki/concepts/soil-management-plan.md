## Definition

A **Soil Management Plan (SMP)** is a site-specific document that describes how soil will be handled, protected, reused, moved, stockpiled, tested, and restored during a project or land-use activity. Its purpose is to prevent soil degradation, minimize environmental impacts, maintain soil productivity, and ensure compliance with applicable permits, approvals, and best management practices.

In practice, an SMP defines procedures for managing different soil horizons or soil types, especially when excavation, grading, construction, remediation, agriculture, or land development may disturb the native soil profile.

## How it works

A Soil Management Plan typically works by translating site conditions and project activities into explicit handling rules for soil. It usually includes:

- **Baseline characterization**
  - Identifies existing soil conditions, such as texture, structure, moisture, organic matter, contamination risk, erosion susceptibility, and depth of topsoil/subsoil layers.
  - May incorporate soil testing or prior environmental assessments.

- **Activity-specific controls**
  - Specifies when and where soils may be stripped, excavated, hauled, reused, amended, or disposed.
  - Distinguishes among soil layers where relevant, such as topsoil, subsoil, and bedrock interface materials.

- **Protection measures**
  - Reduces compaction, erosion, sediment runoff, mixing of incompatible soil layers, and contamination.
  - May require equipment restrictions, defined haul routes, stabilized access, erosion control, and timing constraints.

- **Stockpile and reuse procedures**
  - Describes how soils are segregated, labeled, stored, and protected from mixing, runoff, and degradation.
  - Defines conditions for reuse on-site versus off-site disposal or treatment.

- **Monitoring and verification**
  - Sets inspection, testing, and documentation requirements.
  - Confirms that soil handling meets regulatory and project requirements.

- **Restoration or revegetation**
  - Outlines how redistributed soil will be placed, graded, amended, and stabilized to support vegetation or future land use.

Operationally, an SMP acts as a decision framework: if soil disturbance is planned, the plan specifies the approved handling method; if field conditions change, the plan provides criteria for escalation, revision, or additional review.

## Variants

Common variants of Soil Management Plans include:

- **Construction Soil Management Plan**
  - Focuses on excavation, grading, stockpiling, erosion control, and reuse during construction projects.

- **Environmental Soil Management Plan**
  - Used where contamination is possible or known; includes sampling, segregation, cleanup thresholds, and disposal requirements.

- **Agricultural Soil Management Plan**
  - Emphasizes preservation of topsoil, compaction avoidance, fertility management, drainage, and long-term productivity.

- **Restoration / Reclamation Soil Management Plan**
  - Used for site rehabilitation, mine reclamation, land restoration, or ecosystem recovery.

- **Erosion and Sediment Control–linked SMP**
  - Integrates soil handling with runoff control measures and stormwater management.

- **Regulatory or permit-driven SMP**
  - Prepared to satisfy conditions in approvals, environmental permits, or consent orders.

Alternative approaches may include shorter soil handling specifications embedded in broader Construction Management Plans or Environmental Management Plans, rather than a standalone SMP.

## Trade-offs

Key trade-offs and considerations include:

- **Protection vs. efficiency**
  - Stronger soil protection measures can slow excavation, grading, and material transport.

- **Segregation vs. flexibility**
  - Separating soil by type or horizon preserves quality and traceability, but increases storage complexity and handling costs.

- **Reuse vs. disposal**
  - Reusing soil can reduce cost and waste, but only if it meets physical, chemical, and biological criteria.

- **Short-term disturbance vs. long-term performance**
  - Poor soil handling can lead to compaction, reduced infiltration, erosion, poor revegetation, and lower site productivity.

- **Documentation burden**
  - SMPs improve accountability, but require monitoring, recordkeeping, and sometimes laboratory testing.

- **Site variability**
  - Soil conditions can change significantly across a project area, so a plan must be specific enough to handle local differences without becoming impractical.
