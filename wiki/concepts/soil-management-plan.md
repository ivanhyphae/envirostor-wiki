## Definition
A **Soil Management Plan** (SMP) is a formal, site-specific document that outlines strategies for the protection, handling, reuse, and disposal of soil during construction or land development activities. It is typically required by regulatory agencies to prevent environmental contamination, manage erosion and sedimentation, and ensure that excavated or disturbed soil is handled in compliance with applicable laws.

## How it works
An SMP is developed through a multi-step process:
1. **Site Assessment**: Soil sampling and testing to characterize existing conditions, including contaminants (e.g., heavy metals, hydrocarbons), soil type, and groundwater presence.
2. **Regulatory Framework**: Identification of applicable permits (e.g., National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System (NPDES) for stormwater), local ordinances, and cleanup standards.
3. **Plan Components**:
   - **Soil Handling Procedures**: Methods for excavation, stockpiling, and transport to minimize dust and runoff.
   - **Erosion and Sediment Control**: Best management practices (BMPs) such as silt fences, sediment basins, and stabilization techniques.
   - **Material Management**: Decision trees for characterizing soil as clean, contaminated, or special waste, and specifying reuse (e.g., on-site backfill) or off-site disposal at approved facilities.
   - **Monitoring and Reporting**: Frequency of soil testing during construction, chain-of-custody documentation for transported soil, and notification protocols if unexpected contamination is encountered.
4. **Approval Process**: The SMP must be submitted to the relevant regulatory authority (e.g., state environmental agency, local planning board) for review and approval before ground disturbance begins.
5. **Implementation**: On-site staff follow the SMP during construction; deviations require documented justification and often reapproval.

## Variants
- **Construction SMP**: Focused on temporary soil management during building or infrastructure projects, emphasizing erosion control and sediment management.
- **Remedial SMP**: Used for contaminated sites undergoing cleanup; includes detailed procedures for soil removal, treatment (e.g., bioremediation, thermal desorption), and verification sampling.
- **Agricultural SMP**: Applied in farming contexts to prevent soil degradation, manage nutrient runoff, and preserve topsoil quality.
- **Decommissioning SMP**: Developed for site closure of industrial facilities or landfills, addressing soil removal, capping, and long-term monitoring.

## Trade-offs
- **Cost vs. Compliance**: Comprehensive SMPs increase upfront planning and testing costs but reduce risk of regulatory fines and project delays.
- **Flexibility vs. Certainty**: Highly prescriptive plans limit on-site decision-making but provide clear standards; adaptive plans require experienced personnel but can respond to unexpected conditions.
- **Reuse vs. Disposal**: On-site reuse of soil saves transportation costs and landfill fees but requires rigorous testing to avoid reintroducing contaminants; off-site disposal simplifies liability but increases logistical burden.
- **BMP Effectiveness**: Erosion and sediment controls (e.g., silt fences) are effective under normal conditions but may fail during extreme weather, requiring contingency plans.
