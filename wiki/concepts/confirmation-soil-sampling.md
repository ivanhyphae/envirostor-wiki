## Confirmation Soil Sampling

### Definition
Confirmation soil sampling refers to the systematic collection and analysis of soil samples after a remediation, excavation, or construction activity to verify that contamination levels have been reduced to acceptable regulatory standards or that soil meets geotechnical specifications. It is a quality assurance (QA) step used in environmental cleanup, brownfield redevelopment, and geotechnical engineering to confirm that the desired outcome (e.g., removal of contaminants, achieving target compaction) has been achieved.

### How it works
The process follows a pre-defined sampling plan, typically part of a broader Quality Assurance/Quality Control (QA/QC) program:

1. **Establish Criteria** – Regulatory action levels, cleanup goals, or design specifications are documented (e.g., maximum contaminant concentration, density requirements).
2. **Design Sampling Strategy** – Locations, depths, number of samples, and analytical methods are determined based on the site’s history, variability, and statistical requirements.
3. **Sample Collection** – Using appropriate tools (e.g., hand auger, direct-push rig, split-spoon sampler), soil is collected at designated points, often from post-remediation sidewalls and floors of excavations.
4. **Chain of Custody** – Samples are labeled, sealed, and transported to an accredited laboratory.
5. **Analysis** – Laboratory tests address target contaminants (metals, organics, etc.) or geotechnical properties (moisture, grain size, compaction).
6. **Comparison to Criteria** – Results are compared against the previously established thresholds. Statistical evaluation (e.g., upper confidence limits) may be used for multiple samples.
7. **Reporting** – A confirmation letter or memo documents that the site complies with the criteria, or identifies areas requiring further action.

### Variants
Several approaches exist depending on the context:

- **Systematic Grid Sampling** – Soil is collected at evenly spaced points across the excavation or treatment area. Provides spatial coverage but may miss hot spots.
- **Random Sampling** – Locations are chosen randomly to reduce bias, often used for statistical estimation of mean concentrations.
- **Judgmental (Targeted) Sampling** – Field personnel select samples from visually or field‑instrumented areas of concern (e.g., stained soil, highest readings). Common in final confirmation.
- **Composite Sampling** – Multiple subsamples are blended into one sample to estimate average conditions, reducing analytical costs but losing spatial detail.
- **Sector Sampling** – Excavation walls and floor are divided into sectors, with a sample taken from each sector — typical for underground storage tank (UST) removals.
- **Discrete Sampling vs. Incremental Sampling** – Standard discrete samples represent a single point; incremental sampling collects many small aliquots over an area to produce a representative composite.

In geotechnical confirmation, variants include **proof‑rolling** (visual verification) combined with **nuclear density gauge** testing for compaction.

### Trade-offs
| Consideration | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|---------------|------------|---------------|
| **Cost vs. Confidence** | More samples yield higher statistical certainty of meeting criteria. | Increasing sample count raises analytical costs and time. |
| **Grid vs. Judgmental** | Grid can be defended as unbiased. | Judgmental may miss unsuspected contamination; grid may dilute a hot spot. |
| **Composite vs. Discrete** | Composites reduce lab costs and can estimate average. | Composites cannot identify exact location of exceedances; may mask high concentrations. |
| **Field Screening** | Real‑time data (e.g., PID, XRF) can guide judgmental sampling. | Screening may not be precise enough for regulatory confirmation; lab confirmation still needed. |
| **Statistical Methods** | UCL calculations provide defensible “not to exceed” statements. | Require sufficient sample size; assumptions about distribution may not hold. |

Key limitations:
- Confirmation sampling only assesses the sampled points—unsampled areas could still contain contamination.
- Heterogeneous soils can cause high variability, making it difficult to prove compliance with high confidence.
- In geotechnical contexts, compaction confirmation may be destructive (e.g., sand cone tests) or non‑destructive (nuclear gauge), each with its own biases.
