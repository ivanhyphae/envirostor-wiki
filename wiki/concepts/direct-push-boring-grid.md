## Definition

Direct Push Boring Grid is a site investigation layout and execution method in which direct-push sampling or probing points are arranged on a predefined grid pattern across a target area. The purpose is to collect dense, spatially distributed subsurface data—typically for environmental characterization, soil/boring logs, or contaminant delineation—so that conditions can be mapped with better resolution than from a few widely spaced borings.

In practice, the term combines two ideas:

- direct-push technology: a drilling/probing method that advances tools into the ground without a traditional rotating drill string
- a grid-based investigation design: sampling locations are spaced systematically over an area rather than selected only at isolated points

## How it works

A direct push boring grid is planned by overlaying a regular pattern of investigation points on the area of interest. Field crews then advance direct-push tooling at each point, often collecting one or more of the following:

- soil samples
- groundwater samples
- in situ measurements
- lithologic logs
- screening data for contamination extent

The grid design is used to improve spatial coverage and reduce uncertainty between points. Typical steps are:

1. **Define the investigation area**  
   The target area is bounded based on project needs, such as a suspected contamination source area, a borrow area, or a parcel requiring geotechnical/environmental evaluation.

2. **Select grid spacing and pattern**  
   The point spacing is chosen to match the required resolution, expected variability, access constraints, and budget. Patterns may be square, rectangular, or staggered.

3. **Advance direct-push borings at grid nodes**  
   At each point, direct-push equipment is driven into the subsurface to the planned depth. Sampling intervals may be discrete or continuous, depending on the tool set and project objectives.

4. **Log and interpret results**  
   Data from each boring are compiled to identify trends in soil type, groundwater conditions, or contaminant distribution across the grid.

5. **Refine as needed**  
   If anomalies are found, additional borings may be added to close data gaps or better define boundaries.

The value of a grid lies in its ability to support interpolation between points. Rather than relying on judgmental placement alone, the investigator can compare results across a systematic spatial framework.

## Variants

Common variants and related implementations include:

- **Square grid**: equal spacing in both principal directions; the most straightforward layout
- **Rectangular grid**: different spacing along two axes, often used to adapt to site shape or expected anisotropy
- **Staggered or offset grid**: alternating rows offset from one another to increase coverage
- **Adaptive grid**: starts with broader spacing and densifies where anomalies are detected
- **Transect-based direct push layout**: line-based alternative used when full areal coverage is unnecessary or access is limited
- **Judgmental direct push borings**: targeted locations chosen by professional judgment rather than a formal grid
- **Direct-push with field screening only**: used for rapid delineation where full laboratory sampling is not required
- **Direct-push with permanent or temporary well installation**: when the objective includes groundwater monitoring in addition to sampling

Alternatives to a direct push boring grid include conventional drilling, auger borings, test pits, geophysical surveys, and non-systematic targeted borings.

## Trade-offs

Key considerations include:

- **Coverage versus cost**: a tighter grid provides better resolution but increases field time and cost
- **Resolution versus practicality**: fine spacing may be ideal technically but limited by access, utilities, surface obstructions, or property boundaries
- **Data density versus depth capability**: direct-push methods are efficient for many shallow to moderate-depth investigations, but they may not reach as deep or handle as many formation types as conventional drilling
- **Spatial certainty versus subsurface uncertainty**: a grid improves lateral mapping, but conditions can still vary between points
- **Tool limitations**: refusal, cobbles, bedrock, or dense strata can prevent advancement at some nodes
- **Representation of heterogeneity**: a regular grid may miss narrow features if spacing is too wide
- **Project objective fit**: the method is well suited to systematic characterization, but not always ideal for highly targeted source-area investigations or very deep stratigraphy

A direct push boring grid is most effective when the site has moderate spatial variability and the goal is to delineate conditions over an area with repeatable coverage.
