This Final Feasibility Study (FS) Report, prepared by Geocon Consultants, Inc. for the California Department of Transportation (Caltrans) in June 2014, evaluates remedial alternatives for three soil stockpiles (Stockpiles 1, 2, and 3) located along State Route 132 in Stanislaus County, California. The primary contaminant of concern is barium, which is predominantly in the form of the insoluble mineral barite. While past data suggested cadmium was also a concern, the FS concludes these detections were false positives due to barium interference, as later sampling found no cadmium above reporting limits.

Regulatory oversight is provided by the Department of Toxic Substances Control (DTSC) and the Central Valley Regional Water Quality Control Board (CVRWQCB). Key decisions include DTSC’s December 2009 acceptance of the 2007 Human Health Risk Assessment (HHRA), which concluded the stockpiles pose no unacceptable risk under current management conditions (fencing, vegetative cover, access restrictions, and ongoing groundwater monitoring). A subsequent DTSC concurrence in April 2013 with the Site-Specific Investigation and HHRA Update reiterated this conclusion, provided these management practices continue.

The FS evaluates four remedial alternatives:
1.  **No Action:** Deemed not protective for most land uses.
2.  **Institutional Controls:** Involves continuing current management (fencing, monitoring). This is protective but does not address the source of contamination.
3.  **Removal:** The most protective option, involving excavation and offsite disposal, but it is very costly (estimated at ~$21.5 million) and has no identified funding source.
4.  **Containment:** The recommended alternative. This plan integrates the stockpiles into the planned SR-132 roadway construction project. Material would be consolidated, capped with clean soil, and contained beneath roadway pavement, retaining walls, and bridge abutments. This eliminates exposure pathways and reduces the threat to groundwater.

The report recommends Alternative 4, with an interim progress phase planned by 2018 and ultimate build-out by 2028. Until this construction begins, Caltrans must continue existing obligations, including quarterly groundwater monitoring, fence maintenance, and vegetative cover upkeep. A subsequent Remedial Action Plan (RAP) will be prepared for public review to detail the final implementation.
