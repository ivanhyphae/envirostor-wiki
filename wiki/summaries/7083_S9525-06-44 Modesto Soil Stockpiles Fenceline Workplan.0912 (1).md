The source document is a Caltrans/Geocon site-specific workplan and health-and-safety package for field investigations at the Modesto Soil Stockpiles near the SR 99/SR 132 interchange in Stanislaus County, California. Its overall purpose is to support the future SR 132 project by filling remaining data gaps and reducing worker exposure during soil sampling and related non-intrusive field activities. The workplan focuses on characterizing the stockpiles’ extent and contaminant migration—especially at the fenceline/perimeter—and on investigating areas with previously elevated cadmium so that the Remedial Action Plan and construction planning can be finalized.

The document summarizes the site’s history as impacted fill associated with former FMC/barium-processing operations, with barium and PAHs identified as the main contaminants of concern and cadmium flagged in some locations. Prior investigations, including the 2003 ISA, 2004 PSI, 2006 groundwater work, 2007 HHRA, and 2009 PEA, are used to frame the current effort. Those earlier studies found elevated barium in stockpile material, localized elevated cadmium, stockpile fill thickness averaging about 25 feet, groundwater first encountered roughly 30–40 feet below grade, and groundwater results that met drinking water standards for the analyzed constituents. The earlier human-health/risk reviews concluded that, under existing controls, the stockpiles did not pose an unacceptable risk to workers, trespassers, or nearby residents.

For the investigation itself, the workplan lays out a 56-boring program: 29 fenceline borings to 5 feet, 22 perimeter borings to 3 feet, and 5 deeper cadmium-targeted borings to 20 feet in Stockpiles 2 and 3. Field reconnaissance, staking, and a joint Geocon-Caltrans-DTSC review were used to refine boring locations before drilling. Sampling will primarily use a Geoprobe direct-push rig, with hand augers where access is limited, and soil will be collected continuously in shallow borings and at set intervals in deeper borings. Samples will be logged, GPS-located, decontaminated, chain-of-custody controlled, and analyzed by a certified lab with expedited turnaround. Analytical targets include Title 22 metals and strontium for fenceline samples, barium for perimeter samples, and barium/cadmium for the deeper targeted borings. The anticipated deliverable is a Supplemental Site Investigation report for agency review and posting.

Because two of the provided section summaries are health-and-safety focused, the combined document also emphasizes hazard control for field crews. It identifies the main occupational risks as traffic and vehicle hazards, underground utilities/electrical hazards, noise, biological hazards, and exposure to contaminated soils. The safety strategy relies on site isolation, utility clearance, traffic control, wet methods for dust suppression, PPE, decontamination, buddy-system practices, tailgate meetings, and emergency response procedures. The plan states that previous industrial hygiene monitoring and Negative Exposure Assessments indicate airborne exposures for representative tasks are below applicable limits for asbestos and airborne lead when these controls are used, so formal medical surveillance is not required for the described work. Overall, the document is primarily a planning and control framework rather than a results report: it contains no new field findings, but it documents the rationale, scope, and safety measures for completing the supplemental soil investigation.
