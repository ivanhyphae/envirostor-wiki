The document is a human health risk assessment update for Caltrans TO-23/TO-38 soil stockpiles in Modesto, California, evaluating current and future risks from contaminated stockpiled fill, nearby construction-zone soils, and shallow groundwater. The stockpiles are composed of historically excavated material from former FMC evaporation ponds, and the main contaminants of potential concern are metals—especially barium, with some PAHs/SVOCs also present. Lead is treated separately using DTSC blood-lead modeling.

The assessment uses EPA/DTSC-style exposure and toxicity methods to identify COPCs based on detection frequency, comparison to background, and screening criteria, then estimates risk for several receptor scenarios: current off-site residents/trespassers, future construction workers, future off-site residents, and a hypothetical groundwater user. Soil pathways include ingestion, dermal contact, and dust inhalation; groundwater pathways include ingestion and dermal contact. Cancer risk is evaluated as excess lifetime cancer risk, and noncancer effects as hazard quotients and hazard indices.

Across the soil datasets, many metals were detected, but most concentrations remained below levels that would imply unacceptable risk. In the 2012 update, additional fenceline and perimeter sampling found no barium exceedances of residential or commercial screening levels, and cadmium was not detected in targeted borings and remained below residential criteria in other samples. Strontium was also far below its residential screening level. In the original HHRA, stockpile surface soil COPCs included barium, chromium, cobalt, copper, lead, mercury, nickel, and some PAHs, with arsenic in one stockpile initially producing a cancer risk above target; however, arsenic was ultimately excluded because the estimated risk was essentially background-equivalent.

Overall, the risk results show that most modeled pathways and receptors do not pose unacceptable cancer or noncancer risks. The update concludes that the newer 2012 data do not materially alter the earlier conclusions, and the 2007 HHRA remains valid for current and anticipated future exposure conditions.
