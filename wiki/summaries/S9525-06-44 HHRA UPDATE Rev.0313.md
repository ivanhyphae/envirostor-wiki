The source document is a Human Health Risk Assessment (HHRA) Update for the Caltrans Modesto Soil Stockpiles (Stockpiles 1–3, located adjacent to SR-99). It validates the findings of the 2007 HHRA, concluding that the stockpiles do not pose significant human health risks under current or plausible future exposure scenarios. The update incorporates new sampling data from September 2012, including 35 fenceline borings, 28 perimeter borings, cadmium-specific borings (97 total soil samples), and bi-monthly groundwater monitoring from March to November 2012 (10 wells). Chemicals of potential concern (COPCs) included metals (notably barium, lead, zinc, copper) and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs). Arsenic was excluded as a COPC for Stockpile #2 surface soil because its estimated cancer risk was approximately equal to background levels, despite a statistically significant difference.

**Methodology:** The assessment used a comparative analysis approach, updating the 2007 HHRA with new soil and groundwater data. Exposure-point concentrations (EPCs) were derived using maximum detected concentrations (MDCs) or 95% upper confidence limits (UCLs) where datasets permitted. Reasonable Maximum Exposure (RME) assumptions were applied, following EPA RAGS equations for ingestion, dermal contact, and inhalation (including wind-borne dust via a particulate emission factor (PEF) model). Toxicity values were sourced from OEHHA, EPA IRIS, and Region 9 PRGs. Lead was assessed separately using the LeadSpread model (or IEUBK model in later summaries) to estimate blood lead levels. Exposure scenarios evaluated included: current resident/trespasser (soil ingestion, dermal, inhalation), future construction worker (combined stockpile data, 0–20 ft depth), future off‑site resident, and a hypothetical shallow groundwater user (ingestion and dermal).

**Results:** All calculated excess lifetime cancer risks (ELCR) and noncancer hazard indices (HI) were below regulatory benchmarks. Specific findings:
- **Stockpile 1:** cancer risk 8E-8, noncancer hazard 0.04.
- **Stockpile 2:** initial cancer risk of 1E-5 driven by arsenic; after recognizing arsenic as background, revised to 1E-7. Noncancer hazard 0.1.
- **Stockpile 3:** no quantifiable carcinogens; noncancer hazard 0.02.
- **Future construction worker (all stockpiles):** cancer risk 9.2E-7, hazard index 0.4.
- **Future off‑site resident:** cancer risk 6E-10, hazard index 0.017.
- **Hypothetical groundwater user:** hazard index 0.9; all metals below MCLs; lead in groundwater (3.4 μg/L) below the action level.
- **Lead blood levels (95th percentile):** for children with typical/pica behavior ranged from 3.4 to 5.0 μg/dL, all below the 10 μg/dL level of concern.
- Strontium was excluded as a COPC due to low relative toxicity; cadmium was not detected in targeted borings.

**Limitations:** The analysis used conservative RME assumptions, often relying on MDCs (which overestimate exposure). For Stockpile 2, a 95% UCL was used, and new barium MDCs in deeper samples (130,000 mg/kg) were elevated, but the 95% UCL (7,556 mg/kg) was lower than the 2007 EPC (72,000 mg/kg). Small sample sizes prevented UCL calculation for lead in Stockpile 1. Other limitations included: an assumed 1:6 chromium VI/III ratio (based on IRIS), assumed 85% vegetative cover (though model used 50% as a conservative default), and the exclusion of short‑duration mowing workers. Groundwater use is hypothetical (no current use), and dust concentrations were modeled, not measured. Some toxicity values (carcinogenic slope factors) were unavailable for many COPCs, limiting the cancer risk assessment.

**Concepts:** Key terms used throughout the HHRA include California Human Health Screening Levels (CHHSLs), Regional Screening Levels (RSLs), maximum detected concentration (MDC), 95% upper confidence level (UCL), exposure-point concentration (EPC), particulate emission factor (PEF), and the LeadSpread/IEUBK blood lead models.

**Conclusion:** The HHRA Update confirms that the Modesto Soil Stockpiles present no unacceptable human health risks under current or future scenarios (resident, construction worker, off‑site resident, or groundwater user), and all risks fall below applicable benchmarks. The document recommends continued management of the stockpiles in accordance with the 2007 HHRA findings.
