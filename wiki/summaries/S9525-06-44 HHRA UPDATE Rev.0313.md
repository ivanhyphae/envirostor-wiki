This document updates a 2007 Human Health Risk Assessment (HHRA) for three soil stockpiles (Stockpiles 1-3) near SR 99 and Kansas Avenue in Modesto, California, which contain soil from former FMC evaporation ponds with elevated barium and other metals. The update incorporates 2012 soil analytical data (from fenceline, perimeter, and stockpile sampling) and 2012 groundwater monitoring data to verify the validity of the earlier conclusions.

The initial 2007 HHRA concluded that chemicals of potential concern (COPCs) did not pose unacceptable health risks. This update confirms those findings, as the 2012 soil and groundwater data are similar to the 2006 data and do not significantly increase cancer risk or noncancer hazard estimates. The Department of Toxic Substances Control (DTSC) had previously determined in December 2009 that the stockpiles as currently managed pose no risk to Caltrans workers, trespassers, or adjacent residents.

The methodology used a conservative, health-protective approach. For most metals, maximum detected concentrations (MDCs) were used as exposure point concentrations (EPCs), likely overestimating risk. For lead, a different model was used. Exposure assessment employed reasonable maximum exposure (RME) assumptions, including using a default 50% vegetation cover in particulate emission models (when actual cover is ~85%) and relying on national demographic data. Chronic toxicity criteria were applied even to short-term construction exposures, adding further conservatism.

Quantitative results showed that for all evaluated scenarios—current offsite resident/trespasser, future construction worker, future offsite resident, and hypothetical groundwater user—the cancer risks and noncancer hazards remained well below acceptable thresholds (e.g., cancer risks below 1E-6, hazard quotients below 1). For example, the highest cancer risk (9.2E-7) and noncancer hazard (0.4) were for the future construction worker scenario. The increased 2012 barium levels (e.g., up to 130,000 mg/kg in stockpiles) did not invalidate the 2007 conclusions.

Key limitations include the use of upper-bound MDCs, the application of chronic toxicity criteria to acute exposures, and the lack of site-specific data for exposure assumptions. Cadmium and strontium were not identified as COPCs, and background arsenic was excluded with negligible uncertainty. The analyses are considered plausible upper-limit risk estimates, with the true risk likely being considerably lower.
