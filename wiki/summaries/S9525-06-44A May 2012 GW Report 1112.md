## Combined Summary of the Source Document "raw/S9525-06-44A May 2012 GW Report 1112.pdf"

This report presents the results of groundwater monitoring conducted on May 17, 2012, at the Caltrans Modesto Soil Stockpiles site (GEOCON Project No. S9525-06-44). The investigation involved sampling eight monitoring wells (MW-1 through MW-8) installed in the Modesto Formation, plus one surface sample and one equipment blank. Field measurements included depth to groundwater, dissolved oxygen, ORP, pH, electrical conductivity, temperature, and turbidity. Wells were purged using a submersible pump (approximately three well volumes), except MW-5 which went dry and required recovery. Samples were collected using disposable bailers with low-flow release tubes and filtered (0.45-micron) for dissolved metals. All samples were chilled and transported under chain-of-custody to Advanced Technology Laboratories (ATL), a California-certified laboratory.

**Analytical methods** included EPA 6020 (ICP-MS) for dissolved metals (including Title 22 metals and strontium), EPA 7470 for dissolved mercury, EPA 8270/SIM for semivolatile organic compounds (SVOCs), EPA 300.0 for anions, SM 2320B for alkalinity, SM 2540C for TDS, and SM 4500 for sulfide. Data were compared to California Primary and Secondary MCLs, Public Health Goals, EPA IRIS Reference Doses, Notification Levels, and Water Quality for Agriculture criteria. Quality control measures included equipment blanks, method blanks, laboratory control samples (LCS), matrix spikes (MS/MSD), and duplicate analyses.

**Key findings**: No SVOCs (including PAHs) were detected above practical quantitation limits (PQLs) in any sample. Dissolved mercury was not detected (PQL 0.20 µg/L). For dissolved metals, barium ranged 55–310 µg/L (threshold 1,000/700 µg/L), strontium 270–1,400 µg/L (threshold 4,000 µg/L), and lead was below detection (1.0 µg/L). Other metals (arsenic, chromium, vanadium, molybdenum, nickel, selenium, antimony, copper, cobalt, manganese) were all below applicable thresholds. Total dissolved solids (TDS) exceeded the secondary MCL (500 mg/L) in MW-4 (540), MW-5 (690), and MW-6 (630). Nitrate as N exceeded the primary MCL (10 mg/L) in MW-1 (12), MW-4 (10), MW-5 (26), and MW-6 (18). Chloride, sulfate, sulfide, and alkalinity were within thresholds.

**Water chemistry**: Groundwater remained bicarbonate-dominant, with both calcium-dominant (MW-1, MW-2, MW-4, MW-5, MW-7) and sodium-dominant (MW-3, MW-6, MW-8) water types present, consistent with prior events. Stiff diagrams (Appendix C) illustrated these major ion relationships. Cation-anion balances were generally within acceptable limits (e.g., MW-6 and MW-5 both 10.0 meq/L).

**Groundwater elevation** ranged from 29.74 ft (MW-1) to 38.25 ft (MW-5) below TOC, with flow direction southeast and an average gradient of 0.0006 ft/ft.

**Limitations and QC issues**: The equipment blank showed detectable antimony, barium, calcium, magnesium, and sodium—concentrations for these constituents are qualified as estimated and potentially less than reported values. Several MS/MSD recoveries for dissolved metals were outside laboratory control limits (exceeded 130% or below 70%), but no concentrations were near water quality thresholds, so data are considered sufficient. Duplicate RPDs for antimony, potassium, selenium, and zinc exceeded 20%, indicating poor precision. Samples MW-1 through MW-4 were received beyond hold time for nitrate; the client instructed the lab to proceed. Well MW-5 went dry during purging, potentially affecting representativeness. One SVOC surrogate recovery (1,2-dichlorobenzene-d4 in the blank and LCS) exceeded acceptance limits and was documented in a corrective action report. Some sample results were flagged E (estimated due to dilution) or M1 (matrix interference for major cations). Despite these issues, the QA/QC data—including LCS recoveries for dissolved metals (90.5–111%) and SVOCs (87.4–97.0%)—support the overall data usability.
