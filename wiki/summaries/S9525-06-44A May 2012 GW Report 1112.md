The May 2012 groundwater report for the Caltrans Modesto Soil Stockpiles site documents the first sampling event under the DTSC-approved 2006 Groundwater Assessment Work Plan. On May 17, 2012, eight monitoring wells (MW-1 through MW-8) were gauged for depth to water, field-measured for DO/ORP, and sampled for dissolved and total metals, mercury, major ions, nitrate, sulfate, sulfide, alkalinity, TDS, and PAHs/SVOCs using standard EPA and SM methods. Samples were collected after purging where possible, filtered for dissolved metals, and analyzed by a California-certified laboratory under chain of custody with accompanying QA/QC review.

Hydrogeologically, groundwater remained in the shallow upper aquifer and flowed generally to the southeast, with an average gradient of about 0.0006. Depth to groundwater ranged from 29.74 ft bgs in MW-1 to 38.25 ft bgs in MW-5. Stiff diagrams indicated two persistent groundwater types across the site: calcium-dominant and sodium-dominant waters, with little change from the earlier March 2012 conditions.

Chemically, the groundwater was dominated by dissolved major ions and alkaline bicarbonate composition. Alkalinity was consistently high, TDS ranged roughly from 280 to 690 mg/L, and major cations such as calcium, magnesium, sodium, and potassium were elevated in several wells. Some constituents exceeded water-quality benchmarks, especially nitrate and TDS in certain wells, while most trace metals were low and PAHs/SVOCs were not detected at or above reporting limits. Reported metals included barium and strontium at comparatively higher concentrations, but lead was below the reporting limit in all wells and arsenic/chromium were present at low microgram-per-liter levels.

Overall QA/QC results supported the validity of the data, with cation-anion balances generally reasonable and laboratory control performance acceptable, though a few matrix spike, surrogate, or duplicate metrics showed minor method-specific exceptions. The report concludes that groundwater quality beneath the site reflects persistent ion-rich chemistry, with limited evidence of organic contamination and only selected inorganic parameters—primarily nitrate and TDS—of potential concern.
