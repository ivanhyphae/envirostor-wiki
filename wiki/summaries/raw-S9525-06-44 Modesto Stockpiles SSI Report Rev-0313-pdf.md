The document is a Supplemental Site Investigation (SSI) Report prepared by Geocon Consultants, Inc., for the California Department of Transportation (Caltrans – District 6), dated revised March 1, 2013. It addresses three soil stockpiles within the Caltrans right-of-way near State Route 132 in Modesto, Stanislaus County. Field activities occurred September 25–28, 2012, and included 35 fenceline borings (FB1–FB35), 28 perimeter borings (PB1–PB28), and 5 deeper cadmium borings (CB1–CB5), with soil samples analyzed by ATL Global. The investigation was conducted under regulatory oversight by DTSC (in consultation with CVRWQCB), who conditionally accepted preliminary findings in a November 2012 meeting and formalized decisions in a February 15, 2013 review letter. DTSC determined that additional assessment of lateral/vertical barium migration was not warranted, that cadmium is not a contaminant of potential concern (COPC), and that the data should be used to update a prior human health risk assessment (HHRA). The report also reviewed historical aerial photographs from 1963 and 1967, confirming that barium-impacted soil was transported and placed within Caltrans right-of-way.

Analytical results showed barium as the primary contaminant, with surface fenceline samples ranging from 140 to 4,300 mg/kg (below the residential CHHSL of 5,200 mg/kg) and decreasing with depth. Perimeter borings had barium from 76 to 1,600 mg/kg, all below residential CHHSL. However, deeper stockpile material (5–19 ft) in cadmium borings contained very high barium concentrations, up to 130,000 mg/kg at CB2-11.0, exceeding the industrial CHHSL of 63,000 mg/kg. Cadmium was not detected above 1.0 mg/kg in any sample, leading the report to assert that prior elevated cadmium results were likely false positives due to barium interference. Other metals (arsenic, lead, zinc, chromium, copper) were generally within background ranges. For organics, a single sample (CB3-9.0) showed diesel-range organics (120 mg/kg) slightly above the residential ESL of 83 mg/kg, but other hydrocarbons and PAHs were below thresholds.

The laboratory data (appendices) documented extensive quality control issues, including numerous matrix spike recoveries outside acceptance limits for barium (often due to extreme concentrations requiring high dilution), but the report concluded the data were sufficient for its purposes. No physical remediation was conducted during this SSI; the findings are intended to support a future Remedial Action Plan (RAP) for the stockpiles, which are currently subject to existing management requirements (no excavation/grading, fence maintenance, groundwater monitoring). Responsible parties include Caltrans (owner/operator), DTSC (lead regulator), CVRWQCB (co-regulator), and Geocon (consultant), with past work by Shaw Environmental referenced.
