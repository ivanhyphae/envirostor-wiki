The Feasibility Study (FS) for the Caltrans Modesto Soil Stockpiles addresses three soil stockpiles (totaling approximately 160,000 yd³) containing elevated barium from former FMC chemical processing operations. While barium is the primary contaminant of potential concern (COPC), with concentrations detected up to 130,000 mg/kg, updated risk assessments indicate the stockpiles do not pose an unacceptable risk to human health under proper current management. The 2007 Human Health Risk Assessment (HHRA) and its 2013 update, concurred with by the Department of Toxic Substances Control (DTSC), found no unacceptable cancer risk (all estimates <1E-6) and no non-cancer hazard (HI <1) for current and future receptors, including offsite residents, trespassers, and construction workers. Previous cadmium detections were determined to be false positives due to barium interference.

The study followed the CERCLA/NCP process, compiling data from multiple site investigations (Shaw 2004 PSI, Shaw 2006 SI, Geocon 2012 SSI) and updating the 2007 HHRA using USEPA methods. Risk was calculated for various receptors using 95% UCLs, and the stockpile material was found to be geotechnically adequate for use as embankment in the planned SR-132 highway construction. Regulatory requirements (ARARs) were identified from federal, state, and local sources, including Porter-Cologne, Title 27/23, and DTSC guidance.

Four remedial alternatives were evaluated against nine criteria, including protection of human health/environment, long-term effectiveness, implementability, and cost:
1.  **No Action**
2.  **Institutional Controls** (~$50,000/yr)
3.  **Removal** (cost-prohibitive at ~$21.5M for full removal, with significant short-term impacts including 529,200 lb CO₂ emissions and ~175 truckloads/day for ~60 days)
4.  **Containment** (via encapsulation within the SR-132 project or a vegetated clean soil cap)

The recommended alternative is **Alternative 4 (Containment)** , as the no-action and institutional controls alternatives are less protective, and full removal is cost-prohibitive with significant short-term impacts. The cleanup goal is performance-based (exposure management) rather than numerical. Key limitations include that the protective conclusions depend strictly on continued proper stockpile management (fencing, vegetative cover), potential groundwater impacts remain a Central Valley Regional Water Quality Control Board (CVRWQCB) concern, and containment does not reduce toxicity or volume, requiring long-term monitoring and maintenance. Regulatory and community acceptance for the final alternative selection are pending.
