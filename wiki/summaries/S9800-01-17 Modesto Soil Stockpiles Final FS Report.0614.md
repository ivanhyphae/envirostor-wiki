## Combined Summary of *raw/S9800-01-17 Modesto Soil Stockpiles Final FS Report.0614.pdf*

This Feasibility Study (FS) examines three soil stockpiles (Stockpiles 1, 2, and 3) in Modesto, California, that contain elevated barium concentrations from historical chemical processing operations. The report evaluates remedial alternatives and concludes that under current management conditions the stockpiles do not pose an unacceptable risk to human health.

### Key Findings
- The 2007 Human Health Risk Assessment (HHRA) and its 2013 update confirm that cancer risks and non‑cancer hazards for current off‑site residents, trespassers, future construction workers, and hypothetical groundwater users are below regulatory thresholds (cancer risk criterion of 1×10⁻⁶; non‑cancer hazard index threshold of 1.0).
- Cadmium was initially identified as a contaminant of potential concern but subsequent investigations (2006 SI and 2012 SSI) showed the original detections were false positives, likely due to sample interference from high barium concentrations; cadmium was eliminated as a COPC.
- Groundwater beneath the site meets drinking water standards, with all contaminants of potential concern below California Maximum Contaminant Levels (MCLs).
- The recommended remedial alternative is containment by capping, to be achieved through the planned construction of the SR‑132 project, where stockpile material will be used as embankment fill behind retaining walls, bridge abutments, and beneath roadway pavements.

### Methodology
Site characterization was carried out through multiple investigations from 2004 to 2012. The 2004 Preliminary Site Investigation (PSI) used 50 direct‑push borings; the 2006 Site Investigation (SI) included 51 borings with 165 stockpile and 89 native soil samples; the 2012 Supplemental Site Investigation (SSI) added 68 soil borings at fenceline, perimeter, and cadmium‑targeted locations. Soil was analyzed for Title 22 metals, PAHs, nitrate, pH, and soluble barium via WET and DI‑WET tests. Groundwater monitoring employed eight wells installed in 2006 plus two upgradient wells added in 2012, sampled bi‑monthly from March 2012 to March 2013 and quarterly thereafter. Stormwater samples were collected from constructed impoundments during qualifying rain events.

The human health risk assessment evaluated exposure pathways (incidental ingestion, dermal contact, dust inhalation) for current off‑site residents/trespassers, future construction workers, future off‑site residents, and a hypothetical shallow groundwater user. Remedial alternatives were screened against effectiveness, implementability, and cost, and four were retained for detailed evaluation under the first seven of nine National Contingency Plan (NCP) criteria.

### Results
- **Barium concentrations** (maximum detected) varied widely: Stockpile 1 up to 1,730 mg/kg (2004) and 130 mg/kg (2006); Stockpile 2 up to 60,700 mg/kg (2004) and 64,000 mg/kg (2006); Stockpile 3 up to 44,900 mg/kg (2004) and 72,000 mg/kg (2006). Fenceline samples in 2012 SSI showed up to 4,300 mg/kg at surface, decreasing to 42–680 mg/kg at depth. Cadmium was not detected above 1.0 mg/kg in any of 348 samples from the 2006 SI and 2012 SSI.
- **Cancer risk estimates** (2007 HHRA) were below the 1×10⁻⁶ criterion for all scenarios when arsenic was excluded (the main driver for the trespasser scenario was arsenic, which had background concentrations similar to stockpile levels). Non‑cancer hazard indices were all below 1.0.
- **Groundwater**: Depth to groundwater ranged from 31.73 to 40.11 feet below TOC (June 2013); flow direction was east‑southeast. One stormwater sample (SW03) showed barium at 2,000 µg/L, exceeding the MCL of 1,000 µg/L, but this was an isolated event confined to the Caltrans right‑of‑way.
- **Stockpile volumes**: Stockpile 1 ~34,000 yd³; Stockpile 2 ~102,000 yd³; Stockpile 3 ~24,000 yd³.
- **Alternative costs** (in 2013 dollars over a 30‑year period):
  - Alternative 1 (No Action): no cost, least protective.
  - Alternative 2 (Institutional Controls): ~$495,000 (management, fence maintenance, monitoring).
  - Alternative 3 (Removal): ~$21,480,000 (excavation, off‑site disposal, clean fill replacement; includes ~$2.65 million for optional removal of Stockpile 3 alone).
  - Alternative 4 (Containment by capping with SR‑132 project): $1,570,000 (consolidation, grading, clean soil cap, paving, revegetation).
  - Alternative 4 (Containment with clean soil cap, independent of SR‑132): $1,335,000.
- Soil washing and soil mixing were screened out due to high cost and technical difficulties; off‑site non‑landfill placement was not implementable because the soil contains hazardous waste.

### Limitations
- The 2004 PSI cadmium data were unreliable, likely due to sample interference from high barium, and were not reproducible by three different laboratories.
- The 2012 SSI assessed only surface and shallow subsurface (3–5 feet) at fenceline and perimeter locations, not deeper stockpile material.
- Stormwater monitoring was limited to qualifying rain events; the one elevated barium sample was isolated and confined to the Caltrans right‑of‑way.
- The HHRA update relied on maximum detected concentrations (rather than 95% UCLs) for some scenarios, providing conservative estimates.
- DTSC noted that if stockpiles are graded or reworked, elevated concentrations at depth would require re‑evaluation for potential exposure to adjacent residents.
- Background COPC concentrations (e.g., arsenic) were similar to stockpile concentrations, complicating risk attribution.
- Title 22 evaluation criteria for barium may not be strictly applicable because the primary barium compound is barite (barium sulfate), not non‑barite compounds.
- Institutional controls do not reduce toxicity or volume of COPCs and may not deter all trespassers; the no‑action alternative assumes Caltrans would discontinue maintenance, whereas actual conditions include active management.

### Concepts
The report employs a range of regulatory and technical concepts: CERCLA and NCP frameworks, Applicable or Relevant and Appropriate Requirements (ARARs), To Be Considered (TBC) criteria, remedial action objectives (RAOs), general response actions (GRAs), institutional controls, containment/capping, removal, treatment (soil washing/soil mixing), reuse as embankment fill, human health risk assessment (HHRA), exposure‑point concentrations (EPCs), upper confidence limits (UCLs), waste extraction test (WET), soluble threshold limit concentration (STLC), California Human Health Screening Levels (CHHSLs), maximum contaminant levels (MCLs), stormwater and groundwater monitoring, fugitive dust regulations (San Joaquin Valley Unified Air Pollution Control District Rule 8021), and greenhouse gas emissions from trucking. The recommended strategy balances cost, implementability, and human health protectiveness by using the stockpiles for their originally intended purpose—construction of the SR‑132 roadway—while capping and institutional controls ensure long‑term containment.
