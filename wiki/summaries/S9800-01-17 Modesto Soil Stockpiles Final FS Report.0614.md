The report is a Final Feasibility Study under CERCLA/NCP for three Caltrans soil stockpiles in Modesto, California, prepared in support of the SR-132 West Freeway/Expressway Project. It synthesizes prior investigations, soil and groundwater sampling, geotechnical work, and human-health risk assessments to determine how the stockpiled soil should be managed. The central finding is that the stockpiles contain elevated barium, with minor or isolated detections of other constituents, including some suspected false-positive cadmium results in early data; however, the overall site conditions do not present an unacceptable human-health risk under current access controls or the reasonably anticipated future land uses evaluated in the HHRA. Because the contaminant concentrations in later sampling were similar to those used in the original risk assessment, the earlier risk conclusions remained valid.

To support remedy selection, the study reviews applicable regulatory requirements and compares remedial alternatives using standard CERCLA criteria: overall protection, compliance with ARARs, long-term effectiveness, reduction of toxicity/mobility/volume, short-term effectiveness, implementability, cost, and regulatory/community acceptance. The alternatives considered include no action, institutional controls, removal, and containment/capping, along with screened-out treatment and reuse options such as soil washing, mixing, and offsite reuse or non-landfill placement. No action was judged least protective because it leaves contaminant mass in place and does not address potential surface- or groundwater impacts. Institutional controls can be protective only if fencing, vegetation, access restrictions, and monitoring are maintained. Full removal would provide the greatest risk reduction, but at very high cost and with substantial short-term impacts such as truck traffic, dust, diesel emissions, and greenhouse gas emissions. Treatment and reuse options were not retained because they were not practical or cost-effective for the volume and characteristics of the stockpiled soil.

The preferred remedy is containment, specifically use of the stockpiled soil as engineered embankment/backfill within the SR-132 project, with grading, capping, or other containment measures as needed. If the freeway project is not implemented, a standalone clean-soil cap with vegetative cover is the alternative containment approach. This remedy is favored because it isolates the soil, reduces exposure pathways, is compatible with planned construction, and balances protectiveness with implementability and cost. The report also identifies a performance-based cleanup goal: rather than meeting a single numerical removal target, the remedy should maintain conditions that prevent human exposure to COPCs and minimize potential groundwater impact.
