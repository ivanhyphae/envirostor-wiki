The document reports the March 18, 2013 groundwater monitoring event for the Caltrans Modesto Soil Stockpiles site in Stanislaus County, conducted under DTSC-approved work-plan procedures using the full monitoring network of wells MW-1 through MW-10. Field staff measured groundwater levels and field parameters, inspected wells for tampering, and collected samples after purging most wells by about three well volumes; MW-5 and MW-6 went dry during purging and were sampled after recovery. Samples were analyzed by ATL under chain of custody for dissolved metals, major ions, alkalinity, TDS, sulfide, mercury, and related QA/QC checks, and the data were considered usable.

Hydrogeologically, groundwater is present in the shallow upper aquifer and generally flows east-southeast, consistent with historical conditions. Water-level measurements in March 2013 showed depths to groundwater of roughly 31 to 40 ft below ground surface and groundwater elevations around the upper 40s ft MSL, supporting a site-wide flow pattern rather than a localized anomaly. Stiff diagrams and chemistry results indicate two recurring groundwater facies across the wells: calcium-dominant and sodium-dominant water types, both overall bicarbonate-dominant.

Chemically, the groundwater showed moderate mineralization but few exceedances of drinking-water benchmarks. Major ions and alkalinity were present at measurable levels, with bicarbonate dominating the alkalinity and TDS varying across wells from the low hundreds up to about 700 mg/L in the laboratory results. Trace metals such as barium, lead, strontium, arsenic, chromium, and vanadium were detected, but generally below applicable standards. Mercury was consistently non-detect. The main recurring issues were nitrate as nitrogen and TDS, with occasional manganese exceedances. Overall, the results suggest generally coherent analytical quality and site-specific groundwater quality impacts without evidence of a single sharply defined contaminant plume.

Based on the monitoring findings, the report recommended reducing the monitoring frequency from bi-monthly to quarterly and redeveloping MW-5 because of poor purging behavior.
