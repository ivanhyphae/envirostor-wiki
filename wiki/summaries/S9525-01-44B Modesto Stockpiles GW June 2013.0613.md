The document reports groundwater monitoring near Caltrans Modesto soil stockpiles in Stanislaus County, California, based on data from ten monitoring wells (MW‑1 through MW‑10) sampled between 2006 and 2013. The study evaluates compliance with California Title 22 standards, focusing on dissolved metals, general minerals, and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs). Samples were collected using submersible pumps or disposable bailers after purging, and field parameters (pH, conductivity, temperature, DO, turbidity) were recorded. Analytical methods included EPA 6020 (ICP‑MS) for dissolved metals, EPA 7470 for mercury, EPA 300.0 for anions, SM 2320B for alkalinity, SM 2540C for TDS, SM 4500‑S²⁻ for sulfide, and EPA 8270C SIM for PAHs.

Overall, most dissolved metal concentrations were below California Maximum Contaminant Levels (MCLs) or health advisories. For example, arsenic ranged from <0.20 to 2.2 µg/L, barium from 39 to 160 µg/L, chromium from <0.50 to 6.9 µg/L, lead from <1.0 to 2.0 µg/L, and zinc from <10 to 250 µg/L. All 16 priority PAHs were below detection limits (<0.20 µg/L). However, during the June 2013 sampling event, nitrate (as N) exceeded the primary MCL of 10 mg/L in wells MW‑1, MW‑5, MW‑6, MW‑9, and MW‑10, and total dissolved solids (TDS) exceeded the secondary MCL of 500 mg/L in MW‑1, MW‑5, MW‑6, and MW‑10. In contrast, overall nitrate levels across all years ranged from <0.010 to 0.52 mg/L, indicating that exceedances were specific to that event. For MW‑1 and MW‑2 in June 2013, detailed certificate of analysis data showed a balanced cation‑anion ratio (5.6 meq/L for MW‑1), with major cations including calcium (82,000 µg/L in MW‑1), magnesium, sodium, and strontium; TDS was 350 mg/L in MW‑1. MW‑2 showed nitrate at 6.1 mg/L and bicarbonate alkalinity at 230 mg/L as CaCO₃. Stiff diagrams visualized major ion chemistry.

Quality control included split samples submitted to the regulatory board and laboratory reporting limits. Some results were qualified as non‑detect due to equipment blank contamination, and several matrix spike recoveries fell outside acceptable limits (noted as M1). Many samples required dilution due to high analyte concentrations (noted as D6). Method detection limits were not reported for most analytes. Data prior to March 2012 were reproduced from a previous report (Shaw Environmental, 2007). Overall, the monitoring supports ongoing compliance evaluation, with the June 2013 event highlighting localized exceedances of nitrate and TDS that warrant attention.
