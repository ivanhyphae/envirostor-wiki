The report summarizes groundwater monitoring at the Caltrans Modesto Soil Stockpiles site in Stanislaus County, California, conducted under the DTSC-approved Final Work Plan. The June 5–6, 2013 event sampled 10 monitoring wells (MW-1 through MW-10) to evaluate groundwater quality beneath and adjacent to the three soil stockpiles, while also incorporating earlier 2006 and 2012 data for comparison. Field measurements included groundwater levels plus pH, temperature, conductivity, dissolved oxygen, ORP, and turbidity; samples were purged, collected with disposable bailers, filtered for dissolved metals, and analyzed by a certified laboratory under chain of custody. Analytical methods covered Title 22 dissolved metals, mercury, major ions, alkalinity, TDS, sulfide, and related geochemical checks, with QA/QC review of blanks, spikes, duplicates, and control samples. GeoTracker submittals were made to the State Water Resources Control Board.

Hydrogeologically, the site overlies a shallow upper aquifer in the Modesto Formation. June 2013 water levels indicated groundwater flow to the east-southeast, with an average gradient of about 0.0005, consistent with historical conditions. Sampling conditions were generally routine, though MW-5 and MW-6 went dry during purging and required re-purging; MW-5 was redeveloped with a surge block.

Overall groundwater quality showed moderate to elevated mineralization, with two broad water types reflected in Stiff diagrams: calcium-dominant and sodium-dominant waters, all bicarbonate-dominant. TDS and other dissolved salts were elevated in a relative sense, but most constituents remained within or near applicable drinking-water benchmarks. Chloride, sulfate, and nitrate were the main recurring mineral parameters of concern in the broader dataset, while dissolved metals were generally low. In the June 2013 event, metals such as barium and strontium were similar to historical levels and remained below applicable numeric thresholds, with arsenic and chromium also detected at low concentrations. Typical metal results included barium at 45–230 µg/L, strontium at 250–1,000 µg/L, arsenic at 2.0–4.8 µg/L, and chromium at 0.77–7.5 µg/L. For the detailed MW-2 example, mercury was nondetect, TDS was 350 mg/L, chloride was 11 mg/L, nitrate as N was 6.1 mg/L, sulfate was 11 mg/L, and the cation-anion balance was essentially closed. QA/QC generally met method requirements, and the report concludes that quarterly monitoring would continue, with the next event scheduled for September 2013.
