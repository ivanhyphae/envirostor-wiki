The document is a combined remedial design, health-and-safety, and soil-management framework for the Caltrans Modesto Soil Stockpiles project at the SR-132 West/SR-99 interchange in Modesto, California. It describes the DTSC-approved interim remedy for Stockpiles 1–3 as **containment rather than off-site removal**: barium-containing soils, along with eligible aerially deposited lead (ADL)-impacted soils, will be excavated, consolidated, reused as highway embankment fill where allowed, and then covered by pavement and/or clean fill to prevent direct exposure and reduce migration to groundwater and surface water. A separate RDIP is planned for Ultimate Phase 2.

The technical basis for the plan comes from earlier site investigations, feasibility and remedial action planning, groundwater and surface water monitoring, and human health risk assessments. The RDIP/HSP lays out construction-phase implementation details such as site preparation, well protection/abandonment, excavation and transport, verification sampling, stormwater management, dust suppression, air monitoring, and post-construction maintenance. Verification sampling for barium and lead uses EPA Method 6010B, with defined sample densities tied to excavation area, and compliance is measured against removal thresholds and air action levels using real-time particulate monitoring plus laboratory analysis.

The plan reports the scale of the stockpiles and the intended reuse capacity: Stockpile 1 is about 34,000 yd³, Stockpile 2 about 102,000 yd³, and Stockpile 3 about 24,000 yd³. Estimated barium-containing soil removal is about 27,850 yd³, while the consolidation area can hold about 54,500 yd³, leaving room for roughly 26,650 yd³ of ADL-impacted soil. The relevant removal threshold for barium is 1,000 mg/kg, and lead-related reuse is governed by DTSC conditions.

A broader statewide companion document establishes a DTSC-Caltrans Soil Management Agreement for ADL-contaminated soils in California highway rights-of-way. That agreement replaces earlier variance-based oversight with a more structured review and reporting system for future Caltrans projects, while supporting continued reuse of eligible soils in highway projects instead of off-site hazardous waste disposal. Its technical rationale is that lead in these soils is generally strongly bound and has low groundwater leaching potential. Based on 595 contaminated soil samples, it reports a mean Kd of 333 L/kg for northern California soils, well above the immobility benchmark of 20. The agreement sets tiered soil management thresholds, including reference values of 80 mg/kg total lead and 5 mg/L extractable lead, and progressively more protective cover requirements as lead concentrations increase.

The health and safety material in the Modesto RDIP concludes that the project poses low risk when standard controls are used. It identifies key contaminants of concern as arsenic, cadmium, inorganic lead, chromium VI, and barium sulfate, and relies on wet sampling methods, dust suppression, PPE, decontamination, and perimeter air monitoring to keep exposures low. Prior monitoring during Stockpile 3 excavation/loading found airborne lead and barium well below applicable action levels, and September 2012 data showed exposures an order of magnitude below relevant limits. Based on these findings, the document treats the risk to workers and nearby communities as acceptably controlled and does not support additional medical surveillance beyond the planned safety measures.
