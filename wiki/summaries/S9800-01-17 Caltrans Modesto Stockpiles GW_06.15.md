Based on the six section summaries, the source document “raw/S9800-01-17 Caltrans Modesto Stockpiles GW_06.15.pdf” presents the results of groundwater monitoring conducted in April 2015 at the Caltrans Modesto Soil Stockpiles site in Stanislaus County, California. The study is part of a long-term monitoring program (2006–2015) involving 10 monitoring wells (MW‑1 through MW‑10), but during the April 30, 2015 sampling event only 4 wells (MW‑1, MW‑2, MW‑4, and MW‑7) contained sufficient water for purging and sampling; the remaining wells were dry or had insufficient recharge.

Field methods included depth‑to‑water measurements, purging three well volumes with disposable bailers, and monitoring pH, electrical conductivity, and temperature. Samples were collected using disposable bailers, decanted into laboratory‑provided containers, and dissolved‑metal samples were field‑filtered (0.45‑micron). Laboratory analyses were performed by Advanced Technology Laboratories (ATL) using EPA methods for Title 22 dissolved metals (EPA 6020/7470) and general minerals (EPA 300.0, SM 2540C, SM 2320B), with quality control measures including blanks, laboratory control samples (LCS), duplicates, and matrix spikes/matrix spike duplicates. Stiff diagrams were constructed from major cation and anion data to characterize ionic composition.

Key results showed that no dissolved metal concentrations exceeded numeric water quality thresholds (primary/secondary MCLs or health advisories). Barium (100–150 µg/L), strontium (760–1,300 µg/L), chromium (5.5–7.1 µg/L), and vanadium (21–25 µg/L) were detected in all four sampled wells but remained below respective thresholds. Other metals (arsenic, manganese, molybdenum, nickel) were also below MCLs. For general minerals, nitrate‑N exceeded the primary MCL of 10 mg/L in MW‑1 (17 mg/L) and MW‑2 (12 mg/L), while total dissolved solids (TDS) exceeded the secondary MCL of 500 mg/L in MW‑1 (660 mg/L) and MW‑4 (550 mg/L). Sulfide concentrations ranged from 0.015 to 0.13 mg/L. Stiff diagrams indicated a calcium‑dominant, bicarbonate‑rich water type with minor temporal variations, consistent across all sampled wells. Groundwater flow was generally south‑southeast at an average gradient of 0.004, based on elevation contours from depth‑to‑water measurements (36.85 ft to 41.26 ft below top of casing).

Limitations included the inability to sample six of the ten wells due to dry conditions (MW‑3, MW‑5, MW‑8, MW‑9, MW‑10) or insufficient water (MW‑6), which reduced temporal and spatial coverage. Quality control data showed some matrix spike recoveries for dissolved metals (e.g., magnesium, sodium, calcium) fell outside acceptable limits (75–125%), indicating potential matrix interference, but laboratory control samples validated the batch; no data qualification was required. Nitrate in upgradient well MW‑1 may reflect natural background rather than site impact. Additionally, PAH analysis was discontinued after November 2012 per DTSC concurrence.

Overall, the document confirms that for the sampled wells, dissolved metals and most general minerals complied with regulatory standards, though nitrate and TDS exceeded MCLs in specific wells. The groundwater is characterized as calcium‑bicarbonate type, flowing southward. The data are supported by detailed laboratory reports and Stiff diagrams, with limitations primarily due to well dryness and minor matrix effects.
