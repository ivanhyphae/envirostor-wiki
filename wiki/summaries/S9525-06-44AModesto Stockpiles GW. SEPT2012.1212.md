The September 2012 groundwater monitoring report for the Caltrans Modesto Soil Stockpiles (S9525-06-44A) documents a DTSC-approved sampling event conducted by Geocon to evaluate groundwater quality around three stockpiles of excavated soil associated with historical FMC/Barium Products disposal areas. The monitoring network included 10 wells, with different subsets sampled for field and laboratory analysis during September 19–20, 2012. Field work involved measuring groundwater levels and water-quality indicators, purging wells, and collecting groundwater with disposable bailers; laboratory analyses were performed under chain-of-custody using standard EPA and SM methods for dissolved and total metals, major ions, alkalinity, TDS, sulfide, mercury, and semivolatile organic compounds including PAHs.

Overall, the hydrogeology indicates shallow groundwater flow to the southeast in a low-gradient system that appears similar to the adjacent FMC site. Water chemistry shows mineralized groundwater with elevated major cations and anions, moderate to high TDS, and recurring nitrate/chloride/sulfate presence. Most dissolved metals and general inorganic constituents were below applicable drinking-water, public-health, or agricultural screening thresholds, and no PAHs or other SVOCs were detected above reporting limits in the sampled wells; mercury was also nondetect. The main recurring issues remain nitrate and TDS exceedances, with manganese exceeding thresholds in two wells during this event.

Specific analytical results show strong spatial variability but a generally consistent mineralized signature. For example, MW-2 had chloride of 13 mg/L, nitrate as N of 7.3 mg/L, sulfate of 14 mg/L, alkalinity of 250 mg/L as CaCO3, and TDS of 390 mg/L, with low dissolved metals such as arsenic, chromium, nickel, vanadium, and strontium. MW-5 showed higher mineralization, including nitrate as N of 22 mg/L, sulfate of 26 mg/L, alkalinity of 390 mg/L as CaCO3, TDS of 590 mg/L, and elevated major cations; dissolved barium and strontium were present but remained below their applicable thresholds, and mercury was nondetect. MW-6 and MW-10 likewise showed highly mineralized groundwater dominated by sodium, calcium, magnesium, bicarbonate, chloride, and sulfate, with low-to-moderate dissolved metals and nondetect mercury/SVOCs. Some wells were difficult to sample because of low yield or dryness, and turbidity varied, but the analytical batch met general QC expectations even though several matrix spike recoveries were outside limits and were attributed to matrix interference rather than method failure.
