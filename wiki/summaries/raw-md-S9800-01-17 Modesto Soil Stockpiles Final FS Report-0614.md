### Combined Summary: Final Feasibility Study, Caltrans Modesto Soil Stockpiles

**Document Identity**
This Final Feasibility Study (FS) Report for the Caltrans Modesto Soil Stockpiles site (SR-132, Stanislaus County, CA) was prepared by Geocon Consultants, Inc., on behalf of the California Department of Transportation (Caltrans). Finalized in June 2014, the document evaluates remedial alternatives for soil stockpiles contaminated from former FMC chemical processing operations (1930s–1970s), which involved barium sulfate/celestite processing and tailings ponds.

**Key Claims**
Barium is the primary contaminant of potential concern (COPC), found predominantly as barite (barium sulfate) at concentrations up to 130,000 mg/kg in stockpile soil. However, the document claims that current stockpile management poses no unacceptable human health risk to workers, trespassers, or nearby residents. Human health risk assessments (HHRA) found cancer risks below 1E-6 and non-cancer hazards below 1 for all exposure scenarios, including offsite residents and future construction workers. Groundwater meets all California Maximum Contaminant Levels (MCLs), and the stockpile soil is geotechnically adequate for use as embankment fill.

**Regulatory Decisions**
The FS was prepared under a June 22, 2012, Interagency Agreement between Caltrans, the Department of Toxic Substances Control (DTSC), and the Central Valley Regional Water Quality Control Board (CVRWQCB). DTSC (2009) accepted the Preliminary Endangerment Assessment, requiring continued access control and monitoring. DTSC and CVRWQCB accept Alternative 2 (Institutional Controls) as a short-term measure, while Alternative 4 (Containment) is anticipated for regulatory approval as the long-term remedy.

**Analytical Results**
Barium concentrations in stockpile soil reached 72,000 mg/kg (Stockpile 3, 2006) and 130,000 mg/kg (Cadmium Borings, 2012), while fenceline surface soils ranged 140–4,300 mg/kg. Of 33 soil samples, 28 exceeded the Title 22 Soluble Threshold Limit Concentration (STLC) of 100 mg/L for barium (WET method). Cadmium detections from 2004 were determined to be false positives. Groundwater monitoring (2006, 2012–2013) found all constituents below California MCLs, with lead (max 3.4 µg/L) below the federal action level of 15 µg/L. Diesel-range organics (120 mg/kg) exceeded the ESL of 83 mg/kg in one boring.

**Remedial Actions**
Four remedial alternatives were evaluated:
- **Alternative 1: No Action** – Discontinue all management.
- **Alternative 2: Institutional Controls** – Maintain fencing, signage, vegetative cover, groundwater/stormwater monitoring ($495,000).
- **Alternative 3: Removal** – Excavation, transport, and disposal of 216,000 tons of material ($21,480,000).
- **Alternative 4: Containment/Capping** (Recommended) – Encapsulate stockpiles using the planned SR-132 roadway infrastructure (roadways, retaining walls, bridge abutments) with a clean soil cap and synthetic liner in medians. Interim phased progress by 2018, ultimate build-out by 2028 ($1,570,000). Optional removal of Stockpile 3 ($2,649,000) if necessary.

**Responsible Parties**
- **Caltrans** – Owner/operator and responsible party.
- **DTSC** – Lead regulatory oversight.
- **Central Valley RWQCB** – Co-regulator.
- **City of Modesto** – Permit authority (grading/erosion control).
- **San Joaquin Valley Unified Air Pollution Control District** – Fugitive dust regulation.

**Key Terms and Conditions**
The selected remedy must comply with ARARs, including Porter-Cologne Water Quality Control Act, Title 27/Title 23 CCR, and local codes. Post-remedial groundwater monitoring is required for at least three years. Excavation exceeding 350 cubic yards requires a City of Modesto grading permit, and air monitoring must be conducted during earthmoving. The removal alternative's greenhouse gas emissions were estimated at 529,200 pounds CO₂.
