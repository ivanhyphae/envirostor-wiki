The September 2013 groundwater monitoring report for the Caltrans Modesto Soil Stockpiles site in Stanislaus County presents sampling and laboratory results from all ten monitoring wells (MW-1 through MW-10) collected under DTSC-approved protocols. Fieldwork was completed on September 4–5, 2013 and included groundwater-level measurements, field parameter readings, well purging, and sample collection for dissolved metals, major ions, alkalinity, sulfide, total dissolved solids (TDS), and mercury; PAH analysis had been discontinued earlier with DTSC concurrence. The analytical data were generated by Atl/ATL Global using standard EPA and Standard Methods procedures, with extensive QA/QC including blanks, duplicates, matrix spikes, and laboratory control samples that generally met acceptance criteria, supporting the reliability of the results.

Hydrogeologically, the groundwater occurs in a shallow upper aquifer flowing generally southeast, with measured groundwater depths ranging from about 33.74 to 42.05 ft below top of casing and an average hydraulic gradient of 0.0007. The chemistry indicates two main water types across the site—calcium-dominant and sodium-dominant—with only slight compositional changes since monitoring resumed in 2012. Overall groundwater is mineralized, with elevated dissolved solids and major ions in several wells, especially MW-1, MW-4, MW-5, and MW-6.

In terms of water quality, dissolved metals were generally low and below applicable drinking-water thresholds. Barium ranged from 36 to 320 µg/L, strontium from 260 to 1,200 µg/L, and other metals such as arsenic, chromium, nickel, molybdenum, and vanadium were detected in some samples but not at levels highlighted as exceedances in the summary. Mercury was nondetect in the reported wells. The site’s recurring chemicals of concern remain barium, lead, and strontium, though none exceeded their thresholds in this event. The principal exceedances in groundwater continue to be nitrate and TDS: nitrate exceeded the primary MCL in several wells, while TDS exceeded the secondary MCL in several wells. Major-ion chemistry showed chloride, nitrate, sulfate, and alkalinity varying by well, with TDS ranging roughly from 350 to 750 mg/L in the September 2013 samples and sulfide generally nondetect or very low.
