## Combined Summary of "raw/S9525-01-44C Modesto Stockpiles GW September 2013 1013.pdf"

This document presents the results of the September 2013 groundwater monitoring event at the Caltrans Modesto Soil Stockpiles site in Stanislaus County, California. The monitoring was conducted in accordance with a 2006 Final Work Plan approved by the California Department of Toxic Substances Control. The primary goals were to assess concentrations of key chemical constituents against water quality thresholds, characterize groundwater flow and chemistry, and evaluate potential impacts from the stockpiles.

### Key Findings
None of the reported dissolved metals concentrations exceeded their respective numeric thresholds. The primary chemicals of concern—barium and strontium—were detected at levels similar to historical data, with barium reaching a maximum of 320 µg/l (well MW-5) compared to the MCL of 1,000 µg/l, and strontium reaching a maximum of 1,200 µg/l (well MW-1) compared to the EPA Health Advisory of 4,000 µg/l. Lead was not detected above the practical quantitation limit (1.0 µg/l) in any well. However, nitrate and total dissolved solids (TDS) consistently exceeded water quality thresholds in wells MW-1, MW-4, MW-5, MW-6, and MW-10. Notably, the upgradient well MW-1 exhibited nitrate at 12 mg/l (above the primary MCL of 10 mg/l) and TDS at 590 mg/l (above the secondary MCL of 500 mg/l), suggesting that these elevated concentrations may represent natural background conditions rather than impacts from the site. Groundwater flow was generally toward the southeast, as observed in previous events. Two distinct groundwater types were identified via Stiff diagrams: calcium-dominant (wells MW-1, MW-2, MW-4, MW-7, MW-9, MW-10) and sodium-dominant (wells MW-3, MW-6, MW-8), with MW-5 shifting from calcium- to sodium-dominant during this event, possibly indicating water mixing.

### Methodology
Depth-to-groundwater measurements and water samples were collected on September 4–5, 2013 from ten monitoring wells (MW-1 through MW-10). Wells were purged of approximately three well volumes using a submersible pump or disposable bailer until field parameters (pH, EC, temperature, turbidity) stabilized. Samples were collected with disposable bailers, and dissolved metals samples were field-filtered (0.45-micron). Laboratory analyses were performed by Advanced Technology Laboratories (ATL), a California-certified facility. Methods included: Title 22 dissolved metals and strontium by EPA Method 6020/7470; dissolved calcium, magnesium, potassium, and sodium by EPA Method 6020; chloride, nitrate (as N), and sulfate by EPA Method 300.0; sulfide by SM 4500; TDS by SM 2540C; alkalinity species by SM 2320B; and dissolved mercury by EPA 7470A. Quality control measures included method blanks, laboratory control samples (LCS), matrix spikes, matrix spike duplicates (MS/MSD), and sample duplicates, with acceptance criteria generally 80–120% recovery and RPD ≤20%. Groundwater elevations were calculated from surveyed top-of-casing reference points, and flow direction was determined from contour maps. Results were compared to California Primary and Secondary MCLs and EPA Drinking Water Health Advisories.

### Results
**Groundwater Elevation and Flow:** Depth to water ranged from 33.74 ft (MW-1) to 42.05 ft (MW-5). Flow direction was southeast with an average gradient of 0.0007 ft/ft.

**Dissolved Metals:** Barium concentrations ranged from 36 µg/l (MW-3) to 320 µg/l (MW-5); all remained below the threshold of 1,000 µg/l. Strontium ranged from 260 µg/l (MW-3) to 1,200 µg/l (MW-1); all below the 4,000 µg/l HA. Lead was not detected in any sample. Other metals—arsenic (1.4–5.8 µg/l), chromium (1.2–9.4 µg/l), and vanadium (19–38 µg/l)—were detected in all wells but were below their respective thresholds (10, 50, and 50 µg/l). Dissolved mercury was not detected in any sample (PQL 0.20 µg/l).

**General Minerals:** Nitrate (as N) ranged from 2.5 mg/l (MW-3) to 36 mg/l (MW-5). Wells MW-1, MW-4, MW-5, MW-6, and MW-10 met or exceeded the primary MCL of 10 mg/l. TDS ranged from 300 mg/l (MW-3) to 750 mg/l (MW-5), with the same five wells exceeding the secondary MCL of 500 mg/l. Sulfide was detected in several wells at low concentrations (up to 0.28 mg/l in MW-1). Major cation data showed calcium concentrations ranging from 20,000 µg/l (MW-3) to 99,000 µg/l (MW-1), and sodium from 15,000 µg/l (MW-1) to 130,000 µg/l (MW-6).

**Groundwater Chemistry:** Stiff diagrams (presented in Appendix C) confirmed bicarbonate-dominant water with two major compositions: calcium-dominant (MW-1, MW-2, MW-4, MW-7, MW-9, MW-10) and sodium-dominant (MW-3, MW-5, MW-6, MW-8).

### Quality Control and Limitations
Quality control results were generally acceptable, with LCS recoveries within limits for most analytes. However, several limitations were noted. Multiple matrix spike recoveries exceeded acceptance limits (e.g., barium, calcium, sodium, strontium), flagged as "M1" and attributed to sample matrix effects; the data was validated by laboratory control samples and deemed sufficient for the report. Some analytes required dilution due to high concentrations (flag "D6"), which may affect sensitivity for low-level analytes. A few duplicate RPD values exceeded 20% (flag "R"), primarily for low-concentration analytes. One chloride matrix spike had recovery 77.7% (below the 80% lower limit) but was accepted per lab note. Additionally, the shift of MW-5 from calcium-dominant to sodium-dominant during this event is not fully explained and may indicate transient conditions or mixing, warranting further investigation.
