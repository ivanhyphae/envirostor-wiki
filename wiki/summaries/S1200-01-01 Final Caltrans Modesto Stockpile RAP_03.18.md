### Executive Summary

This document details a Remedial Action Plan (RAP) for three soil stockpiles (Stockpiles #1, #2, and #3) in Modesto, California, which originated from the former FMC facility. The primary contaminant of potential concern (COPC) is barium, with other metals and PAHs also present. A 2007 Human Health Risk Assessment (HHRA) and a 2013 update concluded that the stockpiles do not pose an unacceptable health risk to current or future receptors, provided management controls (such as fencing and vegetative cover) are maintained.

The selected and recommended remedial alternative is **containment by capping (Alternative 4)**. This option involves incorporating the stockpile soil as fill within the planned SR-132/SR-99 interchange embankments or beneath a clean soil cap. A land use covenant will be required to restrict future use to transportation-related activities. This alternative is preferred because it is effective, technically feasible, and cost-effective (estimated at $1.57 million to $1.34 million), leveraging the existing schedule and funding of the SR-132 Project. In contrast, the removal and offsite disposal alternative (Alternative 3) would cost approximately $21.5 million with no available funding source.

**Methodology** involved extensive site characterization, including multiple rounds of soil borings and the installation of groundwater monitoring wells. The HHRA evaluated cancer risk and non-cancer hazard indices for multiple exposure scenarios (e.g., offsite resident, construction worker). The Feasibility Study (FS) formally evaluated four alternatives—No Action, Institutional Controls, Removal, and Containment—using the CERCLA nine-criteria analysis.

**Key results** confirm that HHRA risk calculations remain valid, with all cancer risks and hazard indices falling below regulatory thresholds. Maximum barium concentrations in Stockpile #2 reached 130,000 mg/kg, exceeding the residential CHHSL of 5,200 mg/kg but being a primary driver for containment. Groundwater chemistry was similar to background and did not exceed California MCLs. The containment alternative minimizes long-term cost and environmental impacts, whereas removal would generate significant short-term impacts, including 529,200 pounds of CO2 emissions and heavy truck traffic.

**Limitations** include the high heterogeneity of barium concentrations in the soil, which caused variability in analytical results. The 2004 cadmium data are now considered false positives due to analytical interference from high barium levels. The containment alternative relies on the SR-132 Project's schedule, with full containment not achieved until its ultimate build-out phase (2028). Monitoring results regarding contaminant migration from the stockpiles to groundwater remain inconclusive.
