### Combined Summary

The Caltrans Modesto Soil Stockpile site consists of three stockpiles (totaling approximately 160,000 yd³) containing soil with elevated barium from historical FMC chemical processing operations. Multiple investigations were conducted to characterize the contamination: a 2004 Preliminary Site Investigation (194 stockpile samples), a 2006 Site Investigation (165 stockpile samples with 8 groundwater monitoring wells), and a 2012 Supplemental Site Investigation (68 borings). Barium is the primary contaminant of concern, with maximum concentrations of 72,000 mg/kg in Stockpile 3 and 64,000 mg/kg in Stockpile 2, far exceeding background levels (17-120 mg/kg). Notably, elevated cadmium detected in earlier investigations was determined to be a false positive likely due to analytical interference from high barium concentrations, as it was not detected above 1.0 mg/kg in any of the 348 samples from later studies.

The Human Health Risk Assessments (2007 and 2013 update) concluded that under current and proposed future land uses, the stockpiles pose no unacceptable cancer or non-cancer risks to receptors (trespassers, residents, construction workers) when properly managed with fencing and restricted access. However, the assessment had limitations including unresolved cadmium discrepancies between investigations and inability to collect background stormwater samples in 2017.

Four remedial alternatives were evaluated against nine NCP criteria (threshold, balancing, and modifying criteria) for the site. Alternative 1 (No Action) provided lowest protection, Alternative 2 (Institutional Controls) offered moderate protection but lacked regulatory acceptance, Alternative 3 (Removal) was most protective but prohibitively expensive at $21.5 million with no funding source, and Alternative 4 (Containment) was identified as the preferred option. The recommended remedy involves incorporating the stockpiles as highway embankment fill for the SR-132 Project, capped by roadways, retaining walls, or clean soil. This containment approach provides the second-highest level of protection while being cost-effective due to available construction funding, minimizing contaminant migration to groundwater, and using the stockpiles for their originally intended purpose. The document notes that recommendations are based on sampling to date and applicable only as of the document date, and public acceptance will be assessed after the comment period.
