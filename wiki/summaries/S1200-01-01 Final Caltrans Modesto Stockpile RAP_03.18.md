The document is a Final Remedial Action Plan (RAP) for three Caltrans soil stockpiles in Modesto associated with the SR-132 West Freeway/Expressway Project. The stockpiles consist of historical FMC chemical-processing tailings/soils and contain contaminants of potential concern, with barium identified as the primary concern; cadmium and PAHs were also noted in parts of the investigation. Site characterization was developed through multiple investigations from 2003 through 2012, including extensive soil borings and sampling, groundwater monitoring wells and repeated groundwater sampling, and stormwater/runoff sampling.

The human health risk assessments, first completed in 2007 and updated with 2012 supplemental data, concluded that the stockpiles do not pose unacceptable risk to current or future residents, trespassers, construction workers, or hypothetical shallow groundwater users if the site remains properly managed. Based on a CERCLA/NCP feasibility study using the nine remedy-evaluation criteria, four alternatives were compared: no action, institutional controls, removal/offsite disposal, and containment.

The analysis found that full removal would provide the greatest reduction in exposure and contaminant mass, but it would be expensive, disruptive, and not aligned with available project funding. Institutional controls alone were considered less protective because they do not reduce contaminant mass. The preferred remedy is Alternative 4, containment, implemented by incorporating the stockpiles into SR-132 highway embankment fill and/or covering them with pavement and clean fill, or by using a clean soil cap where needed. This approach is favored because it is protective of human health and the environment, technically feasible, cost-effective, and reduces the potential for contaminant migration through groundwater and stormwater pathways.
