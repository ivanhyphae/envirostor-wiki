The document is a Remedial Design Implementation Plan and supporting health-and-safety framework for the Caltrans Modesto Soil Stockpiles remediation at the SR-132 West Project in Stanislaus County, California. It focuses on **Interim Phase 1** work for three stockpiles located south of SR-99/Kansas Avenue and describes how DTSC-approved containment will be implemented by excavating and consolidating barium-containing soils into highway embankment fill, then isolating them beneath structural pavement and/or imported clean fill. The plan also addresses a separate DTSC framework for managing **aerially deposited lead (ADL)** soils in Caltrans rights-of-way, allowing reuse or burial under strict conditions related to lead concentration, depth of placement, cover, groundwater protection, and land-use restrictions.

The RDIP draws on prior site investigations and risk assessments, including the 2004 and 2007 investigations, the 2007 HHRA and 2013 update, and the 2018 Final Remedial Action Plan. For the stockpile remedy, it lays out construction sequencing, retaining wall and cap work, well abandonment/protection, clean-fill import, verification sampling, reporting, and long-term maintenance. Sampling requirements are specified for excavation and activity areas, with analytical confirmation for barium and lead using EPA Method 6010B. Groundwater, stormwater, and perimeter air monitoring are part of the implementation program to confirm the remedy remains protective.

The document reports that the stockpiles contain substantial material: approximately **34,000 yd³** in Stockpile 1, **102,000 yd³** in Stockpile 2, and **24,000 yd³** in Stockpile 3. Planned barium-containing soil removal totals about **27,850 yd³**, while the combined barium/ADL consolidation area has capacity for about **54,500 yd³**, leaving roughly **26,650 yd³** available for ADL placement. Verification thresholds are set at **1,000 mg/kg barium** and **80 mg/kg lead**. The text also notes that partial removal of Stockpile 3 was completed in 2012, with about **2,800 yd³** removed.

A substantial portion of the document describes the regulatory basis for managing **ADL soils**. Caltrans and DTSC conclude that ADL found in highway rights-of-way can be safely managed in place, reused, transported, or buried under controlled conditions without unacceptable risk. The agreement replaces the older variance approach with a statewide system of notification, oversight, and recordkeeping. The technical basis includes sampling from Caltrans sites and partitioning-coefficient data from **595 ADL-contaminated soil samples**, which showed a mean Kd far above the immobility threshold, supporting the conclusion that groundwater leaching risk is low. Decision criteria include total lead and extractable lead thresholds, water-table separation, pavement or soil cover, dust and stormwater controls, and institutional controls such as land-use covenants.

The health and safety sections conclude that airborne exposure risks are low when the prescribed controls are followed. The plan relies on wet sampling, dust suppression, PPE, decontamination, and buddy-system field practices, supported by prior industrial hygiene data and a **Negative Exposure Assessment**. Monitoring from September 2012 during Stockpile 3 excavation/loading showed exposures to lead and barium sulfate well below applicable limits, and perimeter dust monitoring uses a trigger of **4 mg/m³ above background**. At the same time, the document notes a limitation: no new industrial hygiene monitoring will be performed for inorganic lead, barium sulfate, or other toxic metals during general survey, soil sampling, or ambient air monitoring tasks.
