The document addresses the management of barium- and lead‑contaminated soil stockpiles at the Caltrans Modesto site as part of a Remedial Design Implementation Plan (RDIP). For barium‑containing soil, the approved remedial alternative is containment by incorporating the material as highway embankment fill during the SR‑132 West Project, covered with structural pavement and clean fill to eliminate direct exposure and protect groundwater and surface water. An interim vegetated clean soil cap will cover northern slopes until the Ultimate Phase 2.

For lead contamination—primarily from aerially deposited lead (ADL) near roadways—the document describes management under a formal Agreement that waives certain hazardous waste requirements for reuse within highway rights‑of‑way. Sampling data (595 samples) show lead is tightly bound to soil (mean Kd = 333 L/kg, far above the immobility threshold of 20 L/kg), indicating low leaching potential. Soil containing >80 mg/kg total lead (95% UCL) and/or >5 mg/L extractable lead (California WET) is classified as ADL material. Soil with total lead ≤320 mg/kg and extractable lead ≤5 mg/L may be placed without cover; higher concentrations require burial under at least 1 foot of clean soil or pavement, at least 5 feet above the maximum historical water table. Soil with pH ≤5.0 or extractable lead >150 mg/L (DI‑WET) is not eligible for management under the Agreement.

Proposed post‑excavation soil confirmation levels are 1,000 mg/kg for barium and 80 mg/kg for lead, derived from leaching tests (DI‑WET) and attenuation modeling. The barium level corresponds to a DI‑WET concentration of about 1 mg/L (protective of the California MCL of 1.0 mg/L using an attenuation factor of 1). The lead level is based on limited data and the residential soil screening level; only two samples exceeded this value. Limitations include a sparse lead dataset (two outliers considered a “nugget effect”) and the assumption that a single barium screening level suffices despite two chemical forms.

A Health and Safety Plan (HSP) section concludes that the risk of significant airborne exposure during soil characterization tasks is low because wet sampling methods and safe work practices will be used. Negative Exposure Assessments from September 2012 documented airborne lead concentrations consistently below the 30 µg/m³ Action Level and orders of magnitude below permissible exposure limits for lead and barium sulfate. Consequently, no industrial hygiene monitoring is planned for general survey, excavation soil sampling, or ambient air monitoring tasks. The HSP identifies Title 22 (CAM 17) metals as the primary contaminants and outlines site control, engineering controls, PPE, and sanitation procedures.
