This report presents the results of a February 2014 groundwater monitoring event at the Caltrans Modesto Soil Stockpiles site, based on samples collected from ten monitoring wells (MW-1 through MW-10). The study aimed to assess groundwater chemistry, including dissolved metals, general minerals, and major ions, against state and federal water quality standards.

### Key Findings

Groundwater at the site flows towards the southeast at an average gradient of 0.0008. Chemically, the water can be classified into two types: calcium-dominant and sodium-dominant. A notable change occurred in well MW-5, which switched from calcium-dominant to sodium-dominant in 2013.

Regarding water quality, the key findings are:
- **Dissolved Metals:** No dissolved metals exceeded their numeric thresholds, with the exception of manganese in well MW-10 (73 µg/l, exceeding the 50 µg/l secondary MCL). Barium and strontium concentrations remained well below thresholds and consistent with historical levels. Many other trace metals (e.g., antimony, cadmium, lead, selenium) were not detected.
- **Nitrate:** Nitrate (as N) exceeded the primary Maximum Contaminant Level (MCL) of 10 mg/l in four wells: MW-1 (10 mg/l), MW-5 (34 mg/l), MW-6 (15 mg/l), and MW-10 (18 mg/l). The concentration in the upgradient MW-1 may represent natural background levels.
- **Total Dissolved Solids (TDS):** TDS exceeded the secondary MCL of 500 mg/l in three wells: MW-5 (750 mg/l), MW-6 (570 mg/l), and MW-10 (630 mg/l).

### Methodology and Data Quality

Samples were collected on February 25-26, 2014, and analyzed by Advanced Technology Laboratories (ATL). Analytical methods included EPA 6020 (ICP-MS) for dissolved metals, EPA 300 (ion chromatography) for anions, and SM 2540C for TDS, among others. Quality control procedures, including blanks, laboratory control samples (LCS), and matrix spikes (MS/MSD), were performed.

While LCS recoveries were generally acceptable, several MS/MSD recoveries fell outside control limits (e.g., for barium, calcium, and strontium). Analysts attributed these to matrix interference from the high-concentration samples. Consequently, some data required dilution (flagged D6) and/or qualification for matrix effects (flagged M1/M2), but no data were deemed unusable. Well MW-6 was excluded from the February 2014 groundwater elevation map used for calculating the gradient.

### Overall Conclusion

The February 2014 monitoring event confirmed historical groundwater flow patterns but revealed persistent exceedances of nitrate and TDS MCLs in several wells. While dissolved metals were not a widespread concern, isolated exceedances of manganese occurred, and matrix effects presented challenges for the analytical data quality of some cations.
