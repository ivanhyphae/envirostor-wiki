## Combined Summary: Caltrans Modesto Soil Stockpiles – March 2013 Groundwater Monitoring

This document presents the results of a groundwater monitoring event conducted on March 18–19, 2013, at the Caltrans Modesto Soil Stockpiles site (Project No. S9525-01-44B). Ten monitoring wells (MW‑1 through MW‑10) and one equipment blank were sampled to assess water quality, focusing on Title 22 dissolved metals, general minerals, and major ions. The data are compared to California Primary and Secondary Maximum Contaminant Levels (MCLs) and EPA Drinking Water Health Advisories.

### Key Findings and Exceedances

- **Primary Contaminants of Concern**: Barium (43–300 µg/L), lead (2.2–2.5 µg/L), and strontium (250–1,100 µg/L) were all detected below their respective thresholds (barium: 1,000/700 µg/L; lead: 15 µg/L; strontium: 4,000 µg/L). Chromium, arsenic, and vanadium also remained below regulatory limits.
- **Nitrate and TDS Exceedances**: Nitrate (as nitrogen) exceeded the primary MCL of 10 mg/L in MW‑1 (13 mg/L), MW‑5 (30 mg/L), MW‑6 (17 mg/L), and MW‑10 (17 mg/L). Total Dissolved Solids (TDS) met or exceeded the secondary MCL of 500 mg/L in the same wells (MW‑1: 500 mg/L; MW‑5: 700 mg/L; MW‑6: 600 mg/L; MW‑10: 620 mg/L).
- **Other Constituents**: Manganese exceeded its secondary MCL (50 µg/L) in MW‑1 (160 µg/L) and MW‑7 (260 µg/L). Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) were absent from all samples, leading to discontinuation of PAH analysis after November 2012. Total sulfide was detected in seven wells (0.010–0.18 mg/L). Mercury was non-detectable in all samples.

### Groundwater Chemistry and Flow

The shallow aquifer is generally bicarbonate-dominant, with calcium-dominant composition in most wells and sodium-dominant in MW‑3, MW‑6, and MW‑8. Groundwater flows east-southeast with an average hydraulic gradient of 0.0006 ft/ft. Depth to groundwater ranged from 31.15 ft (MW‑1) to 39.62 ft (MW‑5) below TOC.

### Methodology and Quality Control

- **Field**: Wells were purged using submersible pumps or bailers until three well volumes were removed (2–6.5 gallons). MW‑5 and MW‑6 went dry after 1 and 1.5 gallons, respectively, and were allowed to recover. Samples were collected with bailers; dissolved metals were field-filtered (0.45 µm). An equipment blank was collected.
- **Laboratory**: Analyses were performed by Advanced Technology Laboratories (California-certified). Methods included EPA 300.0 (anions), SM 2320B (alkalinity), SM 2540C (TDS), SM 4500-S=D (sulfide), EPA 6020 (dissolved metals by ICP-MS), and EPA 7470 (mercury by cold vapor AA).
- **QA/QC**: Quality control included blanks, laboratory control samples, matrix spikes (MS), and duplicates. Some matrix spike recoveries fell outside acceptance criteria (70–130%) for barium, magnesium, sodium, and strontium due to high native concentrations. Relative percent differences (RPD) exceeding 20% were noted in a few duplicate pairs (e.g., molybdenum). The equipment blank showed trace chromium (0.91 µg/L), but sample concentrations were considered unaffected.

### Limitations

- **Low-yield wells**: MW‑5 and MW‑6 purged dry before reaching three well volumes, potentially reducing sample representativeness. Redevelopment of MW‑5 is recommended.
- **Turbidity**: MW‑5 consistently showed higher turbidity, suggesting possible sediment accumulation near the well screen.
- **Background influence**: Upgradient well MW‑1 exhibited elevated nitrate (13 mg/L) and TDS (500 mg/L), indicating natural background contributions.
- **Analytical issues**: High-concentration analytes required substantial dilutions (up to 50×), increasing uncertainty. Internal standard failures necessitated re-analysis for some samples. The data represent a single sampling event and may not capture temporal variability. No organic parameters (VOCs, pesticides) were analyzed.
