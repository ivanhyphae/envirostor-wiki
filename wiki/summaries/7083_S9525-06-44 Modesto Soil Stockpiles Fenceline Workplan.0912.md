The document combines a supplemental site investigation workplan and a health and safety plan for field activities associated with the Modesto Soil Stockpiles / Modesto Ramp Rehabilitation project near SR 99/SR 132 in Stanislaus County, California. Its main purpose is to support future construction and remedy-selection decisions by filling regulatory data gaps identified by Caltrans, DTSC, and RWQCB, while also protecting workers during intrusive sampling.

The site consists of legacy soil stockpiles, reportedly about 121,000 cubic yards, partly derived from excavation of former FMC evaporation ponds and other barium-processing-related material. Prior assessments found elevated barium in some stockpiles, some cadmium, and PAHs as contaminants of potential concern. The workplan notes that earlier risk evaluations and DTSC review indicated the stockpiles, as then managed, did not present a human health risk to workers or nearby receptors, but additional characterization was still needed to confirm lateral extent, vertical migration, and perimeter conditions for project approval and RAP development.

To address those gaps, the workplan proposes 29 fenceline borings to 5 feet, 22 perimeter borings to 3 feet, and 5 deeper cadmium-focused borings to 20 feet within Stockpiles 2 and 3. Sampling includes continuous soil collection in acetate sleeves for fenceline and perimeter borings, surface and interval samples for shallow borings, and deeper interval sampling in cadmium borings, with extra sampling if visual signs of contamination are observed. Drilling would use a direct-push Geoprobe rig and hand augers where needed, with GPS logging, geologic logging under a California Professional Geologist, decontamination, chain-of-custody, and backfilling procedures. Analytical testing is targeted to Title 22 metals, strontium, mercury, barium, and cadmium, with expedited turnaround and QA/QC that includes blanks, duplicates, and spikes.

The health and safety portion of the document frames the field work as a controlled soil-sampling operation involving direct-push and hand-auger methods near the stockpile area. It identifies the main hazards as vehicle/traffic exposure, mechanical injury, utility/electrical hazards, noise, biological hazards, and potential chemical exposure from contaminated soils. The plan concludes that, with wet methods, traffic controls, utility locates, the buddy system, hygiene, decontamination, and appropriate PPE, exposure risks for metals, asbestos, and lead are low to moderate. It relies on prior negative exposure assessments to support the position that medical surveillance for asbestos or toxic metals is not required for this project, though respirator users must be medically cleared.

Historical information summarized in the document includes prior investigations from 2003, 2004, 2006, 2007, and 2009. These indicate that two stockpiles contained elevated barium, groundwater was first encountered around 30 feet below ground surface with a deeper aquifer at about 165 feet, and groundwater flow was generally toward the southeast. The plan also cites existing wells in the vicinity and uses occupational exposure limits and action levels for noise, asbestos, lead, arsenic, cadmium, barium sulfate, and CAM 17 metals as the compliance framework. No new field results are reported in the excerpt; it is primarily a precautionary, implementation-oriented workplan.
